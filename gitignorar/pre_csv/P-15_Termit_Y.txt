<p>The <b>P-15 <i>Termit</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>: <span lang="ru">П-15 "Термит"</span>; English: <span lang="en"><a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termite</a></span>) is an anti-ship <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> developed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s <a href="/wiki/MKB_Raduga" title="MKB Raduga">Raduga design bureau</a> in the 1950s. Its <a href="/wiki/GRAU" title="GRAU">GRAU</a> designation was <b>4K40</b>, its <a href="/wiki/NATO_reporting_name" title="NATO reporting name">NATO reporting name</a> was <i><b>Styx</b></i> or <b>SS-N-2</b>.  China acquired the design in 1958 and created at least four versions: the <b>CSS-N-1 <i>Scrubbrush</i></b> and <b>CSS-N-2</b> versions were developed for ship-launched operation, while the <b>CSS-C-2 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silkworm_missile" title="Silkworm missile">Silkworm</a></i></b> and <b>CSS-C-3 <i>Seersucker</i></b> were used for <a href="/wiki/Coastal_defence_and_fortification" title="Coastal defence and fortification">coastal defence</a>. Other names for this basic type of missile include: <b>HY-1</b>, <b>SY-1</b>, and <b>FL-1 <i>Flying Dragon</i></b> (Chinese designations typically differ for export and domestic use, even for otherwise identical equipment) . North Korean local produced <b>KN-1</b> or <b>KN-01</b>, derived from both Silkworm variants and Russian &amp; URSS <b>P-15</b>, Rubezh, P-20 P-22 .
</p><p>Despite its massive size, thousands of P-15s were built and installed on many classes of ships from MTBs to destroyers, as well as coastal batteries and even bombers (Chinese versions). The P-15 was quite successful in the conflicts where it was deployed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The P-15 was not the first anti-ship missile in Soviet service; that distinction goes to the <a href="/wiki/KSShch" title="KSShch">SS-N-1 <i>Scrubber</i></a>, and to the aircraft-launched <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raduga_KS-1_Komet" title="Raduga KS-1 Komet">AS-1 Kennel</a>. The SS-N-1 was a powerful but rather raw system, and it was quickly superseded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-5_Pyatyorka" title="P-5 Pyatyorka">SS-N-3 <i>Shaddock</i></a>. This weapon was  fitted to 4,000-ton <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kynda_class_cruiser" title="Kynda class cruiser">Kynda class cruisers</a> and replaced an initial plan for 30,000-ton battlecruisers armed with 305mm and 45mm guns. Rather than rely on a few heavy and costly ships, a new weapons system was designed to fit smaller, more numerous vessels, while maintaining sufficient striking power. The P-15 was developed by the Soviet designer Beresyniak, who helped in the development of the BI rocket interceptor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:INS_Chamak_(K95)_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/INS_Chamak_%28K95%29_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg/220px-INS_Chamak_%28K95%29_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/INS_Chamak_%28K95%29_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg/330px-INS_Chamak_%28K95%29_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/INS_Chamak_%28K95%29_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg/440px-INS_Chamak_%28K95%29_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:INS_Chamak_(K95)_fires_a_P-15_Termit_missile.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/INS_Chamak_(K95)" title="INS Chamak (K95)">INS Chamak (K95)</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Navy" title="Indian Navy">Indian Navy</a> fires a P-15 Termit missile</div></div></div>
<p>The first variant was the P-15, with fixed wings. The basic design of the missile, retained for all subsequent versions, featured a cylindrical body, a rounded nose, two <a href="/wiki/Delta_wing" title="Delta wing">delta wings</a> in the center and three control surfaces in the tail. It was also fitted with a solid-fueled booster under the belly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> This design was based on the <a href="/wiki/Yakovlev_Yak-1000" title="Yakovlev Yak-1000">Yak-1000</a> experimental fighter built in 1951.
</p><p>The weapon was meant to be cheap, but at the same time capable of giving an ordinary <a href="/wiki/Missile_boat" title="Missile boat">missile boat</a> the same 'punch' as a battleship's salvo. The onboard electronics were based on a simple analog design, with a homing <a href="/wiki/Conical_scanning" title="Conical scanning">conical scanning</a> <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> sensor. It used a more reliable rocket engine with acid fuel in preference to a turbojet.
</p><p>Some shortcomings were never totally solved, due to the liquid propellant of the rocket engine: the acid fuel gradually corroded the missile fuselage. Launches were not possible outside a temperature range of -15/+38C°.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The missile weighed around 2,340 kg, had a top speed of Mach 0.9 and a range of 40 km. The explosive warhead was behind the fuel tank, and as the missile retained a large amount of unburned fuel at the time of impact, even at maximum range, it acted as an <a href="/wiki/Incendiary_device" title="Incendiary device">incendiary device</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The warhead itself was a 500 kg hollow charge (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_explosive_anti-tank_warhead" title="High explosive anti-tank warhead">HEAT</a>), larger than the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armor-piercing_shot_and_shell#Armor-piercing_shells" title="Armor-piercing shot and shell">semi-armour piercing (SAP)</a> warhead typical of anti-ship missiles. The launch was usually made with the help of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_support_measures" title="Electronic support measures">electronic support measures</a> (ESM) gear and Garpun <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> at a range of between 5.5 and 27 km due to the limitations of the targeting system. The Garpun's range against a destroyer was about 20 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The onboard <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensor</a> was activated at 11 km from impact, the missile would begin to descend at 1-2° to the target, because the flight pattern was about 120–250m above sea level. In minimum range engagements there was the possibility of using active sensors at shorter distances, as little as 2.75 km.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
The <b>P-15U</b> was introduced in 1965, with improved avionics and folding wings, enabling the use of smaller containers. It was replaced by the <b>P-15M</b> in 1972, which was a further development of the P-15U, with enhanced capabilities (its export simplified variants were designated <b>P-21</b> and <b>P-22</b>, depending on the sensor installed and a whole export system was designated the <b>P-20M</b>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Versions">Versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Termit_Missile.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3508" data-file-width="5262" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Termit_Missile.jpg/220px-Termit_Missile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Termit_Missile.jpg/330px-Termit_Missile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Termit_Missile.jpg/440px-Termit_Missile.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Termit_Missile.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>P-15 missile, Steven F. Udvar-Hazy Center</div></div></div>
<dl><dt><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f3/Flag_of_Russia.svg/23px-Flag_of_Russia.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f3/Flag_of_Russia.svg/35px-Flag_of_Russia.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f3/Flag_of_Russia.svg/45px-Flag_of_Russia.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a></dt></dl>
<p>In total, the P-15 family had the following models:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>P-15</b>: A basic (SS-N-2A) with I-band, a conical search sensor and 40 km range.</li>
<li><b>P-15M</b>: (SS-N-2C), heavier and longer than the P-15, it had a range of 80 km and several minor improvements.</li>
<li><b>P-15MC</b>: Essentially a P-15M, coupled with a Bulgarian-made <a href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasure" title="Electronic countermeasure">electronic countermeasure</a> package for that country's <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Navy" title="Bulgarian Navy">navy</a>.</li>
<li><b>P-20</b>: A P-15 updated with the new guidance system  but with the original shorter range. They were perhaps known as SS-N2 B and used by <i>Komar</i> and <i>Osa</i> class boats.</li>
<li><b>P-20K</b>: A P-15M with a new guidance system.</li>
<li><b>P-20M</b>: A surface version of the P-20L with folding wings. This was the definitive version of the P-15M with radar guidance.</li>
<li><b>P-22</b> other development of or along P-20 ; other variants P-21, P-27</li>
<li><b>4K51 Rubezh</b> and 4K40, SS-N-2 2c SSC-3 Styx, using P-20 and P-22</li></ul>
<dl><dt><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Flag_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China.svg/23px-Flag_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Flag_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China.svg/35px-Flag_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Flag_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China.svg/45px-Flag_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/China" title="China">People's Republic of China</a></dt></dl>
<p>The Chinese used this missile as a basis for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silkworm_missile" title="Silkworm missile"><i>Silkworm</i></a> series, with IR, radar and turbojets or rocket engines depending on the model. It had a fuselage of 75–80 cm width and a mass of over 2 tonnes. This is comparable to the 600–800 kg and 35–40 cm of Western missiles. With improved electronics, the warhead reduced to 250 kg and the original rocket engine replaced with a turbojet, this weapon was much improved with a range of over 100 km. Chinese Silkworm missiles were used in hundreds of ships and shore batteries. The Chinese Navy built more than two hundred modified versions of the 183R (<i>Komar</i>-class), the <i>Hegu</i>-class, (complete with a longer hull and an additional 25mm mount aft) and the <i>Osa</i>-class. Frigates and destroyers were also equipped with the missile. Some were exported and they were used in shore batteries built for North Korea, Iraq and Iran. The Soviet Union developed an equivalent, the <a href="/wiki/P-120_Malakhit" title="P-120 Malakhit">P-120 Malakhit</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="2560" decoding="async" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg/220px-SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg/330px-SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg/440px-SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SY-1_Missile_20170919.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>SY-1 Missile</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b><span id="SY-1">SY-1</span></b> (C.201) (SY is the abbreviation of <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: Shàng Yóu, literal meaning is upper river): The original Chinese copy of P-15 as ship-to-ship missile, called as Project 544, designed and assembled by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nanchang_Aerocraft_Factory&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nanchang Aerocraft Factory (page does not exist)">Nanchang Aerocraft Factory</a> from 1960, first inland test flight in December 1964 and ship-mounted test-fired in August 1965, finished the research tests in June 1966, began definitizing test from Nov 1966, permitted definitize on August 1967. It entered service during 1968 in missile boats and destroyers and later coastal batteries. Dimensions were: 6.55m (length), 0.76m (diameter), 2.4m (wingspan). It weighed 2,095 kg of which 513 kg was the HEAT warhead. Its range was 40 km at mach 0.8, with a flight altitude of 100–300 m, it used inertial and active radar guidance systems. This unit employed conical scanning and was vulnerable to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronic_counter_measures" title="Electronic counter measures">electronic counter measures</a> (ECM), due to its slow onboard computer. The SY-1A entered service after 1984, with a monopulse search radar comparable to the evolution of the <a href="/wiki/AIM-7_Sparrow" title="AIM-7 Sparrow">AIM-7</a>, F to M model.</li>
<li><b>SY-2</b>: An improved version developed from 1976. Used the solid rocket engine and supersonics flight, smaller and lighter than SY-1, extended range to 50 km. The exported version is FL-2.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="2700" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg/220px-HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg/330px-HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg/440px-HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HY-2_Missile_20170902.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>HY-2 Missile</div></div></div>
<ul><li><b><span id="HY-2">HY-2</span></b> (C.201) (HY is the abbreviation of <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>:Hǎi Yīng, literal meaning is Sea Eagle): It was the equivalent of the P-15M, and was known as the C-SS-3 <i>Saccade</i>. Designed for coastal batteries, with a larger airframe, its dimensions were: 7.48m × 0.76m × 2.4m, weight 2,998 kg. extended range from 30 km of SY-1 to 50 km. Trials were carried out from 1967 to 1970 with 10 missiles out of 11 hitting the target. It entered service in China and was also exported. There were several versions:
<ul><li><b>HY-2</b>: Basic, inertial and conical radar search (improved to SY-1), 1970.</li>
<li><b>HY-2A</b>: IR-guidance variant. Developed during the 1970s and in 1980, it did not enter service despite certification in 1982. It was the equivalent of the P-22.</li>
<li><b>HY-2A-II</b>: An improved variant of the HY-2A with an improved IR sensor, it entered service in 1988. It was also available for export.</li>
<li><b><span id="HY-2B">HY-2B</span></b>: Fitted with monopulse-search radar to improve accuracy and reliability, it was test-fired, scoring five hits out of six and entered service two years later in 1984. The <b>YB-2B-II</b> had another radar search system, entering service in 1989. These two missiles were capable of flying at an altitude of 20–50m, so the overall capabilities (altitude, range, reliability, Electronic Counter Counter Measures (ECCM) were greatly superior.</li>
<li><b><span id="C-201W">C-201W</span></b>: Fitted with a turbojet engine instead of a liquid rocket version. It was only used for export, it had a 150 km range. It is arguably also called <b>HY-4</b> or <b>C-SS-N-7 Sadpack</b>, its dimensions are similar to the HY-1 and HY-2, but its weight is only 2,000 kg, demonstrating the differences between turbojet and rocket propulsion systems. It is capable of flying at 70m and attacking at 8m, with a 300–500 kg charge. The <b>XW-41</b> land attack missile was extrapolated from this design, it had a range of about 400 km, (which was enough to attack Taiwan). It is not known if this model entered service.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Substitutes of these missiles are the <b>FL-2</b> and <b>FL-7</b>, which were solid-rocket fuelled and the <b>C-701</b> and <b>C-801</b>, which were similar to the <a href="/wiki/Exocet" title="Exocet">Exocet</a> and other missile systems, among them the <a href="/wiki/SS-N-22" title="SS-N-22">SS-N-22</a> <i>Sunburn</i>, it was bought for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sovremenny_class_destroyer" title="Sovremenny class destroyer">Sovremenny class destroyers</a>.
</p>
<dl><dt><span class="datasortkey" data-sort-value="North Korea"><span class="flagicon"><img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="12" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Flag_of_North_Korea.svg/23px-Flag_of_North_Korea.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Flag_of_North_Korea.svg/35px-Flag_of_North_Korea.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Flag_of_North_Korea.svg/46px-Flag_of_North_Korea.svg.png 2x" width="23"/> </span><a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a></span></dt></dl>
<ul><li><b>KN-1</b> or <b>KN-01</b> locally produced <b>Geum Seong-1</b> Korean <b>금성-1호</b>, derived from both Silkworm and Russian P-15 Termit, Rubezh, P-20 P-22 .</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_usage">Operational usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuban_Missile_Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cuban Missile Crisis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The first use of these weapons was in 1962, during the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>. <i>Komar</i>-class missile boats were deployed in Operation "Anadyr" ("Анадырь"), organized by the Soviet Union to help the Castro government. At least eight were sent in cargo ships, due partly to their small dimensions and were presumably left to the Cuban Navy after the crisis, together with many other weapons of Soviet origin.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SS-N-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1570" data-file-width="2840" decoding="async" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/SS-N-2.jpg/220px-SS-N-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/SS-N-2.jpg/330px-SS-N-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/SS-N-2.jpg/440px-SS-N-2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SS-N-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>P-15 missiles on parade</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_of_Attrition">War of Attrition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: War of Attrition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Attrition" title="War of Attrition">War of Attrition</a>, after the <a href="/wiki/Six-Day_War" title="Six-Day War">Six-Day War</a> in 1967, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INS_Eilat_(1955)" title="INS Eilat (1955)">Israeli destroyer <i>Eilat</i></a> was sailing at low speed outside <a href="/wiki/Port_Said" title="Port Said">Port Said</a> on 21 October. At a range of 17 nautical miles (31 km), she was attacked by two Egyptian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Komar_class_missile_boat" title="Komar class missile boat"><i>Komars</i></a>, acting as a coastal missile battery by firing both their missiles from inside the harbour. <i>Eilat</i> was hit, despite defensive anti-aircraft fire. The first two missiles almost blew the <i>Eilat</i> in two; another hit soon after, and the last exploded near the wreck in the sea. <i>Eilat</i> sank two hours after the first attack. 47 crew were killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Missile_Boat_War_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Missile_Boat_War-2">[2]</a></sup> After this engagement, interest in this type of weapon was raised in both offensive weapons and defensive weapons such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CIWS" title="CIWS">CIWS</a> (Close-in weapon system) and ECM.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-Pakistani_War">Indo-Pakistani War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Indo-Pakistani War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main articles: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Trident_(Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971)" title="Operation Trident (Indo-Pakistani War of 1971)">Operation Trident (Indo-Pakistani War of 1971)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Python" title="Operation Python">Operation Python</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971" title="Indo-Pakistani War of 1971">Indo-Pakistani War of 1971</a>, Indian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Osa_class_missile_boat" title="Osa class missile boat"><i>Osa</i>-class boats</a> raided the port of <a href="/wiki/Karachi" title="Karachi">Karachi</a> in two highly successful operations causing severe damage and sinking several ships with their P-15s, among them the destroyer, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Cadiz_(D79)" title="HMS Cadiz (D79)"><i>Khaibar</i></a>. She was a former <a href="/wiki/Battle-class_destroyer" title="Battle-class destroyer">Battle-class destroyer</a>, originally designed as an anti-aircraft ship. Her armament might be effective against conventional air threats, (mounting 5 × 114mm guns and several 40mm Bofors), but had little chance against anti-ship missiles.
</p><p>These raids were meant to strike Karachi and destroy the Pakistani Navy in Western Pakistan. The first action, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Trident_(Indo-Pakistani_War_of_1971)" title="Operation Trident (Indo-Pakistani War of 1971)">Operation Trident</a>, was carried out by three <i>Osa</i> class missile boats on the night of 5 December <a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111207060927/http://www.bharat-rakshak.com/MONITOR/ISSUE4-4/harry.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. 'Operation Trident' involved:
</p>
<ul><li>INS <i>Nipat</i> (Lt.-Cdr B.N Kavina, <a href="/wiki/Vir_Chakra" title="Vir Chakra">VrC</a>)</li>
<li>INS <i>Nirghat</i> (Lt.-Cdr I.J Sharma, <a href="/wiki/Ati_Vishisht_Seva_Medal" title="Ati Vishisht Seva Medal">AVSM</a>, VrC)</li>
<li>INS <i>Veer</i> (Lt.-Cdr O.P Mehta, VrC, NM)</li></ul>
<p>Around 20:30, a target was acquired by radar, at a distance of over 40 miles (64 km), and <i>Nirghat</i> fired two missiles. This target was the destroyer <i>Khaibar</i>, sailing at 20 knots (37 km/h). The crew of the ship saw a "bright light" in the sky, low on the water. Believing it to be the afterburner of a fighter aircraft, <i>Khaibar</i> opened fire with her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bofors_gun" title="Bofors gun">Bofors guns</a>, but these were not effective against such a small, fast target. The missile struck the starboard side at 22:45, destroying the electrical system. One of the boilers, possibly struck by the HEAT charge, also exploded. Despite thick smoke and a fire, <i>Khaibar</i> was still able to engage the second missile, again mistaking it for an enemy fighter. This missile struck the ship four minutes after the first, destroying and quickly sinking her.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1910" data-file-width="2830" decoding="async" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg/220px-KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg/330px-KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg/440px-KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:KT-97M-P-15U-Project205U-1981.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>P-20 launcher on an Osa II class fast attack craft, with wings folded</div></div></div>
<p>During this action, <i>Nipat</i> attacked another two ships; the cargo vessel <i>Venus Challenger</i>, which was carrying ammunition from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon" title="Saigon">Saigon</a>, was destroyed. Her escort, the destroyer <a href="/wiki/HMS_Charity_(R29)" title="HMS Charity (R29)">PNS <i>Shahjahan</i></a> was severely damaged and later scrapped.
</p><p><i>Veer</i> then attacked <i>Muhafiz</i> at 23:05, (she was a minesweeper that had witnessed the attacks against <i>Khaibar</i>); she was hit and disintegrated, throwing most of the crew into the water before she sank.
</p><p><i>Nipat</i> fired two missiles at the port of Karachi. This is the first known use of an anti-ship missile against land targets. Large oil tanks, identified by radar, were hit by the first missile, destroying it, while the second weapon failed. Over the following nights there were other ship actions. Karachi was again attacked with missiles, while <i>Petja</i>-class frigates provided <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine</a> protection to the <i>Osa</i>-class boats.
</p><p>On the night of 8 December, in the second operation, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Python" title="Operation Python">Operation Python</a>, the <i>Osa</i>-class boat <i>Vinash</i>, escorted by two frigates, fired missiles at Karachi in a six-minute action. One missile hit an oil tank, destroying it. The British ship <i>Harmattan</i> was sunk, the Panamanian ship <i>Gulfstar</i> was set on fire. The Pakistan Navy fleet tanker, PNS <i>Dacca</i>, was badly damaged and only survived because the commanding officer, Captain. S.Q. Raza S.J. P.N., ordered the release of steam in the pipes that prevented the fire reaching the tanks. Though anti-aircraft guns opened fire in response, they only managed to hit a Greek ship, <i>Zoë</i>, that was moored in the port and consequently sank.
</p><p>In all these actions against large ships, the P-15 proved to be an effective weapon, with a devastating warhead. Out of eleven missiles fired, only one malfunctioned, giving a 91% success rate. This gave every <i>Osa</i> <a href="/wiki/Fast_attack_craft" title="Fast attack craft">FAC</a> the possibility of striking several targets. Big ships, without any specialized defence, were targets for P-15s.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yom_Kippur_War">Yom Kippur War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Yom Kippur War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite these early successes, the 1973 <a href="/wiki/Yom_Kippur_War" title="Yom Kippur War">Yom Kippur War</a> saw P-15 missiles used by the Egyptian and Syrian navies prove ineffective against Israeli ships. The Israeli Navy had phased out their old ships, building a fleet of <a href="/wiki/Sa%27ar_3-class_missile_boat" title="Sa'ar 3-class missile boat">Sa'ar-class FACs</a>: faster, smaller, more maneuverable and equipped with new missiles and countermeasures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the range of the P-15 was twice that of the Israeli <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gabriel_missile" title="Gabriel missile">Gabriel</a>, allowing Arab ships to fire first, radar jamming and <a href="/wiki/Chaff_(countermeasure)" title="Chaff (countermeasure)">chaff</a> degraded their accuracy. In the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Latakia" title="Battle of Latakia">Battle of Latakia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Baltim" title="Battle of Baltim">Battle of Baltim</a>, several dozen P-15s were fired and all missed. Arab ships did not possess heavy firepower required for surface combat against enemy vessels, usually only 25 and 30mm guns, and <i>Osa</i> and <i>Komar</i> boats were not always able to outrun their Israeli pursuers.
</p>
<h3><span id="Iran.E2.80.93Iraq_War"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran–Iraq_War">Iran–Iraq War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Iran–Iraq War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>P-15 variants, including the Chinese duplication "<i><a href="/wiki/Silkworm_(missile)" title="Silkworm (missile)">Silkworm</a></i>", were employed by <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> against <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> in the 1980–1988 <a href="/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93Iraq_War" title="Iran–Iraq War">Iran–Iraq War</a>, with some success. As the Iranian coastline is longer than Iraq's, control of the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> was relatively easy.  Shore batteries with missiles can control a large part of this area, especially around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hormuz_Strait" title="Hormuz Strait">Hormuz Strait</a>.
</p><p>Iraq also acquired Silkworms, some with an <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">IR</a> homing capability. Iraqi OSA-class missile boats equipped with SS-N-2 used them against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_Navy" title="Iranian Navy">IRIN</a> navy, managed to hit and sink an Iranian <a href="/wiki/La_Combattante_IIa-class_fast_attack_craft" title="La Combattante IIa-class fast attack craft">La Combattante IIa-class fast attack craft</a>, but sustained heavy losses, especially from Iranian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RGM-84_Harpoon" title="RGM-84 Harpoon">Harpoons</a> and <a href="/wiki/AGM-65_Maverick" title="AGM-65 Maverick">Mavericks</a>. Iraqi forces also combined SS-N-2,  launched from <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-22" title="Tupolev Tu-22">Tu-22</a>, French-made <a href="/wiki/Exocet" title="Exocet">Exocet</a> launched from <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_F1" title="Dassault Mirage F1">Mirage F1</a> &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dassault_Super_%C3%89tendard" title="Dassault Super Étendard">Super Etendard</a> and Chinese-made Silkworm as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-601" title="C-601">C-601</a> launched from <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-16" title="Tupolev Tu-16">Tu-16</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xian_H-6" title="Xian H-6">H-6</a> bombers bought from Soviet Union and China to engage the Iranian Navy and tankers carrying Iranian oil.
</p>
<h3><span id="Gulf_War_.281990-1991.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gulf_War_(1990-1991)"><a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a> (1990-1991)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gulf War (1990-1991)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the First Gulf War an Iraqi missile crew attacked US <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Missouri_(BB-63)" title="USS Missouri (BB-63)">USS <i>Missouri</i></a> with a Silkworm, while it was escorting a fleet of minesweepers engaged in coastal anti-mine operations. <a href="/wiki/HMS_Gloucester_(D96)" title="HMS Gloucester (D96)">HMS <i>Gloucester</i></a> engaged the missile with a salvo shot of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_Dart_missile" title="Sea Dart missile">Sea Dart missiles</a> which destroyed it after it had flown over its initial target.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operators">Operators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P-15_Termit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Operators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P-15_operators.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1400" data-file-width="2753" decoding="async" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/P-15_operators.png/400px-P-15_operators.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/P-15_operators.png/600px-P-15_operators.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/P-15_operators.png/800px-P-15_operators.png 2x" width="400"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:P-15_operators.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map with P-15 Termit operators in blue and former operators in red</div></div></div>
<p>The P-15 missile family and their clones were widely deployed from the 1960s. They were big and powerful weapons, but quite cheap and so made in the thousands. It is difficult even to list all the operators.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2810" data-file-width="1860" decoding="async" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG/220px-P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG/330px-P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG/440px-P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:P-15_DN-SC-93-05863.JPEG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A twin vertical launcher aboard the German corvette <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiddensee_(corvette)" title="Hiddensee (corvette)"><i>Hiddensee</i></a>. Note the support for the ventral booster.</div></div></div>
<p>The German Navy, after reunification, gave its stock of almost 200 P-15s to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> in 1991, these weapons being mainly the P-15M/P-22. They were used for missile defence tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
