<p><b>Azra</b> was a Yugoslav <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_ensemble" title="Musical ensemble">band</a> that was one of the most popular acts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_new_wave_music" title="Yugoslav new wave music">Yugoslav new wave music</a> of the 1980s. Azra was formed in 1977 by its frontman <a href="/wiki/Branimir_%C5%A0tuli%C4%87" title="Branimir Štulić">Branimir "Johnny" Štulić</a>. The other two members of the original line-up were Mišo Hrnjak (<a href="/wiki/Bass_guitar" title="Bass guitar">bass</a>) and Boris Leiner (<a href="/wiki/Drum_kit" title="Drum kit">drums</a>). The band is named after a verse <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> from "Der Asra" by <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Heine" title="Heinrich Heine">Heinrich Heine</a>. They are considered to be one of the most influential bands from the Yugoslav new wave rock era and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_rock_scene" title="Yugoslav rock scene">Yugoslav rock scene</a> in general.
</p><p>They released their first single in 1979 with songs "Balkan" and "A šta da radim". The first album named <i><a href="/wiki/Azra_(album)" title="Azra (album)">Azra</a></i> was published in 1980 and achieved commercial success and popularized Azra in Yugoslavia. Their second album was released in 1981. Azra recorded its last studio album <i>Između krajnosti</i> (<i>Between the Extremes</i>) in 1987. In 1988 the band recorded 4LP live album under the name <i>Zadovoljština</i> (<i>Satisfaction</i>), after which Štulić disbanded the band. Štulić recorded three more solo albums since moving to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, where he lives in seclusion. A 2003 <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sretno_dijete" title="Sretno dijete">Sretno dijete</a></i> depicts Azra as the focus of the rock scene in Yugoslavia during the 1980s, along with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bijelo_dugme" title="Bijelo dugme">Bijelo dugme</a>. Even today, Azra remains very popular among youth in the countries of the former Yugoslavia.
</p><p>In 1998, the music critics compiled a <a href="/wiki/YU_100:_najbolji_albumi_jugoslovenske_rok_i_pop_muzike" title="YU 100: najbolji albumi jugoslovenske rok i pop muzike">list of 100 best albums</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yugoslav_pop_and_rock_music" title="Yugoslav pop and rock music">Yugoslav pop and rock music</a>. Five Azra albums were included, three of them in top 10. On the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_B92" title="Radio B92">Radio B92</a>'s <a href="/wiki/B92_Top_100_Domestic_Songs" title="B92 Top 100 Domestic Songs">list of 100 greatest songs of former Yugoslavia</a>, four Azra songs were included, all of them in top 20.
</p>
