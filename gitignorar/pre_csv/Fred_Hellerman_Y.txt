<p><b>Fred Hellerman</b> (May 13, 1927 – September 1, 2016) was an American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_song" title="Folk song">folk singer</a>, guitarist, producer, and songwriter. Hellerman was an original member of the seminal American folk group <a href="/wiki/The_Weavers" title="The Weavers">The Weavers</a>, together with <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Pete Seeger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Hays" title="Lee Hays">Lee Hays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Gilbert" title="Ronnie Gilbert">Ronnie Gilbert</a>. He produced the record album <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Restaurant_(album)" title="Alice's Restaurant (album)">Alice's Restaurant</a></i> (1967) for <a href="/wiki/Arlo_Guthrie" title="Arlo Guthrie">Arlo Guthrie</a>, played accompaniment guitar on scores of folk albums, and wrote a number of folk and protest songs.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_Hellerman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born on May 13, 1927 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn,_New_York" title="Brooklyn, New York">Brooklyn, New York</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup> to Jewish parents, Hellerman was the youngest of three children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theguardian_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-theguardian-2">[2]</a></sup> His father, Harry, was an immigrant from <a href="/wiki/Riga" title="Riga">Riga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> and mother, Clara (<a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">née</a> Robinson), was born in the United States to immigrants from Riga.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He received a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1949<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> at <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_College" title="Brooklyn College">Brooklyn College</a>. In 1948, Hellerman formed <a href="/wiki/The_Weavers" title="The Weavers">the Weavers</a> with <a href="/wiki/Pete_Seeger" title="Pete Seeger">Seeger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Gilbert" title="Ronnie Gilbert">Ronnie Gilbert</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Hays" title="Lee Hays">Lee Hays</a>. Hellerman wrote and co-wrote some of their hits. He also wrote under the aliases Fred Brooks and Bob Hill. Because of his involvement with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">left-wing</a> groups during the 1930s and 1940s, Hellerman came under suspicion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> sympathies during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McCarthy_era" title="McCarthy era">McCarthy era</a>.
</p><p>In 1950, Hellerman was named, along with the rest of <a href="/wiki/The_Weavers" title="The Weavers">the Weavers</a>, in the anti-communist tract <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Channels" title="Red Channels">Red Channels</a></i> and was placed on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_blacklist" title="Hollywood blacklist">industry blacklist</a>. In February 1952, an FBI informant testified that the Weavers were members of the Communist party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telegraph_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-5">[5]</a></sup> The group, unable to perform on television, radio, or in most music halls, broke up in 1952, but resumed singing in 1955. They continued together until 1963 (with changes in personnel). He also played on <a href="/wiki/Joan_Baez" title="Joan Baez">Joan Baez</a>'s eponymous first album in 1960. The Weavers held several reunion concerts in 1980, shortly before Hays' death, which were documented in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Weavers:_Wasn%27t_That_a_Time!" title="The Weavers: Wasn't That a Time!">The Weavers: Wasn't That a Time!</a></i> (1982).
</p><p>Hellermen, writing under the name of Fred Brooks, arranged "Green Grow the Lilacs" on Harry Belafonte's 1959 album, <i>Love is a Gentle Thing</i>. The song was based on a traditional song of Irish origin that was widely sung in the US in the 19th century with different lyrics.  Hellerman wrote two original verses and adapted the chorus.
</p><p>Hellerman married the writer Susan Lardner, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/John_Lardner_(sports_writer)" title="John Lardner (sports writer)">John Lardner</a>, in 1970. The Hellermans had two children, Caleb and Simeon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hellerman was the last surviving original member of the Weavers. He died on September 1, 2016, at his home in Weston, Connecticut, at the age of 89.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washingtonpost_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-washingtonpost-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
