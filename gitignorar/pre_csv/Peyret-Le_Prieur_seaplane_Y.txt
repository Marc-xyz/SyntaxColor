<p>The <b>Peyret-le Prieur seaplane</b> was a low power, two seat <a href="/wiki/Biplane" title="Biplane">biplane</a> <a href="/wiki/Floatplane" title="Floatplane">floatplane</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Training_aircraft" title="Training aircraft">trainer</a> flown in France in 1924. It did not reach production.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peyret-Le_Prieur_seaplane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yves Le Prieur had identified a need for a simple, economical training aircraft on which pupils could master the behaviour of <a href="/wiki/Seaplane" title="Seaplane">seaplanes</a> and approached the designer and constructer Louis Peyret for a suitable aircraft. The outcome was the Peyret-Le Prieur seaplane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flight_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Flight-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was a <a href="/wiki/Biplane#Bays" title="Biplane">single bay</a> biplane, with rectangular plan wings mounted without <a href="/wiki/Biplane#Stagger" title="Biplane">stagger</a> and braced by two pairs of parallel, vertical <a href="/wiki/Bracing_(aeronautics)#Interplane_struts" title="Bracing (aeronautics)">interplane struts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bracing_(aeronautics)#Bracing_wires" title="Bracing (aeronautics)">bracing wires</a>, with a large interplane gap of 1.90 m (6 ft 3 in). The wings were wooden, two-<a href="/wiki/Spar_(aeronautics)" title="Spar (aeronautics)">spar</a>, two-part structures, with <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leading_edges" title="Leading edges">leading edges</a>. Broad, full-span <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ailerons" title="Ailerons">ailerons</a> on upper and lower wings were externally connected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The seaplane had a rectangular section <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>, built around four <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Longerons" title="Longerons">longerons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a> covered. It was widest under the wing leading edge, where on each side a vertical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabane_strut" title="Cabane strut">cabane strut</a> joined the upper longeron to the forward spar, and where pupil and tutor sat <a href="/wiki/Tandem#Side-by-side_seating" title="Tandem">side-by-side</a> in an open <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a>. The short nose ahead of them remained wide and the engine, unusually, was mounted off-set to port. Initially a 12 kW (16 hp) four-cylinder <a href="/wiki/Inline_engine_(aeronautics)" title="Inline engine (aeronautics)">inline</a> <a href="/wiki/Sergant_A" title="Sergant A">Sergant A</a> was used, its <a href="/wiki/Radiator_(engine_cooling)" title="Radiator (engine cooling)">radiator</a> mounted midway up the portside cabane strut. At the rear the large area <a href="/wiki/Empennage" title="Empennage">empennage</a> was conventional, with a large span, rectangular plan <a href="/wiki/Stabilator" title="Stabilator">all-moving tail</a> mounted on top of the fuselage. Its triangular <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fin_(aeronautics)" title="Fin (aeronautics)">fin</a> carried a roughly <a href="/wiki/Rhombus" title="Rhombus">rhomboidal</a> <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a> which reached down to the keel through an elevator cut-out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The seaplane had simple, rectangular cross-section <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Float_(aeronautics)" title="Float (aeronautics)">floats</a>. The flat undersides curved up towards the nose, reaching just beyond the <a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aeronautics)" title="Propeller (aeronautics)">propeller</a> and the aft ends were under the ailerons. There was no step. Each float was attached with four aluminium tube <a href="/wiki/Strut" title="Strut">struts</a>, two leaning inwards to the lower fuselage longeron and two outwards to the wing spars. They were constructed of wood, with <a href="/wiki/Fraxinus" title="Fraxinus">ash</a> frames and <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">mahogany</a> plywood covering.  The tail was protected with a cylindrical float of the same construction, attached longitudinally under the fin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fitted with the Sergant engine, the seaplane began tests on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Annecy" title="Lake Annecy">Lake Annecy</a> in the summer of 1924 but it was unable to reach water speeds fast enough to unstick. In response a 34 kW (45 hp) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anzani_6" title="Anzani 6">Anzani 6</a> <a href="/wiki/Radial_engine" title="Radial engine">radial engine</a> was substituted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flight_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Flight-2">[2]</a></sup> again mounted off-set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes3_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes3-3">[3]</a></sup> With this, the seaplane left the water easily on its first flight on 24 August 1924.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes2-4">[4]</a></sup> Further tests showed that about 19 kW (25 hp) was required for take-off, though once in the air, 11–15 kW (15–20 hp) was enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Flight_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Flight-2">[2]</a></sup> By mid-September nineteen flights had been made, some lasting about 30 minutes, and a passenger had been carried.  The seaplane then returned to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, where it was fitted with a wheeled <a href="/wiki/Landing_gear" title="Landing gear">undercarriage</a> for further tests,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes3_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes3-3">[3]</a></sup> though by January 1925 it was flying from water again, at <a href="/wiki/Pecq" title="Pecq">Pecq</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes4_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes4-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>By September 1924 the aircraft was judged a success and a second was under construction. Le Prieur had plans for a floatplane flying school at Annecy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes3_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes3-3">[3]</a></sup> It is not known if the second machine was completed or the school set up.
</p>
<h2><span id="Specifications_.28Sergant_engine.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_(Sergant_engine)">Specifications (Sergant engine)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peyret-Le_Prieur_seaplane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Specifications (Sergant engine)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0;"><i>Data from</i> Les Ailes, August 1924<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lailes1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lailes1-1">[1]</a></sup></p><p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span>
</p><ul><li><b>Crew:</b> One tutor, one student</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 6.96 m (22 ft 10 in)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan:</b> 10.50 m (34 ft 5 in)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 37.70 m<sup>2</sup> (405.8 sq ft)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">Airfoil</a>:</b> <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen" title="Göttingen">Göttingen</a> 358</li>
<li><b>Empty weight:</b> 255 kg (562 lb)</li>
<li><b>Gross weight:</b> 340 kg (750 lb)</li>
<li><b>Fuel capacity:</b> 15 l (3.3 imp gal; 4.0 US gal)</li>
<li><b>Powerplant:</b> 1 × <a href="/wiki/Sergant_A" title="Sergant A">Sergant A</a> 4-cylinder <a href="/wiki/Inline_engine_(aeronautics)" title="Inline engine (aeronautics)">inline engine</a>, geared down 1: 2.5, 12 kW (16 hp)   at 3,350 rpm</li>
<li><b>Propellers:</b> 2-bladed, 1.95 m (6 ft 5 in) diameter</li></ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Cruise speed:</b> 55–60 km/h (34–37 mph, 30–32 kn)</li>
<li><b>Endurance:</b> 3 hr</li>
<li><b>Service ceiling:</b> 1,500–2,000 m (4,900–6,600 ft) absolute</li></ul>
