<p><i><b>Birth of the Cool</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Compilation_album" title="Compilation album">compilation album</a> by American <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> musician <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a>, released in 1957 on <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It compiles eleven tracks recorded by Davis's <a href="/wiki/Nonet_(music)" title="Nonet (music)">nonet</a> for the label over the course of three sessions during 1949 and 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Featuring unusual instrumentation and several notable musicians, the music consisted of innovative arrangements influenced by <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a> techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">polyphony</a>, and marked a major development in post-<a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> jazz. As the title suggests, these recordings are considered seminal in the history of <a href="/wiki/Cool_jazz" title="Cool jazz">cool jazz</a>. Most of them were originally released in the 10-inch 78-rpm format and are all approximately three minutes long. As of 2019, <a href="/wiki/Lee_Konitz" title="Lee Konitz">Lee Konitz</a> is the only surviving member of the Birth of the Cool sessions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_of_the_Cool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Charlie_Parker,_Tommy_Potter,_Miles_Davis,_Duke_Jordan,_Max_Roach_(Gottlieb_06851).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3613" data-file-width="4813" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Charlie_Parker%2C_Tommy_Potter%2C_Miles_Davis%2C_Duke_Jordan%2C_Max_Roach_%28Gottlieb_06851%29.jpg/250px-Charlie_Parker%2C_Tommy_Potter%2C_Miles_Davis%2C_Duke_Jordan%2C_Max_Roach_%28Gottlieb_06851%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Charlie_Parker%2C_Tommy_Potter%2C_Miles_Davis%2C_Duke_Jordan%2C_Max_Roach_%28Gottlieb_06851%29.jpg/375px-Charlie_Parker%2C_Tommy_Potter%2C_Miles_Davis%2C_Duke_Jordan%2C_Max_Roach_%28Gottlieb_06851%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Charlie_Parker%2C_Tommy_Potter%2C_Miles_Davis%2C_Duke_Jordan%2C_Max_Roach_%28Gottlieb_06851%29.jpg/500px-Charlie_Parker%2C_Tommy_Potter%2C_Miles_Davis%2C_Duke_Jordan%2C_Max_Roach_%28Gottlieb_06851%29.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Charlie_Parker,_Tommy_Potter,_Miles_Davis,_Duke_Jordan,_Max_Roach_(Gottlieb_06851).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Davis (right center) playing in <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>'s quintet, 1947.</div></div></div>
<p>From 1945 to 1948, <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a> played in <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Charlie Parker</a>'s quintet. Davis recorded several albums worth of material with Parker during this period, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker%27s_Savoy_and_Dial_Sessions" title="Charlie Parker's Savoy and Dial Sessions">Parker's Sessions for the Savoy and Dial labels</a>. Davis' <a href="/wiki/First_Miles" title="First Miles">first records</a> released under his own name were recorded with Parker's band, in 1947, and were more arranged and rehearsed than Parker's usual approach to recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> By 1948, Davis had three years of <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a> playing under his belt, but he struggled to match the speed and ranges of the likes of Gillespie and Parker, choosing instead to play in the mid range of his instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1948, Davis, becoming increasingly concerned about growing tensions within the Parker quintet, left the group and began looking for a new band to work with.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the same time, <a href="/wiki/Arrangement" title="Arrangement">arranger</a> <a href="/wiki/Gil_Evans" title="Gil Evans">Gil Evans</a> began hosting gatherings of like-minded, forward-looking musicians at his small basement apartment, located on 55th Street in Manhattan, three blocks away from the jazz nightclubs of <a href="/wiki/52nd_Street_(Manhattan)" title="52nd Street (Manhattan)">52nd Street</a>. Evans had gained a reputation in the jazz world for his orchestration of bebop tunes for the <a href="/wiki/Claude_Thornhill" title="Claude Thornhill">Claude Thornhill</a> orchestra in the mid-1940s. Keeping an open door policy, Evans' apartment came to host many of the young jazz artists of late-1940s <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. The participants engaged in discussions about the future of jazz, including a proposed group with a new sound. According to jazz historian <a href="/wiki/Ted_Gioia" title="Ted Gioia">Ted Gioia</a>: 
</p>
<blockquote><p>[The participants] were developing a range of tools that would change the sound of contemporary music. In their work together, they relied on a rich palette of harmonies, many of them drawn from European impressionist composers. They explored new instrumental textures, preferring to blend the voices of the horns like a choir rather than pit them against each other as the big bands had traditionally done with their thrusting and parrying sections. They brought down the tempos of their music ... they adopted a more lyrical approach to improvisation ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span id="Davis.27s_nonet"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Davis's_nonet">Davis's nonet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_of_the_Cool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Davis's nonet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While Evans had originally hoped to work with Charlie Parker on this project, Evans felt that Parker was too dedicated to his own solo voice and not an ensemble sound that Evans was hoping to tap into.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup> With Parker out of the picture, Davis took the lead on the project; Davis and Evans met in the summer of 1947, discovering a mutual respect for each other's work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The two men decided to draw from the members of the discussion group for the new band, this group eventually becoming the Miles Davis Nonet. Arranger, and baritone saxophonist <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">Gerry Mulligan</a> joined the project, having previously written for <a href="/wiki/Gene_Krupa" title="Gene Krupa">Gene Krupa</a>'s orchestra, as it was felt he could bring a lighter sound that Davis and Evans were looking for.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The arrangers came to the conclusion that the ensemble should feature two saxophones, four brass, and rhythm section for a total of nine players. Evans and Mulligan spent the winter working out instrumentation, augmenting the traditional bop quintet of saxophone (in this case, <a href="/wiki/Alto_saxophone" title="Alto saxophone">alto saxophone</a>), <a href="/wiki/Trumpet" title="Trumpet">trumpet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_section" title="Rhythm section">rhythm section</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Baritone_saxophone" title="Baritone saxophone">baritone saxophone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trombone" title="Trombone">trombone</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_horn" title="French horn">French horn</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tuba" title="Tuba">tuba</a>. The two men looked to create pairings within the ensemble, Mulligan stating: "We picked instruments [with matching timbres] ... and one of each. We had a high section with a trumpet and the alto, we had a middle section with the trombone and the French horn, and a low section with the baritone and tuba. So we had those ... basic colors to work with."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The omission of tenor saxophone was seen as highly unusual, as it was seen as one of the standard jazz instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Davis, Evans, and Mulligan then went about assembling the members of the nonet, Davis and Mulligan taking trumpet and baritone saxophone respectively. For alto saxophone, Davis originally wanted <a href="/wiki/Sonny_Stitt" title="Sonny Stitt">Sonny Stitt</a> for the part, but it was decided that Stitt's sound, much like Parkers, was too bop for what the nonet was pursuing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers,_101_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers,_101-14">[14]</a></sup> On Gerry Mulligan's suggestion, Davis asked alto saxist <a href="/wiki/Lee_Konitz" title="Lee Konitz">Lee Konitz</a> to join the group. Konitz had played with Mulligan in <a href="/wiki/Claude_Thornhill" title="Claude Thornhill">Claude Thornhill</a>'s orchestra, and was seen by some as a stylistic alternative to Parker, with a much lighter and airier sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers,_101_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers,_101-14">[14]</a></sup>  Tuba player <a href="/wiki/Bill_Barber_(musician)" title="Bill Barber (musician)">Bill Barber</a> and French hornist Sandy Siegelstein came to the nonet via the brass section of the Thornhill band, Siegelstein to be later replaced by <a href="/wiki/Junior_Collins" title="Junior Collins">Junior Collins</a>. Trombonist  <a href="/wiki/J._J._Johnson" title="J. J. Johnson">J. J. Johnson</a> was the first choice for the band, but due to engagements with the <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Jacquet" title="Illinois Jacquet">Illinois Jacquet</a> band could not originally play with the nonet, though he was able to record with the group on the final two sessions.  Both bassist <a href="/wiki/Al_McKibbon" title="Al McKibbon">Al McKibbon</a> and pianist and arranger <a href="/wiki/John_Lewis_(pianist)" title="John Lewis (pianist)">John Lewis</a> had known Davis as members of <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Gillespie" title="Dizzy Gillespie">Dizzy Gillespie</a>'s orchestra. Drummer <a href="/wiki/Max_Roach" title="Max Roach">Max Roach</a> had been a member of Parker's quintet with Davis and was a natural choice for the group due to his enthusiastic engagement in the ideals of the nonet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Davis was able to secure a two-week engagement in September 1948 for the nonet opening for <a href="/wiki/Count_Basie" title="Count Basie">Count Basie</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Roost" title="Royal Roost">Royal Roost</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. For the band's book, Mulligan contributed six arrangements, Lewis three, Evans two, and composer <a href="/wiki/John_Carisi" title="John Carisi">John Carisi</a> arranged his own composition, "<a href="/wiki/Israel_(composition)" title="Israel (composition)">Israel</a>", for the band. On Davis's insistence, a sign was placed outside the Roost saying, "Arrangements by Gerry Mulligan, Gil Evans, and John Lewis," an unusual advertisement that highlighted the collaborative nature of the venture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The band played at the Roost from August 30 to September 18. For these live dates, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Zwerin" title="Mike Zwerin">Mike Zwerin</a> played trombone and former <a href="/wiki/Dizzy_Gillespie" title="Dizzy Gillespie">Dizzy Gillespie</a> vocalist <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Hagood" title="Kenny Hagood">Kenny Hagood</a> sang on "Darn That Dream" and "Why Do I Love You". There was a further short residency the following year at the Clique Club, these three sets making up the nonet's only live appearances. Arranger and <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> <a href="/wiki/Artists_and_repertoire" title="Artists and repertoire">talent scout</a> <a href="/wiki/Pete_Rugolo" title="Pete Rugolo">Pete Rugolo</a> heard the nonet at the Royal Roost and offered them a chance to make a record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_of_the_Cool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pete_Rugolo,_New_York,_N.Y.,_between_1946_and_1948_(William_P._Gottlieb_07531).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1248" data-file-width="928" decoding="async" height="269" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Pete_Rugolo%2C_New_York%2C_N.Y.%2C_between_1946_and_1948_%28William_P._Gottlieb_07531%29.jpg/200px-Pete_Rugolo%2C_New_York%2C_N.Y.%2C_between_1946_and_1948_%28William_P._Gottlieb_07531%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Pete_Rugolo%2C_New_York%2C_N.Y.%2C_between_1946_and_1948_%28William_P._Gottlieb_07531%29.jpg/300px-Pete_Rugolo%2C_New_York%2C_N.Y.%2C_between_1946_and_1948_%28William_P._Gottlieb_07531%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Pete_Rugolo%2C_New_York%2C_N.Y.%2C_between_1946_and_1948_%28William_P._Gottlieb_07531%29.jpg/400px-Pete_Rugolo%2C_New_York%2C_N.Y.%2C_between_1946_and_1948_%28William_P._Gottlieb_07531%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pete_Rugolo,_New_York,_N.Y.,_between_1946_and_1948_(William_P._Gottlieb_07531).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pete_Rugolo" title="Pete Rugolo">Pete Rugolo</a> produced the sessions for <i>Birth of the Cool</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The nonet recorded twelve tracks for Capitol during three sessions over the course of nearly a year and a half. Davis, Konitz, Mulligan and Barber were the only musicians who played on all three sessions, though the instrumental lineup was constant (excepting the omission of piano on a few songs and the addition of Hagood on "Darn That Dream").  The first session occurred on January 21, 1949, recording four tracks: Mulligan's "Jeru" and "Godchild" as well as Lewis's "Move" and "Budo".  Jazz critic <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cook_(journalist)" title="Richard Cook (journalist)">Richard Cook</a> hypothesizes that Capitol, wanting to get a good start, recorded these numbers first because they were the most catchy tunes in the nonet's small repertoire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> That date <a href="/wiki/Kai_Winding" title="Kai Winding">Kai Winding</a> replaced Zwerin on trombone, <a href="/wiki/Al_Haig" title="Al Haig">Al Haig</a> replaced Lewis on piano, and <a href="/wiki/Joe_Shulman" title="Joe Shulman">Joe Shulman</a> replaced McKibbon on bass.
</p><p>The second recording date came three months later on April 22, 1949 with Davis filling in for <a href="/wiki/Fats_Navarro" title="Fats Navarro">Fats Navarro</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tadd_Dameron" title="Tadd Dameron">Tadd Dameron</a>'s band with Charlie Parker during the interim. The band returned to the studio with five substitutions in personnel: <a href="/wiki/J._J._Johnson" title="J. J. Johnson">J. J. Johnson</a> on trombone, Sandy Siegelstein on French horn, <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Boyd" title="Nelson Boyd">Nelson Boyd</a> on bass, and <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Clarke" title="Kenny Clarke">Kenny Clarke</a> on drums, and <a href="/wiki/John_Lewis_(pianist)" title="John Lewis (pianist)">John Lewis</a> returning to piano. At this session, the nonet recorded Mulligan's "Venus de Milo", Lewis's "Rouge", Carisi's "Israel", and "Boplicity", a collaboration between Davis and Evans, credited to the <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> "Cleo Henry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The band did not return to the studio again until March 9, 1950. Davis did not call the band for any rehearsals or live performances between the second and third recording dates. The March 1950 date featured Mulligan's "Darn That Dream", "Rocker", and "Deception", and Evans's arrangement of <a href="/wiki/Chummy_MacGregor" title="Chummy MacGregor">Chummy MacGregor</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Moon_Dreams" title="Moon Dreams">Moon Dreams</a>", which had been released in a jazz arrangement by <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Miller" title="Glenn Miller">Glenn Miller</a> and the AAF Band in 1944 on <a href="/wiki/V-Disc" title="V-Disc">V-Disc</a>. The band saw more substitutions, with <a href="/wiki/Gunther_Schuller" title="Gunther Schuller">Gunther Schuller</a> on French horn and <a href="/wiki/Al_McKibbon" title="Al McKibbon">Al McKibbon</a> on bass. <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Hagood" title="Kenny Hagood">Kenny Hagood</a> returned for vocals on "Darn That Dream".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_of_the_Cool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_and_style">Music and style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_of_the_Cool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music and style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the features of the Davis Nonet was the use of paired instrumentation. An example of this can be heard on the <a href="/wiki/John_Lewis_(pianist)" title="John Lewis (pianist)">John Lewis</a> arrangement "Move". In "Move", Lewis gives the melody to the pairing of trumpet and alto saxophone, baritone saxophone and tuba supply counterpoint, and trombone and French horn provide harmonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook,_p.18_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cook,_p.18-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">Gerry Mulligan</a>'s "Jeru" demonstrates another Nonet hallmark: the use of a <a href="/wiki/Unison" title="Unison">unison</a> sound and rich <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmony</a> throughout the horns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cook,_p.18_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cook,_p.18-21">[21]</a></sup> Davis said, "I wanted the instruments to sound  like human voices singing ... and they did."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Though the album is seen as a departure from traditional bop,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gioia,_The_History_of_Jazz._281_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gioia,_The_History_of_Jazz._281-23">[23]</a></sup> the recordings do feature tunes that are considered close to the bop style, such as "Budo" which has the band bookending solos by Davis, Mulligan, Konitz, and Winding, similar to a bebop <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_arrangement" title="Head arrangement">head arrangement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Thornhill.27s_influence"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thornhill's_influence">Thornhill's influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Birth_of_the_Cool&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thornhill's influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
One of the largest stated influences on the sound of <i>The Birth of the Cool</i> was band leader <a href="/wiki/Claude_Thornhill" title="Claude Thornhill">Claude Thornhill</a> and his orchestra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers,_94_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers,_94-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Out of Thornhill's band came <a href="/wiki/Lee_Konitz" title="Lee Konitz">Lee Konitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">Gerry Mulligan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gil_Evans" title="Gil Evans">Gil Evans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a> calling the Konitz-Mulligan-Evans incarnation "the greatest band" only after "the <a href="/wiki/Billy_Eckstine" title="Billy Eckstine">Billy Eckstine</a> band with <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Parker" title="Charlie Parker">Bird</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers,_94_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers,_94-25">[25]</a></sup> The Thornhill band was known for its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impressionist_music" title="Impressionist music">impressionistic style</a>, innovative use of <a href="/wiki/Instrumentation_(music)" title="Instrumentation (music)">instrumentation</a>, such as the use of tuba and French horn, and a non-<a href="/wiki/Vibrato" title="Vibrato">vibrato</a> playing style, hallmarks that the Miles Davis Nonet adopted for <i>The Birth of the Cool</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> According to Evans: </p><blockquote><p>Miles had liked some of what Gerry and I had written for Claude. The instrumentation for the Miles session was caused by the fact that this was the smallest number of instruments that could get the sound and still express all the harmonies the Thornhill band used. Miles wanted to play his idiom with that kind of sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Davis saw the full 18-piece Thornhill orchestra as cumbersome and thus decided to split the group in half for his desired sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> As arrangers, both Evans and Mulligan gave Thornhill credit for crafting their sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gioia,_The_History_of_Jazz._281_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gioia,_The_History_of_Jazz._281-23">[23]</a></sup> Thornhill's band gave Evans the opportunity to try his hand at arranging small-group bebop tunes for <a href="/wiki/Big_band" title="Big band">big band</a>, a practice few others were participating in. Mulligan recalls Thornhill teaching him "the greatest lesson in dynamics, the art of underblowing." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gioia,_The_History_of_Jazz._281_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gioia,_The_History_of_Jazz._281-23">[23]</a></sup> Thornhill has also been credited with launching the move away from call and response between sections and the move towards unison harmonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The tracks from the January 1949 session were released soon after recording as two pairs of singles. From the April 1949 date, "Israel" and "Boplicity" were doubled together on a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/78_rpm" title="78 rpm">78</a> and released as well. Of the twelve tracks recorded, Capitol released relatively few. In 1954, after persuasion from Rugolo, Capitol released eight of the tracks on a 10" record titled <i>Classics in Jazzâ€”Miles Davis</i> (H-459). In 1957 eleven of the tracks (all except for "Darn That Dream") were released by Capitol as <i>Birth of the Cool</i>. The final track, "Darn That Dream" (the only song with vocals, by Hagood), was included with the other eleven on a 1972 LP <i>Capitol Jazz Classics, Vol. 1: The Complete Birth Of The Cool</i> (M-11026). Subsequent releases have been based on this last arrangement. The album has since been reissued many times in various formats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The recordings of the nonet from its time at the Royal Roost were released as <i>Cool Boppin</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In 1998, Capitol Records released <i>The Complete Birth of the Cool</i>, which was remastered by <a href="/wiki/Mark_Levinson_(audio_equipment_designer)" title="Mark Levinson (audio equipment designer)">Mark Levinson</a> and collected the nonet's live and studio tracks onto a single CD.
</p><p>Note from the 2000 Capitol CD reissue producer <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cuscuna" title="Michael Cuscuna">Michael Cuscuna</a>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>All previous reissues of this material have been derived from the 1957 12-inch LP master, which turns out to be second or third generation. The original tapes of each tune were filed individually and sound considerably better. <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Van_Gelder" title="Rudy Van Gelder">Rudy Van Gelder</a> returned to these masters, transferred them in 24-bit to digital and worked his sonic magic. The result is a clearer and more present sound than ever before on these classic recordings. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>

<p>The band's debut performance at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Roost" title="Royal Roost">Royal Roost</a> received positive, but reserved reactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Count_Basie" title="Count Basie">Count Basie</a>, the Roost's headliner during the Nonet's brief tenure, however, was more open to the group's sound, saying, "Those slow things sounded strange and good. I didn't always know what they were doing, but I listened, and I liked it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Winthrop_Sargeant" title="Winthrop Sargeant">Winthrop Sargeant</a>, classical music critic at <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>, compared the band's sound to the work of an "impressionist composer with a great sense of aural poetry and a very fastidious feeling for tone color... The music sounds more like that of a new <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">Maurice Ravel</a> than it does like jazz ... it is not really jazz."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gioia,_p._283_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gioia,_p._283-39">[39]</a></sup> Though he did not recognize the record as jazz, Sargeant acknowledged that he found the record "charming and exciting".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gioia,_p._283_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gioia,_p._283-39">[39]</a></sup> 
In the short term the reaction to the band was little to none,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gioia,_p._283_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gioia,_p._283-39">[39]</a></sup> but in the long term the recordings' effects have been great and lasting. They have been credited with starting the <a href="/wiki/Cool_jazz" title="Cool jazz">cool jazz</a> movement<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> as well as creating a new and viable alternative to <a href="/wiki/Bebop" title="Bebop">bebop</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1957, after the release of <i>Birth of the Cool</i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Down_Beat" title="Down Beat">Down Beat</a></i> magazine wrote that <i>Birth of the Cool</i> "[influenced] deeply one important direction of modern chamber jazz."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Several tunes from the album, such as Carisi's "Israel", have gone on to become <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jazz_standards" title="Jazz standards">jazz standards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>  The album was included in the book <i><a href="/wiki/1001_Albums_You_Must_Hear_Before_You_Die" title="1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die">1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> <i>Birth of the Cool</i> was voted number 349 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colin_Larkin_(writer)" title="Colin Larkin (writer)">Colin Larkin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/All_Time_Top_1000_Albums" title="All Time Top 1000 Albums">All Time Top 1000 Albums</a> 3rd Edition (2000).
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larkin_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many members of the Miles Davis Nonet went on to have successful careers in cool jazz, notably <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Mulligan" title="Gerry Mulligan">Gerry Mulligan</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Lewis_(pianist)" title="John Lewis (pianist)">John Lewis</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Konitz" title="Lee Konitz">Lee Konitz</a>. Mulligan moved to California and joined forces with trumpeter <a href="/wiki/Chet_Baker" title="Chet Baker">Chet Baker</a> in a piano-less quartet, before creating his Concert Jazz Band<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Lewis would become music director of the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Jazz_Quartet" title="Modern Jazz Quartet">Modern Jazz Quartet</a>, which would become one of the most influential cool jazz groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Evans would go on to collaborate with Davis again on the Davis albums <i><a href="/wiki/Miles_Ahead_(album)" title="Miles Ahead (album)">Miles Ahead</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sketches_of_Spain" title="Sketches of Spain">Sketches of Spain</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Capitol Records were at the time disappointed with the sales of the nonet recordings, and did not offer Davis more work. Instead, Davis signed with the new jazz specialty record label, <a href="/wiki/Prestige_Records" title="Prestige Records">Prestige</a>, for whom he would record his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_Sounds_(Miles_Davis_album)" title="The New Sounds (Miles Davis album)">first album</a> in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
