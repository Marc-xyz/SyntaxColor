<p><a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">Concrete</a> is a brittle material and can only withstand small amount of tensile <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)#Engineering_strain" title="Deformation (mechanics)">strain</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">stress</a> before cracking. When a <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_concrete" title="Reinforced concrete">reinforced concrete</a> member is put in tension, after cracking, the member elongates by widening of cracks and by formation of new cracks.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spacing_of_Primary_cracks">Spacing of Primary cracks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crack_spacing_of_reinforced_concrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacing of Primary cracks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Primary cracks (Figure 1) form when the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_stress" title="Tensile stress">tensile stress</a> at the outer surface of the concrete reaches the tensile strength of concrete. When a primary crack forms, the concrete in the vicinity of the crack is relieved of any tension, resulting in a stress free zone near the crack.
</p>
