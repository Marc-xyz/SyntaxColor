<p><b>"It's Not Your Fault"</b> is a song by American <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/New_Found_Glory" title="New Found Glory">New Found Glory</a>, serving as the <a href="/wiki/Lead_single" title="Lead single">lead single</a> from their fifth studio album <i><a href="/wiki/Coming_Home_(New_Found_Glory_album)" title="Coming Home (New Found Glory album)">Coming Home</a></i> (2006). The track was first announced in July 2006, before the <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> debuted on <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Request_Live" title="Total Request Live">TRL</a></i> on 1 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV2-2">[2]</a></sup> "It's Not Your Fault" was released to radio on July 25, 2006.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FMQB_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FMQB-3">[3]</a></sup> The song was later released to <a href="/wiki/Rock_2.0" title="Rock 2.0">Modern Rock Radio</a> in September,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMG_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMG-4">[4]</a></sup> shortly before the album was released.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CD_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CD-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The single marked a musical departure for the band and was seen by critics as a step away from their usual <a href="/wiki/Pop_punk" title="Pop punk">pop punk</a> sound, with the addition of prominent <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> instrumentation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WW_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WW-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerrang2_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerrang2-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> According to <a href="/wiki/Internet_radio" title="Internet radio">internet radio</a> website <a href="/wiki/Last.fm" title="Last.fm">Last.fm</a>, "It's Not Your Fault" is the most played song from <i>Coming Home</i>, with 145,000 current listeners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LFM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-LFM-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It%27s_Not_Your_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"It's Not Your Fault" is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rock_song" title="Rock song">rock song</a> at a length of three minutes and thirty seven seconds, written by the five members of New Found Glory, all of whom share credits for the music composition and the lyrics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITYF_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ITYF-10">[10]</a></sup> The song is composed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Major" title="A Major">A Major</a> key and has a "moderate tempo" of 129 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beats_per_minute" title="Beats per minute">beats per minute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITYF_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-ITYF-10">[10]</a></sup> The band's primary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_composer" title="Music composer">composer</a> and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Chad_Gilbert" title="Chad Gilbert">Chad Gilbert</a>, notes that the song has "big, full <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guitars" title="Guitars">guitars</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> line that goes throughout. It's pretty anthemic, and Jordan's voice has never sounded better."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTV1-1">[1]</a></sup> Gilbert further explained that, "This one shows how we've changed. We're a band who have done different styles of songs every record. There's some really <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">metal</a> riffs on <i>Catalyst</i> that aren't on <i>Coming Home</i>, and then this had some pianos. We're music fans, so we want every record to sound different".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SF_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-SF-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lyrically, the song is about dealing with time apart in a relationship. The band's <a href="/wiki/Lead_vocalist" title="Lead vocalist">lead vocalist</a> Jordan Pundik has noted, "I want people to think it's about whatever they want, but for me, it's about when you're in love with someone and you're away from them and you have an argument or a fight and it's over the phone, so you can't console them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV4_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV4-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It%27s_Not_Your_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with <i>Coming Home</i>, "It's Not Your Fault" was warmly received by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Music_critics" title="Music critics">music critics</a>. In a review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Palm_Beach_Post" title="The Palm Beach Post">The Palm Beach Post</a></i> it was noted that, "<i>Coming Home</i> is similar, in spirit, to the 1990s crooning of Chicago's brilliant <a href="/wiki/Smoking_Popes" title="Smoking Popes">Smoking Popes</a>. Jordan Pundik's vocal delivery curls into sweetness, rather than a snarl. Wrapped around journal-entry cute lines on the  earnestly protective pleadings of "It's Not Your Fault."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBP_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBP-13">[13]</a></sup> Rae Alexandra of <i><a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">Kerrang!</a></i> magazine, was of the opinion that "It's Not Your Fault" displays "more maturity - and piano - than ever before. It's not fast, it's not forced, but - in an entirely new way - it sours."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerr_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerr-14">[14]</a></sup> Jack Foley of <i>Indie London</i> was also favorable of the track and noted "the chiming piano chords that open "It’s Not Your Fault" give way into an effortlessly inspiring song about young love that unfolds into a majestic chorus."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IL_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-IL-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">Music video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=It%27s_Not_Your_Fault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NFGityfmv.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="273" data-file-width="365" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2b/NFGityfmv.jpg/220px-NFGityfmv.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2b/NFGityfmv.jpg/330px-NFGityfmv.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2b/NFGityfmv.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:NFGityfmv.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jordan Pundik (centre) performs onstage during the music video</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for the single was shot by long term <a href="/wiki/Music_video_director" title="Music video director">music video director</a> <a href="/wiki/Brett_Simon" title="Brett Simon">Brett Simon</a>  (<a href="/wiki/Sugarcult" title="Sugarcult">Sugarcult</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queens_of_the_Stone_Age" title="Queens of the Stone Age">Queens of the Stone Age</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Killers" title="The Killers">The Killers</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV3_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-MTV3-16">[16]</a></sup> The video depicts the band performing the song live on several occasions (see screenshot), in between a story being revealed in <a href="/wiki/Reverse_chronology" title="Reverse chronology">reverse chronological order</a>. The story portrays a man and woman (played by Teddy Van Deusen and Heide Lindgren from the American reality show <i><a href="/wiki/8th_%26_Ocean" title="8th &amp; Ocean">8th &amp; Ocean</a></i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV4_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-MTV4-12">[12]</a></sup> who wake up in bed naked together, and proceed to retrace their steps and retain their several items of clothing along the way. At the climax of the video, they end up where they first met in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Record_store" title="Record store">record store</a> and go their separate ways.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SF_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-SF-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV4_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-MTV4-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept itself was inspired by a German <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> commercial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SF_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-SF-11">[11]</a></sup> The video idea came from the <a href="/wiki/Axe_(brand)" title="Axe (brand)">Axe/Lynx</a> 24-7 spot, "Because you never know when (Getting Dressed)", that won "Gold Lion Cannes 2004" award. Chad Gilbert told <i><a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">Kerrang!</a></i> magazine that, "It starts backwards, with a guy and a girl in bed naked, and then they put on their clothes, finding them strewn as they walk, until they get to their first meeting at the end. We just took the idea and went with it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SF_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-SF-11">[11]</a></sup> The shoot was completed in one afternoon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MTV4_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-MTV4-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
