	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">André de Longjumeau</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">13th-century friar and diplomat</div>
<p><b>André de Longjumeau</b> (also known as <b>Andrew of Longjumeau</b> in English) was a 13th-century <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> missionary and diplomat and one of the most active Occidental diplomats in the East in the 13th century. He led two embassies to the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongols</a>: the first carried letters from <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IV" title="Pope Innocent IV">Pope Innocent IV</a> and the second bore gifts and letters from <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">Louis IX of France</a> to <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCy%C3%BCk_Khan" title="Güyük Khan">Güyük Khan</a>. Well acquainted with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle-East" title="Middle-East">Middle-East</a>, he spoke <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> and "<a href="/wiki/Chaldean_Neo-Aramaic" title="Chaldean Neo-Aramaic">Chaldean</a>" (thought to be either <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> or <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mxpvnk_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mxpvnk-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_for_the_holy_Crown_of_Thorns">Mission for the holy Crown of Thorns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_de_Longjumeau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission for the holy Crown of Thorns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HolyCrown.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2380" data-file-width="1452" decoding="async" height="361" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/HolyCrown.JPG/220px-HolyCrown.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/HolyCrown.JPG/330px-HolyCrown.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/HolyCrown.JPG/440px-HolyCrown.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HolyCrown.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Andrew went to Constantinople to obtain the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Thorns" title="Crown of Thorns">Crown of Thorns</a> bought by <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">Louis IX</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldwin_II_of_Constantinople" title="Baldwin II of Constantinople">Baldwin II</a>. It is preserved today in a 19th-century reliquary, in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Notre_Dame_de_Paris" title="Notre Dame de Paris">Notre-Dame Cathedral</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Andrew's first mission to the East was when he was asked by the French king <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">Louis IX</a> to go and fetch the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crown_of_Thorns" title="Crown of Thorns">Crown of Thorns</a> which had been sold to him by the Latin Emperor of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baldwin_II_of_Constantinople" title="Baldwin II of Constantinople">Baldwin II</a> in 1238, who was anxious to obtain support for his tottering empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mxpvnk_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mxpvnk-1">[1]</a></sup> Andrew was accompanied on this mission by brother Jacques.
</p>
<h2><span id="Papal_mission_to_the_Mongols_.281245.E2.80.931247.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Papal_mission_to_the_Mongols_(1245–1247)">Papal mission to the Mongols (1245–1247)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_de_Longjumeau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Papal mission to the Mongols (1245–1247)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Andrew of Longjumeau led one of four missions dispatched to the Mongols by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Innocent_IV" title="Pope Innocent IV">Pope Innocent IV</a>. He left <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> in the spring of 1245 for the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> 
He visited Muslim principalities in <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a> and representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Nestorianism" title="Nestorianism">Nestorian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church" title="Syriac Orthodox Church">Jacobite</a> churches in Persia, finally delivering the papal correspondence to a Mongol general near <a href="/wiki/Tabriz" title="Tabriz">Tabriz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In Tabriz, André de Longjumeau met with a monk from the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>, named <a href="/wiki/Simeon_Rabban_Ata" title="Simeon Rabban Ata">Simeon Rabban Ata</a>, who had been put in charge by the <a href="/wiki/Khan_(title)" title="Khan (title)">Khan</a> of protecting the Christians in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle-East" title="Middle-East">Middle-East</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERichard376_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichard376-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Second_mission_to_the_Mongols_.281249.E2.80.931251.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_mission_to_the_Mongols_(1249–1251)">Second mission to the Mongols (1249–1251)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_de_Longjumeau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Second mission to the Mongols (1249–1251)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-Mongol_alliance" title="Franco-Mongol alliance">Franco-Mongol alliance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europeans_in_Medieval_China" title="Europeans in Medieval China">Europeans in Medieval China</a></div>
<p>At the Mongol camp near <a href="/wiki/Kars" title="Kars">Kars</a> Andrew had met a certain <a href="/wiki/David_and_Mark" title="David and Mark">David</a>, who in December 1248 appeared at the court of King <a href="/wiki/Louis_IX_of_France" title="Louis IX of France">Louis IX of France</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. Andrew, who was now with Saint Louis, interpreted David's message to the King, a real or pretended offer of alliance from the Mongol general <a href="/wiki/Eljigidei" title="Eljigidei">Eljigidei</a>, and a proposal of a joint attack upon the Islamic powers of <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>. In reply to this the French sovereign dispatched Andrew as his ambassador to <a href="/wiki/G%C3%BCy%C3%BCk_Khan" title="Güyük Khan">Güyük Khan</a>; with Longjumeau went his brother Jacques (also a Dominican) and several others – John Goderiche, John of Carcassonne, Herbert "Le <a href="/wiki/Sommelier" title="Sommelier">Sommelier</a>", Gerbert of Sens, Robert (a clerk), a certain William, and an unnamed clerk of <a href="/wiki/Poissy" title="Poissy">Poissy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911972_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911972-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party set out on 16 February 1249, with letters from King Louis and the <a href="/wiki/Papal_legate" title="Papal legate">papal legate</a>, and rich presents including a chapel-tent lined with scarlet cloth and embroidered with sacred pictures. From Cyprus they went to the port of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> in Syria, and thence traveled for a year to the Khan's court, going ten leagues (55.56 kilometers) per day. Their route led them through <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Persia</a>, along the southern and eastern shores of the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, and certainly through <a href="/wiki/Taraz" title="Taraz">Talas</a>, north-east of <a href="/wiki/Tashkent" title="Tashkent">Tashkent</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911972_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911972-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>On arrival at the supreme Mongol court – either that on the <a href="/wiki/Emil_River" title="Emil River">Imyl river</a> (near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Alakol" title="Lake Alakol">Lake Alakol</a> and the present Russo-Chinese frontier in the <a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai</a>), or more probably at or near <a href="/wiki/Karakorum" title="Karakorum">Karakorum</a> itself, south-west of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">Lake Baikal</a> – Andrew found Güyük Khan dead, poisoned, as the envoy supposed, by <a href="/wiki/Batu_Khan" title="Batu Khan">Batu Khan</a>'s agents. The regent-mother <a href="/wiki/Oghul_Qaimish" title="Oghul Qaimish">Oghul Qaimish</a> (the "Camus" of <a href="/wiki/William_of_Rubruck" title="William of Rubruck">William of Rubruck</a>) seems to have received and dismissed him with presents and a dismissive letter for Louis IX. But it is certain that before the friar had left "Tartary", <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ngke_Khan" title="Möngke Khan">Möngke</a>, Güyük's successor, had been elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911972–973_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911972–973-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Andrew's report to his sovereign, whom he rejoined in 1251 at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesarea_Palaestina" title="Caesarea Palaestina">Caesarea</a> in <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a>, appears to have been a mixture of history and fable; the latter affects his narrative of the Mongols' rise to greatness, and the struggles of their leader <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a> with <a href="/wiki/Prester_John" title="Prester John">Prester John</a>; it is still more evident in the position assigned to the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a>' homeland, close to the prison of <a href="/wiki/Gog_and_Magog" title="Gog and Magog">Gog and Magog</a>. On the other hand, the envoy's account of Mongol customs is fairly accurate, and his statements about <a href="/wiki/Christianity_among_the_Mongols" title="Christianity among the Mongols">Mongol Christianity</a> and its prosperity, though perhaps exaggerated (e.g. as to the 800 chapels on wheels in the nomadic host) are based on fact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911973_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911973-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mounds of bones marked his road, witnesses of devastations which other historians record in detail. He found Christian prisoners from Germany in the heart of "Tartary" (at Talas), and was compelled to observe the ceremony of passing between two fires, as a bringer of gifts to a dead <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Khan</a>, gifts which were treated by the Mongols as evidence of submission. This insulting behaviour, and the language of the letter with which Andrew reappeared, marked the mission a failure: King Louis, says <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Joinville" title="Jean de Joinville">Joinville</a>, "<i>se repenti fort</i>" ("felt very sorry").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911973_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911973-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_de_Longjumeau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The date and location of Andrew's death is unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dorcy1990_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dorcy1990-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>We only know of Andrew through references in other writers: see especially <a href="/wiki/William_of_Rubruck" title="William of Rubruck">William of Rubruck</a>'s in <i>Recueil de voyages</i>, iv. (Paris, 1839), pp. 261, 265, 279, 296, 310, 353, 363, 370; Joinville, ed. Francisque Michel (1858, etc.), pp. 142, etc.; Jean Pierre Sarrasin, in same vol., pp. 254–235; <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_de_Nangis" title="Guillaume de Nangis">William of Nangis</a> in <i>Recueil des historiens des Gaules</i>, xx. 359–367; <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_R%C3%A9musat" title="Charles de Rémusat">Rémusat</a>, <i>Mémoires sur les relations politiques des princes chrétiens… avec les… Mongols</i> (1822, etc.), p. 52.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911973_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBeazley1911973-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
