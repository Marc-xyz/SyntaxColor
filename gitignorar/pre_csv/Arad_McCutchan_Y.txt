<p><b>Arad A. McCutchan</b> (July 4, 1912 – June 16, 1993) was a collegiate <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> coach. The <a href="/wiki/Evansville,_Indiana" title="Evansville, Indiana">Evansville, Indiana</a> native coached his hometown <a href="/wiki/University_of_Evansville" title="University of Evansville">University of Evansville</a> from 1946 to 1977, guiding the Purple Aces to a 514-314 record.
</p><p>McCutchan spent seven years coaching <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Bosse_High_School" title="Benjamin Bosse High School">Benjamin Bosse High School</a> (1936–1943) before serving in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In 1946 he took over the head coaching position at University of Evansville. In the following years he guided them to five <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_II_Men%27s_Basketball_Tournament" title="NCAA Division II Men's Basketball Tournament">NCAA College Division Basketball Championships</a> (1959, 1960, 1964, 1965, 1971) as well as three undefeated seasons in their conference (1964, 1965, 1971). McCutchan was named NCAA College Division Coach of the Year two times (1964, 1965). He was an assistant coach to <a href="/wiki/Gene_Bartow" title="Gene Bartow">Gene Bartow</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_men%27s_national_basketball_team" title="United States men's national basketball team">US national team</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1974_FIBA_World_Championship" title="1974 FIBA World Championship">1974 FIBA World Championship</a>, where he won the bronze medal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> On April 27, 1981 he was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Naismith_Memorial_Basketball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame">Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-halloffamers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-halloffamers-2">[2]</a></sup>  He was inducted in the <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Basketball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Indiana Basketball Hall of Fame">Indiana Basketball Hall of Fame</a> in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After retiring from coaching he and his wife Virginia moved to <a href="/wiki/Santa_Claus,_Indiana" title="Santa Claus, Indiana">Santa Claus, Indiana</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>His first name, Arad, was inherited from a grandfather who was named from the Bible.  He often said the name was Hebrew for "wild ass."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-si_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-si-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
