<p>The <b>red pileated finch</b> (<i>Coryphospingus cucullatus</i>), also known as the <b>red-crested finch</b>, is a species of <a href="/wiki/Bird" title="Bird">bird</a> in the family <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thraupidae" title="Thraupidae">Thraupidae</a>. It is found in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>,  on the eastern side of the Andes.  Its natural <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">habitats</a> are subtropical or tropical dry <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forests</a>, subtropical or tropical moist lowland forests, subtropical or tropical dry <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">shrubland</a>, and heavily degraded former forest. This is a common species, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has rated its conservation status as "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_pileated_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This species was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">first described</a> by the German zoologist <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Ludwig_Statius_M%C3%BCller" title="Philipp Ludwig Statius MÃ¼ller">Philipp Ludwig Statius MÃ¼ller</a> in 1776. Molecular analysis has shown that along with <i>C.  pileatus</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Grey_pileated_finch" title="Grey pileated finch">grey pileated finch</a>, this species belongs in the Thraupidae, the tanager family. The pair form a sister group to a group containing the <a href="/wiki/Fulvous-crested_tanager" title="Fulvous-crested tanager">fulvous-crested tanager</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Black-goggled_tanager" title="Black-goggled tanager">black-goggled tanager</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lanio" title="Lanio">shrike tanagers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grey-headed_tanager" title="Grey-headed tanager">grey-headed tanager</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBWA_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-HBWA-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>There are three recognised subspecies; <i>C. c. cucullatus</i> from Guyana, Suriname and northeastern Brazil; <i>C. c. fargoi</i> from Peru, Bolivia, western Paraguay and northern Argentina; and <i>C. c. rubescens</i> from Brazil, eastern Paraguay, northeastern Argentina and Uruguay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HBWA_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-HBWA-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_pileated_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The red pileated finch is about 13.5Â cm (5.3Â in) long. Both the male and female have a white eye ring. The male has a red crest and a black crown, just as the <a href="/wiki/Grey_pileated_finch" title="Grey pileated finch">grey pileated finch</a> does, however its upper parts are dark wine red, with a reddish rump, and the underparts are a drab reddish colour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ridgely2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ridgely2009-3">[3]</a></sup> The red crest and black head are absent in the female and the upper parts are a little more brown than those of the male. The throat of the female is white and the underparts pink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ridgely2009_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ridgely2009-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_pileated_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This bird has a wide distribution in South America. Its range includes southern Brazil and northern Argentina, extending from <a href="/wiki/Bras%C3%ADlia" title="BrasÃ­lia">BrasÃ­lia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> southwards to <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> and westwards to the Andean foothills. Besides this main block, there are several isolated populations in dry mountain valleys in Ecuador, Peru and Bolivia, another isolated population in northern Brazil and another in Suriname, Guyana and French Guiana. This bird is seldom found above 1,500Â m (4,900Â ft) but in the <a href="/wiki/Cusco" title="Cusco">Cusco</a> area of southeastern Peru, it may be found up to about 2,000Â m (6,600Â ft).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ridgely2009_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ridgely2009-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Red_pileated_finch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>C. cucullatus</i> is generally a common species, and its population trend seems to be steady. It has a very extensive range and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> has assessed its conservation status as being of "<a href="/wiki/Least-concern_species" title="Least-concern species">least concern</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
