<p><i><b>Stylocline citroleum</b></i> is a rare species of flowering plant in the <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">aster family</a> known by the common name <b>oil neststraw</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stylocline_citroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kern_County,_California" title="Kern County, California">Kern County, California</a>, where it is known from about 46 occurrences on and around the <a href="/wiki/Elk_Hills_Oil_Field" title="Elk Hills Oil Field">Elk Hills Oil Field</a>. The occurrences are patchy and variable in size, and some sources consider them to be part of a single widely spread <a href="/wiki/Metapopulation" title="Metapopulation">metapopulation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tnc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tnc-2">[2]</a></sup> The species has been collected from coastal <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_County,_California" title="San Diego County, California">San Diego County</a>, but any occurrences there are probably now <a href="/wiki/Local_extinction" title="Local extinction">extirpated</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tnc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tnc-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fna_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-fna-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The plant has been known for over one hundred years, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_specimen" title="Type specimen">type specimen</a> was collected in 1935, but it was not described to science as a distinct species until 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csus_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-csus-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-more_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-more-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Stylocline citroleum</i> grows in the valley saltbush scrub ecosystem in the sandy flats and clay soils of the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> in areas developed into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">oil fields</a>, the inspiration for the <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">common</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_name" title="Scientific name">scientific names</a> of the species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csus_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-csus-4">[4]</a></sup> The plant probably evolved as a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stylocline_gnaphaloides" title="Stylocline gnaphaloides">mountain neststraw (<i>Stylocline gnaphaloides</i>)</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Filago_californica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Filago californica (page does not exist)">California filago</a> (<i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Filago_californica&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Filago californica (page does not exist)">Filago californica</a></i>), and it is almost always found growing alongside one or both of its parent species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fna_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-fna-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Stylocline_citroleum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This inconspicuous annual herb produces a grayish, trailing, forking stem no more than 13 centimeters long. The grayish, woolly, pointed leaves are up to 1.4 centimeters long. The <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a> bears spherical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_(botany)" title="Head (botany)">flower heads</a> just a few millimeters long with tiny, rough-haired <a href="/wiki/Bract" title="Bract">phyllaries</a> and scaly, woolly florets. It is hard to tell apart from other <i>Stylocline</i> because its defining characteristics are microscopic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-csus_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-csus-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
