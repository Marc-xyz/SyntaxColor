	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Takashi "Halo" Hirose</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This article is about the swimmer. For the writer, see <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Hirose_(writer)" title="Takashi Hirose (writer)">Takashi Hirose (writer)</a>.</div>
<p><b>Takashi "Halo" Hirose</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Americans" title="Japanese Americans">Japanese-American</a> swimmer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcnews1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbcnews1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1937 he joined the "Three Year Swim Club" of Soichi Sakamoto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swimmingworldmagazine1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-swimmingworldmagazine1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was the first Japanese American to represent the United States in any international swimming competition, and the first to set a swimming world record. He set that record in 1938 as a member of the United States’s 4 × 100 m freestyle relay team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishof1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ishof1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honoluluadvertiser2002_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-honoluluadvertiser2002-4">[4]</a></sup> He also won the United States National 100m title in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishof1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ishof1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>He would have competed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Olympics" title="Olympics">Olympics</a> in 1940 and 1944, but it was canceled those years because of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbcnews1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbcnews1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishof1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ishof1-3">[3]</a></sup> During that war he fought as part of the <a href="/wiki/442nd_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="442nd Infantry Regiment (United States)">442nd Infantry Regiment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/100th_Infantry_Battalion_(United_States)" title="100th Infantry Battalion (United States)">100th Infantry Battalion</a>, and earned five battle stars, the <a href="/wiki/Combat_Infantryman_Badge" title="Combat Infantryman Badge">Combat Infantryman Badge</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Unit_Citation_(United_States)" title="Presidential Unit Citation (United States)">Presidential Unit Citation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honoluluadvertiser2002_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-honoluluadvertiser2002-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swimmingworldmagazine1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-swimmingworldmagazine1-2">[2]</a></sup> He also had his legs temporarily paralyzed due to <a href="/wiki/Trench_foot" title="Trench foot">trench foot</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swimmingworldmagazine1_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-swimmingworldmagazine1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the war ended he attended <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a>, where he was an <a href="/wiki/All-America" title="All-America">All-American</a> three times, was an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA">NCAA</a> champion in the 100 free, and helped Ohio State win Big Ten, NCAA, and <a href="/wiki/Amateur_Athletic_Union" title="Amateur Athletic Union">AAU</a> team titles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-swimmingworldmagazine1_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-swimmingworldmagazine1-2">[2]</a></sup> He graduated from that university in 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honoluluadvertiser2002_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-honoluluadvertiser2002-4">[4]</a></sup> He later became Hawaii's chief probation officer, and eventually retired in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honoluluadvertiser2002_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-honoluluadvertiser2002-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1987 he was inducted into Ohio State University's Sports Hall of Fame.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishof1_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ishof1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>He died in 2002 and was survived by his daughter Sono Hirose-Hulbert and his wife Kiyomi.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-honoluluadvertiser2002_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-honoluluadvertiser2002-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017 he was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/International_Swimming_Hall_of_Fame" title="International Swimming Hall of Fame">International Swimming Hall of Fame</a> as a "Pioneer Swimmer".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ishof1_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ishof1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
