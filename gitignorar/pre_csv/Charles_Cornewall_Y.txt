<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_admiral_(Royal_Navy)" title="Vice admiral (Royal Navy)">Vice Admiral</a> <b>Charles Cornewall</b> or <b>Cornwall</b> (1669 – 7 October 1718), of Berrington, Herefordshire, was an officer in the British <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and politician who sat in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> between 1709 and 1718.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Cornewall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cornewall was born in 1669, eldest of the eleven children of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Cornewall" title="Robert Cornewall">Robert Cornewall</a> and Edith Cornwallis, and was baptised at <a href="/wiki/Eye,_Herefordshire" title="Eye, Herefordshire">Eye, Herefordshire</a>, on 5 August 1669.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-corn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Cornewall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cornewall joined the navy in 1683 and was given his first command, the <a href="/wiki/Sloop-of-war" title="Sloop-of-war">Sloop</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=HMS_Portsmouth_(1692)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="HMS Portsmouth (1692) (page does not exist)">HMS <i>Portsmouth</i></a>, on 19 September 1692. The following year he was given command of the 44-gun <a href="/wiki/HMS_Adventure_(1646)" title="HMS Adventure (1646)">HMS <i>Adventure</i></a> and sailed under the command of Admiral <a href="/wiki/Edward_Russell,_1st_Earl_of_Orford" title="Edward Russell, 1st Earl of Orford">Edward Russell</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>, where he would remain until 1696.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 27 January 1695, <i>Adventure</i> was one of a squadron of six <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a> under the command of <a href="/wiki/Commodore_(Royal_Navy)" title="Commodore (Royal Navy)">Commodore</a> James Killegrew aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Plymouth_(1653)" title="HMS Plymouth (1653)">HMS <i>Plymouth</i></a>. The flotilla was spotted by two French warships, the 60-gun <i>Content</i> and the 52-gun <i>Trident</i>, who closed on them believing them to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchant_vessel" title="Merchant vessel">merchant ships</a>. They retreated on discovering their mistake and were pursued by the British ships, the ensuing firefight lasting through the night and into the next morning before the French ships were compelled to <a href="/wiki/Striking_the_colors" title="Striking the colors">surrender</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Killigrew was killed in the action, and Cornewall was appointed to command the <i>Plymouth</i> in his place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cornewall was given command of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Kent_(1679)" title="HMS Kent (1679)">HMS <i>Kent</i></a> in 1697,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3d_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-3d-4">[4]</a></sup> but left the navy after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Ryswick" title="Treaty of Ryswick">Treaty of Ryswick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odnb_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-5">[5]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_general_election,_January%E2%80%93February_1701" title="English general election, January–February 1701">1701</a> he stood for parliament in <a href="/wiki/Weobley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Weobley (UK Parliament constituency)">Weobley</a> against his cousin <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Cornewall_(died_1717)" title="Henry Cornewall (died 1717)">Henry Cornewall</a>, but was defeated having gained just four votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weo_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-weo-6">[6]</a></sup> Returning to sea in March that year, he was given command of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Shrewsbury_(1695)" title="HMS Shrewsbury (1695)">HMS <i>Shrewsbury</i></a> but had to resign a few months later due to the sudden death of his father whose concerns, he wrote on 25 Sept. 1701, "are like to prove more troublesome and tedious than I expected, though when settled may prove of very considerable advantage to my children."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> These affairs having been settled, he was appointed to command <a href="/wiki/HMS_Exeter_(1697)" title="HMS Exeter (1697)">HMS <i>Exeter</i></a> but rejected it when it became clear that he would effectively be a second captain under <a href="/wiki/John_Leake" title="John Leake">John Leake</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colony_of_Newfoundland" title="Colony of Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, protesting that "their sending a private captain to command … me in my own ship [was] a modest way of terming me a blockhead."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odnb_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>This record of resigning from commands, combined with an uneasy political relationship with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Harley,_1st_Earl_of_Oxford_and_Earl_Mortimer" title="Robert Harley, 1st Earl of Oxford and Earl Mortimer">Robert Harley</a>, made it difficult for him to gain a new command, and it was not until 1705 that he was appointed to <a href="/wiki/HMS_Orford_(1698)" title="HMS Orford (1698)">HMS <i>Orford</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odnb_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-5">[5]</a></sup> In that ship he returned to the Mediterranean where he served for the next two years, first under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clowdesley_Shovell" title="Clowdesley Shovell">Clowdesley Shovell</a> and then under <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Dilkes" title="Thomas Dilkes">Thomas Dilkes</a>. In the autumn of 1707 he commanded a detached <a href="/wiki/Squadron_(naval)" title="Squadron (naval)">squadron</a> operating off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Naples</a>, returning to England in March the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1709 Cornewall changed his name to Cornwall, in an effort to distinguish between the different branches of his family, and stood once more for parliament. This time he was successful, being elected member for <a href="/wiki/Bewdley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Bewdley (UK Parliament constituency)">Bewdley</a> on 2 March 1709.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hop90_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-hop90-7">[7]</a></sup> He did not spend much time in the house, being busy commanding squadrons in <a href="/wiki/The_Downs_(ship_anchorage)" title="The Downs (ship anchorage)">the Downs</a> and off <a href="/wiki/Dunkirk" title="Dunkirk">Dunkirk</a>. In October 1710 he led a convoy to <a href="/wiki/Smyrna" title="Smyrna">Smyrna</a> aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Dreadnought_(1691)" title="HMS Dreadnought (1691)">HMS <i>Dreadnought</i></a>, but his <a href="/wiki/Whigs_(British_political_party)" title="Whigs (British political party)">Whig</a> politics made it impossible to gain a further command on his return, and he languished on <a href="/wiki/Half-pay" title="Half-pay">half-pay</a> for some years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odnb_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The accession of <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George I</a> in 1714 brought about a dramatic improvement in Cornwall's prospects, with him being appointed <a href="/wiki/Controller_of_Storekeepers_Accounts" title="Controller of Storekeepers Accounts">Controller of Storekeepers Accounts</a> at  the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Board" title="Navy Board">Navy Board</a>. In 1715 he returned to parliament as member for Weobley.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hop15_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-hop15-8">[8]</a></sup> He was promoted to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral_(Royal_Navy)" title="Rear admiral (Royal Navy)">rear admiral</a> on 16 June 1716, and appointed <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">Commander-in-chief</a> in the Mediterranean in October the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sailing with his flag aboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HMS_Argyll_(1722)" title="HMS Argyll (1722)">HMS <i>Argyll</i></a>, he took up his post in <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>. His first task was to open negotiations with <a href="/wiki/Ismail_Ibn_Sharif" title="Ismail Ibn Sharif">Ismail Ibn Sharif</a>, emperor of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a> to "demand satisfaction for the depredations of the <a href="/wiki/Sal%C3%A9_Rovers" title="Salé Rovers">Salé corsairs</a> and procure the release of all His Majesty's subjects now captive in Barbary." When Ismail refused to release any of his slaves Cornwall established a <a href="/wiki/Blockade" title="Blockade">blockade</a> of his ports, but the measure had no effect on Ismail's policy despite the capture of several corsair vessels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> During this time he engaged in a bitter dispute with the <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Gibraltar" title="Governor of Gibraltar">Governor</a>, who refused to admit the admiral's authority even in matters relating to the ships in the port. Cornwall eventually resolved to put the matter before the King or the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_House_of_Commons_(United_Kingdom)" title="Speaker of the House of Commons (United Kingdom)">Speaker of the House of Commons</a>, but was prevented from doing so by being once more engaged in active service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cornwall was promoted to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice_admiral_(Royal_Navy)" title="Vice admiral (Royal Navy)">vice admiral</a> in March 1718, and became second-in-command to <a href="/wiki/George_Byng,_1st_Viscount_Torrington" title="George Byng, 1st Viscount Torrington">George Byng</a> on the latter's arrival in the Mediterranean in June of that year, hoisting his flag aboard <a href="/wiki/HMS_Shrewsbury_(1695)" title="HMS Shrewsbury (1695)">HMS <i>Shrewsbury</i></a>. Cruising with a combined force of nineteen <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">ships of the line</a>, two frigates and a <a href="/wiki/Galley" title="Galley">galley</a> off <a href="/wiki/Syracuse,_Sicily" title="Syracuse, Sicily">Syracuse</a> they attacked a Spanish fleet in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cape_Passaro" title="Battle of Cape Passaro">battle of Cape Passaro</a> on 31 July 1718. Attempting to avoid capture, the Spanish split into eight groups which the British chased down individually. Cornwall's part of the fleet was charged with the pursuit of the Marquis De Mari, who led a force of six ships of the line, nine frigates and a number of smaller vessels aboard his flagship <i>El Real</i>. Cornwall captured <i>El Real</i> and three other warships, with the Spanish burning seven more to avoid their capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the battle, Cornwall transferred his flag back to HMS <i>Argyll</i> and convoyed the captured Spanish prizes to <a href="/wiki/Mah%C3%B3n" title="Mahón">Port Mahon</a>, from where he set sail for England. However, his health had been poor for some time, and on putting in at <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> on the homeward journey he died there on 7 October 1718.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> His body was conveyed home and buried in the south aisle of <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cornwall's career had been illustrious without being spectacular, as John Charnock put it: "We have at least a very extraordinary, if not unequalled instance in this gentleman, of its being possible for an officer to serve, with the most irreproachable character, and to attain a very high rank in the service, without ever having it in his power to increase his reputation, by any of those brilliant exploits which fortune throws in the way of her greater favourites."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Cornewall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cornwall married twice, but no details of his first marriage survives. His second wife was Dorothy Hanmer daughter of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hanmer_(died_1701)" title="Thomas Hanmer (died 1701)">Thomas Hanmer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hop90_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-hop90-7">[7]</a></sup> with whom he had fourteen children:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-corn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Henry Cornewall (b. 1698)</li>
<li>Thomas Cornewall (b. &amp; d. 1699)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sir_Robert_de_Cornwall" title="Sir Robert de Cornwall">Sir Robert de Cornwall</a> (1700–56), MP for <a href="/wiki/Leominster_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Leominster (UK Parliament constituency)">Leominster</a></li>
<li>Henrietta Cornewall (1701–28)</li>
<li>Cyriac Cornewall (1702–1703)</li>
<li>Charles Cornewall (b. &amp; d. 1704)</li>
<li>Job Cornewall (1705–28)</li>
<li>Theophila Cornewall (1706–21)</li>
<li>Jane Cornewall (b. 1708)</li>
<li>Jacobs Cornewall (1709–38), father of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wolfran_Cornwall" title="Charles Wolfran Cornwall">Charles Wolfran Cornwall</a></li>
<li>Emma Cornwall (1712–77), married <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Vernon_(Member_of_Parliament)" title="Thomas Vernon (Member of Parliament)">Thomas Vernon</a></li>
<li>Edith Cornwall (b. &amp; d. 1712)</li>
<li>Annabella Cornwall (1713–82)</li>
<li>Mary Cornwall (b. 1714)</li></ul>
<p>His estate at <a href="/wiki/Berrington_Hall" title="Berrington Hall">Berrington</a> was inherited on his death by Sir Robert.
</p>
