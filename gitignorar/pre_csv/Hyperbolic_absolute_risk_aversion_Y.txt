	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hyperbolic absolute risk aversion</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Decision_theory" title="Decision theory">decision theory</a>, <b>hyperbolic absolute risk aversion</b> (<b>HARA</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ingersoll_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ingersoll-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p.39,</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merton_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Merton-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>p.389,</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mossin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mossin-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ljungqvist_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ljungqvist-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>   refers to a type of <a href="/wiki/Risk_aversion" title="Risk aversion">risk aversion</a> that is particularly convenient to model mathematically and to obtain empirical predictions from.  It refers specifically to a property of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Von_Neumann%E2%80%93Morgenstern_utility_function" title="Von Neumann–Morgenstern utility function">von Neumann–Morgenstern utility functions</a>, which are typically functions of final wealth (or some related variable), and which describe a decision-maker's degree of satisfaction with the outcome for wealth. The final outcome for wealth is affected both by <a href="/wiki/Random_variable" title="Random variable">random variables</a> and by decisions.  Decision-makers are assumed to make their decisions (such as, for example, <a href="/wiki/Modern_portfolio_theory" title="Modern portfolio theory">portfolio allocations</a>) so as to maximize the <a href="/wiki/Expected_value" title="Expected value">expected value</a> of the utility function.
</p><p>Notable special cases of HARA utility functions include the <a href="/wiki/Risk_aversion#Absolute_risk_aversion" title="Risk aversion">quadratic utility function</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Exponential_utility" title="Exponential utility">exponential utility function</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Isoelastic_utility" title="Isoelastic utility">isoelastic utility function</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavioral_predictions_resulting_from_HARA_utility">Behavioral predictions resulting from HARA utility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperbolic_absolute_risk_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Behavioral predictions resulting from HARA utility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Static_portfolios">Static portfolios</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperbolic_absolute_risk_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Static portfolios">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If all investors have HARA utility functions with the same exponent, then in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Risk-free_interest_rate" title="Risk-free interest rate">risk-free asset</a> a <a href="/wiki/Mutual_fund_separation_theorem" title="Mutual fund separation theorem">two-fund monetary separation theorem</a> results:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cass_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cass-7">[7]</a></sup> every investor holds the available risky assets in the same proportions as do all other investors, and investors differ from each other in their portfolio behavior only with regard to the fraction of their portfolios held in the risk-free asset rather than in the collection of risky assets.
</p><p>Moreover, if an investor has a HARA utility function and a risk-free asset is available, then the investor's demands for the risk-free asset and all risky assets are linear in initial wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cass_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cass-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Capital_asset_pricing_model" title="Capital asset pricing model">capital asset pricing model</a>, there exists a representative investor utility function depending on the individual investors' utility functions and wealth levels, independent of the assets available, if and only if all investors have HARA utility functions with the same exponent. The representative utility function depends on the distribution of wealth, and one can describe market behavior as if there were a single investor with the representative utility function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ingersoll_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ingersoll-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>With a complete set of <a href="/wiki/State_prices" title="State prices">state-contingent securities</a>, a sufficient condition for security prices in <a href="/wiki/Economic_equilibrium" title="Economic equilibrium">equilibrium</a> to be independent of the distribution of initial wealth holdings is that all investors have HARA utility functions with identical exponent and identical rate of time preference between beginning-of-period and end-of-period consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_portfolios_in_discrete_time">Dynamic portfolios in discrete time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperbolic_absolute_risk_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dynamic portfolios in discrete time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intertemporal_portfolio_choice#Discrete_time" title="Intertemporal portfolio choice">Intertemporal portfolio choice § Discrete time</a></div>
<p>In a discrete time dynamic portfolio optimization context, under HARA utility optimal portfolio choice involves partial myopia if there is a risk-free asset and there is <a href="/wiki/Autocorrelation" title="Autocorrelation">serial independence</a> of asset returns: to find the optimal current-period portfolio, one needs to know no future distributional information about the asset returns except the future risk-free returns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mossin_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mossin-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>With asset returns that are <a href="/wiki/Independent_and_identically_distributed_random_variables" title="Independent and identically distributed random variables">independently and identically distributed</a> through time and with a risk-free asset, risky asset proportions are independent of the investor's remaining lifetime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ingersoll_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ingersoll-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>ch.11</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamic_portfolios_in_continuous_time">Dynamic portfolios in continuous time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hyperbolic_absolute_risk_aversion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dynamic portfolios in continuous time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Intertemporal_portfolio_choice#Continuous_time" title="Intertemporal portfolio choice">Intertemporal portfolio choice § Continuous time</a></div>
<p>With asset returns whose evolution is described by <a href="/wiki/Wiener_process" title="Wiener process">Brownian motion</a> and which are independently and identically distributed through time, and with a risk-free asset, one can obtain an explicit solution for the demand for the unique optimal mutual fund, and that demand is linear in initial wealth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merton_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Merton-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
