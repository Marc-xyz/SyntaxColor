<p><b>Dhamma</b> is a set of edicts that formed a policy of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan" title="Mauryan">Mauryan</a> emperor <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a>, who succeeded to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan" title="Mauryan">Mauryan</a> throne in modern-day India around 269 B.C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Many historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2013)">which?</span></a></i>]</sup> consider him one of the greatest kings of <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">ancient India</a> for his policies of public welfare. His policy of <i><a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dhamma</a></i> has been debated by intellectuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <b>Dhamma</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Prakrit" title="Prakrit">Prakrit</a> form of the <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> word <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> There have been attempts to define and find equivalent English words for it, such as "piety", "moral life" and "righteousness" but scholars could not translate it into English because it was coined and used in a specific context. The word <i>Dharma</i>  has multiple meanings in the literature and thought of ancient India. The best way to understand what Ashoka means by Dharma is to read his edicts, which were written to explain the principles of Dharma to the people of that time throughout the empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dharma was not a particular religious faith or practice, or an arbitrary formulated royal policy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Dharma related to generalized norms of social behavior and activities; Ashoka tried to <a href="/wiki/Logic_synthesis" title="Logic synthesis">synthesize</a> various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norm_(social)" title="Norm (social)">social norms</a> which were current in his time. It cannot be understood by assuming it is one of the various religions that existed at that time. To understand why and how Ashoka formulated Dharma and its meaning, one must understand the characteristics of the time in which he lived and to refer to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmanical" title="Brahmanical">Brahmanical</a> and other texts where norms of social behavior are explained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some historians link Ashoka’s policy of Dhamma with Buddhism. According to V.A. Smith,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Ashoka actually became a Buddhist monk for a short span of his life. Smith believes that he was both a monk and a monarch at the same time. D.R. Bhandarkar <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> claims that Ashoka was a Buddhist and his policy of dhamma was actually original Buddhism as preached by the Budhha. Radha Kumud Mookerji also formulates that as far as the personal religion of Ashoka is concerned, it may be taken as Buddhism.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">Historical background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="543" data-file-width="656" decoding="async" height="182" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg/220px-Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg/330px-Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg/440px-Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maurya_Dynasty_in_265_BCE.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Maurya_Empire" title="Maurya Empire">Maurya Empire</a> at the age of Ashoka. The empire stretched from Afghanistan to Bangladesh/<a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> and from Central Asia (Afghanistan) to <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">South India</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socio-economic_conditions">Socio-economic conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Socio-economic conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Mauryan period saw a change in the economic structure of the society. The use of iron resulted in surplus production, and the economy changed from being a simple, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_economy" title="Rural economy">rural economy</a> to a pattern of economy in which urban centres became important. It has been generally argued<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the use of the Northern Black Polished Ware pottery is an indicator of material prosperity in the period. The use of <a href="/wiki/Punch_(combat)" title="Punch (combat)">Punch</a>-marked silver coins and some other varieties of coins, the conscious intervention of the State to safeguard trade routes and the rise of urban centers point to a structural change in the economy, requiring adjustments in the society. The commercial classes had come to the forefront of society. The emergence of <a href="/wiki/Urban_culture" title="Urban culture">urban culture</a> demanded a flexible social organization. The incorporation of tribes and peoples from the outlying areas into the social fabric also presented a problem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google747_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-google747-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google189_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-google189-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The response of the Brahmanical social order, which was based on the four-fold varna division, was to make the <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste system</a> more rigid and deny a higher status to the commercial class. The rigidity of the Brahmanical class system sharpened the divisions within the society. The lower orders turned to various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterodox" title="Heterodox">heterodox</a> sects and this created social tensions. It was this situation which emperor Ashoka inherited when he ascended the Mauryan throne.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google189_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-google189-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_conditions">Religious conditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religious conditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Brahmanical hold over society, assiduously built through the later <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vedic" title="Vedic">Vedic</a> period, was coming under increasing attack. The privileges of the priests, the rigidity of the caste system and the elaborate rituals were being questioned. The lower orders among the four sects began to favour new sects. The vaishyas, who were technically included in the higher social category, were treated as inferior to both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brahmans" title="Brahmans">Brahmans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Kshatriya</a>. The opposition of the commercial class to Brahmanism was to give a fillip to the other sects of the society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google747_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-google747-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google189_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-google189-16">[16]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhism</a> began as schismatic <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">movement</a> from the more <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Hinduism" title="Orthodox Hinduism">orthodox</a> outlook of Brahmanism. Its basic tenet was an emphasis on misery and advocacy of the middle path. It was a set of ethical principles. Buddhism opposed the dominance of the Brahmans and the concept of sacrifices and rituals. It thus appealed to lower social orders and to emerging social classes. The human approach to relations in society preached by Buddhism further attracted different sections to itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google189_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-google189-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_pasts-1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Our_pasts-1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polity">Polity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Polity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahajanapada" title="Mahajanapada">Mahajanapada</a> of sixth century B.C. marked the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">state</a> system in many parts of India. Only a small section of society came to have a monopoly of power, which they exercised over the rest of the society. There were <i>gana-samghas</i> in which the rulers were a group of hereditary <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Kshatriya</a> or members of a clan. By the time Ashoka ascended the throne, the state system had grown very elaborate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google747_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-google747-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google189_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-google189-16">[16]</a></sup> It was characterized by:
</p>
<ul><li>The political supremacy of one region (<a href="/wiki/Magadha" title="Magadha">Magadha</a>) over a vast territory which comprised many previous kingdoms, <i>gana-samghas</i>, and areas where no organised states had previously existed;</li>
<li>Existence within this vast territory of geographical regions, cultural areas, and of different faiths, beliefs and practices;</li>
<li>Monopoly of force by a ruling class of which the emperor was the supreme head.</li>
<li>Appropriation of a very substantial quantity of surpluses from agriculture, commerce and other sources.</li></ul>
<p>The complexity of the state system demanded an imaginative policy from the emperor which required minimal use of force in such a large empire having diverse forms of economy and religions. It could not have been controlled by an army alone. A more feasible alternative was the propagation of a policy that would work at an ideological level and reach out to all sections of the society. The policy of <i>Dharma</i> was such an endeavour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google189_16-5"><a href="#cite_note-google189-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_pasts-1_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Our_pasts-1-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_of_inscriptions">Distribution of inscriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution of inscriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Edicts_of_Ashoka" title="Edicts of Ashoka">Edicts of Ashoka</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EdictsOfAshoka.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1440" data-file-width="1092" decoding="async" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/EdictsOfAshoka.jpg/220px-EdictsOfAshoka.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/EdictsOfAshoka.jpg/330px-EdictsOfAshoka.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/EdictsOfAshoka.jpg/440px-EdictsOfAshoka.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EdictsOfAshoka.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution of the Edicts of Ashoka<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Ashoka expounded his policy of <i>Dhamma</i> through his edicts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By engraving his views about <i>Dhamma</i> on these edicts, Ashoka tried to directly communicate with his subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google44_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-google44-23">[23]</a></sup> These inscriptions were written in different years of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The inscriptions can be divided into two categories. A small group of inscriptions reveal that the king was a follower of Buddhism and were addresses to the Buddhist church—the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samgha" title="Samgha">samgha</a>. These inscriptions are declarations of Ashoka's relationship with the Buddhist order.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Inscriptions of the other category are known as the Major and Minor Rock Edicts, which were inscribed on rock surfaces. This larger group includes the Pillar Edicts inscribed on specially erected pillars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google592_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-google592-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>All the sites of Ashokan inscription were chosen carefully to ensure that they were accessible to large numbers of people. These edicts are proclamations to the public at large. They explain the idea of Dhamma. One must make a distinction between Ashoka's policy of Dhamma which stressed social responsibility and Ashoka's own commitment as a Buddhist. There has been a tendency in the past among historians to study the policy of Dhamma and Ashoka as Buddhist in the same context without making any distinction. An examination of the inscriptions suggests that Ashoka declared his personal association with a Buddhist order and on the other he tried to teach, through the policy of Dhamma, the importance of social responsibility and tolerance amongst different members of the society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dhamma_causes">Dhamma causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dhamma causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The policy of Dhamma was an earnest attempt at solving some of problems and tensions faced by a complex society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Ashoka's private empire were responsible for the formation of the policy. The immediate social environment in which Ashoka grew up influenced him in later years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google592_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-google592-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauryan" title="Mauryan">Mauryan</a> kings adopted an eclectical outlook. <a href="/wiki/Chandragupta_Maurya" title="Chandragupta Maurya">Chandragupta</a> took recourse to <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a> in his later years and <a href="/wiki/Bindusara" title="Bindusara">Bindusara</a> favoured the <a href="/wiki/%C4%80j%C4%ABvika" title="Ājīvika">Ājīvika</a>. Ashoka adopted Buddhism in his personal life, though he never imposed Buddhism on his subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google44_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-google44-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the time Ashoka ascended the throne, the Mauryan imperial system had become complex, encompassing various cultures, beliefs and social and political patterns. Ashoka had to either maintain the structure by force—which would incur tremendous expenses—or to define a set of social norms which would be acceptable to all social practices and religious beliefs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google53_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-google53-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google_a_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google_a-34">[34]</a></sup> He was aware of the tensions which the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterodox" title="Heterodox">heterodox</a> sects—Buddhism, Jainism and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ajivikas" title="Ajivikas">Ajiviksim</a>—had generated in society. They were all opposed to the domination of the Brahmans and had a growing number of supporters. But Brahmans continued to control society and hostility was inevitable. It was essential to bring about a climate of harmony and mutual trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google53_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-google53-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google_a_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google_a-34">[34]</a></sup> There were many areas within the empire where neither the Brahmanical system or the heterodox sects prevailed. Ashoka referred to the country of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yavanas" title="Yavanas">Yavanas</a>, where neither Brahmanical nor <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sramanical&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sramanical (page does not exist)">Sramanical</a> culture were in vogue. In many tribal areas, people were unfamiliar with Brahmanical or heterodox ideas. To make the empire survive and to bring some cohesion within the empire in the midst of this diversity, some common patterns of behaviour and common approaches to the society's problems were needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google53_33-2"><a href="#cite_note-google53-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google_a_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-books.google_a-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Edicts">Edicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Edicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashoka%27s_Major_Rock_Edicts" title="Ashoka's Major Rock Edicts">Ashoka's Major Rock Edicts</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="710" data-file-width="841" decoding="async" height="186" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg/220px-Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg/330px-Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg/440px-Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ashoka_edict_peshawar.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Edicts of Ashoka I-XI in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shahbazgarhi" title="Shahbazgarhi">Shahbazgarhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peshawar" title="Peshawar">Peshawar</a>, along the <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road#Overland_routes" title="Silk Road">Karakoram Route</a>, now the <a href="/wiki/Karakoram_Highway" title="Karakoram Highway">Karakoram Highway</a></div></div></div>
<p>The principles of Dhamma were formulated to be acceptable to people belonging to different <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communities" title="Communities">communities</a> and following any religion. Dhamma was not given any formal definition or structure. It emphasized tolerance of people and the notion of showing consideration towards slaves and servants; there is stress on obedience to elders; generosity towards the needy, Brahmans and Sarmanas. Ashoka pleaded for tolerance of different religious sects in an attempt to create a sense of harmony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> The policy of Dhamma also laid stress on non-violence, which was to be practiced by giving up war and conquests and also as a restraint on the killing of animals. However, Ashoka realized that a certain display of his political might may be necessary to keep the primitive forest-dwelling tribes in check.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Edict_at_Maski.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2248" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Edict_at_Maski.jpg/220px-Edict_at_Maski.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Edict_at_Maski.jpg/330px-Edict_at_Maski.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Edict_at_Maski.jpg/440px-Edict_at_Maski.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Edict_at_Maski.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ashoka's Edict at <a href="/wiki/Maski" title="Maski">Maski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raichur_district" title="Raichur district">Raichur district</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a>. This Edict Confirmed the Name Ashoka for "Devanampiya Piadassi"</div></div></div>
<p>The policy of Dhamma also included other welfare measures, like the planting of trees and digging of wells. Ashoka attacked ceremonies and sacrifices as meaningless. A group of officers known as Dhamma mahamattas were instituted to implement and publicize the various aspects of Dhamma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
Ashoka made them responsible for carrying his message to various sections of society, However, they gradually developed into a type of priesthood of Dhamma with great powers and soon began to interfere in politics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google_a_34-3"><a href="#cite_note-books.google_a-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gujarra_Edict.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2248" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Gujarra_Edict.jpg/220px-Gujarra_Edict.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Gujarra_Edict.jpg/330px-Gujarra_Edict.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Gujarra_Edict.jpg/440px-Gujarra_Edict.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gujarra_Edict.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ashoka's Edict at Gujarra, <a href="/wiki/Madhya_Pradesh" title="Madhya Pradesh">Madhya Pradesh</a></div></div></div>
<p>The aspects of Dhamma were developed chronologically.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Major Rock Edict I</b> prohibits animal sacrifice and holidays of festive gathering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict II</b>  relates to measures of social welfare. It mentions medical treatment for men and animals, construction of roads, wells and tree planting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google235_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-google235-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict III</b>  declares that liberality towards Brahmans and Sramanas is a virtue, and that respecting one's parents is a good quality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict IV</b> comments that because of the policy of Dhamma the lack of morality and disrespect towards Sramanas and Brahmans, violence, unseemly behaviour to friends, relatives and others, and evils of this kind have been checked. The killing of animals to a large extent was also stopped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google236_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-google236-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict V</b> refers to the appointment of Dhamma-mahamatta for the first time in the twelfth year of his reign. These special officers were appointed by the king to look after the interests of all sects and religions and spread the message of Dhamma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict VI</b>  is an instruction to Dhamma-mahamattas. They are told that they could bring their reports to the king at any time. The second part of the Edict deals with speedy administration and the transaction of smooth business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google236_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-google236-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict VII</b> is a plea for tolerance amongst all sects. It appears from the edict that tensions among the sects were intense perhaps in open antagonism. The plea is a part of the overall strategy to maintain unity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google236_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-google236-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict VIII</b>  states that Dhammayatras (tours) would be undertaken by the emperor. The earlier practice of the emperor going out on hunting expeditions was given up. Dhammayatras enabled the emperor to come into contact with various sections of people in the empire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google235_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-google235-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict IX</b>  attacks ceremonies performed after birth, illness, marriage and before going on a journey. A censure passed against ceremonies observed by wives and mothers. Ashoka instead lays stress on practice of Dhamma and the uselessness of ceremonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google238_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-google238-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict X</b>  denounces fame and glory and reasserts the merits of following the policy of Dhamma.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google238_49-1"><a href="#cite_note-google238-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict XI</b>  is a further explanation of the policy of Dhamma. It emphases the respect of elders, abstaining from killing animals, and liberality towards friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google235_45-2"><a href="#cite_note-google235-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict XII</b>  is another appeal for tolerance among sects. This edict reflects the anxiety the king felt because of conflict between sects and carries his plea for harmony.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google235_45-3"><a href="#cite_note-google235-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict XIII</b>  is of paramount importance in understanding the Ashokan policy of Dhamma. The Rock Edict pleads for conquest by Dhamma instead of war. This is logical culmination of the thought processes which began from the first Rock Edict, and by conquest what is perhaps meant is the adaptation of the policy of Dhamma by a country, rather than its territorial control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google235_45-4"><a href="#cite_note-google235-45">[45]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Major Rock Edict XIV</b> Ashoka said, <i>My dominions are wide, and much has been written, and I shall cause still more to be written. And some of this has been stated again and again because of the charm of certain topics and in order that men should act accordingly.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Text_of_the_Edicts">Text of the Edicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Text of the Edicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="378" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="104" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG/220px-6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG/330px-6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG/440px-6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:6thPillarOfAshoka.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fragment of the 6th Pillar Edicts of Ashoka (238 BCE), in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Br%C4%81hm%C4%AB_script" title="Brāhmī script">Brahmi</a>, sandstones.<a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">British Museum</a>.</div></div></div>
<ul><li>"When he had been consecrated eight years the Beloved of the Gods, the king Piyadasi <a href="/wiki/Ashoka" title="Ashoka">Ashoka</a> conquered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalinga_(historical_kingdom)" title="Kalinga (historical kingdom)">Kalinga</a>. A hundred and fifty thousand people were deported, a hundred thousand were killed and many times that number perished. Afterwards, now that Kalinga was annexed, the Beloved of the Gods very earnestly practised "Dhamma", desired "Dhamma". On conquering Kalinga the Beloved of the Gods felt remorse, for, when an independent country is conquered the slaughter, death, and deportation of people in extremely grievous to the Beloved of the Gods, and weighs heavily on his mind. What is even more deplorable to the Beloved to the Gods is that those who dwell there, whether Brahmans, Sarmanas, or those of other sects, or householders who show obedience to their superiors, obedience to their mother and father, obedience to their teachers and behave well and devotedly to their friends, acquiescence, colleagues, relatives, slaves and servants—all suffer violence, murder and separation from their loved ones. Even those who are fortunate to have escaped, and whose love is undiminished (by the <i>brutalizing</i> effect of war), suffer from the misfortunes of their friends, acquaintances colleagues and relatives. This participation of all men in suffering weighs heavily on the mind of Beloved of the Gods. Except among the Greeks, there is no land where religious orders of Brahmans and Sarmanas are not to be found, and there is no land anywhere where men do not support one sect or another. Today, if a hundredth or thousandth part of those people who were killed or died or were deported when Kalinga was annexed were to suffer similarly, it would weighs heavily on the mind of the Beloved of the Gods."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>"This inscription of Dhamma has been engraved so that any sons or great sons that I may have should not think of gaining new conquests, and in whatever victories they may gain should be satisfied with patience and light punishment. They should only consider conquest by Dhamma to be a true a conquest, and delight in Dhamma should be their whole delight, for this is of value in both this word and next."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>This is Ashoka's testament against war. It graphically depicts the tragedy of war and shows why he turned against it. It is a unique event in the annals of the ancient world because one does not knows of any other contemporary monarch who renounced war. Ashoka embarked on the policy of Dhamma after Kalinga war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dhamma_and_The_Mauryan_state">Dhamma and The Mauryan state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dhamma and The Mauryan state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ashoka's Dhamma was not simply a collection of high-sounding phrases. He consciously tried to adopt it as a matter of state policy; he declared that "all men and my children" and "whatever exertion I make, I strive only to discharge debt that I owe to all living creatures." It was totally new and inspiring ideal of kingship. In the <a href="/wiki/Arthashastra" title="Arthashastra">Arthashastra</a>, the king owed nothing to anyone. His only job was to rule the state efficiently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google592_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-google592-27">[27]</a></sup>
Ashoka renounced war and conquest by violence and forbade the killing of many animals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1989_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-google1989-54">[54]</a></sup> Ashoka set an example of vegetarianism by almost stopping the consumption of meat in the royal household. Since he wanted to conquer the world through love and faith, he sent many missions to propagate Dhamma. Such missions were sent to far off places like Egypt, Greece and Sri Lanka. The propagation of Dhamma included many measures of people's welfare. Centers of the treatment of men and beasts founded inside and outside of empire. Shady groves, wells, orchards and rest houses were laid out. This kind of charity work was a radically different attitude from the king of the Arthashastra, who would not incur any expenses unless they brought more revenues in return.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ashoka also prohibited useless sacrifices and certain forms of gatherings which led to waste, indiscipline and superstition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1989_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-google1989-54">[54]</a></sup> To implement these policies he recruited a new cadre of officers called Dhammamahamattas. Part of this group's duties was to see that people of various sects were treated fairly. They were especially asked to look after the welfare of prisoners. Many convicts—who were kept in fetters after their sentence had expired—were to be released. Those sentenced to death were to given grace for three days. Ashoka also started Dhamma yatras. He and his high officials were to tour the country propagating Dhamma and establishing direct contact with his subjects. Because of such attitudes and policies, modern writers like Kern called him "a monk in a king's garb."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">Interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashoka%27s_policy_of_Dhamma&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ashokan policy of Dhamma has been the subject of controversy and debate amongst scholars; Some have said that Ashoka was a partisan Buddhist and have equated Dhamma with Buddhism. It has also been suggested<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that it was the original Buddhist thought that was being preached by Ashoka as Dhamma and later on certain theological additions were made to Buddhism. This kind of thinking is based on some Buddhist chronicles. It is believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that the Kalinga war was a dramatic turning point where out of remorse for the death and destruction of war, Ashoka decided to become Buddhist. The Buddhist records credit him with the propagation of Buddhism in India and abroad. As an emperor, Ashoka did not favour Buddhism at the expenses of other religions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google592_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-google592-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramesh_Chandra_Majumdar" title="Ramesh Chandra Majumdar">Ramesh Chandra Majumdar</a>, Dhamma was not the policy of a heretic but a system of beliefs created out of different religious faiths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
There has been some discussion among historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> about the results Ashoka's propagation of Dhamma. Some historians<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> believe that Ashoka's ban of sacrifices and the favour that he showed to Buddhism led to a Brahmanical reaction, which in turn led to the decline of Mauryan empire. Others<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2013)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> believe that stopping of wars and the emphasis on non-violence crippled the military might of the empire, leading to its collapse after the death of Ashoka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Romila_Thapar" title="Romila Thapar">Romila Thapar</a>, Ashoka's Dhamma is a superb document of his essential humanity and an answer to the socio-political needs of the contemporaneous situation. It was not anti-Brahmanical because respect for the Brahmans and Sarmanas is an integral part of his Dhamma. His emphasis on non-violence did not blind him to the needs of the state. He warned the forest tribes that although he hates to use coercion, he may be required to resort to force if they continued to create trouble. By the time Ashoka stopped war, the entire Indian sub-continent was under his control. In the south he was on friendly terms with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cholas" title="Cholas">Cholas</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pandyas" title="Pandyas">Pandyas</a>. Sri Lanka was an admiring ally. Thus, Ashoka's decline of war came when his empire had reached its natural boundaries. The plea for tolerance was a wise course of action in an ethnically diverse, religiously varied, and class divided society. Ashoka's empire was a conglomerate of diverse groups; farmers, pastoral nomads and hunter-gatherers, there were Greeks, Kambojas, and Bhojas and hundreds of groups with different traditions. In this situation a plea for tolerance was needed. Ashoka tried to transcend the parochial cultural traditions with a board set of ethical principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Preface_4_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Preface_4-63">[63]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ashoka's "Dhamma" could not survive him; as such it was a failure. However, he was not establishing a new religion but was trying to impress upon the society the need for ethical and moral principles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
