	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Barbara Kulaszka</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Barbara Kulaszka</b> (1952/1953 – June 15, 2017) was a Canadian lawyer who practised law in <a href="/wiki/Brighton,_Ontario" title="Brighton, Ontario">Brighton, Ontario</a>, known for her work with far right causes, defending alleged <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war criminals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holocaust_denial" title="Holocaust denial">Holocaust deniers</a>, and free speech cases.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practice">Practice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbara_Kulaszka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Practice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kulaszka earned a law degree and was admitted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_Society_of_Upper_Canada" title="Law Society of Upper Canada">Law Society of Upper Canada</a> in 1980. She began her law practice in 1987 and acted with <a href="/wiki/Doug_Christie_(lawyer)" title="Doug Christie (lawyer)">Doug Christie</a> as co-counsel to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holocaust_denier" title="Holocaust denier">Holocaust denier</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Z%C3%BCndel" title="Ernst Zündel">Ernst Zündel</a> in the 1980s, and then assisted Christie as a legal researcher in a subsequent Zündel case in the 1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aff_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-aff-2">[2]</a></sup>  She also assisted Christie in the defence of alleged Nazi war criminal <a href="/wiki/Imre_Finta" title="Imre Finta">Imre Finta</a>, who was acquitted in 1990 (see <i><a href="/wiki/R_v_Finta" title="R v Finta">R v Finta</a>)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1990, she represented former Nazi rocket scientist <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rudolph" title="Arthur Rudolph">Arthur Rudolph</a> when he entered Canada and unsuccessfully sought visitor status when the government ruled he was inadmissible due to his suspected culpability in war crimes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Rudolph ultimately left Canada of his own accord and an immigration hearing held in his absence, at which he was represented by Kulaszka, ruled that Rudolph could not return to Canada due to his use of slave labour to produce <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 missiles</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>She addressed meetings of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Fromm_(politician)" title="Paul Fromm (politician)">Paul Fromm</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Association_for_Free_Expression" title="Canadian Association for Free Expression">Canadian Association for Free Expression</a>. In 2005, she defended <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Front" title="Heritage Front">Heritage Front</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Marc_Lemire" title="Marc Lemire">Marc Lemire</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Human_Rights_Tribunal" title="Canadian Human Rights Tribunal">Canadian Human Rights Tribunal</a> against a complaint filed against him by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Warman" title="Richard Warman">Richard Warman</a>. While losing the case, resulting in Lemire receiving a lifetime ban from publishing hate speech on the internet, the case is credited with resulting in the decision of the Canadian government to repeal Section 13.1 of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Human_Rights_Act" title="Canadian Human Rights Act">Canadian Human Rights Act</a> which had given the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Human_Rights_Tribunal" title="Canadian Human Rights Tribunal">Canadian Human Rights Tribunal</a> jurisdiction over the communication of hate speech online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> More recently, she unsuccessfully defended the operators of the conservative online forum <a href="/wiki/Free_Dominion" title="Free Dominion">Free Dominion</a> after they were sued for defamation by Warman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-warman_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-warman-6">[6]</a></sup> Her legal practice was based in <a href="/wiki/Brighton,_Ontario" title="Brighton, Ontario">Brighton, Ontario</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbara_Kulaszka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On June 15, 2017, Kulaszka died of <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> at the age of 64.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Her memorial service, held in a <a href="/wiki/Toronto_Public_Library" title="Toronto Public Library">Toronto Public Library</a> branch in <a href="/wiki/Etobicoke" title="Etobicoke">Etobicoke</a> on July 12, 2017, became a source of controversy due to plans to have speeches by <a href="/wiki/White_nationalism" title="White nationalism">white nationalists</a> such as Paul Fromm and former <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Front" title="Heritage Front">Heritage Front</a> leader Marc Lemire; Jewish and anti-racist groups urging the library to cancel the booking due to fears that it would promote <a href="/wiki/Hate_speech" title="Hate speech">hate speech</a> and Holocaust denial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Toronto" title="Mayor of Toronto">Mayor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Tory" title="John Tory">John Tory</a>
was among those calling on the library to cancel the event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Written_works">Written works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barbara_Kulaszka&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Written works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kulaszka edited <i>Did Six Million Really Die? – Report of the Evidence in the Canadian "False News" Trial of Ernst Zündel</i>, 1988<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mem_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-mem-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and wrote a second book, <i>The Hate Crimes Law in Canada, 1970–1994: Effects and Operation</i>. Both books were published by Zündel's <a href="/wiki/Samisdat_Publishers" title="Samisdat Publishers">Samisdat Publishers</a>.
</p>
