	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Nordland (boat)</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nordlandboat.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="471" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Nordlandboat.jpg/300px-Nordlandboat.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Nordlandboat.jpg/450px-Nordlandboat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Nordlandboat.jpg/600px-Nordlandboat.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nordlandboat.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two Nordland Boats, the larger one is low in the water, loaded with a hold of fish or wooden logs. Photo from between 1890-1900.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Nordland boat</b> (or <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a>: <i lang="no">Nordlandsbåt</i>), is a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishing_boat" title="Fishing boat">fishing boat</a> that has been used for centuries in northern counties of <a href="/wiki/Nordland" title="Nordland">Nordland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Troms" title="Troms">Troms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finnmark" title="Finnmark">Finnmark</a> of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> and derives its name from Nordland county where it has a long history.  It has dominated the <a href="/wiki/Lofoten" title="Lofoten">Lofoten</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vester%C3%A5len" title="Vesterålen">Vesterålen</a> islands fishing industry for centuries and is closely related to the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">Viking</a> <a href="/wiki/Longship" title="Longship">longships</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordland_(boat)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nordland boat has a clinker, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lapstrake" title="Lapstrake">lapstrake</a> <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)" title="Hull (watercraft)">hull</a> design and has its rudder on the <a href="/wiki/Sternpost" title="Sternpost">sternpost</a>. Its length varies from 14 to well over 40 feet and usually has a length to <a href="/wiki/Beam_(nautical)" title="Beam (nautical)">beam</a> ratio of 3-1 to 4-1. It has a high <a href="/wiki/Prow" title="Prow">prow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stern" title="Stern">stern</a>, shallow <a href="/wiki/Keel" title="Keel">keel</a>, <a href="/wiki/V-hull_(boat)" title="V-hull (boat)">v-hull</a> and has an inboard <a href="/wiki/Gunwale" title="Gunwale">gunwale</a>, which can be used to drain off the fishing nets when they are drawn on board. Some of the larger Nordlanders have a detachable cabin that is used for shelter, often having a wood-burning stove inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christiansen_1968_p.85_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Christiansen_1968_p.85-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Nordlander normally carries a large single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_sail" title="Square sail">square sail</a> with the largest boats carrying a <a href="/wiki/Topsail" title="Topsail">topsail</a>. It's one of the few types of boats that still carries such a sail to the present day.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oak" title="Oak">Oak</a> had been a favorite wood for ship builders for centuries for its resistance to rot, strength, and durability. However, oak is not native to Norway north of the county of <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%B8ndelag" title="Trøndelag">Trøndelag</a> and is also a heavy wood which would make such boats difficult to pull up on shore, which was done on a daily basis. For these reasons, the materials used for the Nordland is almost exclusively <a href="/wiki/Pine" title="Pine">pine</a>, and in the northern regions, <a href="/wiki/Fir" title="Fir">fir</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Pine and fir are lighter woods which would make it easier to be drawn up on shore, but at the expense of durability.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sami_versions">Sami versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordland_(boat)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sami versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nordland boat has a very long history in the north Norwegian coastline, with both Norwegians and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sami_people" title="Sami people">Sami</a>, who are first recorded as sailing such boats from about 950 onwards.  Around 1000 AD the Sami were described as producing Nordlandbots for their Norwegian customers along the northern coastal farming communities, which the Norwegians soon started to build for themselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the biggest differences between the Norwegian and Sami built Nordlands is that the Sami 'sewed' the lapstrakes together using reindeer intestines, while the Norwegians used iron rivets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christiansen_1968_p.85_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Christiansen_1968_p.85-1">[1]</a></sup>  Nordland boats continued to be built for over 1,000 years, and in the early 20th century it was still used for fishing and coastal transport.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unique_feature">Unique feature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordland_(boat)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unique feature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the unique features of the Nordland is its <a href="/wiki/Sailing_ballast" title="Sailing ballast">ballast</a> system.  Dozens of fist size round rocks are placed at the bottom of the boat to keep the boat weighted down.  In the event that the boat is either swamped or capsizes, the rocks are designed to roll out of the boat which would lighten the boat and thus keep it afloat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Nordland_Boat_today">The Nordland Boat today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nordland_(boat)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Nordland Boat today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nordland boats today are no longer used as primarily fishing boats but as pleasure craft and have annual races.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  The coat-of-arms of Nordland County shows the traditional boat - <i>the Nordlandsbåt</i>.
</p>
