	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Jeannie Rousseau</h1>

<p><b>Jeannie Yvonne Ghislaine Rousseau</b>, married name <b>Jeannie de Clarens</b>, (1 April 1919 – 23 August 2017) was an Allied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intelligence_agent" title="Intelligence agent">intelligence agent</a> in occupied France during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a member of the "Druids" network led by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Georges_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Georges Lamarque (page does not exist)">Georges Lamarque</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;"> [<a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lamarque" title="fr:Georges Lamarque">fr</a>]</span>. Codenamed Amniarix, she evaded Gestapo agents while gathering crucial information on the Germans' emerging rocket weapons programs from behind enemy lines.  Her intelligence reports, forwarded to London, led directly to the <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Peenem%C3%BCnde_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Peenemünde in World War II">British raid on Peenemünde</a> and to delays and disruptions in the <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V-1</a> and <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2</a> programs, saving many thousands of lives in the West. Rousseau was captured twice and spent time in three concentration camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-woolsey_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-woolsey-1">[1]</a></sup> After the war, she worked as a freelance interpreter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeannie_Rousseau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born on 1 April 1919 in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Brieuc" title="Saint-Brieuc">Saint-Brieuc</a>, Jeannie Yvonne Ghislaine Rousseau was the daughter of Jean Rousseau, a <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> veteran and a French foreign ministry official, and his wife Marie, née Le Charpentier. A brilliant linguist, she graduated in languages from <a href="/wiki/Sciences_Po" title="Sciences Po">Sciences Po</a> in 1939. After the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, she moved with her family to <a href="/wiki/Dinard" title="Dinard">Dinard</a> where she became an interpreter for the occupying German forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wartime_work">Wartime work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeannie_Rousseau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wartime work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rousseau began gathering intelligence on German operations even before she made contact with Allied intelligence. She took a job at the French national chamber of commerce as a translator and soon became the organization's top staffer, meeting regularly with the German military commander's staff. She was a frequent visitor with the Germans, discussing commercial issues, such as complaints about Nazi commandeering or offers to sell them goods, such as steel and rubber. "I was storing my nuts, but I had no way to pass them on."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-washpo-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell2012_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell2012-6">[6]</a></sup> She was arrested by the Gestapo in January 1941,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup> but was later released and prohibited from staying in the coastal area.
</p><p>In 1941 she moved to Paris where she began working for a Parisian company that supplied materials to the German war effort, thus positioning herself as a source of valuable information for the Allied forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell2012_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell2012-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-independent-8">[8]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Her formal career as a spy began in 1941, with a chance meeting with Georges Lamarque on a night train from Paris to Vichy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-independent-8">[8]</a></sup> Lamarque remembered Rousseau from the University of Paris, where she had shown talent in languages, including German, and finished first in her class, in 1939. He asked her to work for him, and she immediately agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-washpo-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>During 1943, she filed, among other reports two particularly remarkable ones about Peenemünde. These reports led R.V. Jones, and ultimately, the rest of the British government and the rest of the Allies, directly to the missile and rocket development work going on there. Her collection and forwarding of this intelligence under very difficult circumstances led, through Jones' analysis and persuasive abilities in London, to the British raid on Peenemünde.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-woolsey_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-woolsey-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Victor_Jones" title="Reginald Victor Jones">R.V. Jones</a> relates that when he first inquired about the source of the extraordinary report that had originally tipped off the British government to what was going on at <a href="/wiki/Peenem%C3%BCnde_Army_Research_Center" title="Peenemünde Army Research Center">Peenemünde</a>, all he learned was that it came from "<abbr title="a most remarkable young girl of her generation">one of the most remarkable young women of her generation</abbr>," part of a small espionage network reporting from occupied France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Shortly before <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D-Day" title="D-Day">D-Day</a>, a plan to evacuate her and two other agents was foiled by the Gestapo. She was the first to be caught. But even as she was being captured, she warned her comrades and one escaped. As Jones put it: "AMNIARIX's reports stand brilliantly in the history of intelligence, and three concentration camps — <a href="/wiki/Ravensbr%C3%BCck_concentration_camp" title="Ravensbrück concentration camp">Ravensbruck</a>, Königsberg (a punishment camp), and <a href="/wiki/Torgau" title="Torgau">Torgau</a> could not break her."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-woolsey_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-woolsey-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She was rescued by the Swedish Red Cross shortly before the end of the war. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_the_war">Life after the war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeannie_Rousseau&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Life after the war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="518" data-file-width="675" decoding="async" height="307" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif/400px-Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif/600px-Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6c/Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif 2x" width="400"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jones-Woolsey-deClarens.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jeanne de Clarens with <a href="/wiki/R._James_Woolsey_Jr." title="R. James Woolsey Jr.">James Woolsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Victor_Jones" title="Reginald Victor Jones">Reginald Victor Jones</a> in 1992</div></div></div>
<p>While recovering in Sweden from <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> contracted during her imprisonment, she met Henri de Clarens, who had been in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buchenwald" title="Buchenwald">Buchenwald</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auschwitz" title="Auschwitz">Auschwitz</a> concentration camps. The two married and had two children. De Clarens died in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rousseau worked as a freelance interpreter after the war, for the United Nations and other agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup> Avoiding interviews with reporters and historians, her story remained largely untold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SFGate_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-SFGate-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1993, then as Madame Jeannie de Clarens, she agreed to accept the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Agency_Seal_Medal" title="Agency Seal Medal">Agency Seal Medal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup> She revealed more details of her story to journalist David Ignatius in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washpo_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-washpo-4">[4]</a></sup> Other awards included the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Legion of Honour</a> in 1955, supplemented by the grand officer medal in 2009. She also received the <a href="/wiki/Resistance_Medal" title="Resistance Medal">Resistance Medal</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Croix_de_guerre_1939%E2%80%931945_(France)" title="Croix de guerre 1939–1945 (France)">Croix de guerre</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>She died on 23 August 2017 in <a href="/wiki/Montaigu,_Vend%C3%A9e" title="Montaigu, Vendée">Montaigu, Vendée</a>, aged 98.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
