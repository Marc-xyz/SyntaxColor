<p><b>Jules-Félix Coutan</b> (22 September 1848 – 23 February 1939) was a French sculptor and educator.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jules_Coutan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a student at the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux-Arts" title="École des Beaux-Arts">École des Beaux-Arts</a>, Coutan was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Prix_de_Rome" title="Prix de Rome">Prix de Rome</a> in 1872; after his return to Paris he executed the fountain group <i>France Bearing the Torch of Civilization</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1889)" title="Exposition Universelle (1889)">Exposition Universelle (1889)</a>, one of the two prominent sculptural commissions for the exposition grounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Later he taught at the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux-Arts" title="École des Beaux-Arts">École des Beaux-Arts</a> from 1900, where he expressed his disdain for the researches of <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Rodin" title="Auguste Rodin">Rodin</a> (as <i lang="fr" title="French language text">fumiste</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impressionist" title="Impressionist">Impressionist</a> sculptors who followed him.  He was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_Beaux-Arts" title="Académie des Beaux-Arts">Académie des Beaux-Arts</a> in 1905.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Coutan is best known in the United States for the sculptural group above the entrance to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a> in New York City.  For Grand Central Terminal, Coutan was contracted to provide a quarter-size scale plaster model of the three-figure allegorical <i>Transportation</i> group, which he developed from 1911 through 1914.  (Coutan never visited the U.S.)  The carving was performed by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Bradley_%26_Son&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Bradley &amp; Son (page does not exist)">William Bradley &amp; Son</a> of Long Island City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The small bronzes, some stamped by the founders Thiebaut Frères, that represented a constant source of income for Coutan and a genre typical of his output, appear with some frequency on the art market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among Coutan's students were <a href="/wiki/Hippolyte_Lef%C3%A8bvre" title="Hippolyte Lefèbvre">Hippolyte Lefèbvre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Delamarre" title="Raymond Delamarre">Raymond Delamarre</a>, and the Argentine sculptor <a href="/wiki/Rogelio_Yrurtia" title="Rogelio Yrurtia">Rogelio Yrurtia</a>.
</p>
