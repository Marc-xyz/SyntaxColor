<p>The <b>Radisson Hotel Baltimore Downtown-Inner Harbor</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-rise" title="High-rise">high-rise</a> hotel complex located in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore, Maryland</a>. The complex contains two nearly identical towers, rising to 302 feet/92 meters, containing 27 floors and making them among the <a href="/wiki/List_of_tallest_buildings_in_Baltimore" title="List of tallest buildings in Baltimore">tallest buildings in Baltimore</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radisson_Hotel_Baltimore_Downtown-Inner_Harbor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hotel opened as <b>The Statler Hilton Baltimore</b> on July 15, 1967<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> with one tower, containing 352 rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> The Statler Hilton was a part of the Charles Center urban renewal project. The sponsors and developers, the Hilton Hotels Corporation and the Metropolitan Structures, Inc., signed a contract on July 25, 1964. The hotel was expected to cost $12 million and to contain 500 to 800 rooms in two towers. The first tower was scheduled to be completed by mid-1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The head architect of the project was <a href="/wiki/William_B._Tabler" title="William B. Tabler">William B. Tabler</a>; a famous hotel architect who designed Hiltons throughout the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The second tower was a later addition.
</p><p>The hotel eventually became the <b>Baltimore Hilton</b>. In 1984, it was renamed the <b>Omni International Baltimore</b>, in 2000 the <b>Wyndham Baltimore</b>, in 2006 the <b>Sheraton Baltimore City Center</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> on May 29, 2014 the <b>Baltimore Harbor Hotel</b>, and on May 18, 2016 the <b>Radisson Hotel Baltimore Downtown-Inner Harbor</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The hotel was the site of a 1980 Presidential debate between <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_B._Anderson" title="John B. Anderson">John B. Anderson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup> TV
</p><p>It also played host to the first WWE (then WWF) Hall of Fame induction ceremony.
</p><p>The North Tower has 23 floors and the South Tower (which is now a separate <b>Crowne Plaza Hotel</b>)  has 27 floors.
</p>
