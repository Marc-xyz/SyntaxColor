		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>David of Ashby</b> (<a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl.</a> 1260 – 1275) was an English-born <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> friar who was sent from the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Land" title="Holy Land">Holy Land</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Acre,_Israel" title="Acre, Israel">Acre</a> to the Mongol ruler <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hulagu" title="Hulagu">Hulagu</a> in 1260, by the Papal legate <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Agni_de_Lentino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Agni de Lentino (page does not exist)">Thomas Agni de Lentino</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He stayed around 15 years among the Mongols, and only returned from Iran in 1274.
</p><p>David of Ashby was a member of the Mongol embassy to the <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Lyon" title="Second Council of Lyon">Second Council of Lyon</a> in 1274, sent by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abaqa" title="Abaqa">Abaqa</a>. The embassy arrived on 5 July 1274. David was accompanied by the clerk <a href="/wiki/Rychaldus" title="Rychaldus">Rychaldus</a>, in the service of the Mongols, and 14 Mongol dignitaries. The leader of the Mongols underwent baptism on this occasion, on 16 July.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>David of Ashby wrote a book <i>Les fais des <a href="/wiki/Tatars" title="Tatars">Tatars</a></i> ("The facts about the Tartars") describing the culture of the Tatars and their remarkable military organization and discipline.
</p>
