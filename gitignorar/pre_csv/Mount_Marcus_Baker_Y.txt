<p><b>Mount Marcus Baker</b> is the highest peak of the <a href="/wiki/Chugach_Mountains" title="Chugach Mountains">Chugach Mountains</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>. 
It is located approximately 75 miles (121 km) east of Anchorage. This peak is very prominent because of its proximity to tidewater and is only 12 miles (19 km) north of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_calving" title="Ice calving">calving</a> face of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Glacier" title="Harvard Glacier">Harvard Glacier</a>. 
When ranked by <a href="/wiki/Topographic_prominence" title="Topographic prominence">topographic prominence</a>, Mount Marcus Baker is one of the top 75 peaks in the world.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Marcus_Baker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mount Marcus Baker was originally called "Mount Saint Agnes"; according to <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Washburn" title="Bradford Washburn">Bradford Washburn</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_W._Bagley" title="James W. Bagley">James W. Bagley</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">USGS</a> named it after his wife Agnes, adding the "Saint" in hopes of making the name stick. The name was later changed to honor a cartographer and geologist named <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Baker" title="Marcus Baker">Marcus Baker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood_coombs_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-wood_coombs-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The peak was first climbed on June 19, 1938 by a party led by famed explorer <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Washburn" title="Bradford Washburn">Bradford Washburn</a>; the climb took almost two months owing to weather delays. Today's standard route is the North Ridge. Despite being much lower in elevation than <a href="/wiki/Denali" title="Denali">Denali</a>, Marcus Baker is a similarly serious ascent, due to the remoteness of the peak and the resulting length of the approach and climb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wood_coombs_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-wood_coombs-3">[3]</a></sup>  A number of noted climbers have perished or sustained permanent injury in attempting to summit the peak as climbing conditions can change rapidly as storms arise.  In early 1988, a State of Alaska Fish and Game biologist, 28-year-old Sylvia Jean Lane, succumbed to hypothermia as a two-day storm separated her from the two others in the climbing party attempting to dash to the top in a winter ascent.
</p>
