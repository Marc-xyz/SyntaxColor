<p>The <b>1952 United States Senate election in Massachusetts</b> was held on November 4, 1952. <a href="/wiki/Incumbent" title="Incumbent">Incumbent</a> <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr." title="Henry Cabot Lodge Jr.">Henry Cabot Lodge Jr.</a> lost to <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> nominee. This election marked the end of the <a href="/wiki/Lodge_family" title="Lodge family">Lodge family</a> dynasty and the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_family" title="Kennedy family">Kennedy family</a> dynasty.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_election">General election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1952_United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: General election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Campaign">Campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1952_United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1952 Massachusetts Senate election was a contest between two representatives of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>'s most prominent political families: the Republican Lodges and the Democratic Kennedys. The Lodges were a much older political dynasty; the family could trace its roots to the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> pioneers who had first settled the state in the early seventeenth century. The Lodges were a "<a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">Blue blood</a>" family, and along with several other Boston-area Protestant families, were considered to be at the apex of Massachusetts High Society, and they had been prominent in Boston political and business circles for generations. Lodge's grandfather, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge,_Sr." title="Henry Cabot Lodge, Sr.">Henry Cabot Lodge, Sr.</a>, had been a powerful United States Senator from Massachusetts, as well as a close friend and ally of President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>; he was also a foe of Woodrow Wilson. His grandson and namesake, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge,_Jr." title="Henry Cabot Lodge, Jr.">Henry Cabot Lodge, Jr.</a>, had first been elected to the U.S. Senate in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts,_1936" title="United States Senate election in Massachusetts, 1936">1936</a>, when he was the only Republican Senate candidate in the nation to defeat a Democratic incumbent. He was easily reelected in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts,_1942" title="United States Senate election in Massachusetts, 1942">1942</a>. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> he had resigned his Senate seat and served in the U.S. Army. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts,_1946" title="United States Senate election in Massachusetts, 1946">1946</a> Lodge reclaimed a Senate seat when he defeated Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/David_I._Walsh" title="David I. Walsh">David Walsh</a>.
</p><p>Lodge's Democratic opponent in the 1952 Senate race was three-term Congressman <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, then only 35 years old. Although the Kennedys were a much newer political dynasty than the Lodges, they had amassed a considerably larger financial fortune, thanks in large part to the business activities of Kennedy's father, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy" title="Joseph P. Kennedy">Joseph P. Kennedy</a>. The Kennedys were Irish Catholics, and in many ways the 1952 Massachusetts Senate campaign was the climax of a longstanding battle between the older Protestant families like the Lodges, who had controlled politics in the Bay State for generations, and the newer Irish Catholic families such as the Kennedys, who for demographic reasons now outnumbered the Protestants. The Kennedys also viewed the 1952 race as something of a grudge match, as Lodge's grandfather had defeated Kennedy's grandfather, Boston Mayor <a href="/wiki/John_F._Fitzgerald" title="John F. Fitzgerald">John F. Fitzgerald</a>, in a 1916 Senate race in Massachusetts.
</p><p>Congressman Kennedy's Senate campaign was managed by his younger brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Kennedy" title="Robert Kennedy">Robert Kennedy</a>, who would perform the same function for his brother in the 1960 presidential campaign. Kennedy launched his campaign early in 1952 and made an intensive effort, by election day in November 1952 he had visited every city, town, and village in Massachusetts at least once. He also collected a record number of signatures for his petition for office, assembling a petition of over a quarter-million signatures. Many of those who signed the petition would later become campaign volunteers or workers for Kennedy in their hometowns. A famous innovation by the Kennedys in the 1952 Senate race were a series of "tea parties" sponsored by Kennedy's mother and sisters in the fall. Congressman Kennedy attended each of the tea parties and shook hands and charmed the voters (usually female) who were present; it is estimated that a total of 70,000 voters attended the tea parties, which was roughly his margin of victory over Lodge.
</p><p>Lodge, meanwhile, neglected his Senate campaign for most of 1952. Instead, he focused on persuading <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>, the popular <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> general, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draft_Eisenhower" title="Draft Eisenhower">run for</a> and win the Republican presidential nomination over Ohio Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Taft" title="Robert A. Taft">Robert A. Taft</a>, the leader of the party's conservatives. Lodge, a moderate and <a href="/wiki/Internationalism_(politics)" title="Internationalism (politics)">internationalist</a>, strongly disagreed with Taft's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isolationist" title="Isolationist">isolationist</a> foreign-policy views and felt that Taft could not win a presidential election. Lodge served as Eisenhower's campaign manager and played a key role in helping Eisenhower to beat Taft and win the Republican nomination. However, Lodge's prominent role in defeating Taft angered many of Taft's supporters in Massachusetts, and they vowed revenge. Congressman Kennedy privately courted many of Taft's more prominent backers in Massachusetts, and some of them, such as Basil Brewer, the publisher of the <i>New Bedford Standard-Times</i>, supported Kennedy over Lodge in their newspapers and editorials.
</p><p>When the usually Democratic-leaning but financially unstable <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_Post" title="Boston Post">Boston Post</a></i> planned to endorse Lodge, Joseph Kennedy arranged for a $500,000 loan so the paper would endorse his son; John Kennedy stated that "we had to buy that fucking paper or I'd have been licked."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leamer2001_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-leamer2001-3">[3]</a></sup> Kennedy and Lodge engaged in one public debate, which was held on radio; the debate was generally considered a draw, although some observers felt that Kennedy's ability to hold his own with the older and more distinguished Lodge gave him the advantage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The nationally-known and Catholic Senator <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCarthy" title="Joseph McCarthy">Joseph McCarthy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> refused to campaign for Lodge, a fellow Republican, due to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCarthy#Support_from_Catholics_and_Kennedy_family" title="Joseph McCarthy">his friendship with the Kennedy family</a>. McCarthy was popular among many Catholic voters in Massachusetts due to his Communist-hunting activities in Congress; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_F._Buckley,_Jr." title="William F. Buckley, Jr.">William F. Buckley, Jr.</a> believed that Lodge probably would have won the election with McCarthy's help.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amex_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-amex-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the weekend before the election Eisenhower visited Boston and energetically campaigned for Lodge, but it was not enough. Although Eisenhower carried Massachusetts by over 200,000 votes, Kennedy narrowly upset Lodge, winning by 70,000 votes and three percentage points.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1952_United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kennedy's narrow victory marked the end of the Lodge dynasty and beginning of the Kennedy dynasty. Since January 1953 no member of the Lodge family has held political office in Massachusetts, and the family has largely retired from politics. Conversely, the Kennedy family controlled the Senate seat they won in 1952 from January 1953 until <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>'s death in August 2009, as John Kennedy, family friend <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_A._Smith_II" title="Benjamin A. Smith II">Benjamin A. Smith II</a>, and then Ted Kennedy each held the seat. Lodge served for eight years as President Eisenhower's <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_the_United_Nations" title="United States Ambassador to the United Nations">United Nations Ambassador</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/1960_United_States_presidential_election" title="1960 United States presidential election">1960 presidential election</a>, he was <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>'s running mate, but the Democratic ticket of Senators Kennedy and <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> won the election. Lodge's son <a href="/wiki/George_C._Lodge" title="George C. Lodge">George C. Lodge</a> lost the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts,_1962" title="United States Senate election in Massachusetts, 1962">1962 Massachusetts Senate race</a> to Ted Kennedy, the last time that the two families opposed one another in a political campaign.
</p>
