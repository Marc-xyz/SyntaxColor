<p><b>Sarai Sherman</b> (September 2, 1922 – October 24, 2013) was a <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Pennsylvania</a>-born Jewish <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> artist whose work, both in America and Europe shaped international views of women and <a href="/wiki/Abstract_expressionism" title="Abstract expressionism">abstract expressionism</a>. She was a significant twentieth century painter and sculptor known for her abstract paintings, prints and ceramics.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sherman was born in the Germantown section of <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> in 1922. She showed an early predisposition towards painting and was enrolled in an arts and graphics program around the age of 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venturolli_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venturolli-1">[1]</a></sup> These early years began to shape her artistic interests in people, nature and the built environment. She attended <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kensington_High_School_for_Girls" title="Kensington High School for Girls">Kensington High School</a> and continued to explore artistic themes and painting.  Sherman studied at the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Barnes_Foundation" title="Barnes Foundation">Barnes Foundation</a> in Pennsylvania where she was exposed to seminal works of modern masters<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ragghianti_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ragghianti-2">[2]</a></sup> and she attended the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyler_School_of_Art" title="Tyler School of Art">Tyler School of Art</a> at <a href="/wiki/Temple_University" title="Temple University">Temple University</a>, headed at the time by Russian-born artist Boris Blai with faculty including Earl Horter and Honga Holm. Sherman graduated with a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Fine_Arts" title="Bachelor of Fine Arts">Bachelor of Fine Arts</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in education and then enrolled into the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> program at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>, graduating with a degree in art history and painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venturolli_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Venturolli-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1948, at the age of 26 Sherman showed a painting titled “Hericane Time” at the <a href="/wiki/Pyramid_Club_(Philadelphia)" title="Pyramid Club (Philadelphia)">Pyramid Club</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sherman moved to New York City to pursue art. During this period she designed fabrics and wallpaper that were sold though stores in Philadelphia and New York. In a <a href="/wiki/United_Press_International" title="United Press International">United Press International</a> article published in June 1958 Sherman provided a glimpse into her textile and interior architectural design philosophy: "Designers must have a real sense about people and machines or they'll produce designs which are cultural lags.  We must understand the American Women especially, and realize that she is in a state of transition." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>During WWII, Sherman lived in <a href="/wiki/Eagle_Pass,_Texas" title="Eagle Pass, Texas">Eagle Pass, Texas</a> where her husband was stationed with the 45th Massachusetts Regiment.
</p>
<h3><span id="Italy.2C_1952-1954"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy,_1952-1954">Italy, 1952-1954</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Italy, 1952-1954">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sarai_Sherman,_Canto_Mezzogiorno_(1953).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1560" data-file-width="2000" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Canto_Mezzogiorno_%281953%29.jpg/220px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Canto_Mezzogiorno_%281953%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Canto_Mezzogiorno_%281953%29.jpg/330px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Canto_Mezzogiorno_%281953%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Canto_Mezzogiorno_%281953%29.jpg/440px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Canto_Mezzogiorno_%281953%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sarai_Sherman,_Canto_Mezzogiorno_(1953).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>painting by Sarai Sherman</div></div></div>
<p>Sherman was awarded a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulbright" title="Fulbright">Fulbright</a> Grant to paint in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>.  There she gained a deep perspective into the poverty of postwar <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The experiences touched Sherman and became a turning point in her artistic development. For her, Italy became a platform from where she could look and participate in culture completely. She wrote from Matera in the Spring of 1953 "I feel at home...not because these caves remind me of Philadelphia, but because somehow a passage of light or color, or the motion of an animal, recall to my mind moments of my childhood, distant places.  I feel as if it were my former self contemplating this forbidding white region. I see a women with her child in her arms: His small body is in continuous motion, he looks around, he screams. I see mother and child in the sunlight, and it seems to me that I saw them years before, exhausted, yet beautiful.  The old image is superimposed in the new one. The painting process is automatic, spontaneous, it leads me.  The finished product is all chrome yellow, with ochre hues hovering between white and ivory. They reveal a sense of the ancient."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roma_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Roma-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sherman's conceptual ideas that were expressed in her painting were comparisons between reality and reminiscences, a compelling concern with the world of others, with the dejected southern Italian population and an identification of her own origin within that dejection and that poverty. During these years her art proceeded autobiographically, in the sense that, following an irrepressible necessity she identified with the subjects of her paintings.  This period is full of symbolic content, the tenderness, the thoughtfulness of her paintings, which often become the moment of completion of an experience instead of simply leading to the discovery of new worlds of characters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roma_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Roma-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The exceptional episodic importance which was at the base of works done before 1955 becomes evident in the late 1950s.  What used to be the figurative concentration of a tragedy becomes a portrayal of persons and things perceived in the midst of reality <i>in fieri</i> until the fleeting moment sets in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roma_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Roma-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1955-1960">1955-1960</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1955-1960">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sarai_Sherman,_Baby_Doll_(1957).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="399" decoding="async" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Baby_Doll_%281957%29.jpg/220px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Baby_Doll_%281957%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Baby_Doll_%281957%29.jpg/330px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Baby_Doll_%281957%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Baby_Doll_%281957%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sarai_Sherman,_Baby_Doll_(1957).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Baby Doll, 1957</div></div></div>
<p>From 1955 to 1960 Sherman worked in the United States. During this period, she amplified the typology and the background of her experiences, weighting the density of her painting. She retracted her steps from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picasso" title="Picasso">Picasso</a> to the Impressionists. Like <a href="/wiki/Francis_Bacon" title="Francis Bacon">Francis Bacon</a>, who after his surrealist Phicassian phase reverted to color painting, Sherman transferred onto her canvas a number of principles of the French masters of the late nineteenth century, filtered through schemes, vision and images, which were neither nineteenth century nor post-romantic in character, largely because internal plastic contraditions of the work and because of her awareness of the dimension of time. The ever-changing spectacle offered by the streets, are elements which have entered her fantasy by force, obliging her to move in a humble effort towards an increased objectivity, but which in the same act of reduction emerge more autobiographical then ever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roma_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-Roma-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sherman was a part of the emergency response to the <a href="/wiki/1966_flood_of_the_Arno" title="1966 flood of the Arno">1966 flood of the Arno</a> in Florence Italy. A group of international women artists called the “<a href="/wiki/Flood_Ladies" title="Flood Ladies">Flood Ladies</a>” donated work to the city as a sign of solidarity following the Arno's catastrophic damage. The work donated to the city is now housed in the "Museum of the 1900s." As part of the 50th anniversary of the flooding, Sherman's work titled <i>Icarus</i> was one of 28 in a representative traveling exhibition and featured in the Book “When the World Answered” and the 2015 PBS documentary of the same name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sherman was represented by the ACA Galleries in New York throughout the 1950s and then by the Forum Gallery. Sherman has had numerous significant solo exhibitions throughout the United States and in Europe including the ACA Galley, New York City (1951, 1955, 1958, 1960); Galleria La Nuova Pesa, Rome (1961), and Galleria Viotti, Turin (1963) – both in Italy; Forum Gallery, New York City (1963, 1967, 1970, 1974, 1986); Fairweather-Hardin Gallery, Chicago Illinois (1964); Museum of Contemporary Art, Skopje, Yugoslavia (1965); Galerie Weltz, Salzburg, Austria and Salon Tribune Mladih, Novi Sad, Yugoslavia (1966); Galleria dell’Orso, Milan (1973), and Studio 5, Bologna (1976) – both in Italy; Madison Gallery, Toronto Canada (1976); Galleria Giulia, Rome, Italy (1982).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sarai_Sherman,_Bear_Cat_(1957).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="354" decoding="async" height="318" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Bear_Cat_%281957%29.jpg/220px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Bear_Cat_%281957%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Bear_Cat_%281957%29.jpg/330px-Sarai_Sherman%2C_Bear_Cat_%281957%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4b/Sarai_Sherman%2C_Bear_Cat_%281957%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sarai_Sherman,_Bear_Cat_(1957).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bear Cat, 1957</div></div></div>
<p>Sherman created numerous lithographs and prints. Many of these editioned works were produced at the renowned atelier Il Bisonte in Florence who also produced significant works for <a href="/wiki/Henry_Moore" title="Henry Moore">Henry Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Picasso</a>.
</p><p>Sherman won numerous awards and prizes for her work including a Pepsi Cola award (1945), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fulbright_Foundation" title="Fulbright Foundation">Fulbright Foundation</a> fellowship to Italy (1952-1954), a painting award from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Academy_and_Institute_of_Arts_and_Letters" title="American Academy and Institute of Arts and Letters">American Academy and Institute of Arts and Letters</a>, New York City (1964) and their Childe Hassam Prize (1970); in addition to many medals, citations and prizes in exhibitions throughout Italy and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Proctor_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Proctor Prize (page does not exist)">Proctor Prize</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Academy_of_Design" title="National Academy of Design">National Academy of Design</a>, New York City (1976).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style_and_influences">Style and influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Style and influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sherman's major, and critically acclaimed, work produced in Italy during the mid 1950 was focused on the sun drenched settings, people and post World War II milieu of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">Southern Italy</a>. The tonal and ethereal quality of her paintings from this period capture the arid colors and blinding light of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mezzogiorno" title="Mezzogiorno">Mezzogiorno</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mario Penelope recalled in his 1983 essay on Sherman titled Coherence And Reality: "a group of paintings done during that first stay which were exhibited in nineteen hundred and fifty five in her first Roman one-person show at the Zodiac Gallery. In these canvases there were images of women, children, and sharecroppers; portrayed austerely in their most typical attitudes in a strange archaic manner. They seemed encamped with weighted solemmity in a timeless spatiality; in an understated drama of transcendent sadness which laid bare with bitter emotion the dark pathetic solitude of that sharecropper world. Shapes and forms were conceived within a rigorous formal scheme. These shapes were layered into a rhythm of concrete volumes in which at intervals the profound sense of the value of color soberly restrained filtered through.  Forms were emphasized by a concise pre-meditated linear drawing in which there were residual suggestions of the lesson of Picasso and <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ragghianti_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ragghianti-2">[2]</a></sup>   
</p><p>The Italian art critic <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Duilio_Morosini&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Duilio Morosini (page does not exist)">Duilio Morosini</a> framed Sherman in an artistic and cultural context in her 1977 essay for the book Sarai Sherman. Morosini wrote that one needs now to place “Sherman in a historical relationship to other exponents of American critical realism, both of the first and second generation.  The major recurring theme of an isolated urban solitude (expressed by an standardization of choices, in personal ideals and practical solutions, and hence the disappearance of feeling and communication) is not  seen by this artist as a grievous meditation of the state of things where nothing can be achieved, as one sees in <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hopper" title="Edward Hopper">Edward Hopper</a>’s revealing objectivism; nor is it an irascible accusation as in the post war II revival of <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionism</a>. In her painting (not only the earlier period from 1954 to 1965, but in all her developments) the characters do not rely upon indicative details, rather upon the “whole” with its resolute “aura” (inherent in such pictorialization of the thought imagery). The projection of light in the painting in which every presence, every act seems to be caught in an equivocal transition between day and night or vice versa; in other words, in the inquietude and expectation of an unpredictable change.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duilio_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Duilio-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>“In Sherman’s work there is a consistent involvement with a visualization of distorted values in the continuum of social generalization.  During 1962 she completed a series of large composition paintings that focused on the death of the matrix of post World War II sex symbols. The capacity of Sherman to use the ambivalence inherent in metaphorical values, as a springboard in the employ of tonal color, immersed in diffused light has also made it possible to retain a sensitivity towards a younger generation’s milieu.  Her work in the 1970s reflected a shift in the pictorialization of generalized meanings; a constantly precarious situation for all painters of Sherman’s generations.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Duilio_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Duilio-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Overarching themes in Sherman's work focused on concerns with humankind's inhumanity to one another, where form and content are one.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceramics">Ceramics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ceramics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sherman began exploring three-dimensional form in ceramic sculpture in the early 1980s.  The contemplative work from this period presents serne animal and human forms to the viewers gaze. The organic sensual shapes evoke or are explicitly feminine in form. The tactile and often delicate porcelain work were metaphorical juxtapositions of fundamental dualities: peace and war, life and death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Guzzetti_Chapel_.28Cappella_Guzzetti.29.2C_Camera_Picta.2C_Cortona_Italia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Guzzetti_Chapel_(Cappella_Guzzetti),_Camera_Picta,_Cortona_Italia"><a href="/wiki/Guzzetti_Chapel" title="Guzzetti Chapel">Guzzetti Chapel</a> (Cappella Guzzetti), Camera Picta, Cortona Italia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Guzzetti Chapel (Cappella Guzzetti), Camera Picta, Cortona Italia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1987 and 1994 Sherman created major site-specific fresco and <a href="/wiki/Altarpiece" title="Altarpiece">altarpiece</a> commission in <a href="/wiki/Cortona" title="Cortona">Cortona</a>, Italy for the <a href="/wiki/Guzzetti_Chapel" title="Guzzetti Chapel">Guzzetti Chapel</a> on the grounds of the Villa Corono. The <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">fresco</a> cycle called Camera Picta are painted on the walls of the 18th century building owned by the Italian art patrons the Guzzettis. The allegorical work focus on the biblical and secular themes including sheep, bucolic landscape and flora.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  The paintings are saturated in muted tones and soft umber, ochre and cream colors that recall her work of the 1950s.  The full commission included paintings, sculpture and architectural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trompe_l%E2%80%99oeil" title="Trompe l’oeil">trompe l’oeil</a>. The altar-piece, (1992-1994) utilizes three-dimensional ceramic forms, a rendering of sheep in repetition as biblical symbolism.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarai_Sherman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sherman's work is included in numerous private and public permanent collections including the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">Museum of Modern Art</a> (MoMA) New York; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitney_Museum_of_Art" title="Whitney Museum of Art">Whitney Museum of Art</a>, New York;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Syracuse_University" title="Syracuse University">Syracuse University</a>; <a href="/wiki/Hirshhorn_Museum_and_Sculpture_Garden" title="Hirshhorn Museum and Sculpture Garden">Hirshhorn Museum and Sculpture Garden</a>, Washington, DC; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Modern_Art_in_Rome" title="National Gallery of Modern Art in Rome">National Gallery of Modern Art in Rome</a>; the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uffizi_Gallery" title="Uffizi Gallery">Uffizi Gallery</a>, Florence, Italy; Sconci Art Gallery, Dubai; Jerusalem Museum, Israel; National Gallery of Modern Art, Bratislava, Czechoslovakia; International Museum of Modern Art, Skopje, Yugoslavia; Gramsci Museum, Ghilarza, Sardinia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sherman died in New York City on October 24, 2013.
</p>
