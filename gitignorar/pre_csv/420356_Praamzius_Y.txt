<p><b>420356 Praamzius</b>, provisional designation <span class="nowrap">2012 BX<sub>85</sub></span>, is a <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian object</a> from the classical <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a>, located in the outermost region of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, approximately 190–320 kilometers (120–200 miles) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive-TNO-list_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-TNO-list-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-6">[6]</a></sup> It was discovered on 23 January 2012, by astronomers <a href="/wiki/Kazimieras_%C4%8Cernis" title="Kazimieras Černis">Kazimieras Černis</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_P._Boyle" title="Richard P. Boyle">Richard Boyle</a> with the Vatican's <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Advanced_Technology_Telescope" title="Vatican Advanced Technology Telescope">VATT</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Graham_Observatory" title="Mount Graham Observatory">Mount Graham Observatory</a> in Arizona, United States. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_classical" title="Cold classical">cold classical Kuiper belt object</a> is a weak <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">dwarf planet candidate</a> and possibly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distant_object_spectral_type" title="Distant object spectral type">very red</a> in color. It was named after the chief god <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praam%C5%BEius" title="Praamžius">Praamžius</a> from Lithuanian mythology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-object_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><i>Praamzius</i> orbits the Sun at a distance of 42.1–43.0 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> once every 277 years and 11 months (101,512 days; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a> of 42.59 AU). Its orbit has an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> of 0.01 and an <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">inclination</a> of 1<a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">°</a> with respect to the <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">ecliptic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[3]</a></sup> It has an excite_mean of 0.053.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buie-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">classical Kuiper belt object</a> located in between the resonant <a href="/wiki/Plutino" title="Plutino">plutino</a> (39.4 AU) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twotino" title="Twotino">twotino</a> (47.8 AU) populations and has a low-eccentricity orbit. With an inclination significantly less than 4–7°, it belongs to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_classical" title="Cold classical">cold</a> rather than to the "stirred" <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hot_classical" title="Hot classical">hot population</a>.
</p><p>With an eccentricity of 0.003 in 2016,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-object-2016_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-2016-9">[b]</a></sup> <i>Praamzius</i> had one of the lowest eccentricities of any trans-Neptunian object, and a more circular orbit than any major planet (including <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>, the least eccentric planet at 0.007). But the object's eccentricity varies over time due to the position of the planets <i>(also see table)</i>. A 10 million year simulation of the orbit shows the eccentricity (emax) does not get greater than 0.03.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Buie-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proximity_to_orbital_resonance">Proximity to orbital resonance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=420356_Praamzius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proximity to orbital resonance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Its orbital radius is near, but not actually on the 3:5 <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">resonance</a> with <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>, with both period and radius being slightly too large to achieve an exact <a href="/wiki/Resonant_trans-Neptunian_object#3:5" title="Resonant trans-Neptunian object">3:5 ratio</a>. This could be due to any of several reasons, e.g. their apparent relationship being merely coincidental and <i>Praamzius</i> a typical non-resonant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubewano" title="Cubewano">cubewano</a> instead of resonant, or an artefact of librating cycle centred on 3:5 and currently on the longer/wider limb of its oscillation around that, or due to it exhibiting some more exotic resonance such as 7:12, 10:17 or 17:28 which are better fits for its observed track.
</p><p>However, it is difficult to yet determine which of these is true, as although <i>Praamzius</i> has a comparatively good observational record (with more than 200 confirmed professional sightings over nearly 14 years), with an otherwise decent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uncertainty_parameter_U" title="Uncertainty parameter U">orbital uncertainty</a> (<i>U</i>) of 2, or a mere 4.4 to 19.6 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcseconds" title="Arcseconds">arcseconds</a> of drift expected per decade, its great orbital distance and long period mean each of its approximately 28-decade orbits accumulate 0.034 to 0.153 degrees of potential error. Whilst still a very small amount, and unlikely to cause any difficulty with predicting target areas for future imaging or the object's position for probe rendezvous, it is enough to make nearly all of the above scenarios possible (the exception being a non-librating 7:12 resonance; however, it would only require minor, long term libration for 7:12 to become realistic).
</p><p>The apparent resonance even depends on what source data and calculation methods are employed - the best fit for the given orbital <i>periods</i> within a 25,000 year cycle time, 44:75, is still imperfect and exhibits a final error of more than 1.5 Earth years for the final conjunction (approx 0.5%, or 2 degrees of arc). If we instead use the two bodies' respective <i>semi-major axes</i>, i.e. their mean orbital <i>radii</i> as the basis for calculation, and purely Keplerian 2-body equations (ignoring interference by all other bodies), the decimal ratio returns 1.699943. This is actually close enough to 1.7 that a 10:17 resonance can be considered a credible possibility, not only with <i>U</i> = 2, but even in the looser reaches of a notional "U=-1" band, 4.4× more accurate than the usual <i>U</i> = 0 gold standard and about 85× more than band 2. However, appearing to be dead centre is no guarantee of accuracy when the uncertainty IS actually that great, so either of the other explanations could still be correct.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=420356_Praamzius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initial discovery was from images acquired on 23 January 2012 at <a href="/wiki/Vatican_Advanced_Technology_Telescope" title="Vatican Advanced Technology Telescope">VATT</a>, on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Graham" title="Mount Graham">Mount Graham, Arizona</a> using a 1.8 meter <a href="/wiki/Reflecting_telescope" title="Reflecting telescope">reflecting telescope</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPEC_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-MPEC-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">precovery</a> observations from VATT and the <a href="/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey" title="Sloan Digital Sky Survey">Sloan Digital Sky Survey</a> dating back to 31 December 2002 have been accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">Minor Planet Center</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-object_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-1">[1]</a></sup> The object has been repeatedly tracked through January 2016, mostly by VATT with some supporting observations by <a href="/wiki/Las_Campanas_Observatory" title="Las Campanas Observatory">Las Campanas Observatory</a>. 420356 <i>Praamzius</i> is one of the most recently discovered minor planets to receive a numeric designation, confirming it as a distinct body with a well determined orbit. This is due to the large number of observations since and indeed before its discovery: about one every 23 to 24 days on average from 2002 to 2016, and as many as one per 10 days in the period between discovery and assignment alone. Precovery images refined the orbit even more.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">Naming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=420356_Praamzius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Naming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">minor planet</a> was named after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Praam%C5%BEius" title="Praamžius">Praamžius</a> (an epithet of <a href="/wiki/Dievas" title="Dievas">Dievas</a>), the Lithuanian mythological god of the sky, peace, and friendship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-object_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-object-1">[1]</a></sup> The official <a href="/wiki/Meanings_of_minor_planet_names:_420001%E2%80%93421000#356" title="Meanings of minor planet names: 420001–421000">naming citation</a> was published by the <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">Minor Planet Center</a> on 22 February 2016 (<span style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Circulars" title="Minor Planet Circulars">M.P.C.</a> 98717</span>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Circulars-Archive_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-MPC-Circulars-Archive-10">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=420356_Praamzius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Johnston's archive, <i>Praamzius</i> measures 321 kilometers in diameter based on an assumed, generic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_albedo" title="Astronomical albedo">albedo</a> of 0.09.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnstonsarchive-TNO-list_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-johnstonsarchive-TNO-list-4">[4]</a></sup> This would qualify the object as a weak <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">dwarf planet candidate</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets#Brown's_assessment" title="List of possible dwarf planets">5-class taxonomic system</a> of American astronomer <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">Michael Brown</a>. However, on his website, Brown estimates only a diameter of 191 kilometers due to a much higher (assumed) albedo of 0.20. As a consequence, he no longer considers <i>Praamzius</i> to be a <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets#list" title="List of possible dwarf planets">possible dwarf planet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2018, neither the body's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distant_object_color_indices" title="Distant object color indices">color indices</a>, nor its rotational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lightcurve" title="Lightcurve">lightcurve</a> has been obtained from <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">photometric</a> observations, and its <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">rotation period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">pole</a> and shape remain unknown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-jpldata-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lcdb_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-lcdb-11">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Analysis of precovery observations in the visible spectrum between 2002 and 2007 show that it may be amongst the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distant_object_spectral_type" title="Distant object spectral type">reddest objects</a> in the Solar System, being more than 1.5 magnitudes fainter in the G (green) filtered versus the R (red) filtered images, which may offer some clues about its composition and origin <i>(also see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distant_object_color_indices" title="Distant object color indices">color indices</a>)</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="(added on 27/9/2017 to original, earlier CN tag) Be nice to have a link to any actual evidence for these claimed figures, or even better some imagery that could be added to the page. As well as any information about the blue channel, whether any attempt was made to recover a light curve, and how its albedo - and thus size - has been estimated (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This is expected from a fairly small "orbitally unexcited" cold Classical Kuiper belt object as they are generally mostly red. The cold populations distance does not vary from the Sun very much so their surfaces are older, more irradiated, and covered in <a href="/wiki/Tholin" title="Tholin">tholins</a>. There is a chance it <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">accreted</a> in basically the same orbit it has today.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
