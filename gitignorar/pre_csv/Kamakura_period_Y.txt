<p>The <b>Kamakura period</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">鎌倉時代</span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>Kamakura jidai</i>, 1185–1333)</span> is a period of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Japan" title="History of Japan">Japanese history</a> that marks the governance by the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">Kamakura shogunate</a>, officially established in 1192 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamakura,_Kanagawa" title="Kamakura, Kanagawa">Kamakura</a> by the first <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Shōgun">shōgun</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Yoritomo" title="Minamoto no Yoritomo">Minamoto no Yoritomo</a>. The period is known for the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>, the warrior caste, and for the establishment of feudalism in Japan.
</p><p>The Kamakura period ended in 1333 with the destruction of the shogunate and the short re-establishment  of imperial rule under <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Daigo" title="Emperor Go-Daigo">Emperor Go-Daigo</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Takauji" title="Ashikaga Takauji">Ashikaga Takauji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitta_Yoshisada" title="Nitta Yoshisada">Nitta Yoshisada</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kusunoki_Masashige" title="Kusunoki Masashige">Kusunoki Masashige</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Shogunate_and_H.C5.8Dj.C5.8D_Regency"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Shogunate_and_Hōjō_Regency">Shogunate and Hōjō Regency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Shogunate and Hōjō Regency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Kamakura period marks the transition to land-based economies and a concentration of advanced military technologies in the hands of a specialized fighting class. Lords required the loyal services of vassals, who were rewarded with <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a> of their own. The fief holders exercised local military rule.
</p><p>Once Minamoto Yoritomo had consolidated his power, he established a new government at his family home in Kamakura. He called his government a <i>bakufu</i> (幕府, tent government), but because he was given the ancient high military title <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Shōgun">Sei-i Taishōgun</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Toba" title="Emperor Go-Toba">Emperor Go-Toba</a>, the government is often referred to in Western literature as the shogunate. Yoritomo followed the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_clan" title="Fujiwara clan">Fujiwara</a> form of house government and had an administrative board <a href="/wiki/Mandokoro" title="Mandokoro">Mandokoro</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">政所</span>)</span>, a board of retainers <a href="/wiki/Samurai-dokoro" title="Samurai-dokoro">Samurai-dokoro</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">侍所</span>)</span>, and a board of inquiry <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Monch%C5%ABjo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Monchūjo (page does not exist)">Monchūjo</a><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">問注所</span>)</span>. After confiscating estates in central and western Japan, he appointed <a href="/wiki/Jit%C5%8D" title="Jitō">stewards</a> for the estates and <a href="/wiki/Shugo" title="Shugo">constables</a> for the provinces. As <i>shōgun</i>, Yoritomo was both the steward and the constable general. The Kamakura shogunate was not a national regime, however, and although it controlled large tracts of land, there was strong resistance to the stewards. The regime continued warfare against the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Fujiwara" title="Northern Fujiwara">Northern Fujiwara</a>, but never brought either the north or the west under complete military control. However, the 4th leader of the Northern Fujiwara <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Yasuhira" title="Fujiwara no Yasuhira">Fujiwara no Yasuhira</a> was defeated by Yoritomo in 1189, and the 100-year-long prosperity of the north disappeared. The old court resided in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>, continuing to hold the land over which it had jurisdiction, while newly organized military families were attracted to Kamakura.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1525" data-file-width="1051" decoding="async" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg/220px-Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg/330px-Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg/440px-Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nio_guardians_by_Unkei_in_Nara.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A famous Japanese wooden <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kongorikishi" title="Kongorikishi">kongorikishi</a> statue of <a href="/wiki/T%C5%8Ddai-ji" title="Tōdai-ji">Tōdai-ji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a>. It was made by <a href="/wiki/Busshi" title="Busshi">Busshi</a> <a href="/wiki/Unkei" title="Unkei">Unkei</a> in 1203.</div></div></div>
<p>Despite a strong beginning, Yoritomo failed to consolidate the leadership of his family on a lasting basis. Intrafamily contention had long existed within the Minamoto, although Yoritomo had eliminated most serious challengers to his authority. When he died suddenly in 1199, his son <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Yoriie" title="Minamoto no Yoriie">Minamoto no Yoriie</a> became <i>shōgun</i> and nominal head of the Minamoto, but Yoriie was unable to control the other eastern warrior families. By the early thirteenth century, a regency had been established for the <i>shōgun</i> by <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Tokimasa" title="Hōjō Tokimasa">Hōjō Tokimasa</a>—a member of the <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_clan" title="Hōjō clan">Hōjō clan</a>, a branch of the Taira that had allied itself with the Minamoto in 1180.  The head of Hōjō was installed as a regent for the <i>shōgun</i>; the regent was termed the <i><a href="/wiki/Shikken" title="Shikken">Shikken</a></i> during the period, although later positions were created with similar power such as the  <a href="/wiki/Tokus%C5%8D" title="Tokusō">Tokusō</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Rensho" title="Rensho">Rensho</a>.  Often the Shikken was also the Tokuso and Rensho.  Under the Hōjō, the shogun became a powerless figurehead.
</p><p>With the protector of the Emperor (<i>shōgun</i>) a figurehead himself, strains emerged between Kyoto and Kamakura, and in 1221 the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dky%C5%AB_War" title="Jōkyū War">Jōkyū War</a> broke out between the Cloistered <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Toba" title="Emperor Go-Toba">Emperor Go-Toba</a> and the second regent <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Yoshitoki" title="Hōjō Yoshitoki">Hōjō Yoshitoki</a>. The Hōjō forces easily won the war, and the imperial court was brought under the direct control of the shogunate. The <i>shōgun</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s constables gained greater civil powers, and the court was obliged to seek Kamakura's approval for all of its actions. Although deprived of political power, the court retained extensive estates.
</p><p>Several significant administrative achievements were made during the Hōjō regency. In 1225 the third regent <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_Yasutoki" title="Hōjō Yasutoki">Hōjō Yasutoki</a> established the Council of State, providing opportunities for other military lords to exercise judicial and legislative authority at Kamakura. The Hōjō regent presided over the council, which was a successful form of collective leadership. The adoption of Japan's first military code of law—the <a href="/wiki/Goseibai_Shikimoku" title="Goseibai Shikimoku">Goseibai Shikimoku</a>—in 1232 reflected the profound transition from court to militarized society. While legal practices in Kyoto were still based on 500-year-old <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> principles, the new code was a highly legalistic document that stressed the duties of stewards and constables, provided means for settling land disputes, and established rules governing <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritances</a>. It was clear and concise, stipulated punishments for violators of its conditions, and parts of it remained in effect for the next 635 years.
</p><p>As might be expected, the literature of the time reflected the unsettled nature of the period. The <i><a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8Dki" title="Hōjōki">Hōjōki</a></i> describes the turmoil of the period in terms of the Buddhist concepts of impermanence and the vanity of human projects. The <i>Heike monogatari</i> narrated the rise and fall of the Taira, replete with tales of wars and samurai deeds. A second literary mainstream was the continuation of anthologies of poetry in the <i><a href="/wiki/Shin_Kokin_Wakash%C5%AB" title="Shin Kokin Wakashū">Shin Kokin Wakashū</a></i>, of which twenty volumes were produced between 1201 and 1205.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Expansion_of_Buddhist_Teachings">The Expansion of Buddhist Teachings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Expansion of Buddhist Teachings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="1181" decoding="async" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg/220px-Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg/330px-Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg/440px-Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Head_of_a_Guardian_13thcentury_Japan.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Head of a Guardian</i>, 13th century. Hinoki wood with lacquer on cloth, pigment, rock crystal, metal. Before entering most Japanese Buddhist temples, visitors must pass large and imposing sculptures of ferocious guardian figures whose role is to protect the premises from the enemies of the religion. The aggressive stances and exaggerated facial features of these figures stand in sharp contrast to the calm demeanor of the Buddha enshrined inside. <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>During the Kamakura period six new Buddhist schools (classified by scholars as "New Buddhism" or <i>Shin Bukkyo</i>) were founded:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dnen" title="Hōnen">Hōnen</a> (1133–1212) founded the Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_Land" title="Pure Land">Pure Land</a> school or <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Ddo-sh%C5%AB" title="Jōdo-shū">Jōdo-shū</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shinran" title="Shinran">Shinran</a> (1173–1263) founded the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Ddo_Shinsh%C5%AB" title="Jōdo Shinshū">Jōdo Shinshū</a> sect.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eisai" title="Eisai">Eisai</a> (1141–1215) founded the <a href="/wiki/Rinzai_school" title="Rinzai school">Rinzai</a> school of Zen.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Dgen" title="Dōgen">Dōgen</a> (1200–1253) founded the <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dt%C5%8D" title="Sōtō">Sōtō</a> school of Zen.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a> (1222–1282) founded the <a href="/wiki/Nichiren_Buddhism" title="Nichiren Buddhism">Nichiren school</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ippen" title="Ippen">Ippen</a> (1239–1289) founded the Ji-shū branch of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_Land" title="Pure Land">Pure Land</a> Buddhism.</li></ul>
<p>During this time the pre-existing schools of <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">Tendai</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/Saich%C5%8D" title="Saichō">Saichō</a> (767–822), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shingon" title="Shingon">Shingon</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/K%C5%ABkai" title="Kūkai">Kūkai</a> (774–835), and the great temples of <a href="/wiki/Nara_period" title="Nara period">Nara</a>, collectively classified by scholars as "Old Buddhism" or <i>Kyū Bukkyo</i>, continued to thrive, adapt, and exert influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dobbins1998_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dobbins1998-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24–25</span></sup> For example, all of the above six reformers had studied at the Tendai <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mt._Hiei" title="Mt. Hiei">Mt. Hiei</a> at some point in their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>562</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Old_Buddhism.22_.28Ky.C5.AB_Bukkyo.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"Old_Buddhism"_(Kyū_Bukkyo)'>"Old Buddhism" <i>(Kyū Bukkyo)</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "Old Buddhism" (Kyū Bukkyo)'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the Kamakura period older Buddhist sects including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shingon" title="Shingon">Shingon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">Tendai</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nara_period" title="Nara period">Nara temple</a> schools such as <a href="/wiki/Kegon" title="Kegon">Kegon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hoss%C5%8D" title="Hossō">Hossō</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanron" title="Sanron">Sanron</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rissh%C5%AB_(Buddhism)" title="Risshū (Buddhism)">Ritsu</a> continued to thrive  and adapt to the trend of the times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>561–563</span></sup>  
</p><p>At the start of the Kamakura period, the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Hiei" title="Mount Hiei">Mount Hiei</a> monasteries had become politically powerful, appealing primarily to those capable of systematic study of the sect's teachings.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shingon" title="Shingon">Shingon</a> sect and its esoteric ritual continued to enjoy support largely from the noble families in <a href="/wiki/Kyoto" title="Kyoto">Kyoto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, with the increasing popularity of the new Kamakura schools, the older schools partially eclipsed as the newer "Kamakura" schools found followers among the new Kamakura government, and its <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.  
</p><p>The times that gave way to the Kamakura period were marked by political and military conflict, natural disasters, and social malaise attributed to the perceived arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Ages_of_Buddhism" title="Three Ages of Buddhism">Latter Day of the Law</a>.  The new social order of a declining aristocracy and ascending military and peasant classes resulted in new forms of religion, both indigenous<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne1998_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Payne1998-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup> and Buddhist while Indian and Chinese influence continued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>556-557</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne1998_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Payne1998-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>11,13</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Den" title="Shōen">Shōen</a> manor system which had taken root in this era resulted in the increased prosperity and literacy of peasants which in turn provided more financial support for Buddhist teachers and their studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Payne1998_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Payne1998-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22New_Buddhism.22_.28Shin_Bukkyo.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"New_Buddhism"_(Shin_Bukkyo)'>"New Buddhism" <i>(Shin Bukkyo)</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: "New Buddhism" (Shin Bukkyo)'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first originators of Kamakura Buddhism schools were <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dnen" title="Hōnen">Hōnen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shinran" title="Shinran">Shinran</a> who emphasized belief and practice over formalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>546</span></sup> 
</p><p>In the latter part of the 12th-century <a href="/wiki/D%C5%8Dgen" title="Dōgen">Dōgen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eisai" title="Eisai">Eisai</a> traveled to China and upon their return to Japan founded, respectively, the <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dt%C5%8D" title="Sōtō">Sōtō</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rinzai_school" title="Rinzai school">Rinzai</a> schools of Zen. Dōgen rejected affiliations with the secular authorities whereas Eisai actively sought them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>574</span></sup> Whereas Eisai thought that Zen teachings would revitalize the <a href="/wiki/Tendai" title="Tendai">Tendai</a> school, Dōgen aimed for an ineffable absolute, a pure Zen teaching that was not tied to beliefs and practices from Tendai or other orthodox schools<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>566</span></sup> and with little guidance for leading people how to live in the secular world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>556</span></sup>
</p><p>The final stage of Kamakura Buddhism, occurring some 50 years after Hōnen, was marked by new social and political conditions as the aristocracy declined, the military class asserted new influence, and Buddhist-infused local <i><a href="/wiki/Kami" title="Kami">kami</a></i> practice among peasants flourished. These changing conditions created a climate that encouraged religious innovation.  <a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ippen" title="Ippen">Ippen</a> attempted at this time to create down-to-earth teachings that were rooted in the daily concerns of people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>555–556</span></sup> Nichiren rejected the focus on "next-worldly" salvation such a rebirth in a Pure Land and instead aimed for "this-worldly" personal and national liberation through a simple and accessible practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>557</span></sup>  Ippen emphasized a popularized form of <a href="/wiki/Nianfo" title="Nianfo">nenbutsu</a> recitation with an emphasis on practice rather than concentrating on an individual's underlying mental state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Osumi1999_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Osumi1999-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>559</span></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_of_Kamakura_Buddhism">Legacy of Kamakura Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy of Kamakura Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As time evolved the distinctions between "Old" and "New" Buddhisms blurred as they formed "cultic centers" and various forms of founder worship.  The medieval structures of these schools evolved into hierarchical head temple-branch temple structures with associated rituals and forms of worship.  This culminated in the state-sanctioned formalized schools of the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa</a> period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dobbins1998_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dobbins1998-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>36-37</span></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongol_invasions">Mongol invasions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mongol invasions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasions_of_Japan" title="Mongol invasions of Japan">Mongol invasions of Japan</a></div>
<p>The repulsions of two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mongol" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> invasions were momentous events in Japanese history. <a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a> had predicted these invasions years earlier, in his <a class="external text" href="https://www.nichirenlibrary.org/en/wnd-1/Content/2/" rel="nofollow">Rissho Ankoku Ron</a>, a letter to the regency. Japanese relations with <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> had been terminated in the mid-ninth century after the deterioration of late <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> China and the turning inward of the Heian court. Some commercial contacts were maintained with the Southern <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> of China in later centuries, but <a href="/wiki/Wokou" title="Wokou">Japanese pirates</a> made the open seas dangerous. At a time when the shogunate had little interest in foreign affairs and ignored communications from China and the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> kingdom, news arrived in 1268 of a new Mongol regime in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>. Its leader, <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a>, demanded that the Japanese pay tribute to the new <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan dynasty</a> and threatened reprisals if they failed to do so. Unused to such threats, Kyoto raised the diplomatic counter of Japan's divine origin, rejected the Mongol demands, dismissed the Korean messengers, and started defensive preparations.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:442px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1044" data-file-width="4262" decoding="async" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg/440px-Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg/660px-Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg/880px-Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg 2x" width="440"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Takezaki_suenaga_ekotoba3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Japanese <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> boarding Mongol ships in 1281</div></div></div>
<p>After further unsuccessful entreaties, the first Mongol invasion took place in 1274. More than 600 ships carried a combined Mongol, Chinese, and Korean force of 23,000 troops armed with <a href="/wiki/Catapult" title="Catapult">catapults</a>, combustible missiles, and bows and arrows. In fighting, these soldiers grouped in close cavalry formations against samurai, who were accustomed to one-on-one combat. Local Japanese forces at <a href="/wiki/Hakata-ku,_Fukuoka" title="Hakata-ku, Fukuoka">Hakata</a>, on northern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ky%C5%ABsh%C5%AB" title="Kyūshū">Kyūshū</a>, defended against the advantageous mainland force, which, after one day of fighting was destroyed by the onslaught of a sudden <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a>. Kublai realized that nature, not military incompetence, had been the cause of his forces' failure so, in 1281, he launched a second invasion. Seven weeks of fighting took place in northwestern Kyūshū before another typhoon struck, again destroying the Mongol fleet, which was mostly composed of hastily acquired, flat-bottomed Chinese ships especially vulnerable to powerful typhoons.
</p><p>Although <a href="/wiki/Shinto" title="Shinto">Shinto</a> priests attributed the two defeats of the Mongols to a "divine wind" or <a href="/wiki/Kamikaze_(typhoon)" title="Kamikaze (typhoon)">kamikaze</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>, a sign of heaven's special protection of Japan, the invasion left a deep impression on the shogunate leaders. Long-standing fears of the Chinese threat to Japan were reinforced. The victory also convinced the warriors of the value of the shogunate form of government.
</p><p>The Mongol war had been a drain on the economy, and new taxes had to be levied to maintain defensive preparations for the future. The invasions also caused disaffection among those who expected recompense for their help in defeating the Mongols. There were no lands or other rewards to be given, however, and such disaffection, combined with overextension and the increasing defense costs, led to a decline of the Kamakura bakufu. Additionally, inheritances had divided family properties, and landowners increasingly had to turn to moneylenders for support. Roving bands of <a href="/wiki/R%C5%8Dnin" title="Rōnin">rōnin</a> further threatened the stability of the shogunate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_war">Civil war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Civil war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Hōjō reacted to the ensuing chaos by trying to place more power among the various great family clans. To further weaken the Kyoto court, the bakufu decided to allow two contending imperial lines—known as the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Court" title="Southern Court">Southern Court</a> or junior line and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Court" title="Northern Court">Northern Court</a> or senior line—to alternate on the throne. The method worked for several successions until a member of the Southern Court ascended to the throne as <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Daigo" title="Emperor Go-Daigo">Emperor Go-Daigo</a>. Go-Daigo wanted to overthrow the shogunate, and he openly defied Kamakura by naming his own son his heir. In 1331 the shogunate exiled Go-Daigo, but loyalist forces, including <a href="/wiki/Kusunoki_Masashige" title="Kusunoki Masashige">Kusunoki Masashige</a>, rebelled. They were aided by <a href="/wiki/Ashikaga_Takauji" title="Ashikaga Takauji">Ashikaga Takauji</a>, a constable who turned against Kamakura when dispatched to put down Go-Daigo's rebellion. At the same time, <a href="/wiki/Nitta_Yoshisada" title="Nitta Yoshisada">Nitta Yoshisada</a>, another eastern chieftain, rebelled against the shogunate, which quickly disintegrated, and the Hōjō were defeated.
</p><p>In the swell of victory, Go-Daigo endeavored to restore imperial authority and tenth-century Confucian practices. This period of reform, known as the <a href="/wiki/Kenmu_Restoration" title="Kenmu Restoration">Kenmu Restoration</a>, aimed at strengthening the position of the Emperor and reasserting the primacy of the court nobles over the warriors. The reality, however, was that the forces who had arisen against Kamakura had been set on defeating the Hōjō, not on supporting the Emperor. Ashikaga Takauji finally sided with the Northern Court in a civil war against the Southern Court represented by Go-Daigo. The long War Between the Courts lasted from 1336 to 1392. Early in the conflict, Go-Daigo was driven from Kyoto, and the Northern Court contender was installed by Ashikaga, who established a new line of shoguns.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kamakura_period&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>1185: the rival <a href="/wiki/Taira_clan" title="Taira clan">Taira clan</a> is defeated at sea at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dan-no-ura" title="Battle of Dan-no-ura">Battle of Dan-no-ura</a> by Yoritomo's brother <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Yoshitsune" title="Minamoto no Yoshitsune">Minamoto no Yoshitsune</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>1192: The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Go-Toba" title="Emperor Go-Toba">Emperor Go-Toba</a> appoints Yoritomo as <i><a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dgun" title="Shōgun">shōgun</a></i> (military leader) with a residence in <a href="/wiki/Kamakura" title="Kamakura">Kamakura</a>, establishing the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakufu" title="Bakufu">bakufu</a></i> system of government</li>
<li>1199: <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Yoritomo" title="Minamoto no Yoritomo">Minamoto no Yoritomo</a> dies</li>
<li>1207: <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dnen" title="Hōnen">Hōnen</a> and his followers are exiled from Kyoto or executed.  This inadvertently spread the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pure_Land" title="Pure Land">Pure Land</a> doctrine to a wider audience.</li>
<li>1221: The Kamakura army defeats the imperial army in the <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dky%C5%AB_War" title="Jōkyū War">Jōkyū Disturbance</a>, thereby asserting the supremacy of the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">Kamakura shogunate</a> (<a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dj%C5%8D_clan" title="Hōjō clan">Hōjō regents</a>) over the emperor</li>
<li>1227: The <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dt%C5%8D" title="Sōtō">Sōtō</a> sect of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" title="Zen Buddhism">Zen Buddhism</a> is introduced to Japan by the monk <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C5%8Dgen_Zenji" title="Dōgen Zenji">Dōgen Zenji</a></li>
<li>1232: The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dei_Shikimoku" title="Jōei Shikimoku">Jōei Shikimoku</a></i> code of law is promulgated to enhance control by the Hōjō regents</li>
<li>1271: <a href="/wiki/Nichiren" title="Nichiren">Nichiren</a> is banished to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sado_Island" title="Sado Island">Sado Island</a></li>
<li>1274: The <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a> try to <a href="/wiki/Japan#Feudal_era" title="Japan">invade Japan</a> but are repelled by a typhoon.</li>
<li>1293: On May 27, a <a href="/wiki/1293_Kamakura_earthquake" title="1293 Kamakura earthquake">major earthquake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">tsunami</a> hit <a href="/wiki/Sagami_Bay" title="Sagami Bay">Sagami Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kamakura" title="Kamakura">Kamakura</a>, killing 23,034 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  It followed a 1241 and 1257 earthquake/tsunami in the same general area, which both were magnitude 7.0.</li>
<li>1333: <a href="/wiki/Nitta_Yoshisada" title="Nitta Yoshisada">Nitta Yoshisada</a> conquers and destroys Kamakura during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Kamakura_(1333)" title="Siege of Kamakura (1333)">Siege of Kamakura</a> ending the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_shogunate" title="Kamakura shogunate">Kamakura shogunate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li></ul>
