<p>"<b>The Doomed</b>" is a song by American <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/A_Perfect_Circle" title="A Perfect Circle">A Perfect Circle</a>. The song was first released as a single on October 16, 2017. It is the band's first single since 2013's "<a href="/wiki/By_and_Down" title="By and Down">By and Down</a>" for their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_hits" title="Greatest hits">greatest hits</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Sixty" title="Three Sixty">Three Sixty</a></i>, and was the lead single for their fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Eat_the_Elephant" title="Eat the Elephant">Eat the Elephant</a></i>. It peaked at number 16 on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> US <a href="/wiki/Mainstream_Rock_(chart)" title="Mainstream Rock (chart)">Mainstream Rock Songs</a> chart in November 2017.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song was first teased on October 13, 2017, through a short, fifteen second soundless video posted to the band's social media, saying nothing other than "A Perfect Circle - The Doomed".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The ambiguity of the tease led to much speculation among music journalists, on whether it was about a song title, album title, or something else altogether.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glide_Magazine1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glide_Magazine1-8">[8]</a></sup> On Monday, October 16, the band revealed to a song, with the band releasing it as a single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The song is their first in four years, since "<a href="/wiki/By_and_Down" title="By and Down">By and Down</a>" in 2013, from their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greatest_hits" title="Greatest hits">greatest hits</a> album <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Sixty" title="Three Sixty">Three Sixty</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The song was released just prior to the band starting their second leg of their North American tour, which is scheduled to run from October to December 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It is planned to be on the band's upcoming fourth studio album, scheduled for release in 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork1-12">[12]</a></sup> The song was debuted live on October 21, 2017, at the <a href="/wiki/Aftershock_Festival" title="Aftershock Festival">Aftershock Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 16, 2017, a <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a>, directed by Jeremy Danger and Travis Shinn, was released for the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The video features the five band members, in black and white, looking pensive and bleak, with little movement other than slowly looking towards, or away from, the camera, with alternative white and black backdrops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_and_recording">Writing and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song's origins date back to a song idea by band guitarist <a href="/wiki/Billy_Howerdel" title="Billy Howerdel">Billy Howerdel</a>, who was working on writing the soundtrack for the indie film <i>D-Love</i> during the band's downtime in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spokesman1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spokesman1-16">[16]</a></sup> Howerdel had written a small orchestral part for the film that he was unable to fit into the finished project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spokesman1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spokesman1-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PostGazette1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PostGazette1-3">[3]</a></sup> Feeling there was still something there, Howerdel sent the part to band vocalist <a href="/wiki/Maynard_James_Keenan" title="Maynard James Keenan">Maynard James Keenan</a> during the band's 2017 writing sessions for a fourth studio album, not necessarily as a song idea, but rather, just as a starting point for writing material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PostGazette1_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-PostGazette1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spokesman1_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Spokesman1-16">[16]</a></sup> Keenan approved of the part, requesting that Howerdel write a song around the idea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PostGazette1_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-PostGazette1-3">[3]</a></sup> Despite generally slow writing sessions tracing back to 2008, the two moved quickly on the track; Howerdel wrote the song's music in a day and a half, and upon hearing his work, Keenan put down demo vocals just a day and a half later, with the song being completed shortly after.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PostGazette1_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-PostGazette1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spokesman1_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Spokesman1-16">[16]</a></sup> The song's lyrics were written entirely by Keenan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PostGazette1_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-PostGazette1-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spokesman1_16-4"><a href="#cite_note-Spokesman1-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes_and_composition">Themes and composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Themes and composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song's lyrics deals with topics such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wealth_inequality" title="Wealth inequality">wealth inequality</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Class_inequality" title="Class inequality">class inequality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin1_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spin1-17">[17]</a></sup> Specifically, journalists interpreted the lyrics to be attacking the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservative" title="Conservative">conservative</a> ideal that <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> isn't responsible for the well being of the less fortunate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stereogum1_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stereogum1-18">[18]</a></sup> The song's lyrics, written by band <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frontman" title="Frontman">frontman</a> <a href="/wiki/Maynard_James_Keenan" title="Maynard James Keenan">Maynard James Keenan</a>, conveyed the message through lines such as "What of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pious" title="Pious">pious</a>, the pure of heart, the peaceful? What of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meek" title="Meek">meek</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mourning" title="Mourning">mourning</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merciful" title="Merciful">merciful</a>? All doomed. All doomed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glide_Magazine1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Glide_Magazine1-8">[8]</a></sup> Upon the release of the single, Keenan said of the song:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>14 years have passed since we released <i>Emotive</i>. A new release is long overdue. In light of this current difficult and polarized social, spiritual and political climate, we artist types need to open our big mouths and share the light a little louder."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spin1_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spin1-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>It was also interpreted to be a statement against modern <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, a topic Keenan frequently has addressed in his work,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetalSucks1_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-MetalSucks1-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glide_Magazine1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Glide_Magazine1-8">[8]</a></sup> with  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AZ_Central" title="AZ Central">AZ Central</a> comparing it to a "<a href="/wiki/Nihilism" title="Nihilism">nihilistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount" title="Sermon on the Mount">Sermon on the Mount</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZC1_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AZC1-20">[20]</a></sup> The song has been described as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ominous" title="Ominous">ominous</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radio.com1_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Radio.com1-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggressive" title="Aggressive">aggressive</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radio.com1_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Radio.com1-21">[21]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apocalypse" title="Apocalypse">apocalyptic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStone1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone1-1">[1]</a></sup> and intense<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>. While not the lyrics writer, Howerdel explained that, to him, the song was about the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a> of power corrupting the people in control of the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PostGazette1_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-PostGazette1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Musically, the song has been described as a "surging, sweeping <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guitar_riff" title="Guitar riff">riff</a>-<a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rocker</a> that builds to a raging conclusion".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stereogum1_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stereogum1-18">[18]</a></sup> The song alternates between soft and loud dynamics, the song starts with a prominent drum beat and guitarist <a href="/wiki/Billy_Howerdel" title="Billy Howerdel">Billy Howerdel</a>'s swirling guitar notes, before building into a <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> sound with distorted guitars and Keenan's angry singing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RollingStone1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-RollingStone1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZC1_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-AZC1-20">[20]</a></sup> The song then dials back to segments consisting only of Keenan's soft-spoken, melodic vocals over <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>/<a href="/wiki/Xylophone" title="Xylophone">xylophone</a> parts, leading to a sound described as "a band’s take on what an orchestra could accomplish if stripped to its bare bones, coming across as grand yet minimalistic."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loudwire1_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Loudwire1-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Radio.com1_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-Radio.com1-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Doomed&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song's initial release was generally well received by critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">Kerrang!</a></i> named it their top song of the week upon release, praising it for taking  the style of "<a href="/wiki/Counting_Bodies_Like_Sheep_to_the_Rhythm_of_the_War_Drums" title="Counting Bodies Like Sheep to the Rhythm of the War Drums">Counting Bodies Like Sheep to the Rhythm of the War Drums</a>" and giving it scathing lyrics relevant to the polarized political landscape of 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Loudwire" title="Loudwire">Loudwire</a> described the song as "incredible" and "glorious", praising the track's <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive</a> and atmospheric qualities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Loudwire1_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Loudwire1-23">[23]</a></sup> <i>Glide</i> magazine praised the song for being "stunning addition to A Perfect Circle catalogue" and "a fantastic example of how music and art should react to the horrors of contemporary life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glide_Magazine1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Glide_Magazine1-8">[8]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_Sucks" title="Metal Sucks">Metal Sucks</a> praised the song for being "unsurprisingly, it’s good...there are parts that sound almost like <a href="/wiki/Viking_metal" title="Viking metal">viking metal</a>, which is pretty rad. I do kinda miss <a href="/wiki/Josh_Freese" title="Josh Freese">Josh Freese</a>’s drums, which were less <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aggressive" title="Aggressive">aggro</a> and more eloquent than Jeff Friedl’s, but it’s not a deal breaker or whatever. On the whole, I can’t really imagine any APC fan feeling let down by this."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MetalSucks1_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-MetalSucks1-19">[19]</a></sup> The song was named the fifth best <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> song of 2017 by <a href="/wiki/Loudwire" title="Loudwire">Loudwire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LoudwireHR17_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-LoudwireHR17-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
