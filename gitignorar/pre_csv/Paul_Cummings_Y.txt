<p><b>Paul Richard Cummings</b> (5 September 1953 â 17 September 2001) was a world-class middle and long distance runner who ran competitively from the 1,500 meters to the marathon, breaking several American records and one world record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> His ability to have a middle distance runner's kick and also have the stamina to compete in distances up to the marathon place him as one of the most versatile American track and road racers of his era.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Paul Cummings was born in <a href="/wiki/Tempe,_Arizona" title="Tempe, Arizona">Tempe, Arizona</a> but his family moved to <a href="/wiki/Santa_Maria,_California" title="Santa Maria, California">Santa Maria, California</a> when he was 11 years old. Cummings was the third of thirteen children and had ten sisters. Cummings did not always see running as his strength. He played basketball in junior high, and wanted to continue in high school, but failed to make the team. However, when he came in first in his Physical Education class in the mile run, a new opportunity opened up for him; he was asked to join the track team at <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Righetti_High_School" title="Ernest Righetti High School">Ernest Righetti High School</a>, and by his senior year was running the mile in 4:10, the second best time in California,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> seventh best in the country. Cummings was recruited by several top universities and chose to attend <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Brigham_Young_University_.281971-1975.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brigham_Young_University_(1971-1975)">Brigham Young University (1971-1975)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Brigham Young University (1971-1975)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r923042769/mw-parser-output/.tmulti">.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbinner{display:flex;flex-direction:column}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .trow{display:flex;flex-direction:row;clear:left;flex-wrap:wrap;width:100%;box-sizing:border-box}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{margin:1px;float:left}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .theader{clear:both;font-weight:bold;text-align:center;align-self:center;background-color:transparent;width:100%}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbcaption{text-align:left;background-color:transparent}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbcaption-center{text-align:center;background-color:transparent}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .text-align-left{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .text-align-right{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .text-align-center{text-align:center}@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbinner{width:100%!important;box-sizing:border-box;max-width:none!important;align-items:center}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .trow{justify-content:center}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:100%!important;box-sizing:border-box;text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbcaption{text-align:center}}</style>
<p>Cummings was named All-American five years in a row while running on the <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a> Track and Cross Country teams. However, after his Freshman year, he was diagnosed with an extra bone in his foot and in order to run again, would have to undergo surgery to remove the bone. Cummings recovered, and by his junior year became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA" title="NCAA">NCAA</a> champion in the mile run. Cummings was the first runner from the <a href="/wiki/Intermountain_West" title="Intermountain West">Intermountain West</a> to break the <a href="/wiki/Four-minute_mile" title="Four-minute mile">Four-minute mile</a> barrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> His personal best for the mile, 3:56.4, was run at his birthplace, Tempe, Arizona on March 16, 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> A video of the race can be seen here.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Cummings was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latter-day_Saint" title="Latter-day Saint">Latter-day Saint</a>. He wanted to serve a two-year <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mormon_missionary" title="Mormon missionary">LDS mission</a>, but since LDS Missions are served voluntarily and missionaries serve without compensation, he was unable to raise the required funds. After meeting with LDS Church President Spencer W. Kimball, Paul was encouraged to stay in school on an athletic scholarship, earn his degree, and continue to run and compete, sharing his beliefs with those he met through running. Paul did this well and shared his beliefs with many athletes and friends.
</p><p>BYU Track Coach Clarence Robison said Cummings was "a great performer and has great courage. He's a tenacious, fierce competitor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_distance_track_racing">Middle distance track racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Middle distance track racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cummings was invited to several of the most prestigious track and field meets during the 1970s. He won events at the <a href="/wiki/Millrose_Games" title="Millrose Games">Millrose Games</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunkist_Invitational" title="Sunkist Invitational">Sunkist Invitational</a>, the LA Times Indoor Games, the Jack in the Box Indoor Games, the <a href="/wiki/Modesto_Relays" title="Modesto Relays">Modesto Relays</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and other events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>He competed against <a href="/wiki/Steve_Prefontaine" title="Steve Prefontaine">Steve Prefontaine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Shorter" title="Frank Shorter">Frank Shorter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Waldrop" title="Tony Waldrop">Tony Waldrop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marty_Liquori" title="Marty Liquori">Marty Liquori</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filbert_Bayi" title="Filbert Bayi">Filbert Bayi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Waigwa" title="Wilson Waigwa">Wilson Waigwa</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Walker_(athlete)" title="John Walker (athlete)">John Walker</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Scott_(athlete)" title="Steve Scott (athlete)">Steve Scott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eamonn_Coghlan" title="Eamonn Coghlan">Eamonn Coghlan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dick_Buerkle" title="Dick Buerkle">Dick Buerkle</a>, and several other top tier runners of the era, besting everyone on this list at least once. He ran in the <a href="/wiki/Millrose_Games" title="Millrose Games">Millrose Games</a> <a href="/wiki/Wanamaker_Mile" title="Wanamaker Mile">Wanamaker Mile</a> in 1974, 1975,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> 1976, and 1977, winning the event in 1976 in a Millrose Games record time of 3:57.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 25, 1976, Cummings won the <a href="/wiki/Penn_Relays" title="Penn Relays">Penn Relays</a> 1500 Meter event at <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Field" title="Franklin Field">Franklin Field</a> in Philadelphia in 3:38.9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1976 he won the 3,000 meter event at Los Angeles's Sunkist Invitational Track Meet in 8:29.6, just five seconds off Steve Prefontaine's American Record set at the same meet the year before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1977 he won the Sunkist Invitational indoor mile in 3:57.2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Also in 1977, Cummings, representing the US, won the mile at Canada's Tri-Country indoor track and field meet, competing against Canada and the Soviet Union.
</p><p>In January 1978 at the Muhammad Ali Track and Field Invitational at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Beach_Arena" title="Long Beach Arena">Long Beach Arena</a> in California, Cummings broke the indoor American record for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1500_meters" title="1500 meters">1500 meters</a> by .4 with a time of 3:39.8.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>A year later, in January 1979 at the same Muhammad Ali Track and Field Invitational at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_Beach_Arena" title="Long Beach Arena">Long Beach Arena</a> in California, both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Walker_(athlete)" title="John Walker (athlete)">John Walker</a> and Cummings finished under the indoor world record for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1500_meters" title="1500 meters">1500 meters</a> by .4 and .2 seconds respectively. Walker set a world record with a time of 3:37.4. Cummings set an American Record with a time of 3:37.6.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings ran at a time when there was very little financial support for athletes after college. When attending track meets, several athletes would share the same hotel room, sleeping on floors, and carpool together to save costs. He worked at a <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mill</a> rebuilding open hearth furnaces to support his family and ran for the Tobias Striders, Beverly Hills Striders and Pacific Coast track clubs to maintain his amateur status.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="1984_US_Olympic_Team">1984 US Olympic Team</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1984 US Olympic Team">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hoping to earn a spot on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1980_US_Olympic_Team" title="1980 US Olympic Team">1980 US Olympic Team</a>, working out over five hours per day, and working full-time six days a week at a steel mill, Cummings was one of many athletes disappointed by the U.S.'s boycott of the <a href="/wiki/1980_Summer_Olympics" title="1980 Summer Olympics">1980 Moscow Summer Games</a>. Due to work demands and disappointment at the 1980 boycott, Cummings came close to retiring altogether from running. When he was laid off from the mill in late 1980, he began running full-time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1984, Cummings won the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Trials_(track_and_field)" title="United States Olympic Trials (track and field)">US Olympic Trials</a> in the <a href="/wiki/10,000_metres" title="10,000 metres">10,000 Meter run</a> in Los Angeles, CA and finally became a member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Olympic_Team" title="US Olympic Team">US Olympic Team</a>. A video of the race can be seen here. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> 
</p><p>He was able to compete in the 10,000 meter run at the <a href="/wiki/1984_Summer_Olympics" title="1984 Summer Olympics">Olympics held in Los Angeles</a>, but did not make the finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sports-reference.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sports-reference.com-20">[20]</a></sup> Cummings suffered from <a href="/wiki/Allergy" title="Allergy">allergies</a> that hindered his performance at the games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Road_racing.2C_American_and_World_records"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_racing,_American_and_World_records">Road racing, American and World records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Road racing, American and World records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1981, Cummings shifted his focus from track to <a href="/wiki/Road_running" title="Road running">road racing</a>. One of his first marathons was the <a href="/wiki/St._George_Marathon" title="St. George Marathon">St. George Marathon</a>, which he won in 2:15.16, a course record that stood until 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> He won the St. George Marathon again in 1982.
</p><p>Taking advantage of new rules that enabled runners to compete professionally, Cummings represented <a href="/wiki/New_Balance" title="New Balance">New Balance</a> shoes beginning in 1982.
</p><p>On June 5, 1982, Cummings ran his PR in the 5,000 meters at the Prefontaine Classic track meet in Eugene, Oregon, running against some of the best American middle distance runners of the time, including <a href="/wiki/Matt_Centrowitz" title="Matt Centrowitz">Matt Centrowitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Salazar" title="Alberto Salazar">Alberto Salazar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doug_Padilla" title="Doug Padilla">Doug Padilla</a>. He ran towards the front for most of the race and ran 13:19.62, a Personal Record by 9 seconds, and just short of the American Record set by Matt Centrowitz in the same race. The following link shows the entire race on video in two parts. Part 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Part 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Paul Cummings was very busy and successful running road races in 1983. He ran in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon" title="Boston Marathon">Boston Marathon</a> with the lead pack for the first 10 miles of the race.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The following link shows a picture of Paul running with <a href="/wiki/Benji_Durden" title="Benji Durden">Benji Durden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greg_Meyer" title="Greg Meyer">Greg Meyer</a> (eventual race winner and only American Male to win until 2014).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He set an American Record in the 15K by almost 28 seconds at the Cascade Run Off in Portland, Oregon on June 26, 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Cummings placed in the top 10 at Spokane Washington's <a href="/wiki/Lilac_Bloomsday_Run" title="Lilac Bloomsday Run">Bloomsday 12K Run</a> (8th in 1983 and 2nd in 1984).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Paul also ran in the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Marathon" title="New York City Marathon">New York City Marathon</a> in 1983.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 25, 1983, Cummings set the World Record in the <a href="/wiki/Half_marathon" title="Half marathon">Half marathon</a> at the Dayton River Corridor Classic in <a href="/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio" title="Dayton, Ohio">Dayton, Ohio</a>, with a time of 1:01:32.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings represented <a href="/wiki/Converse_(shoe_company)" title="Converse (shoe company)">Converse</a> shoes beginning in 1984 and was included in Runner's World Magazine's list of the top 20 highest paid runners, ranked 15th worldwide, in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Paul's best marathon time came in winning the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Houston_Marathon" title="Houston Marathon">Houston Marathon</a> in 2:11:31.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> His best finish at the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon" title="Boston Marathon">Boston Marathon</a> was 8th place overall in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> In the 1983 Stockholm Marathon, Cummings built up a 1-minute 12 second lead but suffered from cramps during the last 3 miles and narrowly lost to Hugh Jones of Great Britain, coming in 3rd place overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings is the only runner to finish second in San Francisco's <a href="/wiki/Bay_to_Breakers" title="Bay to Breakers">Bay to Breakers</a> 12K road race (Guinness World Record largest road race) three years in a row (1984, 1985, and 1986).
</p><p>On March 15, 1987, Cummings broke the American Record in the 20K, at New Bedford, Massachusetts, in a time of 59.13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings continued running into his 40s. He won the US Master's National Championship for the Marathon in 1993 at the <a href="/wiki/Twin_Cities_Marathon" title="Twin Cities Marathon">Twin Cities Marathon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> He was also awarded the USA Track and Field (USATF) Master's Age Division (Age 40+) Runner of the Year in 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings won the 1994 Walt Disney World Marathon Master's division in 2:27:50.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions">Contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late 1980s, Cummings, along with fellow Utah world class runners <a href="/wiki/Ed_Eyestone" title="Ed Eyestone">Ed Eyestone</a> and Paul Pilkington started a summer high school high altitude running camp in <a href="/wiki/Park_City,_Utah" title="Park City, Utah">Park City, Utah</a> which ran for 15 years and helped hundreds of high school runners from across the country reach their potential.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1990, Cummings created a running video entitled "Running Theory: From Mile to Marathon" that expounded on "Consistency Training" which is an adaptation to <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Lydiard" title="Arthur Lydiard">Arthur Lydiard</a>'s four step "Phase Training". The four components of consistency training include: upper body strength training, endurance training, 70% effort training, and anaerobic capacity training.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings explored the outer reaches of human endurance in his road race and marathon training, approaching the legendary endurance strategies of <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Lindgren" title="Gerry Lindgren">Gerry Lindgren</a>. He ran up to 240 miles in one week, training three times a day with runs of 13 miles in the morning, 14 in the afternoon, and another 13 in the evening for six days. He rested on Sundays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cummings was inducted into the Brigham Young University Athletics Hall of Fame in 1986.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>  He was posthumously inducted into the Utah Sports Hall of Fame in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Cummings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi,_Utah.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="650" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi%2C_Utah.jpg/220px-Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi%2C_Utah.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi%2C_Utah.jpg/330px-Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi%2C_Utah.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi%2C_Utah.jpg/440px-Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi%2C_Utah.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paul_Cummings_Gravesite_Lehi,_Utah.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paul Cummings Gravesite, Lehi City Cemetery, Utah</div></div></div>
<p>Cummings enjoyed <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>, especially with his best friend Jay Woods. While fishing together on September 17, 2001, their canoe capsized under high winds at <a href="/wiki/Strawberry_Reservoir" title="Strawberry Reservoir">Strawberry Reservoir</a>, Utah. He drowned in the accident. Paul was married for 27 years to Debra Gaye Anhder. They were the parents of four children; Heidi, Scott, Holly and Kelly. He is interred at the Lehi City Cemetery, <a href="/wiki/Lehi,_Utah" title="Lehi, Utah">Lehi, Utah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
