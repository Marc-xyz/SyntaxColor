<p><b>Laumeier Sculpture Park</b> is a 105-acre open-air museum and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sculpture_park" title="Sculpture park">sculpture park</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Hills,_Missouri" title="Sunset Hills, Missouri">Sunset Hills, Missouri</a>, near <a href="/wiki/St._Louis" title="St. Louis">St. Louis</a> and is maintained in partnership with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=St._Louis_County_Parks_and_Recreation_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="St. Louis County Parks and Recreation Department (page does not exist)">St. Louis County Parks and Recreation Department</a>. It houses over 60 outdoor <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculptures</a> and features a 1.4-mile (2.3 km) walking trail,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsbank_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newsbank-2">[2]</a></sup> and educational programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_Today_2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-STL_Today_2-3">[3]</a></sup> There is also an indoor gallery, an 1816 <a href="/wiki/Tudor_Revival_architecture" title="Tudor Revival architecture">Tudor</a> stone mansion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toledo_Blade_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toledo_Blade-4">[4]</a></sup> which was the former residence of Henry and Matilda Laumeier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-5">[5]</a></sup> Laumeier is accredited by the <a href="/wiki/American_Alliance_of_Museums" title="American Alliance of Museums">American Alliance of Museums</a>. The park sees about 300,000 visitors each year and operates on a $1.5 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_Today_2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-STL_Today_2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laumeier_Sculpture_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The park was founded in 1968 by a property grant in the will of Matilda C. Laumeier, and was originally intended as a memorial to her deceased husband, Henry H. Laumeier. The will gave their land and country house to the county, and specified that would be used for passive purposes (e.g., no sport fields).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toledo_Blade_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Toledo_Blade-4">[4]</a></sup> The park was 76 acres (0.31 km<sup>2</sup>) at its opening in 1975, but did not attract many visitors until a year later, when St. Louis sculptor <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Trova" title="Ernest Trova">Ernest Trova</a> donated about 40 pieces of his work to the park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_Today_3_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-STL_Today_3-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> It soon became a popular tourist attraction, and received an additional 20 acres (0.081 km<sup>2</sup>) from the Friends of Laumeier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-5">[5]</a></sup> The additional land was mostly woods and is for site-specific sculptures, including an abandoned Depression-era concrete pool from the Orchard Valley estate that once occupied the land which was transformed into a large sculpture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toledo_Blade_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Toledo_Blade-4">[4]</a></sup>  <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The founding executive director of the Sculpture Park was Dr. Beej Nierengarten-Smith, whose tenure lasted 22 years from October 1979 to May 2001. During  these early years the park won 6 operating grants and 2 conservation grants from the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Museum_and_Library_Services" title="Institute of Museum and Library Services">Institute of Museum and Library Services</a>, including a grant to create an informative video on the park and an architectural assessment of its buildings. The park also received 22 grants from the <a href="/wiki/National_Endowment_for_the_Arts" title="National Endowment for the Arts">National Endowment for the Arts</a> for exhibitions and sculpture commissions. An endowment fund was created, valued at $2 million by 2000, and the park's operating fund increased from an initial $30,000 from St Louis County to over one million from combined resources. A variety of national and international sculptors were featured, including Terry Allen, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Neri" title="Manuel Neri">Manuel Neri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Goldsworthy" title="Andy Goldsworthy">Andy Goldsworthy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judith_Shea" title="Judith Shea">Judith Shea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joyce_J._Scott" title="Joyce J. Scott">Joyce J. Scott</a>.  Fifty percent of featured artists were women. Nierengarten-Smith also created the Contemporary Arts and Crafts Fair for education revenue, the Winter Solstice Program Fire and Ice, the Sand Castle Festival, Safari Camp in the woods for children and parents, and numerous other festivals for diverse audiences, including children. A volunteer program for the park and its special events was also created.  During the first 22 years, attendance rose from several hundred to 500,000 people, and the park twice received museum accreditation from the American Association of Museums. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
When Nierengarten-Smith retired in 2001, Glen Gentele became executive director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_Business_Journal_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-STL_Business_Journal-11">[11]</a></sup>  In September 2009 when Gentele accepted a position at another museum, Marilu Knode came from Arizona State University, where she was head of research for Future Arts Research, to become the current executive director. Knode was the former head of research at Future Arts Research at <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_University" title="Arizona State University">Arizona State University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_Today_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-STL_Today-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future">Future</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laumeier_Sculpture_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Future">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The park has raised significant funds for improvements and a new fine arts center. <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Scarpa" title="Lawrence Scarpa">Lawrence Scarpa</a> of the firm <a href="/wiki/Brooks_%2B_Scarpa" title="Brooks + Scarpa">Brooks + Scarpa</a> Architects, which won the 2010 <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Architects" title="American Institute of Architects">AIA</a> Architecture Firm Award, initially designed the Adam Aronson Fine Arts Center (named to honor an institutional founder member, long-term board member and major donor). Plans for this building were tabled and a less ambitious facility was constructed in 2015—designed by a regional architecture firm.
</p>
<h2><span id=".22The_Way.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='"The_Way"'>"The Way"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Laumeier_Sculpture_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "The Way"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman,_1980.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="273" data-file-width="365" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e5/The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman%2C_1980.jpg/220px-The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman%2C_1980.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e5/The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman%2C_1980.jpg/330px-The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman%2C_1980.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e5/The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman%2C_1980.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_Way_by_Alexander_Liberman,_1980.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Alexander_Liberman" title="Alexander Liberman">Alexander Liberman</a>'s "The Way" (1980) is often cited as the park's signature sculpture.</div></div></div>
<p>One of the park's best-known works, "The Way", was completed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Liberman" title="Alexander Liberman">Alexander Liberman</a> in 1980. Constructed from eighteen salvaged steel oil tanks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSP_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-LSP-13">[13]</a></sup> the sculpture is 65 feet (20 m) tall, 102 feet (31 m) wide, and 100 feet (30 m) deep,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STLPD927_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-STLPD927-14">[14]</a></sup> and weighs 55 short tons (50 t).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-STL-15">[15]</a></sup> It is painted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadmium_red" title="Cadmium red">cadmium red</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSP_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-LSP-13">[13]</a></sup> The park website describes the sculpture as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist" title="Modernist">modernist</a> work, "meant to represent the awe-inspiring impact of classical Greek temples and mammoth Gothic-style cathedrals" and modeled on <a href="/wiki/Post_and_lintel" title="Post and lintel">post and lintel</a> architecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSP_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-LSP-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>"The Way" was composed on-site in a clearing later named "Way Field".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LSP_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-LSP-13">[13]</a></sup> The work was funded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Endowment_for_the_Arts" title="National Endowment for the Arts">National Endowment for the Arts</a> and a donation by Alvin J. Siteman. In September 2011, it was restored by two workers using a hydraulic lift and 50 US gallons (190 litres) of paint.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STLPD927_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-STLPD927-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/St._Louis_Post-Dispatch" title="St. Louis Post-Dispatch">St. Louis Post-Dispatch</a></i> described it as "iconic",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-STL_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-STL-15">[15]</a></sup> while another St. Louis newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Riverfront_Times" title="Riverfront Times">Riverfront Times</a></i>, described the sculpture as having become "a symbol of both the park and the city".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
