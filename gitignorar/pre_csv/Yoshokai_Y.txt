<p>The Aikido <b>Yoshokai</b> Association of North America - AYANA (合氣道耀尚會)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/Aikido" title="Aikido">aikido</a> organization founded in 1991 by former <a href="/wiki/Yoshinkan" title="Yoshinkan">Yoshinkan</a>-affiliated master <a href="/wiki/Takashi_Kushida" title="Takashi Kushida">Takashi Kushida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hombu" title="Hombu">hombu</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dojo" title="Dojo">dojo</a> is the Genyokan (玄耀館) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dojo" title="Dojo">Dojo</a>  in <a href="/wiki/Ann_Arbor,_Michigan" title="Ann Arbor, Michigan">Ann Arbor, Michigan</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yoshokai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yoshokai Aikido is a "hard" style of aikido by common parlance, and very similar to <a href="/wiki/Yoshinkan" title="Yoshinkan">Yoshinkan</a>.
Full tenkan movements are present, but partial pivots and more conservative blending motions are perhaps more usual.  <a href="/wiki/Atemi" title="Atemi">Atemi</a> is common.  Yoshokai <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ukemi" title="Ukemi">ukemi</a> is also distinct (similar to Yoshinkan ukemi), with more slapping of the spare hand/foot, and a more forward-feeling 'high fall' (called 'jumping breakfall' or 'hiyaku ukemi') than the other sideways motions employed in different aikido styles. A special emphasis is placed on aikido as a form of conflict resolution and a cooperative study of making harmony between two people.  Like Yoshinkan Aikido, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daito-ryu_aiki-jujutsu" title="Daito-ryu aiki-jujutsu">Daito-ryu aiki-jujutsu</a> names for basic techniques are retained (e.g. "ikkajō" rather than "ikkyō")
</p><p>Yoshokai Aikido is organized in a rather centralized fashion relative to other styles, with technique lists and explanations distributed annually.  This goes hand in hand with its emphasis on meticulous attention to detail.
</p><p>Yoshokai Aikido closely links techniques, basic movements, and weapons techniques.  Weapons techniques (buki-waza) are prominent in the curriculum, mostly with <a href="/wiki/Bokken" title="Bokken">bokken</a>, <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8D" title="Jō">jō</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tant%C5%8D" title="Tantō">tantō</a>.
</p>
