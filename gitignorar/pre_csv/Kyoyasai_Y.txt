<p><b>Kyōyasai</b> is the term for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heirloom_vegetables" title="Heirloom vegetables">heirloom vegetables</a> originating in Japan's <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Prefecture" title="Kyoto Prefecture">Kyoto Prefecture</a>. According to the research of the Laboratory of Health and Environment of Kyoto, Kyoyasai have more minerals, fibers and vitamins than many other vegetables. According to research, Kyoyasai have more nutrients that repair DNA than other vegetables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup> Kyoyasai are relatively expensive. Japanese consumers consider many Kyoyasai strange, because of their appearance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kyoyasai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kyo-yasai are eaten mostly in the home or in upscale restaurants. They play an important role in Kyo-ryori, the traditional cuisine of Kyoto.
</p><p>Kyo-ryori does not employ seasoning. Traditionally, Kyo-yasai are prepared without seasoning because of their rich flavor.
</p><p>Kyo-yasai are also used for Italian, French and other cuisines in Kyoto.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kyoyasai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> began in Kyoto some twelve thousand years ago. It was the ancient capital of Japan and the home to the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocracy</a>. They supported a market for high-grade food. Kyoto is surrounded by mountains, making it difficult to transport seafood to the area. Instead, people cultivated the most delicious vegetables possible. The climate and soil quality contributed to making delicious vegetables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Kamakura_period" title="Kamakura period">Kamakura period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zen" title="Zen">Zen Buddhism</a> became popular. At that time Kyoto hosted many temples. Many practiced <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_cuisine" title="Buddhist cuisine"><i>syōjin ryōri</i></a>, a Buddhist vegetarian diet. This improved the quality of vegetables and preparation methods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960 the name Kyō-yasai was adopted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-4">[4]</a></sup> In the 1970s the vegetables lost popularity as imported vegetables replaced them. Such vegetables were easier to cook than Kyō-yasai, leaving Kyō-yasai on the verge of extinction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kyoto-area growers improved their products so that people could easily cook them. They made them smaller without losing flavor and spread new methods of cooking. Administrative organizations cooperated with agricultural and distribution organizations, attempting to increase sales of Kyō-yasai. In 1989, they started to certify Kyō-yasai as name-brand products. They also promoted Kyō-yasai to other cities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto2_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kyoyasai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most general definition covers all vegetables grown in Kyoto prefecture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto3-5">[5]</a></sup> More limited definitions include: Dentou no Kyō-yasai (Traditional Kyo-yasai) that originated before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meiji_period" title="Meiji period">Meiji period</a> and were grown throughout Kyoto. Bracken, springs<sup>[?]</sup>, extinct species and <a href="/wiki/Bamboo#Uses" title="Bamboo">bamboo</a> are also included according to the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_cooperative" title="Agricultural cooperative">agricultural cooperative</a> JA Kyoto. In this sense they are a type of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heirloom_vegetable" title="Heirloom vegetable">heirloom vegetable</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto3-5">[5]</a></sup> This provision was made in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nineteen species are included.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marketing">Marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kyoyasai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Kyo-yasai has a strong image as delicate, high-quality and nostalgic. Kyoto City started promoting the foods in 1998 followed by Kyoto Prefecture in 1999.
