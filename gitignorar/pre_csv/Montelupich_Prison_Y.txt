	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Montelupich Prison</h1>

<p>The <b>Montelupich prison</b>, so called from the street in which it is located, the <i>ulica Montelupich</i> ("street of the Montelupi family"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[note 1]</a></sup> is a historic prison in <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w" title="Kraków">Kraków</a> from early 20th century, which was used by the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> in World War II. It is universally recognized as "one of the most terrible Nazi prisons in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Poland_(1939%E2%80%9345)" title="Occupation of Poland (1939–45)"><span style="color:gray">[</span>occupied<span style="color:gray">]</span> Poland</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[1]</a></sup> The Gestapo took over the facility from the German <i><a href="/wiki/Sicherheitspolizei" title="Sicherheitspolizei">Sicherheitspolizei</a></i> at the end of March 1941. One of the Nazi officials responsible for overseeing the Montelupich Prison was <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Hahn" title="Ludwig Hahn">Ludwig Hahn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>World War II <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prisoners</a> at Montelupich were made up predominantly of the ethnically Polish political prisoners and victims of the <a href="/wiki/Gestapo" title="Gestapo">Gestapo</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C5%81apanka" title="Łapanka">street raids</a>, but also German members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS" title="SS">SS</a> and Security Service (<a href="/wiki/Sicherheitsdienst" title="Sicherheitsdienst">SD</a>) who had been sentenced to jail terms, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">spies</a> and parachutists, soldiers who had deserted the <a href="/wiki/Waffen-SS" title="Waffen-SS">Waffen-SS</a>, and regular convicts.  The number of political prisoners who passed through or ended their lives in the Montelupich in the years 1940–1944 is estimated at 50,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[3]</a></sup> Kurkiewiczowa (see <a href="#Bibliography">Bibliography</a>) states that "medieval tortures" constituted the fundamental and principal interrogation method of the Germans.
</p><p>Although the inscription on the plaque by the (side) door of the prison in the 1939 photograph pictured at right actually reads, "<a href="/wiki/Sicherheitspolizei" title="Sicherheitspolizei">Sicherheits-Polizei</a>-Gefängnis Montelupich", the name "Montelupich Prison" is strictly informal, based on common popular convention, even if it has now passed in that form into history.  The Montelupich facility was the detention centre of the first instance used by the Nazis to imprison the Polish professors from the <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">Jagiellonian University</a> arrested in 1939 in the so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Sonderaktion_Krakau" title="Sonderaktion Krakau">Sonderaktion Krakau</a></i>, an operation designed to eliminate Polish intelligentsia. In January 1944, 232 prisoners from Montelupich were executed by a Nazi firing squad at <a href="/wiki/Pe%C5%82kinie" title="Pełkinie">Pełkinie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup> In late January or early February 1944, <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Koppe" title="Wilhelm Koppe">Wilhelm Koppe</a> issued an order for the execution of 100 Montelupich prisoners as a reprisal for the unsuccessful attempt on the life of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Frank" title="Hans Frank">Hans Frank</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup> In the locality called <a href="/wiki/Wola_Filipowska" title="Wola Filipowska">Wola Filipowska</a> near Krakow there is a monument commemorating the execution by the Nazis of 42 hostages, all Montelupich prisoners who died on the spot before a firing squad on 23 November 1943.
</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Montelupich became a <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> prison where <a href="/wiki/NKVD" title="NKVD">NKVD</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urz%C4%85d_Bezpiecze%C5%84stwa" title="Urząd Bezpieczeństwa">Urząd Bezpieczeństwa</a> tortured and murdered Polish soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/Home_Army" title="Home Army">Home Army</a>. At present, the building serves as a temporary arrest and detention facility for men and women, with 158 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jail_cell" title="Jail cell">jail cells</a> and a prison hospital with additional 22 cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-a-7">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_property">History of the property</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montelupich_Prison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the property">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The building housing the prison was not originally constructed for its purpose, but instead, was a historical property that was redecorated in the Italianate Renaissance style in 1556 by the Italian <a href="/wiki/Sebastiano_Montelupi" title="Sebastiano Montelupi">Montelupi family</a> who introduced the first postal service in Poland for the court of <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_III_Vasa" title="Sigismund III Vasa">Sigismund III Vasa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[7]</a></sup> Their Kraków manor house, known in Polish as the <b><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kamienica_(architecture)" title="Kamienica (architecture)">Kamienica</a> Montelupich</b> (<i>Palazzo Montelupi</i> in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>), at Number 7 of the street to which it gave the name, was the starting point of the first international postal coach in Poland which departed from here for <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1558.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Jalu_Kurek" title="Jalu Kurek">Jalu Kurek</a> Park (see <i><a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Park_Jalu_Kurka" title="pl:Park Jalu Kurka">Park Jalu Kurka</a></i>) in Krakow was formerly the palace garden of the palazzo Montelupi.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">Current status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Montelupich_Prison&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The prison was the site of the last administration of the death penalty in Poland, performed by hanging on 21 April 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite being officially recognized as both a historical monument and a place of martyrdom, the facility continues to be operated to this day as a combination of <a href="/wiki/Remand_(detention)" title="Remand (detention)">remand prison</a> and ordinary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Correctional_facility" title="Correctional facility">correctional facility</a> by the Polish Prison Administration (the <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/S%C5%82u%C5%BCba_Wi%C4%99zienna" title="pl:Służba Więzienna">Służba Więzienna</a>), a unit of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(Poland)" title="Ministry of Justice (Poland)">Polish Justice Ministry</a>. Its current official name is <i>Areszt Śledczy w Krakowie</i>.  The infamous history of this facility continues to the present day, as evident in the 2008 death of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_people" title="Romanian people">Romanian</a> detainee, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Claudiu_Crulic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Claudiu Crulic (page does not exist)">Claudiu Crulic</a> (1975–2008; see <a class="extiw" href="https://ro.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudiu_Crulic" title="ro:Claudiu Crulic">Claudiu Crulic</a>), an incident condemned by human rights groups (such as the Human Rights House Foundation of <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>) which occasioned the resignation of the Romanian <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Romania)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Romania)">Foreign Affairs Minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Cioroianu" title="Adrian Cioroianu">Adrian Cioroianu</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Vincent A. Lapomarda writes in his book on the Nazi terror that</p><blockquote><p>On inquiring about Montelupich, on Montelupi Street, when I was in Krakow on 18 August 1986, I was able to view it from outside and learned that even today, while still operating, it has not lost the evil reputation that it had during the Nazi occupation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p></blockquote>

<p>On 24 January 1948, twenty-one Nazi German war criminals, including two women, were hanged at the Montelupich Prison as a result of the death sentences handed down in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auschwitz_Trial" title="Auschwitz Trial">First Auschwitz Trial</a>. Their names are listed below along with the names of the Nazi war criminals executed at Montelupich at other dates.
