<p>The <b>Château d'Acquigny</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Château">French castle</a> located in the department of <a href="/wiki/Eure" title="Eure">Eure</a> in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Normandy" title="Upper Normandy">Upper Normandy</a>.
</p><p>The present castle was built in 1557 by Anne de <a href="/wiki/House_of_Laval" title="House of Laval">Laval</a> and was listed in the French National Inventory of Historical Monuments in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography_and_climate">Topography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Acquigny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Topography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Acquigny" title="Acquigny">Acquigny</a> sits at the confluence of two rivers: the <a href="/wiki/Eure_(river)" title="Eure (river)">Eure</a>, formerly navigable to <a href="/wiki/Chartres" title="Chartres">Chartres</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Iton" title="Iton">Iton</a>. The two rivers were dammed and redirected during the 12th century by the monks of <a href="/wiki/Conches-en-Ouche" title="Conches-en-Ouche">Conches-en-Ouche</a> to power mills in the region. These newly created branches also fed into the castle's moats, which protected the monastery of Saint-Mals and the medieval village located directly behind the current castle.
</p><p>On either side of the château are heavily wooded hills which shelter the valley in which the château lies, creating a <a href="/wiki/Microclimate" title="Microclimate">microclimate</a> slightly warmer than the surrounding areas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Acquigny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_fortress">Medieval fortress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Acquigny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval fortress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Renaissance château that exists today was built on the site of a medieval fortress that had been built in the 11th century to protect <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> while <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_England" title="Norman invasion of England">campaigning in England</a>.
</p><p>Following the imprisonment of <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Navarre" title="Charles II of Navarre">Charles II of Navarre</a> in 1356, the Duke of Lancaster was sent by the King of England to rescue <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_of_Navarre" title="Philip of Navarre">Philip of Navarre</a>, brother of Charles. His army of twelve hundred horsemen, sixteen thousand archers and two thousand armed brigandines convened in Évreux.
</p>

<p>The original fortress was taken by Navarre and his army. Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cocherel" title="Battle of Cocherel">Battle of Cocherel</a>, the castle served as a refuge and stronghold for Navarre. Control of this strategic location along the two rivers caused much anxiety for the king of France, who feared a loss of influence in the High Normandy Region and ordered his forces to retake Acquigny. After a six-month siege by the French forces, the fortress was retaken. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1378, the fortress was razed by Charles V, who wished to destroy any fortifications and castles in Normandy that stood for the King of Navarre.  Charles V was successful in this aim with the exception of Cherbourg,  which the French could not capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>When the English were forced out of Normandy in 1450, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anne_de_Laval_(1385-1466)" title="Anne de Laval (1385-1466)">Anne de Laval</a> took possession of Acquigny. In an act to pay homage to the king of France, Anne de Laval combined the baronies Acquigny and Crevecoeur Crevecoeur.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_castle_today">The castle today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Acquigny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The castle today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The present castle was built in 1557 by Anne de Laval, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Guy_XVI_de_Laval" title="Guy XVI de Laval">Guy XVI de Laval</a> and widow of Louis Silly. She was also the cousin of the king and first lady of honor of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catherine_de_Medici" title="Catherine de Medici">Catherine de Medici</a>. She wanted the architects <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philibert_Delorme" title="Philibert Delorme">Philibert Delorme</a> and Jacques Androuet Hoop to design a castle inspired by the eternal love she bore for her husband. The castle's crest is made from the couple's intertwined initials (ALS); the floorplan of the château also takes the form of this triglyph. On the central turret there is a vault in the form of a seashell in tribute to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Way_of_St._James" title="Way of St. James">Way of St. James</a>. This facade of honor is coated with many other decorative elements that celebrate the love she held for her husband.
</p><p>The castle was purchased in 1656 by Claude Roux Cambremont.  In 1745, Pierre-Robert Roux Esneval, known as the President of Acquigny and the great-grandson of Claude Roux Cambremont, expanded the castle. Pierre-Robert Roux Esneval employed the architect Charles Thibault to rebuild the chapel of Saint-mals as well as stables and sheds. It was at this time that the <a href="/wiki/Orangery" title="Orangery">orangery</a> was built, along with the Church of Saint Cécile and the Little Castle, which was designed to be attached to a hermitage.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gardens">Gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ch%C3%A2teau_d%27Acquigny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg/220px-Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg/330px-Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg/440px-Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chapelle_saint_mauxe.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Saint Mauxe chapel seen from the château park.</div></div></div>
<p>The vast park created during the 17th century follows a circular route. The forest is filled with large <a href="/wiki/Chestnut" title="Chestnut">chestnut trees</a>, many over two hundred years old.
</p><p>At the beginning of the 19th century, the park à la française was converted into a Romantic garden, with elements such as a cascade and a path of rocks based on a theme dear to <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau" title="Jean-Jacques Rousseau">Jean-Jacques Rousseau</a> in<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reveries_of_the_Solitary_Walker" title="Reveries of the Solitary Walker">Reveries of the Solitary Walker</a></i>. 
</p>
