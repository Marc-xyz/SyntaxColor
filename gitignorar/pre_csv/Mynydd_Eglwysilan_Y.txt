<p><b>Mynydd Eglwysilan</b> is a 355-metre-high <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hill</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Caerphilly" title="Caerphilly">Caerphilly</a> county borough in South <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> 3 km to the south of <a href="/wiki/Nelson,_Caerphilly" title="Nelson, Caerphilly">Nelson</a> and a similar distance southwest of <a href="/wiki/Ystrad_Mynach" title="Ystrad Mynach">Ystrad Mynach</a>. To its south is <a href="/wiki/Senghenydd" title="Senghenydd">Senghenydd</a> at the head of the <a href="/wiki/Aber_Valley" title="Aber Valley">Aber Valley</a>. Like neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Cefn_Eglwysilan" title="Cefn Eglwysilan">Cefn Eglwysilan</a>, the hill is named after the hamlet of <a href="/wiki/Eglwysilan" title="Eglwysilan">Eglwysilan</a> 4 km to the southwest, the church here being dedicated to an obscure Saint Ilan. Remains of a linear <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthwork_(archaeology)" title="Earthwork (archaeology)">earthwork</a> known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Senghenydd_Dyke&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Senghenydd Dyke (page does not exist)">Senghenydd Dyke</a> stretch across the southern side of the hill. It is considered to mark the edge of an extensive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_deer_park" title="Medieval deer park">13th-century deer park</a> associated with nearby <a href="/wiki/Caerphilly_Castle" title="Caerphilly Castle">Caerphilly Castle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mynydd_Eglwysilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The hill is formed from the Hughes <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">Sandstone</a> (formally the ‘Hughes Member’ and formerly the ‘Hughes Beds’) of the <a href="/wiki/Pennant_Measures" title="Pennant Measures">Pennant Sandstone Formation</a> laid down late in the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> Period (c. 309–306 million years ago). <a href="/wiki/Siltstone" title="Siltstone">Siltstones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mudstone" title="Mudstone">mudstones</a> appear beneath the main upper bed of sandstone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Access">Access</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mynydd_Eglwysilan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Access">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The summit area of the hill is mapped as <a href="/wiki/Freedom_to_roam" title="Freedom to roam">open access</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Countryside_and_Rights_of_Way_Act_2000" title="Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000">Countryside and Rights of Way Act 2000</a> thereby giving walkers freedom to explore it on foot. There are additionally a couple of <a href="/wiki/Rights_of_way_in_England_and_Wales#Byways_open_to_all_traffic" title="Rights of way in England and Wales">byways</a> which traverse the eastern and southern margins of the summit plateau. The unfenced minor public road from Nelson to Senghenydd runs along the northwestern margin of the hill. A rough track passes by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trig_point" title="Trig point">trig point</a> which marks the summit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
