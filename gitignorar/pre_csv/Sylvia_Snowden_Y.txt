<p><b>Sylvia Snowden</b> is an <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African American</a> abstract painter that uses acrylics to create texture and color.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvia_Snowden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sylvia Snowden was born in 1942 in Raleigh, NC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Snowden attended <a href="/wiki/Howard_University" title="Howard University">Howard University</a> where she studied under <a href="/wiki/David_C._Driskell" title="David C. Driskell">David C. Driskell</a> and graduated with a Bachelor of Arts degree and a Master of Fine Arts degree. She received a scholarship to <a href="/wiki/Skowhegan_School_of_Painting_and_Sculpture" title="Skowhegan School of Painting and Sculpture">Skowhegan School of Painting and Sculpture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Skowhegan,_Maine" title="Skowhegan, Maine">Skowhegan, Maine</a> and has a certificate from Le Grande Chaumier in Paris, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sylvia_Snowden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She has taught at Howard University, Cornell and Yale, has served as an artist-in-residence, a panelist, visiting artist, lecturer/instructor and curator in universities, galleries and art schools both in the United States and abroad.
</p><p>She has exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Corcoran_Gallery_of_Art" title="Corcoran Gallery of Art">Corcoran Gallery of Art</a>, Women's Museum, <a href="/wiki/Montclair_Art_Museum" title="Montclair Art Museum">Montclair Art Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Museum_of_Art" title="Baltimore Museum of Art">Baltimore Museum of Art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_F._Johnson_Museum_of_Art" title="Herbert F. Johnson Museum of Art">Herbert F. Johnson Museum of Art</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Phillips_Collection" title="The Phillips Collection">The Phillips Collection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heckscher_Museum_of_Art" title="Heckscher Museum of Art">Heckscher Museum of Art</a>, and the Mary McLeod Bethune Memorial Museum and National Archives for Black Women's History.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (December 2017)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup>  Her works have been shown in Chile, the Netherlands, Ethiopia, Australia, the Bahamas, France, Mexico, Italy and Japan. Her 2000 exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Corcoran_Gallery_of_Art" title="Corcoran Gallery of Art">Corcoran Gallery of Art</a> featured work inspired by the death of her son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She has received a number of awards including the Lois M. Jones Award for Recognition.
</p>
