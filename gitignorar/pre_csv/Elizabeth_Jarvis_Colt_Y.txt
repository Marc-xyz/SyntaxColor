<p><b>Elizabeth Jarvis Colt</b> (born <b>Elizabeth Hart Jarvis</b>, October 5, 1826 – August 23, 1905) was the widow and heir of firearms manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Colt" title="Samuel Colt">Samuel Colt</a>, founder of <a href="/wiki/Colt%27s_Manufacturing_Company" title="Colt's Manufacturing Company">Colt's Manufacturing Company</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Jarvis_Colt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hart_Jarvis_House,_Portland,_CT_-_October_2017.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1712" data-file-width="2288" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Hart_Jarvis_House%2C_Portland%2C_CT_-_October_2017.jpg/220px-Hart_Jarvis_House%2C_Portland%2C_CT_-_October_2017.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Hart_Jarvis_House%2C_Portland%2C_CT_-_October_2017.jpg/330px-Hart_Jarvis_House%2C_Portland%2C_CT_-_October_2017.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Hart_Jarvis_House%2C_Portland%2C_CT_-_October_2017.jpg/440px-Hart_Jarvis_House%2C_Portland%2C_CT_-_October_2017.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hart_Jarvis_House,_Portland,_CT_-_October_2017.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hart Jarvis House, Portland, CT (October 2017).</div></div></div><p>Elizabeth Hart Jarvis was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saybrook,_Connecticut" title="Saybrook, Connecticut">Saybrook, Connecticut</a> to Reverend William Jarvis, an Episcopal Minister, and Elizabeth Jarvis. She was the eldest of five children in an affluent and socially prominent family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coller2009_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coller2009-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boynton2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boynton2014-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bendici2012_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bendici2012-5">[5]</a></sup> She "grew up in a lovely 1830s Greek Temple Revival house in Portland,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which fell into disrepair and was threatened with demolition, but (as of 2017) may be moved and rehabilitated as part of other area development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_to_Samuel_Colt">Marriage to Samuel Colt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Jarvis_Colt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage to Samuel Colt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She met Samuel Colt in 1851 in Newport, Rhode Island, and the two were married in 1856.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boynton2014_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boynton2014-4">[4]</a></sup> The couple resided at <a href="/wiki/Armsmear" title="Armsmear">Armsmear</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coller2009_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Coller2009-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Colts had four children. Two died in infancy; a daughter, named Elizabeth, died at the age of three. Only one, <a href="/wiki/Caldwell_Hart_Colt" title="Caldwell Hart Colt">Caldwell</a>, survived to adulthood, but he drowned at sea at the age of 35.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boynton2014_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Boynton2014-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (February 2015)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>In 1861, Samuel Colt died from complications associated with <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">gout</a> and left Elizabeth a pregnant widow. Seven months after his death, the baby was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stillborn" title="Stillborn">stillborn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boynton2014_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Boynton2014-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="At_the_helm_of_Colt">At the helm of Colt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Jarvis_Colt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: At the helm of Colt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following her husband's death in 1862, Mrs. Colt inherited a controlling interest in the manufacturing company (worth $3.5 million at the time and closer to $88 million in 2018 dollars), and played a key role in rebuilding the main armory following arson in 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2004_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith2004-8">[8]</a></sup> Her brother, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Jarvis" title="Richard Jarvis">Richard Jarvis</a> took over as president of the company in 1865, following the death of <a href="/wiki/Elisha_K._Root" title="Elisha K. Root">Elisha K. Root</a>, and the two transitioned the company from the end of the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> through the early 20th century, seeing the evolution from <a href="/wiki/Percussion_cap" title="Percussion cap">percussion</a> <a href="/wiki/Revolver" title="Revolver">revolvers</a> to cartridge <a href="/wiki/Revolver" title="Revolver">revolvers</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiautomatic_pistol" title="Semiautomatic pistol">semiautomatic pistols</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machinegun" title="Machinegun">machineguns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Colt served for 22 years as the president of the Union for Home Work, an organization that provided daycare for the children of working mothers. She became the first President of the Hartford Soldiers Aid Society and, in 1869, organized the first <a href="/wiki/Suffragette" title="Suffragette">Suffragette</a> convention in Connecticut. For these actions, she was dubbed "The First Lady of Hartford".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boynton2014_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Boynton2014-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1867, she had an Episcopal church designed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Tuckerman_Potter" title="Edward Tuckerman Potter">Edward Tuckerman Potter</a> built as a memorial to her husband and the three children they lost. The church's architecture contains guns and gun-smithing tools sculpted in marble to commemorate her husband's life as an arms maker. In 1896, a parish house was built on the site as a memorial to their son, Caldwell, who died in 1894. In 1975, the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_the_Good_Shepherd_and_Parish_House" title="Church of the Good Shepherd and Parish House">Church of the Good Shepherd and Parish House</a> was listed in the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement_and_death">Retirement and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elizabeth_Jarvis_Colt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Retirement and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She sold her interest in Colt's Manufacturing Company in 1901. She was involved in society life in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hartford,_CT" title="Hartford, CT">Hartford, CT</a> and President of the Hartford Women's Auxiliary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Convention1905_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Convention1905-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Colt died of paralysis in <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a> on August 23, 1905. The <i><a href="/wiki/Hartford_Courant" title="Hartford Courant">Hartford Courant</a></i> ran a full page obituary of Colt on the front page of the newspaper the following day, calling her the "First Lady of Connecticut". It was the first time that the newspaper recognized the death of a woman in this manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boynton2014_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-Boynton2014-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In her will, Elizabeth Colt left a collection of nearly 1,000 objects, artworks, firearms and documents to the <a href="/wiki/Wadsworth_Atheneum" title="Wadsworth Atheneum">Wadsworth Atheneum</a> as well as a fund to build the Colt Memorial. The Elizabeth Hart Jarvis Colt Memorial Wing was the first American museum wing bearing the name of a woman patron.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wertkin2004_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wertkin2004-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>She is buried along with her husband and children in Hartford's historic <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Hill_Cemetery_(Hartford,_Connecticut)" title="Cedar Hill Cemetery (Hartford, Connecticut)">Cedar Hill Cemetery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
