<p>An <b>exoskeleton</b> (from Greek έξω, <i>éxō</i> "outer" and σκελετός, <i>skeletós</i> "skeleton"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OnlineEtDict_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-OnlineEtDict-1">[1]</a></sup>) is the external <a href="/wiki/Skeleton" title="Skeleton">skeleton</a> that supports and protects an animal's body, in contrast to the internal skeleton (<a href="/wiki/Endoskeleton" title="Endoskeleton">endoskeleton</a>) of, for example, a <a href="/wiki/Human_skeleton" title="Human skeleton">human</a>. In usage, some of the larger kinds of exoskeletons are known as "<b><a href="/wiki/Armour_(anatomy)" title="Armour (anatomy)">shells</a></b>". Examples of animals with exoskeletons include <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Grasshopper" title="Grasshopper">grasshoppers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cockroach" title="Cockroach">cockroaches</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crustacean" title="Crustacean">crustaceans</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">crabs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lobster" title="Lobster">lobsters</a>, as well as the shells of certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sponges" title="Sponges">sponges</a> and the various groups of <a href="/wiki/Mollusc_shell" title="Mollusc shell">shelled molluscs</a>, including those of <a href="/wiki/Snail" title="Snail">snails</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clam" title="Clam">clams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tusk_shell" title="Tusk shell">tusk shells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiton" title="Chiton">chitons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nautilus" title="Nautilus">nautilus</a>. Some animals, such as the <a href="/wiki/Tortoise" title="Tortoise">tortoise</a>, have both an endoskeleton and an exoskeleton.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role">Role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exoskeleton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Exoskeletons contain rigid and resistant components that fulfill a set of functional roles in many animals including protection, excretion, sensing, support, feeding and acting as a barrier against <a href="/wiki/Desiccation" title="Desiccation">desiccation</a> in terrestrial organisms. Exoskeletons have a role in defense from pests and predators, support and in providing an attachment framework for <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">musculature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bengtson2004_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bengtson2004-2">[2]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Exoskeletons contain <a href="/wiki/Chitin" title="Chitin">chitin</a>; the addition of <a href="/wiki/Calcium_carbonate" title="Calcium carbonate">calcium carbonate</a> makes them harder and stronger.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ingrowths of the <a href="/wiki/Arthropod_exoskeleton" title="Arthropod exoskeleton">arthropod exoskeleton</a> known as <b>apodemes</b> serve as attachment sites for muscles. These structures are composed of chitin and are approximately six times stronger and twice the stiffness of vertebrate <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendons</a>. Similar to tendons, apodemes can stretch to store <a href="/wiki/Elastic_energy" title="Elastic energy">elastic energy</a> for jumping, notably in <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locusts</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Calcium carbonates constitute the shells of molluscs, <a href="/wiki/Brachiopod" title="Brachiopod">brachiopods</a>, and some tube-building <a href="/wiki/Polychaete" title="Polychaete">polychaete</a> worms. <a href="/wiki/Silicon_dioxide" title="Silicon dioxide">Silica</a> forms the exoskeleton in the microscopic <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatoms</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiolarian" title="Radiolarian">radiolaria</a>. One species of mollusc, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scaly-foot_gastropod" title="Scaly-foot gastropod">scaly-foot gastropod</a>, even makes use of the iron sulfides <a href="/wiki/Greigite" title="Greigite">greigite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a>.
</p><p>Some organisms, such as some <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agglutination_(biology)" title="Agglutination (biology)">agglutinate</a> exoskeletons by sticking grains of sand and shell to their exterior. Contrary to a common misconception, <a href="/wiki/Echinoderm" title="Echinoderm">echinoderms</a> do not possess an exoskeleton, as their <a href="/wiki/Test_(biology)" title="Test (biology)">test</a> is always contained within a layer of living tissue.
</p><p>Exoskeletons have evolved independently many times; 18 lineages evolved <a href="/wiki/Calcification" title="Calcification">calcified</a> exoskeletons alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porter2007_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Porter2007-4">[4]</a></sup> Further, other lineages have produced tough outer coatings analogous to an exoskeleton, such as some mammals. This coating is constructed from bone in the <a href="/wiki/Armadillo" title="Armadillo">armadillo</a>, and hair in the <a href="/wiki/Pangolin" title="Pangolin">pangolin</a>. The armor of reptiles like turtles and dinosaurs like <a href="/wiki/Ankylosauria" title="Ankylosauria">Ankylosaurs</a> is constructed of bone; crocodiles have bony <a href="/wiki/Scute" title="Scute">scutes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horn_(anatomy)" title="Horn (anatomy)">horny</a> scales.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exoskeleton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">Ecdysis</a></div>
<p>Since exoskeletons are rigid, they present some limits to growth. Organisms with open shells can grow by adding new material to the aperture of their shell, as is the case in snails, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bivalve" title="Bivalve">bivalves</a> and other molluscans. A true exoskeleton, like that found in arthropods, must be shed (<a href="/wiki/Ecdysis" title="Ecdysis">moulted</a>) when it is outgrown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ewer_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ewer-5">[5]</a></sup> A new exoskeleton is produced beneath the old one. As the old one is shed, the new skeleton is soft and pliable. The animal will pump itself up<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag is ambiguous, and needs clarification. (October 2015)">ambiguous</span></a></i>]</sup> to expand the new shell to maximal size, then let it harden. When the shell has set, the empty space inside the new skeleton can be filled up as the animal eats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ewer_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ewer-5">[5]</a></sup> Failure to shed the exoskeleton once outgrown can result in the animal being suffocated within its own shell, and will stop subadults from reaching maturity, thus preventing them from reproducing. This is the mechanism behind some insect pesticides, such as <a href="/wiki/Azadirachtin" title="Azadirachtin">Azadirachtin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Paleontological_significance">Paleontological significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exoskeleton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Paleontological significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BoredEncrustedShell.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/BoredEncrustedShell.JPG/280px-BoredEncrustedShell.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/BoredEncrustedShell.JPG/420px-BoredEncrustedShell.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/BoredEncrustedShell.JPG/560px-BoredEncrustedShell.JPG 2x" width="280"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BoredEncrustedShell.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Borings in exoskeletons can provide evidence of animal behavior. In this case, boring <a href="/wiki/Sponge" title="Sponge">sponges</a> attacked this <a href="/wiki/Hard_clam" title="Hard clam">hard clam</a> shell after the death of the clam, producing the trace fossil <i><a href="/wiki/Entobia" title="Entobia">Entobia</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Exoskeletons, as hard parts of organisms, are greatly useful in assisting preservation of organisms, whose soft parts usually rot before they can be fossilized. Mineralized exoskeletons can be preserved "as is", as shell fragments, for example. The possession of an exoskeleton also permits a couple of other routes to <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">fossilization</a>. For instance, the tough layer can resist compaction, allowing a mold of the organism to be formed underneath the skeleton, which may later decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fedonkin2007_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fedonkin2007-7">[7]</a></sup> Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Lagerst%C3%A4tte" title="Lagerstätte">exceptional preservation</a> may result in chitin being mineralized, as in the <a href="/wiki/Burgess_Shale" title="Burgess Shale">Burgess Shale</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butterfield2003_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butterfield2003-8">[8]</a></sup> or transformed to the resistant polymer <a href="/wiki/Keratin" title="Keratin">keratin</a>, which can resist decay and be recovered.
</p><p>However, our dependence on fossilized skeletons also significantly limits our understanding of evolution. Only the parts of organisms that were already <a href="/wiki/Mineralization_(biology)" title="Mineralization (biology)">mineralized</a> are usually preserved, such as the shells of molluscs. It helps that exoskeletons often contain "muscle scars", marks where muscles have been attached to the exoskeleton, which may allow the reconstruction of much of an organism's internal parts from its exoskeleton alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fedonkin2007_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Fedonkin2007-7">[7]</a></sup> The most significant limitation is that, although there are 30-plus <a href="/wiki/Phylum" title="Phylum">phyla</a> of living animals, two-thirds of these phyla have never been found as fossils, because most animal species are soft-bodied and decay before they can become fossilized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CowenHistLife_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-CowenHistLife-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mineralized skeletons first appear in the fossil record shortly before the base of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambrian_period" title="Cambrian period">Cambrian period</a>, <span class="nourlexpansion plainlinks" style="white-space:nowrap;"><a class="external text" href="http://tools.wmflabs.org/timescale/?Ma=550">550</a> million years ago</span>. The evolution of a mineralized exoskeleton is seen by some as a possible driving force of the <a href="/wiki/Cambrian_explosion" title="Cambrian explosion">Cambrian explosion</a> of animal life, resulting in a diversification of predatory and defensive tactics. However, some Precambrian (<a href="/wiki/Ediacaran" title="Ediacaran">Ediacaran</a>) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ediacara_biota" title="Ediacara biota">organisms</a> produced tough outer shells<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fedonkin2007_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Fedonkin2007-7">[7]</a></sup> while others, such as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cloudinid" title="Cloudinid">Cloudina</a></i>, had a calcified exoskeleton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hua2003_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hua2003-10">[10]</a></sup>
Some <i>Cloudina</i> shells even show evidence of predation, in the form of borings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hua2003_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hua2003-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Exoskeleton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Small_shelly_fauna" title="Small shelly fauna">Small shelly fauna</a></div>
<p>On the whole, the fossil record only contains mineralised exoskeletons, since these are by far the most durable. Since most lineages with exoskeletons are thought to have started out with a non-mineralised exoskeleton which they later mineralised, this makes it difficult to comment on the very early evolution of each lineage's exoskeleton. It is known, however, that in a very short course of time, just before the Cambrian period, exoskeletons made of various materials – silica, <a href="/wiki/Calcium_phosphate" title="Calcium phosphate">calcium phosphate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">calcite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">aragonite</a>, and even glued-together mineral flakes – sprang up in a range of different environments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dzik2007_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dzik2007-11">[11]</a></sup> Most lineages adopted the form of calcium carbonate which was stable in the ocean at the time they first mineralised, and did not change from this mineral morph - even when it became the less favorable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porter2007_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Porter2007-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Precambrian (Ediacaran) organisms produced tough but non-mineralized outer shells,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fedonkin2007_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-Fedonkin2007-7">[7]</a></sup> while others, such as <i>Cloudina</i>, had a calcified exoskeleton,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hua2003_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hua2003-10">[10]</a></sup> but mineralized skeletons did not become common until the beginning of the Cambrian period, with the rise of the "<a href="/wiki/Small_shelly_fauna" title="Small shelly fauna">small shelly fauna</a>". Just after the base of the Cambrian, these miniature fossils become diverse and abundant – this abruptness may be an illusion, since the chemical conditions which preserved the small shellies appeared at the same time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dzik1994_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dzik1994-12">[12]</a></sup> Most other shell-forming organisms appear during the Cambrian period, with the <a href="/wiki/Bryozoa" title="Bryozoa">Bryozoans</a> being the only calcifying phylum to appear later, in the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician" title="Ordovician">Ordovician</a>. The sudden appearance of shells has been linked to a change in <a href="/wiki/Ocean_chemistry" title="Ocean chemistry">ocean chemistry</a> which made the calcium compounds of which the shells are constructed stable enough to be precipitated into a shell. However this is unlikely to be a sufficient cause, as the main construction cost of shells is in creating the <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polysaccharide" title="Polysaccharide">polysaccharides</a> required for the shell's <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite structure</a>, not in the precipitation of the mineral components.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bengtson2004_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bengtson2004-2">[2]</a></sup> Skeletonization also appeared at almost exactly the same time that animals started <a href="/wiki/Burrow" title="Burrow">burrowing</a> to avoid predation, and one of the earliest exoskeletons was made of glued-together mineral flakes, suggesting that skeletonization was likewise a response to increased pressure from predators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dzik2007_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dzik2007-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ocean chemistry may also control which mineral shells are constructed of. Calcium carbonate has two forms, the stable calcite, and the <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">metastable</a> aragonite, which is stable within a reasonable range of chemical environments but rapidly becomes unstable outside this range. When the oceans contain a relatively high proportion of magnesium compared to calcium, aragonite is more stable, but as the magnesium concentration drops, it becomes less stable, hence harder to incorporate into an exoskeleton, as it will tend to dissolve.
</p><p>With the exception of the molluscs, whose shells often comprise both forms, most lineages use just one form of the mineral. The form used appears to reflect the seawater chemistry – thus which form was more easily precipitated – at the time that the lineage first evolved a calcified skeleton, and does not change thereafter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Porter2007_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Porter2007-4">[4]</a></sup> However, the relative abundance of calcite- and aragonite-using lineages does not reflect subsequent seawater chemistry – the magnesium/calcium ratio of the oceans appears to have a negligible impact on organisms' success, which is instead controlled mainly by how well they recover from mass extinctions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kiessling2008_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kiessling2008-13">[13]</a></sup> A recently discovered<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waren2003_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Waren2003-14">[14]</a></sup> modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropod</a> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chrysomallon_squamiferum" title="Chrysomallon squamiferum">Chrysomallon squamiferum</a></i> that lives near deep-sea <a href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent" title="Hydrothermal vent">hydrothermal vents</a> illustrates the influence of both ancient and modern local chemical environments: its shell is made of aragonite, which is found in some of the earliest fossil mollusks; but it also has armor plates on the sides of its foot, and these are mineralized with the iron sulfides <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">pyrite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greigite" title="Greigite">greigite</a>, which had never previously been found in any <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">metazoan</a> but whose ingredients are emitted in large quantities by the vents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bengtson2004_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bengtson2004-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
