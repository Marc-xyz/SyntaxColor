	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ammineite</h1>

<p><b>Ammineite</b> is the first recognized mineral containing ammine groups. Its formula is [CuCl<sub>2</sub>(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>]. The mineral is chemically pure. It was found in a guano deposit in Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bojar2010_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bojar2010-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mindat_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mindat-2">[2]</a></sup> 
At the same site other ammine-containing minerals were later found:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Chanabayaite" title="Chanabayaite">Chanabayaite</a>, CuCl(N<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>)(NH<sub>3</sub>)·0.25H<sub>2</sub>O (an alternative formula), a <a href="/wiki/Triazolate" title="Triazolate">triazolate</a> mineral</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joanneumite" title="Joanneumite">Joanneumite</a>, Cu(C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>3</sub>O<sub>3</sub>H<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isocyanurate" title="Isocyanurate">isocyanurate</a> mineral</li>
<li><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Shilovite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Shilovite (page does not exist)">Shilovite</a>, Cu(NH<sub>3</sub>)<sub>4</sub>(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_structure">Crystal structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammineite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Crystal structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The characteristic features of the structure of ammineite are:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bojar2010_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bojar2010-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>layers of trans form of the copper complex, parallel to (001), connected by Cu-Cl bonds</li>
<li>presence of CuN<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>4</sub> distorted octahedron ([4+2] coordination)</li>
<li>edge-sharing of the octahedra produce zigzag chains along the [001] direction</li>
<li>hydrogen bonds between NH<sub>3</sub> and Cl atoms</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_minerals">Associated minerals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammineite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Associated minerals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ammineite coexists with <a href="/wiki/Atacamite" title="Atacamite">atacamite</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Darapskite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Darapskite (page does not exist)">darapskite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">halite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salammoniac" title="Salammoniac">salammoniac</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bojar2010_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bojar2010-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammineite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ammineite is supposed to be a result of an interaction of an earlier copper mineral, likely from a plutonic rock, with ammonia in guano. Ammonia may be produced in decomposition of compounds like <a href="/wiki/Urea" title="Urea">urea</a> or <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uric_adic&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uric adic (page does not exist)">uric adic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bojar2010_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bojar2010-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
