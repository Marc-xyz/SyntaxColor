<p><b>Christian Schwarz-Schilling</b> (born November 19, 1930 in <a href="/wiki/Innsbruck" title="Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>), is an <a href="/wiki/Austrians" title="Austrians">Austrian</a>-born <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Germany" title="Politics of Germany">German</a> politician, entrepreneur, philanthropist and media and telecommunications innovator. He is the son of the composer <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Schwarz-Schilling" title="Reinhard Schwarz-Schilling">Reinhard Schwarz-Schilling</a> and is married to the author <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marie-Luise_Schwarz-Schilling&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marie-Luise Schwarz-Schilling (page does not exist)">Marie-Luise Schwarz-Schilling</a> with whom he has two children.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_career">Professional career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Schwarz-Schilling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Professional career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1950, Schwarz-Schilling got his <a href="/wiki/Abitur" title="Abitur">Abitur</a> at the Ernst-Moritz-Arndt <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">Gymnasium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>. He continued to study <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">History</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> Languages and Culture at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ludwig-Maximilians-Universit%C3%A4t_M%C3%BCnchen" title="Ludwig-Maximilians-Universität München">Ludwig Maximilian University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>. In 1956 he was granted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> for his thesis on Chinese History,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-setimes.com_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-setimes.com-1">[1]</a></sup> "Der Friede von Shan-Yüan 1005 n. Chr. und seine Auswirkungen auf die Beziehungen zwischen dem Chinesischen Reich und dem Liao-Reich der Kitan" (The Peace of Shan Yuan 1005 AD, and its Effects on the Relations between the Chinese Empire and the Liao-Empire of Kitan).
</p><p>In 1957, he became manager of the battery manufacturer Accumulatorenfabrik Sonnenschein in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Budingen" title="Budingen">Büdingen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a>, which he remained until 1982. 1993-2002 he was CEO of Dr. Schwarz-Schilling &amp; Partner GmbH, his own telecommunications consultancy in Büdingen.
</p><p>In 1971, he became a member of the Television Council of the <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">ZDF</a>, one of Germany's two public service TV stations, which he left in 1982. Between 1975 and 1983 he was chairman of the coordination council for Media Politics of the CDU/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Social_Union_of_Bavaria" title="Christian Social Union of Bavaria">CSU</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Schwarz-Schilling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During this period he began to form an interest in regional politics, joining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic Union</a> in 1960. In 1964, he joined the regional board of the CDU in Hesse. In 1966, Schwarz-Schilling was elected into the regional parliament of Hesse and in 1967 he became the general secretary of the CDU in Hesse. Since 1971, Schwarz-Schilling became involved in national politics, becoming member of several councils. In 1976 Schwarz-Schilling was elected into the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a> and remained a member until 2002. During this time he served as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vice-chairman" title="Vice-chairman">vice-chairman</a> of the Small Business Union of the <a href="/wiki/CDU/CSU" title="CDU/CSU">CDU/CSU</a> between 1977 and 1997. In 1979, he became president of the Executive Bureau of the European Small Business Union, which he left in 1982. Between 1981 and 1982, he was  <a href="/wiki/Chairperson" title="Chairperson">chairperson</a> of the Research Committee on New Information and Communication Technology of the Bundestag furthering innovative communications technology.
</p><p><br/>
In 1982, he was appointed <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Minister_for_Post_and_Communication&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Federal Minister for Post and Communication (page does not exist)">Federal Minister for Post and Communication</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl#Kohl.27s_First_Cabinet.2C_October_4.2C_1982_-_March_29.2C_1983" title="Helmut Kohl">first cabinet Kohl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>Before his appointment, he was  He retained his post for the next three cabinets Kohl, Schwarz-Schilling was never part of Kohl's inner circle and is, by some, regarded as unremarkable minister. Others see him as cabinet minister who pursued a long-term strategy of modernisation and actually got things done. Under his ministry cable television was introduced in Germany and commercial television was allowed to broadcast. Deutsche Post was privatised, including its Telecom business. Schwarz-Schilling also introduced GSM nationwide. By the time he left office, Germany had one of the most modern communications infrastructures in the world.
</p><p>In 1992, Schwarz-Schilling resigned his post in anger at Germany's inaction over atrocities in the then <a href="/wiki/Yugoslavia" title="Yugoslavia">Yugoslavia</a> — rebuffing Chancellor Kohl's protestations that Germany's post-war constitution barred it from stepping in. He told the Chancellor he was "ashamed" to belong to such a government, saying he had entered politics in the first place to ensure that atrocities like those perpetrated by the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> "never happen again." The <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a> daily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sueddeutsche_Zeitung" title="Sueddeutsche Zeitung">Sueddeutsche Zeitung</a> later commented that "most notable act in office was leaving it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>As Yugoslavia lurched into chaos, Schwarz-Schilling began to try to mediate between the factions — a role later formalised in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_agreement" title="Washington agreement">Washington agreement</a> of 1994, and which he held until 2004.
</p><p>During and after the war, Schwarz-Schilling travelled around the country, trying to resolve disputes and later overseeing the return of some of the 2.2 million refugees — half the population — created by the conflict.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.
</p><p>In 1995, he became chairperson of the sub-committee on Human Rights and Humanitarian Aid. In 1998 the sub-committee became a full committee and Schwarz-Schilling became its vice-chairperson, serving until 2002.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critics">Critics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Schwarz-Schilling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To speed up the cable project, Schwarz-Schilling decided to involve private companies in the cable laying. Sonnenschein KG also had its wife's company involved in the "Projektgesellschaft für Kabel-Kommunikation mbH", where he had been managing director for many years. His shares on the Sonnenschein KG, he resigned only a few hours before his appointment as Post Minister. Buyer of these shares was the Nixdorf Group. His decision to use copper, met both at home and abroad to wonder: It was already foreseen in the early 1980s that fiber optic cables are the "technology of the future."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>During his tenure, Black Schilling was known as "Kohl's most affair rich minister". The trigger for these affairs were usually the complications of his wife's family business in Schwarz-Schilling's political decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the employees of the Deutsche Bundespost Schwarz-Schilling was anything but popular, as he was the last post minister before privatization. So there was a joke among the staff: What does Schwarz-Schilling do when he comes to the office in the morning? He does the post.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_political_career">International political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Schwarz-Schilling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1536" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg/220px-Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg/330px-Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg/440px-Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Christian-Schwarz-Schilling-2107.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Christian Schwarz-Schilling, 2010</div></div></div>
<p>On December 14, 2005, he was confirmed to replace <a href="/wiki/Paddy_Ashdown" title="Paddy Ashdown">Lord Ashdown</a> both as the <a href="/wiki/High_Representative_for_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="High Representative for Bosnia and Herzegovina">High Representative</a> (OHR) — a post created by the 1995 <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Agreement" title="Dayton Agreement">Dayton Agreement</a> — and as the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Special_Representative#Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="European Union Special Representative">EU's special representative in Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>. On January 31, 2006 he was appointed as such.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-setimes.com_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-setimes.com-1">[1]</a></sup> Schwarz-Schilling was nominated by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosniak" title="Bosniak">Bosniak</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnian_Serb" title="Bosnian Serb">Bosnian Serb</a> presidents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-news.bbc.co.uk_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-news.bbc.co.uk-3">[3]</a></sup> He has cast his role as that of "advisor" to the country who wants to "listen to the people"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> — in contrast to his predecessor Ashdown, who attracted criticism particularly from Bosnian Serbs for relying too heavily on his Bonn-powers to force through legislation and sack elected officials.
</p><p>Under Schwarz-Schilling, the OHR seemed to soften its invasiveness, thanks to pressures from the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">Council of Europe</a> and a growing EU involvement. The number of OHR legislative initiatives and of dismissed officials lowered. The EU decision to shut down the OHR by June 2007 unexpectedly arose disappointment and concern in the Bosnian population, NGOs, and politicians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venneri_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-venneri-8">[8]</a></sup> During his time in office, nationwide research by Oxford Research International, which Schwarz-Schilling oversaw, showed that the silent majority of Bosnia and Herzegovina was significantly more tolerant and forward-looking than the politicians who represented them. It also showed that several policies implemented by national politicians and the international community were out of step with what the population wanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Slovak diplomat <a href="/wiki/Miroslav_Laj%C4%8D%C3%A1k" title="Miroslav Lajčák">Miroslav Lajčák</a> replaced Christian Schwarz-Schilling - who was originally intended to be the last holder of the post - on 30 June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
Lajčák retook a more intrusive approach in the work of the OHR, making it seem that apparently decreased intrusiveness was mostly due to the "weak personality" of Schwarz-Schilling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venneri_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-venneri-8">[8]</a></sup> However, Schwarz-Schilling remains popular with the Bosnian population.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Christian_Schwarz-Schilling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In 1992, Schwarz-Schilling received the Achievement Cross with Star of the Order of Achievement of the Federal Republic of Germany</li>
<li>In 1995, he became Honorary Doctor of Business Administration of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bryant_College" title="Bryant College">Bryant College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Smithfield,_Rhode_Island" title="Smithfield, Rhode Island">Smithfield</a>, USA.</li>
<li>In 2004, he was made an honorary citizen of the town <a href="/wiki/B%C3%BCdingen" title="Büdingen">Büdingen</a></li>
<li>In 2005, he received the Manfred Wörner Medal for "special meritorious service to peace and freedom in Europe".</li>
<li>In 2007, he received the Hessian Peace Prize for his efforts to end the atrocities in <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> during and after the war.</li>
<li>Since 2007, Schwarz-Schilling has been teaching as a professor at Sarajevo School of Science and Technology</li></ul>
