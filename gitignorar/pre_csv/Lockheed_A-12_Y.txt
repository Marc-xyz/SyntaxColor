<p>The <b>Lockheed A-12</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">reconnaissance aircraft</a> built for the United States <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA) by <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Skunk_Works" title="Skunk Works">Skunk Works</a>, based on the designs of <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Johnson_(engineer)" title="Kelly Johnson (engineer)">Clarence "Kelly" Johnson</a>. The aircraft was designated A-12, the 12th in a series of internal design efforts for "Archangel", the aircraft's internal code name. In 1959 it was selected over <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a>'s FISH and <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish" title="Convair Kingfish">Kingfish</a> designs as the winner of <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish#Project_Gusto" title="Convair Kingfish">Project GUSTO</a>, and was developed and operated under Project Oxcart.
</p><p>The CIA's representatives initially favored Convair's design for its smaller <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross-section</a>, but the A-12's specifications were slightly better and its projected cost was much lower. The companies' respective track records proved decisive. Convair's work on the <a href="/wiki/Convair_B-58_Hustler" title="Convair B-58 Hustler">B-58</a> had been plagued with delays and cost overruns, whereas Lockheed had produced the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a> on time and under budget. In addition, Lockheed had experience running a "black" project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The A-12 was produced from 1962 to 1964 and flew from 1963 to 1968. It was the precursor to the twin-seat <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_YF-12" title="Lockheed YF-12">YF-12</a> prototype <a href="/wiki/Interceptor_aircraft" title="Interceptor aircraft">interceptor</a>, M-21 launcher for the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_D-21" title="Lockheed D-21">D-21 drone</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">SR-71 Blackbird</a>, a slightly longer variant able to carry a heavier fuel and camera load. The A-12 began flying missions in 1967 and its final mission was in May 1968; the program and aircraft were retired in June. The program was officially revealed in the mid-1990s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A CIA officer later wrote, "Oxcart was selected from a random list of codenames to designate this <a href="/wiki/Research_and_development" title="Research and development">R&amp;D</a> and all later work on the A-12. The aircraft itself came to be called that as well."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The crews named the A-12 the <i>Cygnus</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> suggested by pilot Jack Weeks to follow the Lockheed practice of naming aircraft after celestial bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Archangel1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="594" data-file-width="792" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Archangel1.jpg/220px-Archangel1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Archangel1.jpg/330px-Archangel1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Archangel1.jpg/440px-Archangel1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Archangel1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Archangel 1 design, July 1958</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Archangel2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="594" data-file-width="792" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Archangel2.jpg/220px-Archangel2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Archangel2.jpg/330px-Archangel2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Archangel2.jpg/440px-Archangel2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Archangel2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Archangel 2 design, September 1958</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LockheedA-11.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="594" data-file-width="792" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/LockheedA-11.jpg/220px-LockheedA-11.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/LockheedA-11.jpg/330px-LockheedA-11.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/LockheedA-11.jpg/440px-LockheedA-11.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LockheedA-11.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A-11 design, March 1959</div></div></div>
<p>With the failure of the CIA's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Rainbow" title="Project Rainbow">Project Rainbow</a> to reduce the <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross-section</a> (RCS) of the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_U-2" title="Lockheed U-2">U-2</a>, preliminary work began inside Lockheed in late 1957 to develop a follow-on aircraft to overfly the Soviet Union.  Designer Kelly Johnson said, "In April 1958 I recall having long discussions with [CIA Deputy Director for Plans] Richard Bissell over the subject of whether there should be a follow-on to the U-2 aircraft.  We agreed ... that there should be one more round before satellites would make aircraft reconnaissance obsolete for covert reconnaissance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHistory_of_the_Oxcart_Program1968_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHistory_of_the_Oxcart_Program1968-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish#Project_Gusto" title="Convair Kingfish">Project Gusto</a> the designs were nicknamed "Archangel", after the U-2 program, which had been known as "Angel". As the aircraft designs evolved and configuration changes occurred, the internal Lockheed designation changed from Archangel-1 to Archangel-2, and so on. These names for the evolving designs soon simply became known as "A-1", "A-2", etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The CIA program to develop the follow-on aircraft to the U-2 was code-named <i>Oxcart</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>These designs had reached the A-11 stage when the program was reviewed. The A-11 was competing against a <a href="/wiki/Convair" title="Convair">Convair</a> proposal called <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish" title="Convair Kingfish">Kingfish</a>, of roughly similar performance. However, the Kingfish included a number of features that greatly reduced its radar cross-section, which was seen as favorable to the board. Lockheed responded with a simple update of the A-11, adding twin canted fins instead of a single right-angle one, and adding a number of areas of non-metallic materials. This became the A-12 design. On 26 January 1960, the CIA ordered 12 A-12 aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_materials_and_production_techniques">New materials and production techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New materials and production techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because the A-12 was well ahead of its time, many new technologies had to be invented specifically for the Oxcart project with some remaining in use in present day. One of the biggest problems that engineers faced at that time was working with <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his book <i>Skunk Works: A Personal Memoir of My Years at Lockheed</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Rich" title="Ben Rich">Ben Rich</a> stated, "Our supplier, Titanium Metals Corporation, had only limited reserves of the precious alloy, so the CIA conducted a worldwide search and using third parties and dummy companies, managed to unobtrusively purchase the base metal from one of the world's leading exporters – the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. The Russians never had an inkling of how they were actually contributing to the creation of the airplane being rushed into construction to spy on their homeland."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERichJanos1994_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichJanos1994-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the A-12, titanium was used only in high-temperature exhaust fairings and other small parts directly related to supporting, cooling, or shaping high-temperature areas on aircraft like those subject to the greatest <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_heating" title="Aerodynamic heating">kinetic heating</a> from the airstream, such as wing leading edges. The A-12, however, was constructed mainly of titanium. Titanium is quite rigid and difficult to machine, which made it difficult to form into curves given available techniques. This made the construction of the leading edges of the wing and similar surfaces difficult to form. The solution was found by machining only small "fillets" of the material with the required shape and then gluing them onto the underlying framework which was more linear. A good example is on the wing; the underlying framework of spars and stringers formed a grid, leaving triangular notches along the leading edge that were filled with fillets.
</p><p>With the move to the A-12, another improvement in RCS was made by replacing the fillets with new radar-absorbing <a href="/wiki/Composite_material" title="Composite material">composite materials</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_iron" title="Allotropes of iron">iron ferrite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">silicon</a> laminate, both combined with <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a> to absorb radar returns and make the aircraft more stealthy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_testing">Flight testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Flight testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A12radartesting.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="567" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/A12radartesting.jpg/220px-A12radartesting.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/A12radartesting.jpg/330px-A12radartesting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/A12radartesting.jpg/440px-A12radartesting.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A12radartesting.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A-12 60-6925, No. 122, mounted inverted for radar testing at <a href="/wiki/Area_51" title="Area 51">Area 51</a></div></div></div>
<p>After development and production at the Skunk Works, in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank</a>, California, the first A-12 was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Area_51" title="Area 51">Groom Lake test facility</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On 25 April 1962 it was taken on its first (unofficial and unannounced) flight with Lockheed test pilot <a href="/wiki/Louis_Schalk" title="Louis Schalk">Louis Schalk</a> at the controls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The first official flight later took place on 30 April and subsequent supersonic flight on 4 May 1962, reaching speeds of Mach 1.1 at 40,000 ft (12,000 m).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first five A-12s, in 1962, were initially flown with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_JT4A" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney JT4A">Pratt &amp; Whitney J75</a> engines capable of 17,000 lbf (76 kN)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Thrust" title="Thrust">thrust</a> each, enabling the J75-equipped A-12s to obtain speeds of approximately Mach 2.0. On 5 October 1962, with the newly developed <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_J58" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney J58">J58</a> engines, an A-12 flew with one J75 engine, and one J58 engine. By early 1963, the A-12 was flying with J58 engines, and during 1963 these J58-equipped A-12s obtained speeds of Mach 3.2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis_p16-7_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis_p16-7-17">[17]</a></sup> Also, in 1963, the program experienced its first loss when, on 24 May, "Article 123"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lacitis20100327_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-lacitis20100327-18">[18]</a></sup> piloted by Kenneth S. Collins crashed near <a href="/wiki/Wendover,_Utah" title="Wendover, Utah">Wendover</a>, Utah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Collins safely ejected and was wearing a standard flight suit, avoiding unwanted questions from the truck driver who picked him up. He called Area 51 from a highway patrol office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afmag-richelson_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-afmag-richelson-20">[20]</a></sup> The reaction to the crash illustrated the secrecy and importance of the project. The CIA called the aircraft a <a href="/wiki/Republic_F-105_Thunderchief" title="Republic F-105 Thunderchief">Republic F-105 Thunderchief</a> in news articles and official records;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacobsen_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-jacobsen-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afmag-richelson_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-afmag-richelson-20">[20]</a></sup> two nearby farmers were told that the plane was carrying atomic weapons to dissuade them from approaching the crash site;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afmag-richelson_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-afmag-richelson-20">[20]</a></sup> and local law enforcement and a passing family were strongly warned to keep quiet about the crash. Each was also paid $25,000 in cash to do so; the project often used such cash payments to avoid outside inquiries into its operations. The project received ample funding; contracted security guards were paid $1,000 monthly with free housing on base, and chefs from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada" title="Las Vegas, Nevada">Las Vegas</a> were available 24 hours a day for steak, <a href="/wiki/American_lobster" title="American lobster">Maine lobster</a>, or other requests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lacitis20100327_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-lacitis20100327-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 1964, the last A-12 was delivered to Groom Lake,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> from where the fleet made a total of 2,850 test flights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jacobsen_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-jacobsen-21">[21]</a></sup> A total of 18 aircraft were built through the program's production run. Of these, 13 were A-12s, three were prototype <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_YF-12" title="Lockheed YF-12">YF-12A</a> interceptors for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> (not funded under the OXCART program), and two were M-21 reconnaissance drone carriers. One of the 13 A-12s was a dedicated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trainer_(aircraft)" title="Trainer (aircraft)">trainer aircraft</a> with a second seat, located behind the pilot and raised to permit the Instructor Pilot to see forward. The A-12 trainer, known as "Titanium Goose", retained the J75 power plants for its entire service life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis_p16_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis_p16-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Three more A-12s were lost in later testing. On 9 July 1964, "Article 133" crashed while making its <a href="/wiki/Final_approach_(aeronautics)" title="Final approach (aeronautics)">final approach</a> to the runway when a pitch-control <a href="/wiki/Servomechanism" title="Servomechanism">servo device</a> froze at an altitude of 500 ft (150 m) and airspeed of 200 knots (230 mph; 370 km/h) causing it to begin a smooth steady roll to the left. Lockheed test pilot Bill Parks could not overcome the roll. At about a 45-degree bank angle and 200 ft (61 m) altitude he <a href="/wiki/Ejection_seat" title="Ejection seat">ejected</a> and was blown sideways out of the aircraft. Although he was not very high off the ground, his parachute did open and he landed safely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_21–23_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_21–23-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 28 December 1965, the third A-12 was lost when "Article 126" crashed 30 seconds after takeoff when a series of violent <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">yawing and pitching</a> actions was followed very rapidly with the aircraft becoming uncontrollable. Mele Vojvodich was scheduled to take aircraft number 126 on a performance check flight which included a rendezvous beacon test with a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_KC-135_Stratotanker" title="Boeing KC-135 Stratotanker">KC-135 tanker</a> and managed to eject safely 150 to 200 ft (46 to 61 m) above the ground. A post-crash investigation revealed that the primary cause of the accident was a maintenance error in that the flight line technician was negligent in performing his duty when he connected the wiring harnesses for the <a href="/wiki/Rate_integrating_gyroscope" title="Rate integrating gyroscope">yaw and pitch rate gyroscopes</a> of the stability augmentation system in reverse. It was also found that a contributing cause was a design deficiency which made it possible to physically connect the wiring harnesses in reverse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first fatality of the Oxcart program occurred on 5 January 1967, when "Article 125" crashed, killing CIA pilot Walter Ray when the aircraft ran out of fuel while on its descent to the test site. No precise cause could be established for the loss and it was considered most probable that a fuel gauging error led to fuel starvation and engine <a href="/wiki/Flameout" title="Flameout">flameout</a> 67 miles (108 km) from the base. Ray ejected successfully but was unable to separate from the seat and was killed on impact.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_31–38_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_31–38-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="517" data-file-width="685" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg/220px-LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg/330px-LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg/440px-LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LockheedA-12-pilots.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A-12 pilots and managers: from left to right, Ronald J. "Jack" Layton, Dennis B. Sullivan, Mele Vojvodich Jr, Barrett, Jack W. Weeks, Kenneth B. Collins, Ray, Brig Gen Ledford, Skliar, Perkins, Holbury, Kelly, and squadron commander Col. Slater.</div></div></div>
<p>Although originally designed to succeed the U-2 in overflights over the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, the A-12 was never used for either role. After <a href="/wiki/1960_U-2_incident" title="1960 U-2 incident">a U-2 was shot down in May 1960</a>, the Soviet Union was considered too dangerous to overfly except in an emergency (and overflights were no longer necessary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> thanks to <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">reconnaissance satellites</a>) and, although crews trained for flights over Cuba, U-2s continued to be adequate there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Director of the CIA decided to deploy some A-12s to Asia. The first A-12 arrived at <a href="/wiki/Kadena_Air_Base" title="Kadena Air Base">Kadena Air Base</a> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawa" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a> on 22 May 1967. With the arrival of two more aircraft on 24 May, and 27 May this unit was declared to be operational on 30 May, and it began <b>Operation Black Shield</b> on 31 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mele_%22Mel%22_Vojvodich" title='Mele "Mel" Vojvodich'>Mel Vojvodich</a> flew the first Black Shield operation, over <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a>, photographing <a href="/wiki/Surface-to-air_missile" title="Surface-to-air missile">surface-to-air missile</a> (SAM) sites, flying at 80,000 ft (24,000 m), and at about Mach 3.1. During 1967 from the Kadena Air Base, the A-12s carried out 22 sorties in support of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. During 1968 further Black Shield operations were conducted in Vietnam. Additional sorties were also carried out during the <a href="/wiki/USS_Pueblo_(AGER-2)" title="USS Pueblo (AGER-2)">Pueblo Crisis</a> with <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_profile">Mission profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mission profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Operations and maintenance at Kadena AB began with the receipt of alert notification. Both a primary aircraft and pilot and a back-up aircraft and pilot were selected. The aircraft were given thorough inspection and servicing, all systems were checked, and the cameras equipped. Pilots received a detailed route briefing in the early evening prior to the day of flight. On the morning of the flight a final briefing occurred, at which time the condition of the aircraft and its systems was reported, last-minute weather forecasts reviewed, and other relevant intelligence communicated, together with any amendments or changes in the flight plan. Two hours prior to take-off the primary pilot had a medical examination, got into his suit, and was taken to the aircraft. If any malfunctions developed on the primary aircraft, the back-up could execute the mission one hour later.
</p><p>A typical route profile for a mission over North Vietnam included a refueling shortly after take-off, south of Okinawa, the planned photographic pass or passes, withdrawal to a second aerial refueling in the <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> area, and return to Kadena. Its turning radius of 86 miles (138 km) was such, however, that on some mission profiles it might intrude into <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">Chinese</a> airspace during the turn.
</p><p>Once landed, the camera film was removed from the aircraft, boxed, and sent by special plane to the processing facilities. Film from earlier missions was developed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastman_Kodak" title="Eastman Kodak">Eastman Kodak</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>. Later an Air Force Center in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> carried out the processing in order to place the photointelligence in the hands of American commanders in Vietnam within 24 hours of completion of a mission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="SAM_evasion_over_North_Vietnam">SAM evasion over North Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: SAM evasion over North Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There were a number of reasons leading to the retirement of the A-12, but one major concern was the growing sophistication of Soviet-supplied surface-to-air missile (SAM) sites that it had to contend with over mission routes. In 1967, the vehicle was tracked with acquisition radar over North Vietnam, but the SAM site was unsuccessful with the <a href="/wiki/Fan_Song" title="Fan Song">Fan Song</a> guidance radar used to home the missile to the target.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> On 28 October, a North Vietnamese SAM site launched a single, albeit unsuccessful, missile. Photography from this mission documented the event with photographs of missile smoke above the SAM firing site, and with pictures of the missile and of its <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrail</a>. <a href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasure" title="Electronic countermeasure">Electronic countermeasures</a> equipment appeared to perform well against the missile firing.
</p><p>During a flight on 30 October 1967, pilot Dennis Sullivan detected radar tracking on his first pass over North Vietnam. Two sites prepared to launch missiles but neither did. During the second pass, at least six missiles were fired, each confirmed by missile vapor trails on mission photography. Looking through his rear-view periscope, Sullivan saw six missile contrails climb to about 90,000 ft (27,000 m) before converging on his aircraft. He noted the approach of four missiles, and although they all detonated behind him, one came within 300 to 700 ft (100 to 200 m) of his aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Post-flight inspection revealed that a piece of metal had penetrated the lower right wing fillet area and lodged against the support structure of the wing tank. The fragment was not a warhead pellet but may have been a part of the debris from one of the missile detonations observed by the pilot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/S-75_Dvina" title="S-75 Dvina">SA-2 'Guideline'</a> was an early missile design intended to counter lower-flying and slower aircraft such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress" title="Boeing B-52 Stratofortress">B-52</a> and <a href="/wiki/Convair_B-58_Hustler" title="Convair B-58 Hustler">B-58</a>. In response to faster, higher-flying designs like the <a href="/wiki/North_American_XB-70_Valkyrie" title="North American XB-70 Valkyrie">B-70</a>, the Soviets had begun development of greatly improved missile systems, notably the <a href="/wiki/S-200_(missile)" title="S-200 (missile)">SA-5 'Gammon'</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Air_Defence_Forces" title="Soviet Air Defence Forces">Soviet Air Defence Forces</a> (<i>Protivo-Vozdushnaya Oborona</i>, PVO) cleared the SA-5 for service in 1967;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> if deployed to Vietnam, it would have provided an additional risk to the A-12.
</p><p>The final Black Shield mission over North Vietnam and the <a href="/wiki/Demilitarized_zone" title="Demilitarized zone">Demilitarized Zone</a> (DMZ) was flown on 8 March 1968. Good quality photography was obtained of <a href="/wiki/Khe_Sanh_Combat_Base" title="Khe Sanh Combat Base">Khe Sanh</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnamese</a> border areas. No usable photography was obtained of North Vietnam due to adverse weather conditions. There was no indication of a hostile weapons reaction and no ECM systems were activated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_missions_over_North_Korea">Final missions over North Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Final missions over North Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1968, three missions were flown over North Korea. The first mission occurred during a very tense period following seizure of the Navy intelligence ship <a href="/wiki/USS_Pueblo_(AGER-2)" title="USS Pueblo (AGER-2)">Pueblo</a> on 23 January. The aim was to discover whether the North Koreans were preparing any large scale hostile move following this incident and to actually find where the Pueblo was hidden. The ship was found anchored in an inlet in <a href="/wiki/Wonsan" title="Wonsan">Wonsan Bay</a> attended by two North Korean patrol boats and guarded by three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Komar_class_missile_boat" title="Komar class missile boat">Komars</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Chinese tracking of the flight was apparent, but no missiles were fired at the Oxcart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcInich1996-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The second mission on 19 February 1968, was also the first two-pass mission over North Korea. The Oxcart vehicle photographed 84 primary targets plus 89 bonus targets. Scattered clouds covered 20 percent of the area, concealing the area in which the USS Pueblo was photographed on the previous mission. One new SA-2 site was identified near Wonsan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A12instorage.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="469" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/A12instorage.jpg/220px-A12instorage.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/A12instorage.jpg/330px-A12instorage.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/A12instorage.jpg/440px-A12instorage.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A12instorage.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A-12s in storage at Palmdale, note the spurious USAF markings and serial numbers</div></div></div>
<p>Even before the A-12 became operational, its intended purpose – replacing the U-2 in overflights of the Soviet Union – had become less likely. Soviet air defenses had advanced to the point that even an aircraft flying faster than a rifle bullet at the edge of space could be tracked. Upgrades to Soviet radar systems increased their <a href="/wiki/Blip-to-scan_ratio" title="Blip-to-scan ratio">blip-to-scan ratios</a>, rendering the A-12 vulnerable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In any event, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">President Kennedy</a> had stated publicly that the United States would not resume such missions. By 1965, moreover, the <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite" title="Reconnaissance satellite">photoreconnaissance satellite</a> programs had progressed to the point that manned flights over the Soviet Union were unnecessary to collect strategic intelligence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_31–38_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_31–38-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>The A-12 program was ended on 28 December 1966<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> — even before Black Shield began in 1967 – due to budget concerns<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_41–44_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_41–44-39">[39]</a></sup> and because of the SR-71, which began to arrive at Kadena in March 1968.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> The twin-seat SR-71 was heavier and flew slightly lower and slower than the A-12.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_41–44_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_41–44-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ronald L. Layton flew the 29th and final A-12 mission on 8 May 1968, over North Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> On 4 June 1968, just 2½ weeks before the fleet's retirement, an A-12 from Kadena, piloted by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Weeks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jack Weeks (page does not exist)">Jack Weeks</a>, was lost over the Pacific Ocean near the Philippines while conducting a functional check flight after the replacement of one of its engines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_41–44_39-2"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_41–44-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Frank Murray made the final A-12 flight on 21 June 1968, to <a href="/wiki/Palmdale,_California" title="Palmdale, California">Palmdale, California</a>, storage facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_42_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_42-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 26 June 1968, Vice Admiral Rufus L. Taylor, the Deputy Director of Central Intelligence, presented the CIA <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_Star" title="Intelligence Star">Intelligence Star</a> for valor to Weeks' widow and pilots Collins, Layton, Murray, Vojvodich, and Dennis B. Sullivan for participation in Black Shield.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_41–44_39-3"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_41–44-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>The deployed A-12s and the eight non-deployed aircraft were placed in storage at Palmdale. All surviving aircraft remained there for nearly 20 years before being sent to museums around the U.S. On 20 January 2007, despite protests by Minnesota's legislature and volunteers who had maintained it in display condition, the A-12 preserved in Minneapolis, Minnesota, was sent to CIA headquarters to be displayed there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeline">Timeline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Timeline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Major events in the development and operation of the A-12 and its successor, the SR-71, include:
</p>
<ul><li>16 August 1956: Following Soviet protests about U-2 overflights, <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Bissell_Jr." title="Richard M. Bissell Jr.">Richard M. Bissell Jr.</a> conducts the first meeting on reducing the <a href="/wiki/Radar_cross-section" title="Radar cross-section">radar cross section</a> of the U-2. This evolves into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Project_Rainbow" title="Project Rainbow">Project Rainbow</a>, a bid to prolong the aircraft's operational life through a package of modifications. Called "Trapeze", these added wires and paints impregnated with tiny <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_iron" title="Allotropes of iron">iron ferrite</a> beads and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasure" title="Electronic countermeasure">ECM</a> systems. The modified U-2s were called "Dirty Birds". Ultimately, the program failed to substantially reduce the U-2's RCS, leading to the decision to develop a new aircraft with <a href="/wiki/Stealth_aircraft" title="Stealth aircraft">stealth</a> characteristics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></li>
<li>December 1957: Lockheed begins designing subsonic stealthy aircraft under what will become <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish#Project_Gusto" title="Convair Kingfish">Project Gusto</a>.</li>
<li>24 December 1957: First J-58 engine run.</li>
<li>21 April 1958: Kelly Johnson makes first notes on a Mach-3 aircraft, initially called the U-3, but eventually evolving into Archangel I. Kelly noted in his A-12 diary, "I drew up the first Archangel proposal for a Mach 3.0 cruise airplane having a 4,000 nmi [4,600 mi; 7,400 km] range at 90,000 to 95,000 ft [27,000 to 29,000 m]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>November 1958: The Land panel provisionally selects Convair <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish#FISH" title="Convair Kingfish">FISH</a> (B-58-launched parasite) over Lockheed's A-3. The A-3 was an unstaged (non-parasite) aircraft that cruised at Mach 3.2 at 95,000 ft (29,000 m). The Land Panel favored Convair's design, which had a smaller radar cross section than the A-3. On 22 December, Convair was instructed to continue FISH's development and to plan for production. While Convair struggled with aerodynamic issues, Lockheed pursued its own efforts on high-speed, high-altitude reconnaissance designs, evolving from A-4 through A-11. The first three configurations, A-4 through A-6, were smaller, self-launched aircraft with vertical surfaces hidden above the wing. The aircraft used a variety of propulsion schemes that included <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjets</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rocket_engine" title="Rocket engine">rockets</a>. None met the required mission radius of 2,000 nmi (2,300 mi; 3,700 km), leading Lockheed to conclude that maximum performance and low radar cross section were mutually exclusive. The A-10 and A-11 configurations were larger aircraft that also focused on performance at the expense of radar cross section. Lockheed submitted the more refined A-11 at the next Land Panel review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></li>
<li>June 1959: The Land panel provisionally selects the A-11 over FISH, instructing both companies to re-design their aircraft. In July, the Land panel rejected both the Convair and Lockheed proposals. The Convair FISH used unproven <a href="/wiki/Ramjet" title="Ramjet">ramjet</a> engine technology and would be launched from a modified <a href="/wiki/Convair_B-58_Hustler" title="Convair B-58 Hustler">B-58B Hustler</a> which was canceled in June. The susceptibility of the A-11 to radar detection was considered too great. On 20 August, both firms provided specifications for their revised proposals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A-12_Nose_View.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3872" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/A-12_Nose_View.jpg/220px-A-12_Nose_View.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/A-12_Nose_View.jpg/330px-A-12_Nose_View.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/A-12_Nose_View.jpg/440px-A-12_Nose_View.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A-12_Nose_View.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Head-on view of an A-12 on the deck of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrepid_Sea-Air-Space_Museum" title="Intrepid Sea-Air-Space Museum">Intrepid Sea-Air-Space Museum</a>, illustrating the <a href="/wiki/Chine_(aeronautics)" title="Chine (aeronautics)">chines</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>14 September 1959: CIA awards antiradar study, aerodynamic structural tests, and engineering designs, selecting the A-12 over rival Convair's <a href="/wiki/Convair_Kingfish" title="Convair Kingfish">Kingfish</a>. Project <i>Oxcart</i> established. The A-12 design, a combination of their A-7 and A-11 submissions, emphasized low radar cross section, extremely high altitude and high-speed performance. Earlier, on 3 September, Project GUSTO was concluded and Project OXCART, to build the A-12, was begun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>26 January 1960: The CIA formally placed an order for 12 A-12 aircraft.</li>
<li>1 May 1960: <a href="/wiki/Francis_Gary_Powers" title="Francis Gary Powers">Francis Gary Powers</a> is <a href="/wiki/1960_U-2_incident" title="1960 U-2 incident">shot down in a U-2</a> over the Soviet Union. He safely ejected and was turned over to Soviet authorities. A well-publicized trial followed and he was sentenced to 10 years "deprivation of liberty," serving three years in prison before being exchanged in 1962 for Soviet spy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vilyam_Genrikhovich_Fisher" title="Vilyam Genrikhovich Fisher">Rudolf Abel</a>. Upon return he was debriefed extensively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A-12_Schalk_Flight,_1962.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6724" data-file-width="6000" decoding="async" height="247" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/A-12_Schalk_Flight%2C_1962.jpg/220px-A-12_Schalk_Flight%2C_1962.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/A-12_Schalk_Flight%2C_1962.jpg/330px-A-12_Schalk_Flight%2C_1962.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/A-12_Schalk_Flight%2C_1962.jpg/440px-A-12_Schalk_Flight%2C_1962.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A-12_Schalk_Flight,_1962.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A-12 piloted by Louis Schalk takes off from Groom Lake in 1962.</div></div></div>
<ul><li>26 April 1962: First flight of A-12 with Lockheed test pilot Louis Schalk at Groom Lake. The previous day, it had made an unofficial and unannounced flight, in keeping with Lockheed tradition. Schalk flew the plane less than two miles (three km), at an altitude of about twenty feet (six meters), because of serious wobbling caused by improper hookup of some navigational controls. Instead of circling around and landing, Schalk landed in the lake bed beyond the end of the runway. The next day, the official flight took place with the landing gear down, just in case. The flight lasted about 40 minutes. The takeoff was perfect, but after the A-12 got to about 300 ft (90 m) it started shedding all the "pie slice" fillets of titanium on the left side of the aircraft and one fillet on the right. (On later aircraft, those pieces were paired with triangular inserts made of radar-absorbing composite material.) Technicians spent four days finding and reattaching the pieces. Nonetheless, the flight pleased Johnson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_11–17_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_11–17-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></li>
<li>13 June 1962: SR-71 mock-up reviewed by USAF.</li>
<li>30 July 1962: J58 engine completes pre-flight testing.</li>
<li>October 1962: A-12s first flown with J58 engines</li>
<li>28 December 1962: Lockheed signs contract to build six SR-71 aircraft. Earlier in the month, on 17 December the 5th A-12 arrived at Groom Lake and the Air Force expressed an interest in obtaining reconnaissance versions of the Blackbird. Lockheed begins weapons systems development for the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_YF-12" title="Lockheed YF-12">AF-12</a>. Kelly Johnson obtained approval to design a Mach 3 Blackbird fighter/bomber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></li>
<li>January 1963: A-12 fleet operating with J58 engines</li>
<li>24 May 1963: Loss of first A-12 (#60–6926)</li>
<li>20 July 1963: First Mach 3 flight</li>
<li>7 August 1963: First flight of the YF-12A with Lockheed test pilot James Eastham at Groom Lake.</li>
<li>June 1964: Last production A-12 delivered to Groom Lake.</li>
<li>25 July 1964: President Johnson makes public announcement of SR-71.</li>
<li>29 October 1964: SR-71 prototype (#61-7950) delivered to Palmdale.</li>
<li>22 December 1964: First flight of the SR-71 with Lockheed test pilot Bob Gilliland at AF Plant #42. First mated flight of the MD-21 with Lockheed test pilot Bill Park at Groom Lake.</li>
<li>28 December 1966: Decision to terminate A-12 program by June 1968.</li>
<li>31 May 1967: A-12s conduct Black Shield operations out of Kadena</li>
<li>3 November 1967: A-12 and SR-71 conducted a reconnaissance fly-off, codenamed NICE GIRL. Between 20 October and 3 November 1967, A-12s and SR-71s flew three identical routes along the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> about one hour apart with their collection systems on. The results were inconclusive. The A-12's camera had a wider swath but the SR-71 collected types of intelligence the A-12 could not of a good quality; however, some sensors would typically be removed to make room for <a href="/wiki/Electronic_countermeasure" title="Electronic countermeasure">ECM</a> gear.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> There was little difference in range – the SR-71 carried more fuel – the A-12 had an altitude advantage of from 2,000 to 5,000 ft (600 to 1,500 m) over the SR-71 at the same <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach number</a>, being a lighter aircraft. The radar cross section of both aircraft in a clean configuration was relatively low; the SR-71 in a full sensor configuration was somewhat higher due to its larger size and was appreciably larger again with the <a href="/wiki/Side_looking_airborne_radar" title="Side looking airborne radar">side-looking radar</a> antenna installed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The A-12 was designed to optionally utilize one of three different types of high resolution cameras; the highest of which provided a 63 nmi (72 mi; 117 km) wide continuous swath of one foot (<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>3</sub></span> m) resolution. The SR-71 had the simultaneous capability for photography and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ELINT#Electronic_signals_intelligence" title="ELINT">ELINT</a>. Its imagery was one foot resolution of two separate five-mile (8.0 km) swath wide strips positioned up to 19.6 mi (31.5 km) apart on either side of the aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>26 January 1968: North Korea A-12 overflight by Jack Weeks photo-locates the captured <a href="/wiki/USS_Pueblo_(AGER-2)" title="USS Pueblo (AGER-2)">USS <i>Pueblo</i></a> in Changjahwan Bay harbor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup></li>
<li>5 February 1968: Lockheed ordered to destroy A-12, YF-12 and SR-71 tooling.</li>
<li>8 March 1968: First SR-71A (#61-7978) arrives at Kadena AB to replace A-12s.</li>
<li>21 March 1968: First SR-71 (#61-7976) operational mission flown from Kadena AB over Vietnam.</li>
<li>8 May 1968: Jack Layton flies last operational A-12 sortie, over North Korea.</li>
<li>5 June 1968: Loss of last A-12 (#60–6932) during Functional Checkout Flight (FCF) flown from <a href="/wiki/Kadena_Air_Base" title="Kadena Air Base">Kadena</a>, Jack W. Weeks became the second and last CIA pilot killed in the line of duty during <i>Oxcart</i> and is so honored in the <a href="/wiki/CIA_Memorial_Wall" title="CIA Memorial Wall">"Book of Honor"</a> at <a href="/wiki/George_Bush_Center_for_Intelligence" title="George Bush Center for Intelligence">CIA Headquarters</a>. The A-12 had a <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">radio telemetry</a> system called "Birdwatcher", monitoring the most critical aircraft systems and transmitting data to ground monitoring stations. Following <a href="/wiki/Aerial_refueling" title="Aerial refueling">aerial refueling</a>, the ground station was informed via "Birdwatcher" that the starboard engine exhaust gas temperature was in excess of 1,580 °F (860 °C), the fuel flow on that engine was less than 7,500 lb (3,400 kg) per hour and that the aircraft was below 68,500 ft (20,900 m). Several attempts to make contact were made without success. Monitoring continued until the time that the aircraft's fuel would have been depleted. The aircraft was declared missing 520 mi (840 km) east of the Philippines and 625 mi (1,000 km) south of <a href="/wiki/Okinawa_Prefecture" title="Okinawa Prefecture">Okinawa</a> in the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>. The loss was due to an in-flight emergency. To maintain security the official news release identified the loss as an SR-71. An intense air and sea search was conducted but no wreckage of "Article 129" was ever recovered. It was presumed totally destroyed at sea. The "Birdwatcher" system provided the only clues to what happened and was the basis for the accident report. It was ascertained that a malfunction involving an engine over-temperature and low fuel flow on the starboard engine had contributed to a catastrophic failure and subsequent aircraft break-up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_42_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_42-43">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li>21 June 1968: Final A-12 flight to Palmdale, California.</li></ul>
<p>See <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_SR-71_Blackbird#Timeline" title="Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird">SR-71 timeline</a> for later SR-71 events.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Training_variant">Training variant</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Training variant">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A12Blackbird.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="797" decoding="async" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/A12Blackbird.jpg/220px-A12Blackbird.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/A12Blackbird.jpg/330px-A12Blackbird.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/A12Blackbird.jpg/440px-A12Blackbird.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:A12Blackbird.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The only two-seat trainer A-12 built was nicknamed "Titanium Goose". It is on display at the <a href="/wiki/California_Science_Center" title="California Science Center">California Science Center</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The A-12 training variant (60-6927 "Titanium Goose") was a two-seat model with two cockpits in <a href="/wiki/Tandem" title="Tandem">tandem</a> with the rear cockpit raised and slightly offset. In case of emergency, the variant was designed to allow the flight instructor to take control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_21–23_24-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_21–23-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robarge_2012_11–17_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robarge_2012_11–17-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="YF-12A">YF-12A</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: YF-12A">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_YF-12" title="Lockheed YF-12">Lockheed YF-12</a></div>
<p>The YF-12 program was a limited production variant of the A-12. Lockheed convinced the U.S. Air Force that an aircraft based on the A-12 would provide a less costly alternative to the recently canceled North American Aviation <a href="/wiki/North_American_XF-108_Rapier" title="North American XF-108 Rapier">XF-108</a>, since much of the design and development work on the YF-12 had already been done and paid for. Thus, in 1960 the Air Force agreed to take the seventh to ninth slots on the A-12 production line and have them completed in the YF-12A interceptor configuration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Land_Jenk_p40-1_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Land_Jenk_p40-1-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="M-21">M-21</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: M-21">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_D-21" title="Lockheed D-21">Lockheed D-21</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LockheedM21-D21.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="916" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/LockheedM21-D21.jpg/220px-LockheedM21-D21.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/LockheedM21-D21.jpg/330px-LockheedM21-D21.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/LockheedM21-D21.jpg/440px-LockheedM21-D21.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LockheedM21-D21.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>M-21 carrying D-21 in flight</div></div></div>
<p>The <i>M-21</i>, a two-seat variant, carried and launched the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_D-21" title="Lockheed D-21">Lockheed D-21</a>, an unmanned, faster and higher-flying reconnaissance drone. The M-21 had a pylon on its back for mounting the drone and a second cockpit for a Launch Control Operator/Officer (LCO) in the place of the A-12's Q bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landis_p22-4_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-Landis_p22-4-63">[63]</a></sup> The D-21 was autonomous; after launch, it would fly over the target, travel to a predetermined rendezvous point, eject its data package, and self-destruct. A C-130 Hercules would catch the package in midair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donald_p154-5_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donald_p154-5-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>The M-21 program was canceled in 1966 after a drone collided with the mother ship at launch. The crew safely ejected, but LCO Ray Torick drowned when his flight suit filled with water after landing in the ocean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The D-21 lived on in the form of a B-model launched from a pylon under the wing of the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress" title="Boeing B-52 Stratofortress">B-52</a> bomber. The D-21B performed operational missions over China from 1969 to 1971.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donald_p155-6_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donald_p155-6-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_and_incidents">Accidents and incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Accidents and incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Six of the 15 A-12s were lost in accidents, with the loss of two pilots and an engineer: 
</p>
<ul><li>24 May 1963: 60-6926 (Article 123) crashed near <a href="/wiki/Wendover,_Utah" title="Wendover, Utah">Wendover, Utah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore236_71-1"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore236-71">[71]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> The aircraft was flying a subsonic engine test flight when it entered a cloud, pitched up, and went out of control; the CIA pilot ejected successfully.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore19_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore19-73">[73]</a></sup> The investigation found that cloud vapor had formed ice in the pitot tube, causing the airspeed indicator to show an erroneous reading. The aircraft pitched up sharply and entered an unrecoverable stall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore19_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore19-73">[73]</a></sup> The pilot was wearing a standard flight suit for this low-altitude flight, and did not look suspicious to the truck driver who recovered him or the highway patrol office where he was taken. The press was told that a Republic F-105 Thunderchief had crashed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore19_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore19-73">[73]</a></sup></li>
<li>9 July 1964: 60-6939 (Article 133) was lost on approach to Groom Dry Lake due to a complete hydraulic failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore236_71-2"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore236-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>30 July 1966: 60-6941 (Article 135), one of the two <a href="#M-21">drone carriers</a>, was lost during a test flight off the California coast. The pilot and launch control engineer ejected safely but the engineer drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore236_71-3"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore236-71">[71]</a></sup> Article 135 was operating 300 miles from the California coast to carry out a test launch of a D-21. The aircraft was flying at Mach 3.2+ when the crew made sure that the Marquardt engine on the D-21 had the required airflow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore38_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore38-74">[74]</a></sup> The drone was launched but the D-21 engine failed to start and it slammed down on the launch pylon causing the mother ship to pitch up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore38_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore38-74">[74]</a></sup> The pressure from the Mach-3.2 airflow "ripped the fuselage forebody from the wing planform". The crew were unable to escape at this high speed but managed to eject as the forebody tumbled towards the sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore38_74-2"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore38-74">[74]</a></sup> The pilot was picked up by helicopter from the sea but the LCO drowned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore38_74-3"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore38-74">[74]</a></sup></li>
<li>5 January 1967: 60-6928 (Article 125) was lost during a training flight. The pilot ejected but failed to separate from his seat and was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore236_71-4"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore236-71">[71]</a></sup> Due to a faulty fuel gauge, Article 125 ran out of fuel 70 miles from Groom Dry Lake. The pilot glided to a lower altitude to perform a controlled bailout but could not separate his parachute from his ejection seat. He was the first Cygnus pilot to be killed in an A-12 accident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore24_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore24-75">[75]</a></sup></li>
<li>28 December 1967: 60-6929 (Article 126) was lost on take-off from Groom Lake due to incorrect installation of the Stability Augmentation System (SAS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore236_71-5"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore236-71">[71]</a></sup></li>
<li>5 June 1968: 60-6932 (Article 129) was lost off the Philippines during a functional check flight. The pilot was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crickmore236_71-6"><a href="#cite_note-crickmore236-71">[71]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aircraft_on_display">Aircraft on display</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lockheed_A-12&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Aircraft on display">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All nine surviving aircraft are on display in the U.S. at the following locations.
</p>
<ul><li>A-12 60-6924 at the Air Force Flight Test Center Museum Annex, <i>Blackbird Airpark</i>, at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_42" title="Plant 42">Plant 42</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palmdale,_California" title="Palmdale, California">Palmdale, California</a>.</li>
<li>A-12 60-6925 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intrepid_Sea-Air-Space_Museum" title="Intrepid Sea-Air-Space Museum">Intrepid Sea-Air-Space Museum</a>, parked on the <a href="/wiki/Flight_deck" title="Flight deck">deck</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carrier</a> <a href="/wiki/USS_Intrepid_(CV-11)" title="USS Intrepid (CV-11)">USS <i>Intrepid</i></a>, New York City.</li>
<li>A-12 60-6927, the two-seat trainer, at the <a href="/wiki/California_Science_Center" title="California Science Center">California Science Center</a> in Los Angeles, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup></li>
<li>A-12 60-6930 at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Space_and_Rocket_Center" title="US Space and Rocket Center">U.S. Space and Rocket Center</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>.</li>
<li>A-12 60-6931 at CIA headquarters, <a href="/wiki/Langley,_Virginia" title="Langley, Virginia">Langley, Virginia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[N 1]</a></sup></li>
<li>A-12 60-6933 at the <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_Air_%26_Space_Museum" title="San Diego Air &amp; Space Museum">San Diego Air &amp; Space Museum</a>, Balboa Park, <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, California.</li>
<li>A-12 60-6937 at the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Museum_of_Flight" title="Southern Museum of Flight">Southern Museum of Flight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>.</li>
<li>A-12 60-6938 at <a href="/wiki/Battleship_Memorial_Park" title="Battleship Memorial Park">Battleship Memorial Park</a> (<a href="/wiki/USS_Alabama_(BB-60)#Museum_ship" title="USS Alabama (BB-60)">USS <i>Alabama</i></a>), <a href="/wiki/Mobile,_Alabama" title="Mobile, Alabama">Mobile</a>, Alabama.</li>
<li>M-21 60-6940 at the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Flight" title="Museum of Flight">Museum of Flight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, Washington.</li></ul>
