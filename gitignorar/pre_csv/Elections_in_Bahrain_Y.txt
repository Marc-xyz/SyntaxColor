<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(Bahrain)" title="National Assembly (Bahrain)">National Assembly</a> is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicameral" title="Bicameral">bicameral</a> with the lower house, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_(Bahrain)" title="Chamber of Deputies (Bahrain)">Chamber of Deputies</a>, having 40 members elected in single-seat constituencies for a four-year term. The upper house, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shura_Council_(Bahrain)" title="Shura Council (Bahrain)">Shura Council</a>, has 40 members appointed by the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Bahrain" title="King of Bahrain">King of Bahrain</a>, with the stated aim of giving a voice to minority communities and technocratic experts within the legislative process. Supporters of the system refer to long established democracies the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> operating with this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bicameral" title="Bicameral">bicameralism</a> with an appointed upper chamber and an elected lower chamber. Opponents of this system point out that unlike the bicameral systems in the UK and Canada, the Bahraini system gives the unelected upper house equal or more legislative power than the elected lower house, allowing the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Bahrain" title="King of Bahrain">King</a> to control all legislation. Opponents also point out that the current system was imposed unilaterally by the King, violating the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Constitution_of_the_State_of_Bahrain_(1973)" title="wikisource:Constitution of the State of Bahrain (1973)">1973 Constitution</a> and a 2001 signed agreement with the Bahraini opposition.
</p><p>Bahrain’s electoral framework is unfair, with electoral districts deliberately designed to underrepresent Shiites, who form a majority of the citizen population. The government has also allegedly drawn district boundaries to put certain political societies, including leftist and Sunni Islamist groups, at a disadvantage. The government agency responsible for administering elections is not an independent and is headed by the justice minister, a member of the royal family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latest_elections">Latest elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elections_in_Bahrain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Latest elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2010_Bahraini_general_election" title="2010 Bahraini general election">2010 Bahraini general election</a></div>
<p>Forty seats were elected on 24 October 2010, with the runoffs that were needed on 30 October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-almanar_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-almanar-2">[2]</a></sup> A total of 127 candidates nominated for the election, which was also for the election of municipal councils.
</p><p>More thant 318,000 were eligible to vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aljaz_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-aljaz-3">[3]</a></sup> Head of the electoral commission and Justice Minister, Sheikh Khaled bin Ali al-Khalifa, gave an estimate of turnout of "at least 67%," less than the 72% in 2006 and 53.4% in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-afp-4">[4]</a></sup> 127 candidates stood in the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-presstv2ndround_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-presstv2ndround-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Wefaq National Islamic Society won 18 of the 40 seats, 1 more than at the previous election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-almanar_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-almanar-2">[2]</a></sup> Combined Shiite and independent (Sunni) candidates won a majority of seats for the first time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-presstv2ndround_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-presstv2ndround-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The constitution of parliament was altered following the results of the 2010 elections by the <a href="/wiki/2011_Bahraini_parliamentary_by-election" title="2011 Bahraini parliamentary by-election">Bahraini parliamentary by-election</a> in September 2011.
</p><p><br/>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_Parliamentary_election">2002 Parliamentary election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elections_in_Bahrain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2002 Parliamentary election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2002_Bahraini_general_election" title="2002 Bahraini general election">2002 Bahraini general election</a></div>
<p>The elections in 2002 were the first since the late emir Shaikh <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isa_ibn_Salman_al-Khalifa" title="Isa ibn Salman al-Khalifa">Isa ibn Salman al-Khalifa</a> dissolved the first elected parliament in 1975 and abrogated the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bahrain" title="Constitution of Bahrain">1973 Constitution</a> (see: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Bahrain" title="History of Bahrain">History of Bahrain</a>).
</p><p>On 24 October 2002, men and women went to the polls in <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a> to vote in parliamentary elections for the new National Assembly. The <a href="/wiki/Opinion_poll" title="Opinion poll">poll</a> was the first national election in which women had the right to vote and stand as candidates (see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women%27s_political_rights_in_Bahrain" title="Women's political rights in Bahrain">Women's political rights in Bahrain</a>). 
</p><p>The largest opposition party <a href="/wiki/Al_Wefaq" title="Al Wefaq">Al Wefaq</a> called for the restriction of the rights of elected MPs, campaigning for their legislative powers on issues related to the rights of women and children to be given to unelected clerics. In November 2005 Al Wefaq began a campaign to amend the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Bahrain" title="Constitution of Bahrain">Bahrain's constitution</a> to reflect this. 
</p><p>According to <a class="external text" href="http://www.rulers.org" rel="nofollow">Rulers</a> in parliamentary elections at 24 October 2002 the Sunni <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamists" title="Islamists">Islamists</a> and independents won 16 of 40 seats. Turnout was 53.2%. In a second round held on 31 October, the independents won 12 seats and the Islamists 9. The secular representatives or independents secured a total of 21 of the 40 seats. No women candidates were elected.
</p><p>Candidates ran as individuals in the 2002 elections, but were organised in parliament as members of de facto political parties known as 'political societies'.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1973_Parliamentary_elections">1973 Parliamentary elections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elections_in_Bahrain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1973 Parliamentary elections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/1973_Bahraini_general_election" title="1973 Bahraini general election">1973 Bahraini general election</a></div>
<p>The first ever parliamentary elections to be held in <a href="/wiki/Bahrain" title="Bahrain">Bahrain</a> took place in December 1973. Thirty members were elected to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Bahrain" title="National Assembly of Bahrain">National Assembly of Bahrain</a>, by a franchise restricted to male citizens. An additional 14 ministers of the royally-appointed government became unelected <a href="/wiki/Ex_officio_member" title="Ex officio member">ex officio members</a> of National Assembly to make a total of 44 parliamentarians.
</p>
