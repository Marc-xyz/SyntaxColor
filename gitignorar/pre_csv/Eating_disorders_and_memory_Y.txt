	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Eating disorders and memory</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p>Many <b>memory impairments</b> exist as a result from or cause of <b>eating disorders</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>. <i>Eating Disorders (ED)</i> are characterized by abnormal and disturbed <a href="/wiki/Eating" title="Eating">eating</a> patterns that affect the lives of the individuals who worry about their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Body_weight" title="Body weight">weight</a> to the extreme. These abnormal eating patterns involve either inadequate or excessive food intake, affecting the individual's <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a>.
</p><p>In regard to mental health, individuals with eating disorders appear to have <i>memory impairments</i> in <a href="/wiki/Executive_functions" title="Executive functions">executive functioning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spatial_visualization_ability" title="Spatial visualization ability">visual-spatial ability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">divided and sustained attention</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verbal_Behavior" title="Verbal Behavior">verbal functioning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lena_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lena-2">[2]</a></sup> Some memory impairments found in individuals with ED, are due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutrition_disorder" title="Nutrition disorder">nutritional deficiencies</a>, as well as various <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_bias" title="Cognitive bias">cognitive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attentional_bias" title="Attentional bias">attentional biases</a>. <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">Neurobiological</a> differences have been found in individuals with ED compared to healthy individuals, and these differences are reflected in specific memory impairments. There are certain <a href="/wiki/Therapy" title="Therapy">treatments</a> and effects of treatments, aimed at these ED-specific memory impairments. <a href="/wiki/Animal_testing" title="Animal testing">Animal research</a> and areas of future research in relation to ED and memory, are also integral to understanding the effects of ED on memory. There are three particular diagnoses of eating disorders that have been linked to memory impairments: <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">Anorexia Nervosa (AN)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulimia_nervosa" title="Bulimia nervosa">Bulimia Nervosa (BN)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Binge_eating_disorder" title="Binge eating disorder">Binge Eating Disorder (BED)</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memory_Impairments">Memory Impairments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Memory Impairments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Memory_Biases">Memory Biases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Memory Biases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Individuals with eating disorders show increased tendencies to direct their attention towards irregular eating-related thought processing and <a href="/wiki/Attentional_bias" title="Attentional bias">attentional bias</a>, compared to non-ED individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-von_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-von-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shafran_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shafran-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Butow_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Butow-5">[5]</a></sup> Studies have suggested a strong link between eating disorders and information processing such as attention and memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shafran_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Shafran-4">[4]</a></sup> All types of eating disorders (<a href="/wiki/Bulimia_nervosa" title="Bulimia nervosa">bulimia nervosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">anorexia nervosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eating_disorder_not_otherwise_specified" title="Eating disorder not otherwise specified">EDNOS</a>) consistently display attentional biases towards disorder-related stimuli specific to their ED.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-von_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-von-3">[3]</a></sup> Examples of disorder-related stimuli include food, shape, weight and size. This heightened attention to disorder-related stimuli leads to higher levels of encoding, consolidation and retrieval of this information, acting as a potential cause for the mental maintenance of the disorder(s).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teckcan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Teckcan-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Individuals with eating disorders display several memory and attentional biases to food, shape, weight and size.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-King_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-King-7">[7]</a></sup> Specific memory biases include:</b>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Directed-forgetting:</b> individuals with eating disorders, particularly anorexia nervosa, display more difficulty in forgetting information or cues related to body, shape and food than those without eating disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teckcan_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Teckcan-6">[6]</a></sup> This leads to greater availability of such memories, facilitating the maintenance of the eating disorder.</li>
<li><b>Schema-related:</b> display maladaptive perceptions of food, shape, weight and self that lead to obsessive attention on and enhanced memory for these items,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fairburn_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Fairburn-8">[8]</a></sup> leading to maintaining the eating disorder thought and eating behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legenbauer_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Legenbauer-9">[9]</a></sup> Memories for these items are more easily encoded and retrieved compared to other information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williamson_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Williamson-10">[10]</a></sup> Most of the research in this area has been on individuals with anorexia nervosa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legenbauer_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Legenbauer-9">[9]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cued_recall" title="Cued recall">Cued recall</a> tasks, <a href="/wiki/Recognition_memory" title="Recognition memory">recognition tasks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stroop_effect" title="Stroop effect">Stroop task</a> tests are used to study these effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-11">[11]</a></sup> Some studies have shown contradictory results to ED individuals' heightened attention and enhanced memory, however the difference could be attributed to an anxiety-induced response and avoidance behaviour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legenbauer_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Legenbauer-9">[9]</a></sup> This could cause impairments in the individuals' ability to remember the information learned, and suggests that more research needs to be done in this area to better understand the relationship between schema-related biases and ED's.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legenbauer_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Legenbauer-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Selective memory bias:</b> studies have been done on individuals with bulimia nervosa, suggesting selective memory bias exists for positive and negative weight-related items compared to emotional items.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-12">[12]</a></sup> Biases towards food-related items were also found, a common finding in individuals with depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hunt_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hunt-12">[12]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Explicit_memory">Explicit memory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Explicit memory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Anorexia nervosa (AN)</b>
</p><p>Patients with AN show a strong <a href="/wiki/Explicit_memory" title="Explicit memory">explicit memory</a> bias towards anorexia-related words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hermans_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hermans-13">[13]</a></sup> In one study, participants (AN group compared to a control group) were presented with a list of words divided into four categories: positive, negative, neutral and anorexia-related.  They were then tested explicitly with cued recall and it was found that the AN participants better favored the anorexia-related words, showing a <i>schema-related memory bias</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hermans_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hermans-13">[13]</a></sup> Participants were also tested implicitly with word stem completion tests, but no implicit bias was found.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hermans_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hermans-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In another study, AN participants were found to have less ability to concentrate in the presence of explicit distractors, as well as to have a conscious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognitive" title="Cognitive">cognitive</a> bias towards illness-related words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickson_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-14">[14]</a></sup> These explicit biases were associated with longer duration of the illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dickson_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dickson-14">[14]</a></sup> A different study characterized AN patients as having trouble integrating positive and negative experiences and that the length of the illness also affected these symptoms and reinforced these impairments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The results of these studies suggest that there are clear differences in the explicit cognitive processing of stimuli between AN individuals and healthy controls and that length of the illness can affect the extent of these memory biases.
</p><p>A different study showed that currently ill AN patients had problems with immediate and delayed verbal recall; these disadvantages were also found in weight-restored AN individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nandrino_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nandrino-16">[16]</a></sup> There was no difference between healthy controls and AN patients in <a href="/wiki/Working_memory" title="Working memory">working memory</a>, just memory function.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nandrino_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nandrino-16">[16]</a></sup> This study demonstrates that there are not only memory biases found in AN individuals, but memory impairments as well.
</p><p><i>Autobiographical memory</i> deficits have been found in individuals with AN. One study found that AN patients with a history of sexual abuse had impairments in their <a href="/wiki/Autobiographical_memory" title="Autobiographical memory">autobiographical memory</a> characterized by their increased general memory recall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalgleish_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dalgleish-17">[17]</a></sup> Another study found that anorexic patients are characterized by an over-generalization of both positive and negative autobiographical memories, which positively correlates with the duration of the illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nandrino_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nandrino-16">[16]</a></sup> The impairment of both positive and negative memories suggests a general impairment in the access to emotional memories, therefore anorexic patients are more prone to suppress or control not only negative but also positive affect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nandrino_16-3"><a href="#cite_note-Nandrino-16">[16]</a></sup> One hypothesis suggests that these more general memories are what allow these patients to reduce the impact of a negative event.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Raes_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Raes-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Bulimia nervosa (BN)</b>
</p><p>In one study, participants (BN and healthy controls) were exposed to television commercials that were neutral, food-related or body-related.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legenbauer_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-Legenbauer-9">[9]</a></sup> Recall and recognition tests were carried out to test for an explicit memory bias. When compared to healthy controls, BN patients had less recall and recognition for body-related stimuli.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Legenbauer_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-Legenbauer-9">[9]</a></sup> This suggests that BN individuals avoid encoding/processing stimuli related to body image and have a <i>selective memory bias.</i>
</p><p><b>Binge eating disorder (BED)</b>
</p><p>Obese individuals with binge eating disorder have been compared with obese controls to see if there are different explicit memory biases between these two groups of people.  It was found that both groups showed a bias towards negative words, but individuals with BED retrieved positive words less often.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Svaldi,_J._2010_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Svaldi,_J._2010-19">[19]</a></sup> This demonstrates an explicit memory bias in which individuals with BED avoid encoding or pay less attention to positive words and focus their conscious attention almost exclusively on negative words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Svaldi,_J._2010_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Svaldi,_J._2010-19">[19]</a></sup> This is similar to the <i>selective memory bias</i> mentioned above.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implicit_memory">Implicit memory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Implicit memory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was once thought that individuals with eating disorders had different <a href="/wiki/Implicit_memory" title="Implicit memory">implicit memory</a> biases and attitudes towards food, depending on the type of eating disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huijding_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huijding-20">[20]</a></sup> BED was associated with positive evaluation of food and anorexia and bulimia were associated with negative evaluation of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huijding_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huijding-20">[20]</a></sup> This turns out to not be the case.  There were no implicit differences in affective attitudes towards foods between high and low-restraint eaters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huijding_20-2"><a href="#cite_note-Huijding-20">[20]</a></sup> This suggests that regardless of the type of eating disorder, individuals with eating disorders view food in similar ways and have similar implicit attitudes towards food.
</p><p>Focusing on obesity, it has been found that obese individuals have more negative attitudes towards high-fat foods than a normal weight control group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huijding_20-3"><a href="#cite_note-Huijding-20">[20]</a></sup> It has also been found that children, particularly obese children, were faster at pushing a positive key than a negative key for food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huijding_20-4"><a href="#cite_note-Huijding-20">[20]</a></sup> These different attitudes towards food at different ages could represent different stages in development of obesity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huijding_20-5"><a href="#cite_note-Huijding-20">[20]</a></sup> Future research could be done to explore these effects found in obesity and determine if similar effects are seen in individuals with binge eating disorder and perhaps also in individuals with anorexia and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulimia" title="Bulimia">bulimia</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Reflected_sadness.jpg"><img alt="Sad individual" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="754" data-file-width="527" decoding="async" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Reflected_sadness.jpg/220px-Reflected_sadness.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Reflected_sadness.jpg/330px-Reflected_sadness.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Reflected_sadness.jpg/440px-Reflected_sadness.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Reflected_sadness.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Individuals with eating disorders have been shown to develop negative affect in social situations, and therefore learn to expect others to be unavailable and insensitive to their needs.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study on the effects of <b>priming</b> combined <a href="/wiki/Event-related_potential" title="Event-related potential">event-related potential (ERP)</a> and behavioural reactions, and investigated explicit and implicit associations between shape, weight, and self-evaluations. This was done by means of shape/weight related <a href="/wiki/Priming_(psychology)" title="Priming (psychology)">priming</a> sentences and target words. ERP, <a href="/wiki/Mental_chronometry" title="Mental chronometry">reaction times</a>, and subject ratings were collected and priming effects were analyzed. Results showed that there were stronger affective priming effects in patients with AN and BN compared to healthy controls, showing that eating disorder (ED) patients associate shape/weight concerns not only with appearance, but also nonappearance-related self-evaluation domains of <a href="/wiki/Interpersonal_relationship" title="Interpersonal relationship">interpersonal relationships</a> and also with achievement and performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blechert_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blechert-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Social cognition</b> is the understanding and action in interpersonal situations, and include cognitive processes involved in how people <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perceive</a> and interpret information about themselves, others, and social situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Servi_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Servi-22">[22]</a></sup> The dysfunction of <a href="/wiki/Social_control" title="Social control">social control</a> may play a role in eating disorders. Women with ED have been shown to have lower levels of negative <a href="/wiki/Affect_(psychology)" title="Affect (psychology)">affect</a> <a href="/wiki/Attribution_(psychology)" title="Attribution (psychology)">attribution</a> compared to healthy controls, which suggests that they learn to expect others to be unavailable and insensitive to their needs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothschild-Yakar_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild-Yakar-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition, these patients were less successful at correctly encoding <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">cause-effect</a> relations in a <a href="/wiki/Social_environment" title="Social environment">social contexts</a> and it has been suggested that their capacity to mentalize experiences is impaired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothschild-Yakar_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rothschild-Yakar-23">[23]</a></sup> In addition to impairments in social cognition, it has been found that individuals with ED have an inability to recognize, label, and respond to different emotional states, and are impaired in visual recognition tasks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canetti_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Canetti-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zonnevijlle-Bender2001_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zonnevijlle-Bender2001-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zonnevijlle-Bender2004_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zonnevijlle-Bender2004-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kucharska-Pietura_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kucharska-Pietura-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Dementia</b> is a disorder characterized by multiple deficits in cognition, including memory impairments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dementia_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dementia-28">[28]</a></sup> Patients with various forms of <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a> have impairments in their <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">activities of daily living</a> including eating, and eating disorders have been found in patients with dementia. Patients with <a href="/wiki/Frontotemporal_dementia" title="Frontotemporal dementia">frontotemporal dementia (FTD)</a> tend to have an eating disorder where they have <a href="/wiki/Food_craving" title="Food craving">food cravings</a> and difficulty controlling the amount and type of food eaten but their memory and spatial functioning is not affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kertesz_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kertesz-29">[29]</a></sup> Meanwhile, patients with <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's Disease (AD)</a>, do not have this impairment, but their memory and spatial loss is negatively affected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kertesz_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kertesz-29">[29]</a></sup> Similar findings were shown where patients with <a href="/wiki/Frontotemporal_dementia" title="Frontotemporal dementia">fronto variant-frontotemporal dementia (fvFTD)</a> show more severe and frequent symptoms of eating disorders than patients with AD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennera_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jennera-30">[30]</a></sup> ED in patients with dementia have been tracked back to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lesions" title="Lesions">lesions</a> in the frontal subcortical circuits including the anterior cingulate circuit, and data suggests that ED seem to be distinctive features of behavioural syndromes in groups of patients with fvFTD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jennera_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jennera-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurobiology">Neurobiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Neurobiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">Neurobiological</a> differences have been found between individuals with eating disorders (ED) and healthy individuals. These differences are reflected in memory abilities and capabilities. Some of the main neurobiological differences are highlighted below:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hippocampus-mri.jpg"><img alt="MRI scan of brain" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="510" data-file-width="510" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hippocampus-mri.jpg/220px-Hippocampus-mri.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hippocampus-mri.jpg/330px-Hippocampus-mri.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Hippocampus-mri.jpg/440px-Hippocampus-mri.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hippocampus-mri.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An MRI scan with the hippocampus indicated in a coronal, lateral and horizontal view.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anorexia_Nervosa">Anorexia Nervosa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Anorexia Nervosa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Imbalances found in certain <a href="/wiki/5-HT_receptor" title="5-HT receptor">serotonin receptor</a> activity in cortical association regions, including the <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe" title="Frontal lobe">frontal lobes</a>, are found in individuals with AN and may be the cause of impairment in their <a href="/wiki/Working_memory" title="Working memory">working memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Concentration" title="Concentration">concentration</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martin-Ruiz_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Martin-Ruiz-32">[32]</a></sup> In addition, the ability of individuals with AN to activate remote <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learn</a> new information, plan ahead, regulate actions according to environmental <a href="/wiki/Stimulus_(psychology)" title="Stimulus (psychology)">stimuli</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_flexibility" title="Cognitive flexibility">shift behavioural sets</a> appropriately are all implicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye-31">[31]</a></sup> Some individuals with anorexia nervosa (AN) have an inability to change their <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_flexibility" title="Cognitive flexibility">pattern response behaviours</a>, which has been linked to disturbances found in the <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_cortex" title="Cerebral cortex">cortical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brodmann_area_25" title="Brodmann area 25">subgenual</a> <a href="/wiki/Cingulate_cortex" title="Cingulate cortex">cingulate - mesial</a> <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal</a> pathways of these individuals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>The reduced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_flow" title="Blood flow">blood flow</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Limbic_system" title="Limbic system">limbic system</a> of individuals with AN is what mostly accounts for their impairment in <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive functioning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lask_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lask-33">[33]</a></sup> More specifically, the set of structures in the limbic system including the <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobes</a> and adjacent structures like the <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amygdala" title="Amygdala">amygdala</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hippocampus" title="Hippocampus">hippocampus</a> are important in memory as well as <a href="/wiki/Emotion" title="Emotion">emotion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Appetite" title="Appetite">appetite</a> regulation, motivation, and <a href="/wiki/Perception" title="Perception">perception</a>, and are therefore implicated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lask_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lask-33">[33]</a></sup> Reduced cerebral blood flow to these areas have also been associated with impairments in complex <a href="/wiki/Visual_memory" title="Visual memory">visual memory</a>, enhanced <a href="/wiki/Information_processing" title="Information processing">information processing</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visuospatial" title="Visuospatial">visuospatial</a> ability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jáuregui-Lobera_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jáuregui-Lobera-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>Individuals with AN have been reported to have prolonged exposure to high levels of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corticosteroids" title="Corticosteroids">corticosteroids</a>, a class of chemicals involved in things such as <a href="/wiki/Fight-or-flight_response" title="Fight-or-flight response">stress</a> and behaviour,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirschbaum_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kirschbaum-35">[35]</a></sup> and prolonged exposure to corticosteroids has been associated with impairments in memory and <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">learning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kirschbaum_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kirschbaum-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lupien_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lupien-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newcomer_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-Newcomer-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolkowitz_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wolkowitz-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-Young-39">[39]</a></sup> The hippocampus is an area of the brain that is dense with corticosteroid receptors, and therefore may be what is mediating these impairments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lupien_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lupien-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newcomer_37-1"><a href="#cite_note-Newcomer-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wolkowitz_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wolkowitz-38">[38]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Young_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-Young-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Diamond_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Diamond-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McEwan_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-McEwan-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pavlides_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pavlides-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulimia_Nervosa">Bulimia Nervosa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bulimia Nervosa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Activation of the medial prefrontal cortex has been shown in some studies to reflect <a href="/wiki/Schema_(psychology)#Self-schemata" title="Schema (psychology)">self-schemata</a> evaluation of relevant information, and could be used to investigate body image representations in individuals with bulimia nervosa (BN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spangler_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spangler-43">[43]</a></sup> In addition, increased activation in brain areas associated with <a href="/wiki/Information_processing_theory" title="Information processing theory">information processing</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">dorsal and anterior medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)</a>, adjacent areas of the cingulate cortex, and the posterior cingulate and <a href="/wiki/Precuneus" title="Precuneus">precuneus</a> have been implicated in individuals with BN,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spangler_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spangler-43">[43]</a></sup> meaning that the working memory used to actively manipulate information in these individuals is affected.
</p><p>Areas of the brain such as the <a href="/wiki/Insular_cortex" title="Insular cortex">insula</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schienle_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schienle-44">[44]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Anterior_cingulate_cortex" title="Anterior cingulate cortex">anterior circulate cortex (ACC)</a> have been found to be disturbed in individuals with BN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penas-Lledo_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Penas-Lledo-45">[45]</a></sup> These areas are involved in self-regulation as well as <a href="/wiki/Executive_functions" title="Executive functions">executive control</a> which controls cognitive processes including working memory, and may be the reason for impairment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Binge_Eating">Binge Eating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Binge Eating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An increase in <a href="/wiki/Dopamine" title="Dopamine">dopamine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Caudate_nucleus" title="Caudate nucleus">caudate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Putamen" title="Putamen">putamen</a> have been found in binge eaters,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wang_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-47">[47]</a></sup> and studies have found a decrease in a particular serotonin transporter (5-HT) in binge eaters compared to controls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuikka_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kuikka-48">[48]</a></sup> Both the caudate nucleus and putamen make up the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorsal_striatum" title="Dorsal striatum">dorsal striatum</a> and are important as part of the brain's memory system. Dopamine is required to allow these structures to perform properly and thus this is affected in individuals with binge eating disorders, however the exact mechanism is unknown.
</p><p>A dysregulation of the ventral limbic circuit has been found in individuals who binge eat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-46">[46]</a></sup> The ventral limbic circuit is important in the regulation of feeding behaviour and includes the amygdalae, insula, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ventral_striatum" title="Ventral striatum">ventral striatum</a>, ventral regions of the anterior ACC, and <a href="/wiki/Orbitofrontal_cortex" title="Orbitofrontal cortex">orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marsh_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Marsh-46">[46]</a></sup> A stronger activation of the OFC has been found in patients who binge eat compared to normal weight controls, when viewing pictures of food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schienle_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schienle-44">[44]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Woolley_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-Woolley-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditioning">Conditioning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Conditioning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The regulation of <a href="/wiki/Eating" title="Eating">eating</a> is controlled by areas of the brain involved in <a href="/wiki/Operant_conditioning" title="Operant conditioning">behaviour reinforcement</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Reward_system" title="Reward system">rewarding</a> qualities of <a href="/wiki/Food" title="Food">food</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Taste" title="Taste">taste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olfaction" title="Olfaction">smell</a>, activate regions of the brain that are impaired in patients with anorexia nervosa (AN) and bulimia nervosa (BN), including the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), anteromedial temporal, and the insula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rolls_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rolls-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O’Doherty_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-O’Doherty-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>Normally, eating is pleasant when an individual is <a href="/wiki/Hunger" title="Hunger">hungry</a> and less pleasant when an individual is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satiety" title="Satiety">full</a>. Neuronal activation in the OFC decreases when an individual is full, however there is a disturbance in this pathway in individuals with AN and BN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye-31">[31]</a></sup> Thus, patients with AN have little response to food or a quick response to being full, and patients with BN have an exaggerated response to food or a decrease in feeling full.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kaye_31-4"><a href="#cite_note-Kaye-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Areas of the cortex receive signals of being full by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gut_(anatomy)" title="Gut (anatomy)">gut</a> through subcortical mechanisms including the <a href="/wiki/Thalamus" title="Thalamus">thalamus</a> which relays information from associated systems in the hypothalamus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kelley_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kelley-52">[52]</a></sup> The hypothalamus has projections into the <a href="/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens" title="Nucleus accumbens">nucleus accumbens (NAcc)</a>, which is involved in the reward system of feeding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yoshida_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yoshida-53">[53]</a></sup> Increased <a href="/wiki/Extracellular" title="Extracellular">extracellular</a> <a href="/wiki/Acetylcholine" title="Acetylcholine">acetylcholine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Interneuron" title="Interneuron">interneurons</a> in the NAcc have been found to be associated with the discontinuation of eating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark1991_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mark1991-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark1992_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mark1992-55">[55]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mark1995_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mark1995-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rada_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rada-57">[57]</a></sup> and the dysregulation of this mechanism has been found in the food-reward system in eating disorder studies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Body_Image">Body Image</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Body Image">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Neurons located in different structures of the medial temporal lobe are what cause the transformation from an <a href="/wiki/Egocentrism" title="Egocentrism">egocentric</a> to an allocentric representation in space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byrne2008_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Byrne2008-58">[58]</a></sup> The hippocampus generates allocentric representations for <a href="/wiki/Long-term_memory" title="Long-term memory">long-term memory</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Byrne2007_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-Byrne2007-59">[59]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal cortex</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrosplenial_region" title="Retrosplenial region">retrosplenial cortex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Entorhinal_cortex" title="Entorhinal cortex">entorhinal cortex</a>, and hippocampus are part of a network that processes allocentric spatial information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup> The lateral entorhinal cortex carries nonspatial information from the <a href="/wiki/Perirhinal_cortex" title="Perirhinal cortex">perirhinal cortex</a> to the dorsal hippocampus where it is then combined with the medial entorhinal cortex to create object-place or event-place representations in the hippocampus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hargreaves_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hargreaves-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p><p>Impairment of the transformation from an egocentric to allocentric representation of oneself is what is thought to be behind the origin of obesity and eating disorders,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup> where the egocentric perception-driven experience of an individual's real body image cannot change the allocentric memory-driven experience of a negative body, and an individual is therefore “locked” in an allocentric view of a negative representation of their body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2010_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2010-62">[62]</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Stress_(biology)" title="Stress (biology)">stress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chronic_stress" title="Chronic stress">chronic stress</a> can cause damage to the hippocampus through the overwhelming activity of the amygdala on the hippocampus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_Deficiencies_and_Memory">Nutritional Deficiencies and Memory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nutritional Deficiencies and Memory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nutrition" title="Nutrition">Nutrition</a> has proven to show effects on cognitive abilities and spatial memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The brain's neuronal and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuroglia" title="Neuroglia">glial cells</a> require sufficient nutrients for energy to perform important cognitive functions such as attention and memory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-65">[65]</a></sup> and without a steady supply of nutrients including <i>glucose, fatty acids, and vitamins B1 (Thiamine)</i>, neural activation required for memory functions becomes impaired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-Spencer-65">[65]</a></sup> Individuals suffering from eating disorders often lack the ability to consume the required amount of these nutrients, resulting in notable cognitive impairments such as those necessary for proper memory functioning.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Four_lobes_animation_small3.gif"><img alt="Rotating 3D image of regions of the brain" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="150" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/42/Four_lobes_animation_small3.gif" width="150"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Four_lobes_animation_small3.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Areas of the brain effected by glucose (frontal lobe (pink), temporal lobe (yellow) and parietal lobe (blue)).</div></div></div>
<p><b>Glucose</b> is the preferred energy source for the brain, accounting for 25% of the body's glucose consumption, despite being only 2% of the body's total weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squire_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-Squire-66">[66]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lieberman_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lieberman-67">[67]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">Glucose</a>, along with <a href="/wiki/Serotonin" title="Serotonin">serotonin</a>, have been found to have significant effects within the <a href="/wiki/Cingulate_cortex" title="Cingulate cortex">cingulate cortex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frontal_lobe" title="Frontal lobe">frontal lobe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal lobe</a> regions of the brain, including in those with anorexia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frank_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frank-68">[68]</a></sup> Individuals with eating disorders such as bulimia and anorexia show lower neural metabolism of glucose, possibly due to neural consequences of the disorder and/or due to heightened anxiety or depression.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delvenne1999_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delvenne1999-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delvenne1995_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Delvenne1995-70">[70]</a></sup> Studies have indicated the importance of glucose on memory, showing that reduced levels of glucose in the brain impair an individual's ability to retrieve memories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Benton_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Benton-71">[71]</a></sup> Most evidence suggests that severe negative impairments are due to long term, prolonged glucose deprivation or restriction, such as those seen in individuals affected by eating disorders,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bryan_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bryan-72">[72]</a></sup> however effects have been studied on a more short-term basis with negative memory impairments seen in individuals who consumed breakfast compared to those who did not.
</p><p>The brain contains high concentrations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipids" title="Lipids">lipids</a> than any other organ in the body with the most prominent type being <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyunsaturated_fatty_acids" title="Polyunsaturated fatty acids">polyunsaturated fatty acids</a> (PUFAs), such as <a href="/wiki/Omega-3_fatty_acid" title="Omega-3 fatty acid">omega-3 fatty acids (ω-3)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrichs_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrichs-73">[73]</a></sup> Evidence that shows that low-fat intake occurs during weight loss in adolescent girls with eating disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Swenne_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-Swenne-74">[74]</a></sup> Dietary <b>ω-3 fatty acids</b> play a particularly important role in prevention of neuropsychiatric disorders such as depression and Alzheimer's disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrichs_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrichs-73">[73]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bourre_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bourre-75">[75]</a></sup> Studies using animal models have expressed that ω-3 deficiencies result in diminished synaptic plasticity, impaired learning, memory and emotional coping performance later in life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heinrichs_73-2"><a href="#cite_note-Heinrichs-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p><p>All <b>B vitamins</b> play a part in helping the nervous system function properly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Thiamine" title="Thiamine">Vitamin B1 (thiamine)</a> is an important B vitamin and is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korsakoff%27s_syndrome" title="Korsakoff's syndrome">Korsakoff's syndrome</a>, a neurological disorder due to the lack of vitamin B1. Chronic alcohol abuse is the number one cause of this syndrome, but unfortunately, even though supplementation may improve muscle co-ordination, it usually cannot reverse memory loss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Case studies have reported that the co-morbidity between eating disorders and substance abuse as a significant health issue for women, and the subgroup of patients with AN who also misuse alcohol are at particular risk of developing Korsakoff's syndrome.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saad_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saad-78">[78]</a></sup> In other studies regarding thiamine deficiency, impairments in <a href="/wiki/Spatial_memory" title="Spatial memory">spatial memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_amnesia" title="Retrograde amnesia">retrograde amnesia</a>, episodic memory and working memory have all been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carvalho_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Carvalho-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitel_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pitel-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Animal_Models_Used_in_Eating_Disorders">Animal Models Used in Eating Disorders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Animal Models Used in Eating Disorders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animal_models" title="Animal models">Animal models</a> have contributed a fair amount to the current understanding of eating disorders and obesity, in different ways and to different extents; one of the main reasons being the difference in <a href="/wiki/Pathophysiology" title="Pathophysiology">pathophysiology</a> of these disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup> The one specific feature of eating disorders not shared with animal behavior, is the personal choice to curtail food intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup> The suitability and limitations of animal models in studies regarding human eating disorders have been discussed in various reviews. Several various types of animal models have been described which include: etiologic, isomorphic, mechanistic and predictive models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smith-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anorexia_Nervosa_2">Anorexia Nervosa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Anorexia Nervosa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The activity-based anorexia model has been one of the most suitable animal models when studying anorexia nervosa (AN).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Routtenberg_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Routtenberg-83">[83]</a></sup> The important behavioral aspects of AN, the drive for activity, the restricted food intake during hunger, and other physiological consequences of malnutrition, are all reproduced in this model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pirke_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pirke-84">[84]</a></sup> The “activity/stress” model produces starvation-induced <a href="/wiki/Immunodeficiency" title="Immunodeficiency">immunodeficiency</a> and various complications not observed in individuals with AN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup> "Separation" models involve physical separation as a stressor to induce a depression-like condition; this includes decreased feeding, weight loss, and various cognitive changes . Studies with animal models simulating loss of hunger are not well suited to replicate AN because they are essentially based on the assumption of loss of appetite.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-3"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulimia_Nervosa_2">Bulimia Nervosa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Bulimia Nervosa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two major factors found to contribute to binge eating in bulimia nervosa (BN) patients are: stress and negative emotions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-4"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup> One model of BN produces stress-induced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hyperphagia" title="Hyperphagia">hyperphagia</a>, where rats go through periods of restricted food and then are allowed free access to food; this mimics the intermittent self-imposed fasting and yielding to food of BN patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-5"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sham-feeding" title="Sham-feeding">Sham-feeding</a> rat models have been used to present the defect in the satiety mechanisms in BN due to vomiting or purging after food intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davis_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davis-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mook_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mook-86">[86]</a></sup> It is known that multiple mechanisms regulate meal patterning, although in order to study them, precise consumption patterns of BN patients must be known;  these intake patterns are still currently being studied.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kissileff_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kissileff-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other models of binge eating have used various combinations of stress limited access to optional foods, and/or restriction/refeeding cycles, along with scheduled eating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corwin_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corwin-88">[88]</a></sup> These specific models have been able to address the consumptive side of BN, and have proven to be useful for testing drug effects on intake behavior. Due to the pharmacological response differences between rodents and humans, new drug development has been concentrating on drug testing specifically in human subjects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naessen_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-Naessen-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Binge_Eating_Disorder">Binge Eating Disorder</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Binge Eating Disorder">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
The development of animal models for binge eating has been necessary, because the mechanisms underlying the physiological and neural effects are not very well understood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corwin,_R.L.,_Buda-Levin_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Corwin,_R.L.,_Buda-Levin-90">[90]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yanovski_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yanovski-91">[91]</a></sup> Since the emotional aspects such as distress and loss of control prove difficult to model in animals, the central feature of the binge eating disorder, was attempted to be mimicked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-6"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sham-feeding" title="Sham-feeding">Sham-feeding</a> was the most prominent model used to study BED.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Corwin,_R.L.,_Buda-Levin_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-Corwin,_R.L.,_Buda-Levin-90">[90]</a></sup> </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rat_diabetic.jpg"><img alt="Picture of an overweigh rat" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Rat_diabetic.jpg/220px-Rat_diabetic.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Rat_diabetic.jpg/330px-Rat_diabetic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Rat_diabetic.jpg/440px-Rat_diabetic.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rat_diabetic.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Zucker rat which has developed diabetes due to a genetic disorder that causes obesity.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Obesity">Obesity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Obesity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Animal models have been able to provide key knowledge of the central and peripheral biological pathways regulating body weight and energy balance. They have proven effective and critical in examining environmental influences, along with identifying therapeutic targets and treatments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-7"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup>  Animal models have been able to determine that malnutrition, maternal stress, and insulin injections can predispose offspring to adult obesity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-92">[92]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1996_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jones1996-93">[93]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levin_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-Levin-94">[94]</a></sup> Previous studies have identified the effect of the adipocyte hormone leptin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freidman_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Freidman-95">[95]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zhang-96">[96]</a></sup> reveal that leptin treatments reverse obesity in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knockout_mice" title="Knockout mice">knockout mice</a> (ob/ob),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halaas_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-Halaas-97">[97]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelleymounter_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelleymounter-98">[98]</a></sup> and that diabetic (db/db) and Zucker fatty (fa/fa) rats (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zucker_rat" title="Zucker rat">Zucker rat</a>) display similar obesity phenotypes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elmquist_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elmquist-99">[99]</a></sup> Work on characterizing rodent obesity syndromes spontaneously arising from single gene mutations has been critical in obesity research. By 2007 there were 10 spontaneously single gene mutations characterized which deliberate an obesity phenotype.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Speakman_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Speakman-100">[100]</a></sup> Currently, many investigators are using animal models in order to analyze genetic, neural, and physiological influences on susceptibility to diet-induced obesity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones_92-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jones-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatments">Treatments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Treatments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neurocognitive_Reserve">Neurocognitive Reserve</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Neurocognitive Reserve">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">Cognition</a> in individuals with anorexia nervosa (AN) has been shown to improve after <a href="/wiki/Therapy" title="Therapy">treatment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laurer_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-Laurer-101">[101]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moser_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moser-102">[102]</a></sup> It has been found that age, education, <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Body_mass_index" title="Body mass index">body mass index (BMI)</a>, duration of illness, and length of hospitalization were not related to cognitive functioning during hospitalization and neuropsychological improvement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keifer_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-Keifer-103">[103]</a></sup> One of the predictors of cognitive impairment in individuals with AN is their <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_reserve" title="Cognitive reserve">cognitive reserve</a>, where the greater cognitive reserve leads to more <a href="/wiki/Psychological_resilience" title="Psychological resilience">resiliency</a> to cognitive impairment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keifer_103-1"><a href="#cite_note-Keifer-103">[103]</a></sup> The level of cognitive reserve predicts improvement in neuropsychological function including <a href="/wiki/Verbal_memory" title="Verbal memory">verbal memory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> fluency, basic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_(sense)" title="Hearing (sense)">auditory</a> <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">attention</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visuospatial" title="Visuospatial">visuospatial</a> construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keifer_103-2"><a href="#cite_note-Keifer-103">[103]</a></sup> In addition, the level of cognitive reserve has been found to be associated with different AN <a href="/wiki/Prognosis" title="Prognosis">prognosis</a> and therefore treatment may be altered based on the cognitive reserve, where individuals who may experience more severe neurospsychological deficits may need more rehearsal and <a href="/wiki/Practice_(learning_method)" title="Practice (learning method)">repeated practice</a> of skills during treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Keifer_103-3"><a href="#cite_note-Keifer-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="COMET">COMET</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: COMET">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Low <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">self-esteem</a> is considered to be an important aspect of various eating disorders (ED).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Implicit_memory" title="Implicit memory">Implicit</a> and opinions that refer to oneself are the main issues of low self-esteem, and competitive memory training (COMET) was developed as a treatment method for individuals with ED in order to target these opinions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-1"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup> COMET is aimed at making the <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> that patients already know more easily <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_retrieval" title="Knowledge retrieval">retrieved</a> from <a href="/wiki/Long-term_memory" title="Long-term memory">long-term memory</a> by strengthening the retrieval of <a href="/wiki/Mental_representation" title="Mental representation">functional representations</a> that are in competition with <a href="/wiki/Mental_representation" title="Mental representation">dysfunctional representations</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-2"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p><p>COMET emphasizes positive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Memories" title="Memories">memories</a> by using <a href="/wiki/Imagery" title="Imagery">imagery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Posture_(psychology)" title="Posture (psychology)">posture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Facial_expression" title="Facial expression">facial expressions</a>, self-verbalizations, and <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-3"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup> COMET stimulates emotional saliency of functional <a href="/wiki/Self-concept" title="Self-concept">self-concepts</a> by writing stories about scenes where positive characteristics are in action and repeatedly verbalized positive self-statements are connected to the scenes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lange_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lange-105">[105]</a></sup> Other techniques of COMET used to promote emotional salience include the deliberate movements of posture, facial expressions and imagery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-4"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup> Music that is selected by patients with ED is used to stimulate positive <a href="/wiki/Mood_(psychology)" title="Mood (psychology)">mood</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krumhansl_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-Krumhansl-106">[106]</a></sup> COMET promotes higher and competitive retrieval by activating emotionally enhanced positive self-knowledge repeatedly, and then this knowledge is associated with situations and <a href="/wiki/Sensory_cue" title="Sensory cue">cues</a> that trigger these dysfunctional negative self-concepts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-5"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup>
Overall, studies have confirmed that COMET, in addition to regular therapy, enhances self-esteem in individuals with eating disorders and low self-esteem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korrelboom_104-6"><a href="#cite_note-Korrelboom-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Virtual_Reality">Virtual Reality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Virtual Reality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A reference frame is a way someone can represent their location in space, and evidence has shown that our spatial experience involves the combination of our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensory_system" title="Sensory system">sensory inputs</a> from two specific reference frames including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egocentric" title="Egocentric">egocentric</a> and allocentric.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Galati_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-Galati-107">[107]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longo_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-Longo-108">[108]</a></sup> The ability to represent and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recollection" title="Recollection">recall</a> objects, including our own body, changes according to our frame of references, where an egocentric stance represents objects relative to ourselves while an allocentric stance represents objects independent of ourselves.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frith_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frith-109">[109]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Juurmaa_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-Juurmaa-110">[110]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amorim_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Amorim-111">[111]</a></sup> These reference frames influence how memories are stored and retrieved where in the egocentric frame, an individual sees an event from their own perspective while in the allocentric frame, an individual sees themselves engaged in the event as an observer would.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Amorim_111-1"><a href="#cite_note-Amorim-111">[111]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robinson_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robinson-112">[112]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mace_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mace-113">[113]</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>, the body is considered a personal <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a> where slenderness is associated with happiness, success and social acceptability and being overweight is associated with <a href="/wiki/Laziness" title="Laziness">laziness</a>, lack of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Self_control" title="Self control">willpower</a> and being out of control.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grogan_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-Grogan-114">[114]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Social_influence" title="Social influence">Social influence</a> may therefore explain the progression from the “locked” allocentric negative body image, to obesity or eating disorders (ED) because the <a href="/wiki/Media_(communication)" title="Media (communication)">media</a> and <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a> both promote <a href="/wiki/Dieting" title="Dieting">diet</a> and controlled eating as methods to improve <a href="/wiki/Body_image" title="Body image">body-image</a> satisfaction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-3"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup> Individuals with ED are not able to use their sensory inputs to update allocentric representations of the body, therefore they hate their body even after significant weight loss and continue to attempt to improve it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-4"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Virtual_reality" title="Virtual reality">Virtual reality (VR)</a> has been aimed at fixing this issue by helping to change the experience of the body<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva1998_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riva1998-115">[115]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alcañiz_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-Alcañiz-116">[116]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lenggenhager_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lenggenhager-117">[117]</a></sup> and to improve body image in patients suffering from ED or obesity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2006_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2006-118">[118]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2001_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2001-119">[119]</a></sup> In VR sessions, patients enter a virtual environment that causes them to face critical situations and are then helped to develop specific strategies to cope and avoid these situations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-5"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup> Side effects of VR include <a href="/wiki/Nausea" title="Nausea">nausea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dizziness" title="Dizziness">dizziness</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frith_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frith-109">[109]</a></sup> Overall, good results were found during treatment with experiential <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_therapy" title="Cognitive therapy">cognitive therapy</a> that also included specific body-image protocol based on VR in obese and binge-eating patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riva2011_60-6"><a href="#cite_note-Riva2011-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_Research">Future Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eating_disorders_and_memory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Future Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Further research should be carried out on implicit memory and its effects and biases on eating disorders.  There are conflicting results from different studies which should be resolved.  Future studies can focus on correcting these implicit and explicit biases in patients with eating disorders, and see if the way these individuals affectively view and eat food can be changed by teaching these individuals how to consciously change their own thought patterns.
</p><p>More research is also needed to study the specific effects of memory and attentional biases in various eating disorders. Most of the current research has been done in individuals with AN, however to gain a more comprehensive understanding of how memory impairments effect individuals with EDs, BN and EDNOS must also be looked at, and differences and similarities in memory impairments should be compared across eating disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teckcan_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Teckcan-6">[6]</a></sup> Currently many animal models have provided valuable information in regard to eating disorders, but because of the cognitive and physical differences, differences do still arise. Using rodents to examine genetic etiological factors for eating disorders must patiently await breakthroughs in human studies of the same disorders.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casper_81-8"><a href="#cite_note-Casper-81">[81]</a></sup>
</p><p>It would be beneficial to continue research on the COMET and virtual reality treatment methods. COMET, so far, seems to be an effective intervention and the results from studies on the intervention method show that further investigation will be useful. Virtual reality is another treatment tool that has been shown to help modify locked negative body image, and good results were obtained from this study. Improvements can be made for both of these studies including better control of certain variables. The impaired memory systems have been found to be the cause of, or drive these eating disorders. If treatment methods are developed that are able to target these impaired memory systems, it could help not only to individuals in critical state but also as an intervention to individuals in the early stages to prevent their eating disorders from getting worse.
</p><p>Ultimately using the present research that is available, it is important to conduct future research that expands and elaborates on what has already been discovered to find treatment options for each illness.
</p>
