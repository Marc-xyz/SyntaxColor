<p>The <b>New York State Department of Transportation</b> (<b>NYSDOT</b>) is the department of the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_New_York_(state)" title="Government of New York (state)">New York state government</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> responsible for the development and operation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Highways" title="Highways">highways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railroads</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mass_transit" title="Mass transit">mass transit</a> systems, <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">ports</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterways" title="Waterways">waterways</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a> facilities in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.
</p><p>This transportation network includes:
</p>
<ul><li>A state and local <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highway</a> system, encompassing over 110,000 miles (177,000 km) of highway and 17,000 bridges.</li>
<li>A 5,000 mile (8,000 km) rail network, carrying over 42 million short tons (38 million metric tons) of equipment, raw materials, manufactured goods and produce each year.</li>
<li>Over 130 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transit" title="Public transit">public transit</a> operators, serving over 5.2 million passengers each day.</li>
<li>Twelve major public and private <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">ports</a>, handling more than 110 million short tons (100 million metric tons) of freight annually.</li>
<li>456 public and private <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a> facilities, through which more than 31 million people travel each year. It owns two <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airports</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stewart_International_Airport" title="Stewart International Airport">Stewart International Airport</a> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newburgh_(city),_New_York" title="Newburgh (city), New York">Newburgh</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Republic_Airport" title="Republic Airport">Republic Airport</a> on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>. Stewart is currently leased to the <a href="/wiki/Port_Authority_of_New_York_and_New_Jersey" title="Port Authority of New York and New Jersey">Port Authority of New York and New Jersey</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Its regulations are compiled in title 17 of the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Codes,_Rules_and_Regulations" title="New York Codes, Rules and Regulations">New York Codes, Rules and Regulations</a></i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_State_Department_of_Transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of the New York State Department of Transportation and its predecessors spans over two centuries:
</p>
<ul><li>In 1781, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Engineer_and_Surveyor" title="New York State Engineer and Surveyor">Office of Surveyor General</a> was reorganized from its colonial Dutch and English beginnings to survey lands that had been vested in the state during and following the Revolutionary war.</li>
<li>In 1810, the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Canal_Commission" title="Erie Canal Commission">Erie Canal Commission</a> was established to build the <a href="/wiki/Erie_Canal" title="Erie Canal">Erie Canal</a>, and afterwards the canal commissioners oversaw maintenance and enlargement of the canals</li>
<li>In 1848, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Engineer_and_Surveyor" title="New York State Engineer and Surveyor">Office of State Engineer and Surveyor</a> succeeded the Surveyor General's Office.</li>
<li>In 1878, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Public_Works" title="New York State Department of Public Works">Superintendent of Public Works</a> took over the duties of the canal commissioners.</li>
<li>In 1907, the <b>Public Service Commission</b> assumed responsibility for the economic and safety regulation of privately operated transportation; railroad and bus safety inspection; and, approval for the installation of protection for or elimination of at-grade rail highway crossings.</li>
<li>In 1908, the <b>New York State Department of Highways</b> was established by the Highway Act. It was headed by a three-member Highway Commission, appointed in 1909.</li>
<li>In 1911, the Highway Commission was abolished, and was succeeded by a <b>State Superintendent of Highways</b>.</li>
<li>In 1927, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Public_Works" title="New York State Department of Public Works">Department of Public Works</a> took over the duties of the State Engineer and Surveyor, unifying responsibility for highways, canals and public buildings,</li>
<li>In 1967, the <b>Department of Transportation</b> was formed to deal with the state's complex transportation system, and absorbed among others the Department of Public Works.</li></ul>
<p>The first head of the New York State Department of Transportation (effective from 1 September 1967) was the former head of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Public_Works" title="New York State Department of Public Works">New York State Department of Public Works</a> John Burch McMorran (1899–1991).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The first Executive Deputy Commissioner of the New York State Department of Transportation was <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burton_Hughes" title="Edward Burton Hughes">Edward Burton Hughes</a>, who had formerly been Deputy Superintendent of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Department_of_Public_Works" title="New York State Department of Public Works">New York State Department of Public Works</a>, a role he had worked in continuously since 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Both appointments were engaged by Governor <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Rockefeller" title="Nelson Rockefeller">Nelson Rockefeller</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traffic_Management">Traffic Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_State_Department_of_Transportation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Traffic Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NYS DOT has several Traffic Management Centers (TMC) located throughout the 11 regions in New York State.
</p>
<ul><li>Region 1 (Capital Region): The Region 1 TMC or CRTMC (Capital Region Traffic Management Center) is an attachment of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Police" title="New York State Police">New York State Police</a> Communications Section also known as SP COMSEC, formally located at the State Police Division Headquarters, building 22 on the <a href="/wiki/W._Averell_Harriman_State_Office_Building_Campus" title="W. Averell Harriman State Office Building Campus">W. Averell Harriman State Office Building Campus</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albany,_NY" title="Albany, NY">Albany, NY</a>. The TMC moved in September, 2012 along with SP COMSEC to the new NYSP Troop G Headquarters located in Latham, NY.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-M&amp;J_Engineering_P.C._6-0"><a href="#cite_note-M&amp;J_Engineering_P.C.-6">[6]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Region 1 is also the home to the NYS DOT STICC (Statewide Transportation Information Coordination Center), manned 24/7, STICC is responsible for the coordination &amp; logistics of statewide resources during major incidents within New York State and is currently located on the 1st floor of the DOT Headquarters in Colonie, NY.
</p>
