<p><i><b>Khao jee</b></i>, or <i><b>jee khao</b></i> (literally: "grilled [sticky] rice" or "grilling [sticky] rice"), is an ancient Laotian cooking method of grilling <a href="/wiki/Glutinous_rice" title="Glutinous rice">glutinous rice</a> or sticky rice on a stick over an open fire.  
</p><p><i><b>Khao jee</b></i> or, more specifically, <i><b>khao jee joom kai</b></i> (literally: "grilled sticky rice dipped in egg"), also known as Lao sticky rice pancakes with egg coating, is a traditional Lao food from Laos and the ethnic Lao of <a href="/wiki/Isan" title="Isan">Isan</a> or northeastern <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Glutinous rice is the staple of the Lao people in Laos and in Thailand. In fact, the Lao consume more sticky rice than any other group of people in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Khao jee (joom kai)</i> is usually served as a quick meal during breakfast or as a grab-and-go snack. Khao jee can be found at street stalls all over Laos. 
</p><p>At the conclusion if the Vietnam War, between 1975 and 1995, it was estimated that approximately 200,000 Lao refugees, crossed the <a href="/wiki/Mekong" title="Mekong">Mekong</a> River into Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Most stayed in the refugee camps while other moved to <a href="/wiki/Bangkok" title="Bangkok">Bangkok</a> looking for work. 
</p><p>During the economic boom of the 1980s and as demands for labour increased. It was estimated that between 1980 and 1990 approximately 1.1 million ethnic Lao from <a href="/wiki/Isan" title="Isan">Isan</a> had moved from the northeast to central Thailand and Bangkok.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This, in turn, has helped popularize and create an unprecedented demand for Lao food outside of Laos and the northeast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Traditional Lao food such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sticky_rice" title="Sticky rice">sticky rice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papaya_salad" title="Papaya salad">papaya salad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larb" title="Larb">larb</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nam_khao" title="Nam khao">nam khao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kai_yang" title="Kai yang">kai yang</a>, and khao jee etc., can now be found at street stalls and vendors in Bangkok and throughout Thailand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>khao jee</i> is made by forming the sticky rice into a patty or wrapped around a long stick acting as a vehicle or method of reaching the hot fire and flipping the sticky rice patties without burning one's hand, similar to toasting marshmallow over an open flame. The grilled sticky rice will form a cripsy and nutty outer layer while centre are hot and chewy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> 
</p><p>The most common and well-known way of eating Lao grilled sticky rice is with egg coating, but Laotians have been known to eat plain grilled sticky rice with a sprinkle of salt, or even coated in <a href="/wiki/Padaek" title="Padaek">padaek</a>, the traditional Lao fermented fish sauce.
</p><p>In Laos, the term "khao jee" can also refer to a Lao-style French bread or <a href="/wiki/Baguette" title="Baguette">baguette</a>. One of the ever-lasting and fully embraced remnants of French colonial rules in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The khao jee bread is commonly use in <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnamese</a> <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1nh_m%C3%AC" title="Bánh mì">bánh mì</a> sandwiches. The Lao variation of the sandwich is known as <a href="/wiki/Khao_Jee_P%C3%A2t%C3%A9" title="Khao Jee Pâté">Khao Jee Pâté</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
