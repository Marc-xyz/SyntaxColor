<p>The <b>Oberhausen–Arnhem railway</b> (also known in German as the <i>Hollandstrecke</i>, meaning "Holland line") is a two-track, electrified main line railway running close to the lower <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> from <a href="/wiki/Oberhausen_Hauptbahnhof" title="Oberhausen Hauptbahnhof">Oberhausen</a> via <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wesel_railway_station" title="Wesel railway station">Wesel</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emmerich_railway_station" title="Emmerich railway station">Emmerich</a> and the German–Dutch border to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnhem_railway_station" title="Arnhem railway station">Arnhem</a> and forms part of the line between the <a href="/wiki/Ruhr" title="Ruhr">Ruhr</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>. The line was opened by the <a href="/wiki/Cologne-Minden_Railway_Company" title="Cologne-Minden Railway Company">Cologne-Minden Railway Company</a> in 1856 and is one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_the_first_German_railways_to_1870" title="List of the first German railways to 1870">oldest lines in Germany</a>.
</p><p>It branches in Oberhausen from the <a href="/wiki/Duisburg%E2%80%93Dortmund_railway" title="Duisburg–Dortmund railway">Duisburg–Dortmund line</a>, a section of the <a href="/wiki/Cologne-Minden_trunk_line" title="Cologne-Minden trunk line">Cologne-Minden trunk line</a> and connects in Arnhem with the <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway" title="Amsterdam–Arnhem railway">Rhine Railway</a> to Amsterdam. The line is of high importance for international traffic, both for long-distance passenger services and for freight traffic and is listed as a priority project of the <a href="/wiki/Trans-European_Networks" title="Trans-European Networks">Trans-European Networks</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="1178" decoding="async" height="62" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg/220px-Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg/330px-Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg/440px-Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Westvoort_Westervoortse_Brug_1904.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bridge near <a href="/wiki/Westervoort" title="Westervoort">Westervoort</a>, Netherlands over the <a href="/wiki/IJssel" title="IJssel">IJssel</a>, 1904</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="364" decoding="async" height="332" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg/220px-Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg/330px-Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/51/Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Affiche_van_de_N.S.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>All roads lead to Emmerich, 1920s poster</div></div></div>
<p>The first plans to build a railway line on the Lower Rhine emerged in the 1830s. At that time the Amsterdam business community began to think about how they could expand trade with Germany. Plans were developed and the president of the <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussian</a> administration in <a href="/wiki/Cologne" title="Cologne">Cologne</a>, Daniel Heinrich Delius, gave his conditional support for them. The Dutch government subsequently instructed its chief engineer for public works, Bernard Herman Goudriaan to develop a route for the line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>As a result, it was proposed the new railway would run from Amsterdam via <a href="/wiki/Amersfoort" title="Amersfoort">Amersfoort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isselburg" title="Isselburg">Isselburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamminkeln" title="Hamminkeln">Hamminkeln</a>, Wesel and <a href="/wiki/Duisburg" title="Duisburg">Duisburg</a> to Cologne, avoiding large cities such as Utrecht and Arnhem in order to minimise the risk of floods disrupting the line. The city of Emmerich objected to this route, which would have bypassed it. Further difficulties arose with the decision of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_Rhine_Railway_Company" title="Dutch Rhine Railway Company">Dutch Rhine Railway Company</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Nederlandsche Rhijnspoorweg-Maatschappij</i>, NRS) to build its line from Amsterdam to Utrecht (opened in 1843) and on to Arnhem (1845) not as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_gauge" title="Standard gauge">standard gauge</a>, but with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broad_gauge" title="Broad gauge">broad gauge</a> of 1945 mm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the following years several proposals were submitted and rejected until on 18 July 1851 the two governments signed the "Prussian-Dutch agreement for the construction of the Oberhausen–Wesel–Emmerich–Arnhem railway". On 30 December 1852, the <i>Cologne-Minden Railway Company</i> (<i>Cöln-Mindener Eisenbahn-Gesellschaft</i>, CME) was granted a concession to build the line on condition that construction was completed within three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The final route of the line was eventually approved by the Prussian Minister of Trade, Commerce and Public Works on 4 May 1854. This allowed the CME to commence the construction of the 61 km-long line from its <a href="/wiki/Oberhausen_Hauptbahnhof" title="Oberhausen Hauptbahnhof">Oberhausen station</a> on its trunk line to Emmerich. Construction could also start on the 12 km section to the border and on to Arnhem, because the NRS had converted <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway" title="Amsterdam–Arnhem railway">its line from Amsterdam to Arnhem</a> to standard gauge in 1855.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following a construction period of two years, the Oberhausen-<a href="/wiki/Dinslaken" title="Dinslaken">Dinslaken</a> section was opened on 1 July 1856 and the entire double-track line was put into operation on 20 October 1856. On the first day, four passenger trains and a freight train ran on the line. In 1859, 300 passengers, 34 tonnes of freight and 10 head of cattle were carried each day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Wesel originally had a basic station suitable for its military role, which was replaced 25 years later by a half-timbered building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wesel_junction">Wesel junction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wesel junction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="808" data-file-width="2010" decoding="async" height="88" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg/220px-Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg/330px-Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg/440px-Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alte_Rheinbruecke_Wesel.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ruins of the <a href="/wiki/Wesel_Railway_Bridge" title="Wesel Railway Bridge">Wesel Railway Bridge</a></div></div></div>
<p>By the end of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Wesel station had become the major junction of the Lower Rhine. The Holland line had been crossed by the <a href="/wiki/Haltern%E2%80%93Venlo_railway" title="Haltern–Venlo railway">Haltern–Venlo line</a> (part of the CME's Hamburg-Venlo line) since its opening in 1874. Four years later, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bocholt_Railway" title="Bocholt Railway">Bocholt Railway</a> (also owned by the CME) and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Boxtel_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Boxtel Railway (page does not exist)">Boxtel Railway</a> also opened to Wesel. In 1912 the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Wesel_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oberhausen–Wesel railway (page does not exist)">line to Oberhausen via Walsum</a> was opened. Trains to <a href="/wiki/Venlo" title="Venlo">Venlo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boxtel" title="Boxtel">Boxtel</a> ran over the almost two kilometre-long <a href="/wiki/Wesel_Railway_Bridge" title="Wesel Railway Bridge">Wesel Railway Bridge</a> on the Rhine, built between 1872 and 1874.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconstruction_after_the_Second_World_War">Reconstruction after the Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reconstruction after the Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> bridges over the <a href="/wiki/Rhine%E2%80%93Herne_Canal" title="Rhine–Herne Canal">Rhine–Herne Canal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Emscher" title="Emscher">Emscher</a> river in Oberhausen, the <a href="/wiki/Wesel%E2%80%93Datteln_Canal" title="Wesel–Datteln Canal">Wesel–Datteln Canal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lippe_(river)" title="Lippe (river)">Lippe</a> in Wesel that had been destroyed during the war were restored with temporary repairs. As early as 15 November 1945 the tracks between Oberhausen and Arnhem were restored. Operations within the Netherlands were quickly restored. The destroyed Wesel Railway Bridge over the Rhine was not rebuilt, ending operations on the lines to Boxtel and Venlo. The largely destroyed station buildings on the line were rebuilt between 1952 and 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrification_of_the_line">Electrification of the line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electrification of the line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1964/66, the whole two-track main line was electrified. Voltages change between the German 15 kV / 16.7 Hz AC and the Dutch 1.5 kV DC system in Emmerich station. The different electrifical systems, along with the different <a href="/wiki/Train_protection_system" title="Train protection system">train protection systems</a>, force electric locomotives to be changed in Emmerich. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_multiple_units" title="Electric multiple units">Electric multiple units</a> that can operate on the systems of both countries were first developed in 2001 and approved for operation in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PROBAHN_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-PROBAHN-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_long-distance_rail_services">Development of long-distance rail services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development of long-distance rail services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Amsterdam-Cologne <i>D-trains</i> (<i>D-Zug</i>, that is luxury express trains), then stopping in Emmerich, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Empel-Rees_railway_station" title="Empel-Rees railway station">Empel-Rees</a>, Wesel, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dinslaken_railway_station" title="Dinslaken railway station">Dinslaken</a> and Oberhausen, were replaced in June 1991 by <a href="/wiki/EuroCity" title="EuroCity">EuroCity</a> trains that operate without stopping between Emmerich and Oberhausen. The EuroCity trains were mostly replaced in November 2000 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercity-Express" title="Intercity-Express">Intercity-Express</a> (ICE) services between Amsterdam and Cologne, using modern <a href="/wiki/ICE_3#Class_406" title="ICE 3">multi-system electrical multiple units</a> capable of high-speeds, which did not need to stop in Emmerich any more. Since December 2002, the new <a href="/wiki/Cologne%E2%80%93Frankfurt_high-speed_rail_line" title="Cologne–Frankfurt high-speed rail line">Cologne–Frankfurt high-speed rail line</a> has provided a direct through connection to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>. At the same time, the two remaining EuroCity trains were also replaced by electric multiple units. After that the only long-distance train stopping in Emmerich and Wesel was a night train, and since December 2005 it only stops in Emmerich.
</p>
<h3><span id="Completion_of_the_new_.22Betuweroute.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Completion_of_the_new_"Betuweroute"'>Completion of the new "Betuweroute"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: Completion of the new "Betuweroute"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Netherlands the <a href="/wiki/Betuweroute" title="Betuweroute">Betuweroute</a> was put into normal operations on 16 June 2007. The new freight route connects the port of <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a> with the Ruhr and southern Germany. It joins the existing line in <a href="/wiki/Zevenaar" title="Zevenaar">Zevenaar</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_Investments">Future Investments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Future Investments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2013, the German federal government, the state of North-Rhine Westphalia, Deutsche Bahn and other parties signed a €1.5bn funding agreement to upgrade the 73 km Emmerich-Oberhausen segment. Improvements include laying a third track to remove bottlenecks, removal of level crossings, renewal of electrical equipment, installation of ETCS and building of noise attenuating walls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Services">Services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg/220px-ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg/330px-ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg/440px-ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ICE-3-frankfurt.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/ICE_3#Class_406" title="ICE 3">Class 406</a> <i>ICE International</i> train</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="623" data-file-width="779" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg/220px-CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg/330px-CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg/440px-CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CityNightLine_bilevel_sleeping_car_in_Dresden_-_2000.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DB_City_Night_Line" title="DB City Night Line">City Night Line</a> <a href="/wiki/Sleeping_car" title="Sleeping car">sleeping car</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Oberhausen–Arnhem railway is served every two hours each day by long-distance passenger services operated by ICE International trains on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Centraal_railway_station" title="Amsterdam Centraal railway station">Amsterdam Centraal</a>–<a href="/wiki/Utrecht_Centraal_railway_station" title="Utrecht Centraal railway station">Utrecht Centraal</a>–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ln_Hbf" title="Köln Hbf">Köln Hbf</a>–<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_(Main)_Hbf" title="Frankfurt (Main) Hbf">Frankfurt (Main) Hbf</a> / <a href="/wiki/Basel_SBB_railway_station" title="Basel SBB railway station">Basel SBB</a> route.
</p><p>The following services run hourly:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Regional-Express" title="Regional-Express">Regional-Express</a> RE 5 (<i><a href="/wiki/Rhein-Express" title="Rhein-Express">Rhein-Express</a></i>): <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koblenz_Hbf" title="Koblenz Hbf">Koblenz</a>–Cologne–Duisburg–Wesel,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Regionalbahn" title="Regionalbahn">Regional-Express</a> RE 19 (<i><a href="/wiki/Rhein-IJssel-Express" title="Rhein-IJssel-Express">Rhein-IJssel-Express</a></i>): Düsseldorf–Emmerich-Arnhem,</li>
<li>Monday to Friday, RB 35 (<i><a href="/wiki/Emscher-Niederrhein-Bahn" title="Emscher-Niederrhein-Bahn">Emscher-Niederrhein-Bahn</a></i>): <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%B6nchengladbach_Hbf" title="Mönchengladbach Hbf">Mönchengladbach</a>–Duisburg–Wesel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>These services each run twice per hour on the Dutch section between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arnhem_railway_station" title="Arnhem railway station">Arnhem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zevenaar_railway_station" title="Zevenaar railway station">Zevenaar</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>a local train between Arnhem and <a href="/wiki/Winterswijk_railway_station" title="Winterswijk railway station">Winterswijk</a>, operated by <a href="/wiki/Arriva" title="Arriva">Arriva</a>,</li>
<li>a local train on weekdays only between Arnhem and <a href="/wiki/Doetinchem_railway_station" title="Doetinchem railway station">Doetinchem</a>, operated by <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a> under the brand name <i>Breng.</i></li></ul>
<p>In Wesel services on the Oberhausen-Arnhem line connect with RB 32 (<i>Der Bocholter</i>) services on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bocholt_Railway" title="Bocholt Railway">Bocholt Railway</a>.
</p><p>Regional passenger services on the German section are operated by <a href="/wiki/DB_Regio" title="DB Regio">DB Regio</a> NRW and <a href="/wiki/Abellio_Deutschland" title="Abellio Deutschland">Abellio Rail NRW</a>. DB Regio NRW operated the whole passenger services on the German side of the railway line until December 2016 and currently is operating the RE 5 line. The following train compositions are used by DB Regio NRW: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Push-pull_train" title="Push-pull train">push-pull trains</a> composed of five <a href="/wiki/Bilevel_rail_car" title="Bilevel rail car">double-deck</a> carriages hauled by a <a href="/wiki/DBAG_Class_145_and_146" title="DBAG Class 145 and 146">DB class 146</a> electric locomotives at speeds of up to 160 km/h.
</p><p>Abellio Rail NRW started its operation in December 2016 and now is operating the services on the RE 19, RB 32 and RB 35 lines. There are 21 trainsets type <a href="/wiki/Stadler_FLIRT" title="Stadler FLIRT">Stadler FLIRT</a> in the grey Abellio livery which are being used on these lines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Passenger services on the Dutch section were operated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntus" title="Syntus">Syntus</a> until the end of 2012, after that by Arriva and Breng.
</p><p>Cross-border regional passenger services didn't operate between 2006 and 2017. Previously regional services operated between Emmerich and Arnhem (RB 34, <i>Der Arnheimer</i>). From 6 April 2017, Abellio service RE 19 was extended from Emmerich to Arnhem.
</p><p>In addition to passenger traffic, the line has a very high volume of freight traffic.
</p>
<h3><span id="Usage_.282005.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_(2005)">Usage (2005)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberhausen%E2%80%93Arnhem_railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Usage (2005)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The utilisation of the line in 2005—following the introduction of a regular interval timetable in 1998, the increase of long-distance and regional passenger services at the end of 2002 and the increase of freight transport—amounted to six to eight trains per hour in each direction. In each working day there 104 units of regional passenger trains, 16 long-distance trains and 60 freight trains, each way.
</p>
