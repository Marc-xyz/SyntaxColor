	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Irving Harper</h1>

<p><b>Irving Harper</b> (July 14, 1916 – August 4, 2015) was an American industrial designer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> While working for <a href="/wiki/George_Nelson_(designer)" title="George Nelson (designer)">George Nelson</a> Associates, Inc. on designs for <a href="/wiki/Herman_Miller_(manufacturer)" title="Herman Miller (manufacturer)">Herman Miller</a> furniture, Harper became one of the most prolific designers of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modernist" title="Modernist">modernist</a> style. Among his important designs is the Herman Miller company logo, and the company's <a href="/wiki/Marshmallow_sofa" title="Marshmallow sofa">Marshmallow sofa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harper was born in New York City in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After attending <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Union" title="Cooper Union">Cooper Union</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_College" title="Brooklyn College">Brooklyn College</a>, Irving Harper worked as a draftsman for the Gilbert Rohde's office during the 1930s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">[3]</a></sup> While there he was responsible for the creation of the Plexiglas Exhibit, the Anthracite exhibit, and the Home Furnishings Focal exhibit at the <a href="/wiki/1939_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1939 New York World's Fair">1939 New York World's Fair</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">[3]</a></sup> Leaving Rohde's, he worked as an interior designer for noted industrial designer <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Loewy" title="Raymond Loewy">Raymond Loewy</a> at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Raymond_Loewy_Associates&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Raymond Loewy Associates (page does not exist)">Raymond Loewy Associates</a> office, designing interiors in the company's department store division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">[3]</a></sup>
Born Irving Hoffzimer to David and Rebecca Hoffzimer.  Oldest of three children  He had two sisters, Phyliss and Sophie.
</p>
<h2><span id="George_Nelson_Associates.2C_Inc."></span><span class="mw-headline" id="George_Nelson_Associates,_Inc.">George Nelson Associates, Inc.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: George Nelson Associates, Inc.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1940s Harper met George Nelson. In 1947 he was offered a job as an interior designer by Nelson and joined George Nelson Associates, Inc. Initially Harper was mostly responsible for designing trade advertising for the Herman Miller furniture account. In 1947 Harper designed the logo for Herman Miller, Inc. Never trained as a graphic artist, Harper based the logo around a large letter "M", for Miller.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>At first the logo was in wood-grain, since wood figured prominently in Herman Miller furniture. Harper stated, "I continued to use the M and refined it as the ads went on. The Herman Miller logo was something they got for free, and they loved it." He chuckled. "There was no project to do a logo. It was probably the cheapest logo campaign in advertising history." Harper also was responsible for the textile prints used by Nelson's studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clocks">Clocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Later Nelson got the Howard Miller Clock Co. account, and Harper was given the responsibility of handling it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup> Harper decided to create clocks that were a piece of sculpture. "To omit numbers and have an abstract object that moved on the wall was something no one was doing at the time". He would design a group of about eight clocks once or twice a year, which were sent to Howard Miller Clock Co. Most were put into production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marshmallow_sofa">Marshmallow sofa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marshmallow sofa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Circular.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Circular.jpg/220px-Circular.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Circular.jpg/330px-Circular.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Circular.jpg/440px-Circular.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Circular.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A row of marshmallow sofas</div></div></div>
<p>In 1954, Harper was introduced to a salesman from a Long Island plastics company. The salesman offered a product that created 12 inch diameter self-skinning discs. George Nelson embraced the idea as a way to manufacture low-cost furniture. Over a weekend Harper designed a sofa incorporating 18 of the discs in a whimsical pattern. The salesman's product didn't live up to expectations, but Harper's design was put into production by Herman Miller as the Marshmallow sofa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Harper worked for George Nelson Associates, Inc. for 17 years. During his time with the firm most of Harper's designs were attributed to George Nelson, as was the company's practice. John Pile, a designer for the firm in the 1950s, explains, "George's attitude was that it was okay for individual designers to be given credit in trade publications, but for the consumer world, the credit should always be to the firm, not the individual. He didn't always follow through on that policy though."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_George_Nelson">After George Nelson</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After George Nelson">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harper left George Nelson Associates, Inc. in 1963. Together with Phillip George they started Harper+George, a design company. The firm created iconic designs for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penn_Central" title="Penn Central">Penn Central</a> in 1965, <a href="/wiki/Braniff_International_Airways" title="Braniff International Airways">Braniff International Airways</a> from 1967 until 1982, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lenor_Larsen" title="Jack Lenor Larsen">Jack Lenor Larsen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hallmark_Cards" title="Hallmark Cards">Hallmark Cards</a>, among many others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Harper retired from Harper+George in 1983, and resided in <a href="/wiki/Rye,_New_York" title="Rye, New York">Rye, New York</a>, in a 19th-century farmhouse filled with modernist furnishings, and over 300 of his paper sculptures.
</p><p>In early 2001 Harper teamed with textile designer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Maharam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Maharam (page does not exist)">Michael Maharam</a> to re-introduce Harper's original 1950s era Herman Miller textile prints as part of Maharam's "Textiles of the 20th Century" line. Harper's print "Pavement fabric" was the first to be re-introduced in 2001, followed by "China Shop" in the fall of that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irving_Harper&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Harper died on August 4, 2015, aged 99, in <a href="/wiki/Rye,_New_York" title="Rye, New York">Rye, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
