<p><b>Donald Richie Taylor</b> (December 13, 1920 – December 29, 1998) was an American actor and film director.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He co-starred in 1940s and 1950s classics, including the 1948 <a href="/wiki/Film_noir" title="Film noir">film noir</a> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Naked_City" title="The Naked City">The Naked City</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Battleground_(film)" title="Battleground (film)">Battleground</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Father_of_the_Bride_(1950_film)" title="Father of the Bride (1950 film)">Father of the Bride</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Father%27s_Little_Dividend" title="Father's Little Dividend">Father's Little Dividend</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Stalag_17" title="Stalag 17">Stalag 17</a></i>.  He later turned to directing films such as <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_from_the_Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Escape from the Planet of the Apes">Escape from the Planet of the Apes</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Sawyer_(1973_film)" title="Tom Sawyer (1973 film)">Tom Sawyer</a></i>  (1973), and <i><a href="/wiki/Damien:_Omen_II" title="Damien: Omen II">Damien: Omen II</a></i> (1978).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_work">Early life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The son of Mr. and Mrs. D.E. Taylor, in <a href="/wiki/Freeport,_Pennsylvania" title="Freeport, Pennsylvania">Freeport, Pennsylvania</a>, on December 13, 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> he was born Donald Ritchie Taylor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ss-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> (Another source says that he was born "in Pittsburgh and raised in Freeport, Pa.")<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ss-3">[3]</a></sup> He studied speech and drama at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penn_State_University" title="Penn State University">Penn State University</a> and hitchhiked to <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> in 1942. He was signed as a contract player at <a href="/wiki/Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer" title="Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer">Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer</a> and appeared in small roles. Drafted into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> (AAF) during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, he appeared in the Air Forces's <i><a href="/wiki/Winged_Victory_(play)" title="Winged Victory (play)">Winged Victory</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> play<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Winged_Victory_(film)" title="Winged Victory (film)">movie</a> (1944), credited as "Cpl. Don Taylor."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_career">Acting career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acting career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After discharge from the AAF, Taylor was cast in a lead role as the young detective, Jimmy Halloran, working alongside veteran homicide detective Dan Muldoon (<a href="/wiki/Barry_Fitzgerald" title="Barry Fitzgerald">Barry Fitzgerald</a>) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Studios" title="Universal Studios">Universal</a>'s 1948 screen version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Naked_City" title="The Naked City">The Naked City</a></i>, which was notable for being filmed entirely on location in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>. Taylor was later part of the ensemble cast in MGM's classic World War II drama <i><a href="/wiki/Battleground_(film)" title="Battleground (film)">Battleground</a></i> (1949).  He then appeared as the husband of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Taylor" title="Elizabeth Taylor">Elizabeth Taylor</a> in the comedies <i><a href="/wiki/Father_of_the_Bride_(1950_film)" title="Father of the Bride (1950 film)">Father of the Bride</a></i> (1950) and its sequel <i><a href="/wiki/Father%27s_Little_Dividend" title="Father's Little Dividend">Father's Little Dividend</a></i> (1951), starring <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Tracy" title="Spencer Tracy">Spencer Tracy</a>. Another memorable role was Vern "Cowboy" Blithe in <i><a href="/wiki/Flying_Leathernecks" title="Flying Leathernecks">Flying Leathernecks</a></i> (1951). In 1952, Taylor played a soldier bringing his Japanese war-bride back to small-town America in <i><a href="/wiki/Japanese_War_Bride" title="Japanese War Bride">Japanese War Bride</a></i>. In 1953, Taylor had a key role as the escaping prisoner Lt. Dunbar in <a href="/wiki/Billy_Wilder" title="Billy Wilder">Billy Wilder</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Stalag_17" title="Stalag 17">Stalag 17</a></i>. His last major film role came in <i><a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Cry_Tomorrow" title="I'll Cry Tomorrow">I'll Cry Tomorrow</a></i> (1955).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Directorial_career">Directorial career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Directorial career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the late 1950s through the 1980s, Taylor turned to directing movies and TV shows, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock_Presents" title="Alfred Hitchcock Presents">Alfred Hitchcock Presents</a></i>, the short-lived <i><a href="/wiki/Steve_Canyon" title="Steve Canyon">Steve Canyon</a></i>, starring <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dean_Fredericks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dean Fredericks (page does not exist)">Dean Fredericks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rod_Serling" title="Rod Serling">Rod Serling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Gallery" title="Night Gallery">Night Gallery</a></i>.  One of his memorable efforts, in 1973, was the musical film <i><a href="/wiki/Tom_Sawyer_(1973_film)" title="Tom Sawyer (1973 film)">Tom Sawyer</a>,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ct_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ct-6">[6]</a></sup> which boasted a <a href="/wiki/Sherman_Brothers" title="Sherman Brothers">Sherman Brothers</a> song score. Other films that Taylor directed are <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_from_the_Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Escape from the Planet of the Apes">Escape from the Planet of the Apes</a></i> (1971), <i><a href="/wiki/Echoes_of_a_Summer" title="Echoes of a Summer">Echoes of a Summer</a></i> (1976), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Scout_%26_Cathouse_Thursday" title="The Great Scout &amp; Cathouse Thursday">The Great Scout &amp; Cathouse Thursday</a></i> (also 1976), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Island_of_Dr._Moreau_(1977_film)" title="The Island of Dr. Moreau (1977 film)">The Island of Dr. Moreau</a></i> (1977) starring <a href="/wiki/Burt_Lancaster" title="Burt Lancaster">Burt Lancaster</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Damien:_Omen_II" title="Damien: Omen II">Damien: Omen II</a></i> (1978) with <a href="/wiki/William_Holden" title="William Holden">William Holden</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Final_Countdown_(film)" title="The Final Countdown (film)">The Final Countdown</a></i> (1980) with <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Douglas" title="Kirk Douglas">Kirk Douglas</a>.
</p><p>Taylor occasionally performed both acting and directing roles simultaneously, as he did for episodes of the TV detective series <i><a href="/wiki/Burke%27s_Law_(1963_TV_series)" title="Burke's Law (1963 TV series)">Burke's Law</a>.</i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_career">Writing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Writing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Taylor "wrote one-act plays, radio dramas, short stories, and the 1985 TV movie <i>My Wicked, Wicked Ways ... The Legend of Errol Flynn."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ss-3">[3]</a></sup></i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery,_1946.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1873" data-file-width="1298" decoding="async" height="346" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery%2C_1946.jpg/240px-Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery%2C_1946.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery%2C_1946.jpg/360px-Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery%2C_1946.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery%2C_1946.jpg/480px-Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery%2C_1946.jpg 2x" width="240"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Don_Taylor_and_Phyllis_Avery,_1946.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Don Taylor with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phyllis_Avery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phyllis Avery (page does not exist)">Phyllis Avery</a>, 1946</div></div></div>
<p>Taylor was married twice.
</p>
<ul><li>His first wife was <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Phyllis_Avery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Phyllis Avery (page does not exist)">Phyllis Avery</a>, whom he married in 1944; they divorced in 1955,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ss-3">[3]</a></sup> but not before the births of their daughters Anne and Avery.</li>
<li>His second wife was <a href="/wiki/Hazel_Court" title="Hazel Court">Hazel Court</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ct_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ct-6">[6]</a></sup> whom he married in 1964 and stayed with until his death; they had a son, Jonathan, and a daughter, Courtney.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ppg_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ppg-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Don_Taylor_(American_actor_and_director)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Taylor died on December 29, 1998, at the University of California Medical Center in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ss_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ss-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
