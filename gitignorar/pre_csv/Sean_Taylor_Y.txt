<p><b>Sean Michael Maurice Taylor</b> (April 1, 1983 – November 27, 2007) was an <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Safety_(American_football_position)" title="Safety (American football position)">free safety</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington Redskins</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> (NFL). He was drafted by the Redskins with the fifth overall pick of the <a href="/wiki/2004_NFL_Draft" title="2004 NFL Draft">2004 NFL Draft</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> where he played for four seasons until his death in 2007.
</p><p>As a high school player, Taylor led Gulliver Prep to a Florida state championship and rushed for a state record 44 touchdowns in a season. He subsequently played <a href="/wiki/College_football" title="College football">college football</a> as a defensive back for the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>, where he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Hurricanes_football" title="Miami Hurricanes football">Hurricanes</a>' <a href="/wiki/2002_Rose_Bowl" title="2002 Rose Bowl">2001 BCS National Championship</a> team, and earned unanimous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-American" title="All-American">All-American</a> honors.
</p><p>Taylor's success in college led to him being selected in the first round of the 2004 draft by the Redskins where he gained a reputation as a hard-hitting player. Due to his ferocious hits, several of his Redskins teammates nicknamed him "Meast", from the expression "half man, half beast."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He made one <a href="/wiki/Pro_Bowl" title="Pro Bowl">Pro Bowl</a> appearance in 2006.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/2007_NFL_season" title="2007 NFL season">2007 NFL season</a>, on November 26 Taylor was shot by intruders at his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Florida_metropolitan_area" title="South Florida metropolitan area">Miami area</a> home and died the next day on November 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foxnews.com_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews.com-4">[4]</a></sup> His death led to an outpouring of national support and sympathy, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> area, where Taylor had been a fan favorite as a Redskin,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the Miami area, where he had starred in high school and college. Posthumously, he earned a second Pro Bowl selection and First Team <a href="/wiki/All-Pro" title="All-Pro">All-Pro</a> honors.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sean Taylor was born in Florida to Pedro Taylor, a policeman, and Donna Junor. He spent his early years growing up with his great-grandmother Aulga Clarke in <a href="/wiki/Homestead,_Florida" title="Homestead, Florida">Homestead, Florida</a> and later moved to his father's home at the age of 11. Taylor was baptized at the Bethel Seventh-day Adventist Church in <a href="/wiki/Florida_City,_Florida" title="Florida City, Florida">Florida City</a> by Pastor David Peay. And later many members of his family transferred their membership to the Perrine Seventh-day Adventist Church in Miami. He grew up in a low-income neighborhood in Miami, on a street lined with candy-colored houses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Taylor played high school football in <a href="/wiki/Pinecrest,_Florida" title="Pinecrest, Florida">Pinecrest</a>, a suburb of Miami. He originally began his high school football career at <a href="/wiki/Miami_Killian_High_School" title="Miami Killian High School">Miami Killian High School</a>, a Class 2A public school, but transferred to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gulliver_Preparatory_School" title="Gulliver Preparatory School">Gulliver Preparatory School</a>, a Class prep school, where he was a three-sport star in football, track and basketball. He played both <a href="/wiki/Offense_(sports)" title="Offense (sports)">offense</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Defense_(sport)" title="Defense (sport)">defense</a>. Despite missing the first game of the season (the team's only loss), he helped Gulliver win the Florida Class 2A State Championship in 2000 with a 14–1 record. Taylor was a star on both sides of the ball during that season, playing <a href="/wiki/Running_back" title="Running back">running back</a>, <a href="/wiki/Defensive_back" title="Defensive back">defensive back</a> and <a href="/wiki/Linebacker" title="Linebacker">linebacker</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldupdate1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-heraldupdate1-8">[8]</a></sup> He rushed for 1,400 yards and a state-record 44 touchdowns and on two separate occasions, rushed for more than 200 yards during Gulliver's state playoff run. He also compiled more than 100 tackles during the season and scored three touchdowns (two receiving, one rushing) in the state title game victory over <a href="/wiki/Marianna_High_School" title="Marianna High School">Marianna High School</a>. In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Track_%26_field" title="Track &amp; field">track &amp; field</a>, Taylor won the state 2A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/100-meter_dash" title="100-meter dash">100-meter dash</a> in 2000 and was also one of the state's top <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/400-meter_dash" title="400-meter dash">400-meter dash</a> sprinters. At Gulliver, Taylor was a teammate of future NFL player <a href="/wiki/Buck_Ortega" title="Buck Ortega">Buck Ortega</a>. His championship HS team was coached by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Howey_(footballer)" title="Steve Howey (footballer)">Steve Howey</a>, Ralph Ortega, Dirk Moran, Lee Horn, Dave Burns, and John McCloskey.
</p><p>Taylor was considered the No. 1 prospect in <a href="/wiki/Miami-Dade_County,_Florida" title="Miami-Dade County, Florida">Miami-Dade County</a> by the <i><a href="/wiki/Miami_Herald" title="Miami Herald">Miami Herald</a></i>. He was also rated the nation's No. 1 skill athlete and an All-American by <i><a href="/wiki/Super_Prep" title="Super Prep">Super Prep</a></i>. Taylor was also an <i><a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sentinel" title="Orlando Sentinel">Orlando Sentinel</a></i> Super Southern Team selection, the No. 1 athlete on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_Times-Union" title="Florida Times-Union">Florida Times-Union</a></i> Super 75 list and rated the No. 1 player in Florida by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gainesville_Sun" title="The Gainesville Sun">The Gainesville Sun</a></i>.
</p><p>In 2007, he was also named to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FHSAA%27s_All-Century_Team" title="FHSAA's All-Century Team">Florida High School Association All-Century Team</a>, which selected the Top 33 players in the 100-year history of high school football in the state. After his death, Taylor was honored at Gulliver by a plaque that was placed in the school's cafeteria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The football field at Gulliver Prep was renamed Sean Taylor Memorial Field on September 5, 2009.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="College_career">College career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: College career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Taylor was recruited to play for coach <a href="/wiki/Larry_Coker" title="Larry Coker">Larry Coker</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Miami_Hurricanes_football" title="Miami Hurricanes football">Miami Hurricanes football</a> team at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>. He was also a member of the Hurricanes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Track_%26_field" title="Track &amp; field">track &amp; field</a> team, competing in events such as the 100-meter and 200-meters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2001_season">2001 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2001 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a freshman (one of four true freshmen to play for the team), Taylor carved a niche for himself in Miami's secondary in nickel and dime defensive schemes. During the season, Taylor was named Big East <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_East_Conference_football_awards" title="Big East Conference football awards">Special Teams Player of the Week</a> for his performance against the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Panthers_football" title="Pittsburgh Panthers football">Pittsburgh Panthers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldupdate1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-heraldupdate1-8">[8]</a></sup> The Hurricanes won the national championship in 2001.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002_season">2002 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2002 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, his first season as a starter, Taylor was a second-team All-Big East selection by the league's head coaches. He finished third on the team in tackles with 85 (53 solo), broke up 15 passes, intercepted 4 passes, forced one fumble, blocked one kick and returned a punt for a touchdown. He led all Miami defensive backs in tackles, interceptions and passes broken up, and had a career-high 11 tackles (2 solo) and intercepted 2 passes in the <a href="/wiki/Fiesta_Bowl" title="Fiesta Bowl">Fiesta Bowl</a> loss to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University_football" title="Ohio State University football">Ohio State</a>. He made a critical play during the game, in which he intercepted Buckeyes quarterback <a href="/wiki/Craig_Krenzel" title="Craig Krenzel">Craig Krenzel</a> in the endzone and returned the ball out of the endzone. Buckeyes running back <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Clarett" title="Maurice Clarett">Maurice Clarett</a> ran Taylor down, and in the process stripped the ball away from him. Clarett recovered the ball for Ohio State, allowing them to kick a field goal to go up 17-7 at the time. This fumble was one of Miami's 5 turnovers and was viewed as critical in the Hurricanes 31-24 double overtime loss.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2003_season">2003 season</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2003 season">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Taylor produced a historic season during his final year at Miami that culminated with a plethora of honors and awards. He was named a unanimous first-team All-American, the Big East Conference Defensive Player of the Year and a finalist for the <a href="/wiki/Jim_Thorpe_Award" title="Jim Thorpe Award">Jim Thorpe Award</a>, given to the nation's best defensive back. He led the Big East Conference and ranked first nationally in interceptions with 10, tying the record for interceptions in a season with former Hurricanes standout <a href="/wiki/Bennie_Blades" title="Bennie Blades">Bennie Blades</a>. Taylor also finished first in total tackles with 77 (57 solo). He intercepted two passes in Miami's impressive 28-14 win over <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pittsburgh" title="University of Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, playing a key role as the Hurricanes limited All-American receiver <a href="/wiki/Larry_Fitzgerald" title="Larry Fitzgerald">Larry Fitzgerald</a> to just three receptions for 26 yards. He returned interceptions for an average of 18.4 yards, including a 67-yard touchdown return at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boston_College_football" title="Boston College football">Boston College</a>, a 50-yard scoring runback at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florida_State_University_football" title="Florida State University football">Florida State</a> and a 44-yard scoring runback against <a href="/wiki/Rutgers_Scarlet_Knights_football" title="Rutgers Scarlet Knights football">Rutgers University</a>. His three touchdown returns of interceptions is a Miami single-season record.
</p><p>Taylor also competed in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Track_%26_field" title="Track &amp; field">track &amp; field</a> as a senior for the Hurricanes. He placed 4th in the 100-meter at the Gatorade Invitational in <a href="/wiki/Coral_Gables,_Florida" title="Coral Gables, Florida">Coral Gables, Florida</a> with a time of 10.77 seconds, behind teammates Terrell Walden, <a href="/wiki/Roscoe_Parrish" title="Roscoe Parrish">Roscoe Parrish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Travarous_Bain" title="Travarous Bain">Travarous Bain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> His best efforts for the season came at the Big East Outdoor T&amp;F Championships in <a href="/wiki/Storrs,_Connecticut" title="Storrs, Connecticut">Storrs, Connecticut</a>, where he ran personal-bests of 10.74 seconds in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/100-meter_dash" title="100-meter dash">100-meter dash</a> and 21.60 seconds in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/200-meter_dash" title="200-meter dash">200-meter dash</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He also participated as a member of the 4x100 relay.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Washington_Redskins">Washington Redskins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Washington Redskins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington Redskins</a> selected Taylor in the first round (fifth overall) of the <a href="/wiki/2004_NFL_Draft" title="2004 NFL Draft">2004 NFL Draft</a>. He was the first of a record six players selected in the first round from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a>; the other five players were <a href="/wiki/Kellen_Winslow_II" title="Kellen Winslow II">Kellen Winslow II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Vilma" title="Jonathan Vilma">Jonathan Vilma</a>, <a href="/wiki/D._J._Williams_(linebacker)" title="D. J. Williams (linebacker)">D. J. Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Carey" title="Vernon Carey">Vernon Carey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vince_Wilfork" title="Vince Wilfork">Vince Wilfork</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2004">2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On July 27, 2004, the Washington Redskins signed Taylor to a six-year, <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">$</a>18.5 million contract that includes a $13.4 million <a href="/wiki/Signing_bonus" title="Signing bonus">signing bonus</a> and can be worth $40 million with incentives and bonuses. His contract also included a seventh-year option. Taylor became the first top ten pick to sign his contract in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldupdate1_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-heraldupdate1-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sacks_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-sacks-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 4, 2004, it was reported that Taylor fired his agents Eugene Mato and Jeff Moorad after he became unsatisfied with his contract. He fired them after other top ten picks signed their contracts and felt their deals were better in comparison. This marked his second time firing agents within five months after he fired agent <a href="/wiki/Drew_Rosenhaus" title="Drew Rosenhaus">Drew Rosenhaus</a> two days after the NFL draft. He immediately rehired Rosenhaus, who served as his agent for the remainder of his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenhaus_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenhaus-15">[15]</a></sup> Taylor also had an incident that involved his early departure during the NFL's Rookie Symposium which was mandatory for every incoming player from the draft and was held over four days. He left after the first day, but returned for the last two days at the urgence of representatives from the Redskins. Over his first three seasons, Taylor also was fined at least seven times for late hits, uniform violations and other infractions.
</p><p>Throughout training camp, Taylor competed for the job as the starting free safety against <a href="/wiki/Andre_Lott" title="Andre Lott">Andre Lott</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On August 9, 2004, Taylor made his professional NFL debut in the Redskins' preseason-opener as part of their second unit defense against the <a href="/wiki/2004_Denver_Broncos_season" title="2004 Denver Broncos season">Denver Broncos</a>. He recorded two interceptions in the second half off passes by rookie quarterback <a href="/wiki/Matt_Mauck" title="Matt Mauck">Matt Mauck</a>. During the third quarter, Taylor intercepted a pass intended for <a href="/wiki/Jeb_Putzier" title="Jeb Putzier">Jeb Putzier</a> and returned it for a three-yard touchdown to put Washington in the lead 10–9.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Head coach <a href="/wiki/Joe_Gibbs" title="Joe Gibbs">Joe Gibbs</a> named Taylor the backup free safety to begin the regular season, behind starter Andre Lott, after Taylor failed to surpass him on the depth chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rosenhaus_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Rosenhaus-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>He made his professional regular season debut in the <a href="/wiki/2004_Washington_Redskins_season" title="2004 Washington Redskins season">Washington Redskins'</a> season-opener against the <a href="/wiki/2004_Tampa_Bay_Buccaneers_season" title="2004 Tampa Bay Buccaneers season">Tampa Bay Buccaneers</a> and assisted on one tackle in their 16–10 victory. In Week 3, Taylor earned his first career start after surpassing Lott on the depth chart and recorded four combined tackles and deflected two passes in the Redskins' 21–18 loss to the <a href="/wiki/2004_Dallas_Cowboys_season" title="2004 Dallas Cowboys season">Dallas Cowboys</a>. On October 18, 2004, Taylor made four solo tackles, a pass deflection, a sack, and returned his first career interception for 45-yards during a 13–10 win at the <a href="/wiki/2004_Chicago_Bears_season" title="2004 Chicago Bears season">Chicago Bears</a>. He made his first career interception and sack during the game, recording both on quarterback <a href="/wiki/Brian_Griese" title="Brian Griese">Brian Griese</a>. In Week 12, he collected a season-high ten combined tackles (seven solo) and broke up a pass in a 16–7 loss at the <a href="/wiki/2004_Pittsburgh_Steelers_season" title="2004 Pittsburgh Steelers season">Pittsburgh Steelers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> He finished his rookie season in <a href="/wiki/2004_NFL_season" title="2004 NFL season">2004</a> with 76 combined tackles (60 solo), nine pass deflections, four interceptions, two forced fumbles, and a sack in 15 games and 13 starts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-career_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-career-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2005">2005</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2005">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Before the season started, Taylor switched his jersey number from No. 36 to No. 21 after it was available due to the departure of cornerback <a href="/wiki/Fred_Smoot" title="Fred Smoot">Fred Smoot</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Vikings" title="Minnesota Vikings">Minnesota Vikings</a>. Taylor kept the number when Smoot rejoined the Redskins in 2007, with Smoot opting to wear No. 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Assistant head coach/defensive coordinator <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Williams" title="Gregg Williams">Gregg Williams</a> opted to retain Taylor as the starting free safety to begin the regular season, alongside strong safety Ryan Clark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>He started the <a href="/wiki/2005_Washington_Redskins_season" title="2005 Washington Redskins season">Washington Redskins'</a> season-opener against the <a href="/wiki/2005_Chicago_Bears_season" title="2005 Chicago Bears season">Chicago Bears</a> and recorded four solo tackles and broke up a pass in their 9-7 victory. On November 11, 2005, it was reported that Taylor and teammate <a href="/wiki/Clinton_Portis" title="Clinton Portis">Clinton Portis</a> received fines from the NFL due to violating the league's uniform code during the Redskins' 17-10 home victory against the <a href="/wiki/2005_Philadelphia_Eagles_season" title="2005 Philadelphia Eagles season">Philadelphia Eagles</a> in Week 9. Taylor's fine was for $5,000 due to his choice of wearing white socks with a burgundy and gold striped pattern instead of the required all white socks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> He was inactive for the Redskins' Week 10 loss at the <a href="/wiki/2005_Tampa_Bay_Buccaneers_season" title="2005 Tampa Bay Buccaneers season">Tampa Bay Buccaneers</a> due to an injury. On January 1, 2006, he collected a season-high nine combined tackles and deflected two passes during a 31-20 win at the Philadelphia Eagles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats05_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-stats05-23">[23]</a></sup> Taylor finished the <a href="/wiki/2005_NFL_season" title="2005 NFL season">2005</a> season with 70 combined tackles (60 solo), a career-high ten pass deflections, two interceptions, two forced fumbles, and a sack in 15 games and 15 starts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-career_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-career-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Washington Redskins finished second in the <a href="/wiki/NFC_East" title="NFC East">NFC East</a> with a 10-6 record and received a wildcard berth. On January 7, 2006, Taylor started in his first career playoff game and recorded seven combined tackles and returned a fumble recovery for a 51-yard touchdown in the first quarter of their 17-10 victory at the Tampa Bay Buccaneers. Referee Mike Carey ejected Taylor in the third quarter after he spit in Buccaneers' running back <a href="/wiki/Michael_Pittman" title="Michael Pittman">Michael Pittman's</a> face. Defensive coordinator Gregg Williams stated Taylor denied the claim and he believed and stood by his player. During his rookie season, he allegedly spit in the face of wide receiver <a href="/wiki/T._J._Houshmandzadeh" title="T. J. Houshmandzadeh">T. J. Houshmandzadeh</a>, but was not reprehended due to the NFL being unable to recover clear video evidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Two days later, the NFL fined Taylor $17,000 for the incident.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Redskins were eliminated the following game after losing 20-10 to the <a href="/wiki/2005_Seattle_Seahawks_season" title="2005 Seattle Seahawks season">Seattle Seahawks</a> in the NFC Divisional Round. Taylor finished the loss with seven combined tackles and a pass deflection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stats05_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-stats05-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2006">2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Head coach Joe Gibbs named Taylor the starting free safety to start the regular season in 2006, along with starting strong safety <a href="/wiki/Adam_Archuleta" title="Adam Archuleta">Adam Archuleta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 15, 2006, Taylor collected a season-high ten combined tackles (eight solo) and deflected a pass during a 25-22 loss to the <a href="/wiki/2006_Tennessee_Titans_season" title="2006 Tennessee Titans season">Tennessee Titans</a>. In Week 9, Taylor made eight combined tackles and returned a blocked 35-yard field goal attempt by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Vanderjagt" title="Mike Vanderjagt">Mike Vanderjagt</a> for a 30-yard gain with less than six seconds left in the game. A facemask on Taylor by <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Kosier" title="Kyle Kosier">Kyle Kosier</a> added a 15-yard penalty and placed the Redskins in field goal range with an untimed down. Vanderjagt's game-winning field goal was blocked by <a href="/wiki/Troy_Vincent" title="Troy Vincent">Troy Vincent</a> and allowed Redskins' kicker <a href="/wiki/Nick_Novak" title="Nick Novak">Nick Novak</a> to kick a 47-yard game-winning field goal to defeat the <a href="/wiki/2006_Dallas_Cowboys_season" title="2006 Dallas Cowboys season">Dallas Cowboys</a> 22-19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> On November 26, 2006, Taylor recorded five combined tackles, two pass deflections, and intercepted a pass by quarterback <a href="/wiki/Jake_Delhomme" title="Jake Delhomme">Jake Delhomme</a> in the Redskins' 17-13 win against the <a href="/wiki/2006_Carolina_Panthers_season" title="2006 Carolina Panthers season">Carolina Panthers</a>. He made a key fourth down tackle on Jake Delhomme in the fourth quarter and his interception sealed the Redskins' victory. He was voted as the NFC Defensive Player of the Week for his performance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He finished the <a href="/wiki/2006_NFL_season" title="2006 NFL season">2006</a> season with a career-high 111 combined tackles (86 solo), six pass deflections, three forced fumbles, and an interception in 16 games and 16 starts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-career_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-career-19">[19]</a></sup> During the season, Washington Redskins assistant coach <a href="/wiki/Gregg_Williams" title="Gregg Williams">Gregg Williams</a> frequently called Taylor the best athlete that he had ever coached.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foxnews.com_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-foxnews.com-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;float: right; clear: right; margin:0 0 1.5em 1.5em"><caption>External video</caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;text-align: left"><img alt="" data-file-height="128" data-file-width="128" decoding="async" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Nuvola_apps_kaboodle.svg/16px-Nuvola_apps_kaboodle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Nuvola_apps_kaboodle.svg/24px-Nuvola_apps_kaboodle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Nuvola_apps_kaboodle.svg/32px-Nuvola_apps_kaboodle.svg.png 2x" width="16"/> <a class="external text" href="http://www.nfl.com/videos/nfl-videos/0ap2000000308486/Sean-Taylor-hits-Brian-Moorman" rel="nofollow">Sean Taylor's hit on Brian Moorman</a></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Even while playing on a struggling Redskins defensive unit, Taylor's impact on the field was recognized when he was named a first alternate to the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_Conference" title="National Football Conference">NFC's</a> <a href="/wiki/2007_Pro_Bowl" title="2007 Pro Bowl">2007 Pro Bowl</a> team. When the NFC's first choice for safety, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Dawkins" title="Brian Dawkins">Brian Dawkins</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Eagles" title="Philadelphia Eagles">Philadelphia Eagles</a>, chose not to play in the Pro Bowl due to an injury, Taylor was named to the vacated spot, marking his first and only Pro Bowl appearance. A crushing hit by Taylor on <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punter_(American_football)" title="Punter (American football)">punter</a> <a href="/wiki/Brian_Moorman" title="Brian Moorman">Brian Moorman</a> in the Pro Bowl created much fan and media discussion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="2007">2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Prior to the start of the 2007 season, <i><a href="/wiki/Sports_Illustrated" title="Sports Illustrated">Sports Illustrated</a></i> named Taylor the hardest-hitting player in the NFL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the season, in a rare interview, he was quoted as saying, "[Y]ou play a kid's game for a king's ransom. And if you don't take it serious enough, eventually one day you're going to say, 'Oh, I could have done this, I could have done that.'" The season appeared to represent a personal turnaround for Taylor, as teammates said that he had finally gotten his life straightened out because of his daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also before the season, the Redskins decided to use Taylor in a more traditional free safety role with less responsibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotoworld.com_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-rotoworld.com-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the time of his death, Taylor was tied for the most interceptions in the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_Conference" title="National Football Conference">National Football Conference</a> and second in the league with 5 despite having missed Weeks 11 and 12 with a knee injury. Playing at a high level,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rotoworld.com_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-rotoworld.com-32">[32]</a></sup> Taylor had also compiled 42 tackles, 9 passes defended and a forced fumble.
</p><p>On December 18, 2007, Taylor was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Posthumous_recognition" title="Posthumous recognition">posthumously</a> voted to his second Pro Bowl, becoming the first deceased player in NFL history to be elected to the Pro Bowl. During the Pro Bowl, the Redskins players who had been selected, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Samuels" title="Chris Samuels">Chris Samuels</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Cooley_(American_football)" title="Chris Cooley (American football)">Chris Cooley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Albright" title="Ethan Albright">Ethan Albright</a>, wore #21 to honor Taylor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Like the Redskins had done earlier in the season, the NFC lined up with just one safety on the first play of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_issues">Legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following Taylor's early departure from the University of Miami and his drafting by the Redskins, a number of criminal troubles plagued his NFL career.
</p><p>In October 2004, Taylor was arrested in <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_County,_Virginia" title="Fairfax County, Virginia">Fairfax County, Virginia</a> on a charge of <a href="/wiki/Driving_under_the_influence" title="Driving under the influence">driving under the influence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a> following a birthday party in Washington, D.C. for former Redskins receiver <a href="/wiki/Rod_Gardner" title="Rod Gardner">Rod Gardner</a>. Pulled over for driving 82 mph on the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_495_(Capital_Beltway)" title="Interstate 495 (Capital Beltway)">Beltway</a>, where the speed limit is 55 mph, Taylor failed a field sobriety test and then refused a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blood_alcohol" title="Blood alcohol">blood alcohol</a> (BAC) test, which resulted in his arrest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> A Fairfax County judge acquitted Taylor of the charges in March 2005 after viewing a videotape of Taylor's roadside sobriety tests that, according to the judge, failed to demonstrate obvious <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication" title="Alcohol intoxication">intoxication</a>. Taylor was, however, convicted for refusing to take a blood alcohol test requested by a <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> state police officer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> But when this case was heard on appeal in March 2005, Taylor was acquitted of refusing to take the BAC test, with the judge ruling there was a lack of probable cause for the request.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Taylor was sought by police following a June 2005 incident in which bullets were fired into a stolen vehicle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>  During an April 2006 trial, Taylor pled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_contest" title="No contest">no contest</a> to misdemeanor battery and assault charges; he was placed on 18 months probation and ordered to support ten Miami-Dade County schools by speaking about the importance of education and donating $1,000 to each school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-espn_Reds_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-espn_Reds-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On November 17, 2007, burglars pried open a window to Taylor's empty home and rifled through a desk and safe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnnreport_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnnreport-40">[40]</a></sup>
On November 26 at 1:45 a.m., Taylor was shot in the upper leg by an intruder. His fiancée Jackie García (a niece of actor <a href="/wiki/Andy_Garc%C3%ADa" title="Andy García">Andy García</a>) and their 18-month-old daughter Jackie were not injured.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_York_Post_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Post-41">[41]</a></sup>
Taylor had extensive blood loss through a severed <a href="/wiki/Femoral_artery" title="Femoral artery">femoral artery</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heraldupdate1_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-heraldupdate1-8">[8]</a></sup> and he died on November 27.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wshpost_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-wshpost-43">[43]</a></sup> Taylor's funeral was held at the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Bank_Convocation_Center" title="Ocean Bank Convocation Center">Pharmed Arena</a> at <a href="/wiki/Florida_International_University" title="Florida International University">Florida International University</a> in Miami on December 3, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>On November 30, four men – Venjah K. Hunte, 20; Eric Rivera, Jr., 17; Jason Scott Mitchell, 19; and Charles Kendrick Lee Wardlow, 18 – were arrested. Police said more than one confessed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-threedetained_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-threedetained-45">[45]</a></sup>
All four men were charged with <a href="/wiki/Felony_murder_rule_(Florida)#Second_degree_murder" title="Felony murder rule (Florida)">felony second-degree murder</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_burglary" title="Armed burglary">armed burglary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Home_invasion" title="Home invasion">home invasion robbery with a firearm</a>, charges which carried a maximum of life in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-charged_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-charged-46">[46]</a></sup> In May 2008, a fifth suspect, 16-year-old Timothy Brown, was charged with <a href="/wiki/Felony_murder_rule_(Florida)" title="Felony murder rule (Florida)">first-degree murder</a> and burglary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> All the murder charges were subsequently increased to first-degree murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The death penalty, however, was not sought because the gunman, Rivera, was 17 years old.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remembrance">Remembrance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Remembrance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NFL" title="NFL">NFL</a> recognized the death of Taylor by placing a No. 21 decal on the back of all NFL players' helmets during all Week 13 games; additionally, a moment of silence was held before each game that week. Players on other teams were given the option to continue wearing the decals in subsequent weeks.
</p><p>Taylor was posthumously voted starting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_safety" title="Free safety">free safety</a> for the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_Conference" title="National Football Conference">NFC</a> team for the <a href="/wiki/2008_Pro_Bowl" title="2008 Pro Bowl">2008 Pro Bowl</a> and voted a second team All-Pro.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> During their first defensive play, the NFC defense took the field with only 10 players in honor of Taylor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Redskins had the number 21 painted on the field, at a parking lot entrance and in the Redskins Ring of Fame, all three of which became temporary memorials. In addition to the black No. 21 sticker on the back of every NFL helmet, the Redskins also wore the number as a patch on player uniforms, warmup shirts and coaching staff jackets, as well as unveiling a banner bearing his name and number. His locker at <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Redskins Park</a> was encased in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plexiglas" title="Plexiglas">Plexiglas</a> and left the same way Taylor had left it. The organization also established a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trust_fund" title="Trust fund">trust fund</a> for Taylor's daughter, Jackie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first Redskins game after Taylor's death was on December 2, 2007, which was against the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> and held at <a href="/wiki/FedExField" title="FedExField">FedExField</a>. The game began with the Redskins defense playing with 10 men on the field instead of the usual 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Players signaled to the sky, holding up the numbers two and one, on numerous occasions. The team requested everyone arrive 25 minutes before the start of the game at 12:40pm and played a four-minute remembrance video, held a moment of silence and gave attendees commemorative towels with Taylor's number on them in his honor and memory.
</p><p>Teammates <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Cooley_(American_football)" title="Chris Cooley (American football)">Chris Cooley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Samuels" title="Chris Samuels">Chris Samuels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Albright" title="Ethan Albright">Ethan Albright</a> all wore jerseys with No. 21 during the <a href="/wiki/2008_Pro_Bowl" title="2008 Pro Bowl">2008 Pro Bowl</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The three jerseys were auctioned off and the proceeds donated to the Sean Taylor Memorial Trust Fund.
</p><p>The murder was covered on the <a href="/wiki/Investigation_Discovery" title="Investigation Discovery">Investigation Discovery</a> show <i>The Perfect Murder</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trial">Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On May 12, 2008, it was announced the suspects, if convicted, would not face the death penalty, but may be subjected to life imprisonment because the suspected gunman, Eric Rivera, was only 17 at the time of the shooting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> On May 15, 2008, Venjah Hunte, one of the five suspects in Taylor's murder, accepted a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plea_deal" title="Plea deal">plea deal</a> and was sentenced to 29 years in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> On April 1, 2009, Hunte petitioned the court to withdraw his guilty plea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although originally scheduled for April 7, 2008, the trial of the men charged with Sean Taylor's murder was postponed to June 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> It was postponed by petition of the defense, saying that there were still hundreds of potential witnesses who needed to be interviewed before the trial could proceed. The trial was then delayed for a third time on June 9, 2009, for unspecified reasons. A new trial date was set for January 18, 2010, and was postponed again with a trial date of March 14, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> After another delay, the trial was set to begin on January 30, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 20, 2012, a judge set a trial date of April 16 for Eric Rivera, Jr., the alleged gunman in the case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> However, Rivera fired his lawyer on March 16,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> eventually causing delays. On July 12, the judge postponed the trial to November 5,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> but that date was further moved back to April 5, 2013, due to a scheduling conflict with the chief prosecutor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> On April 2, 2013, the trial was again postponed to August 12, 2013. In August, the trial was postponed to September 16, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> This was rescheduled to October 15, 2013. During his trial, Rivera testified that someone else fired the gun.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsnet-2014-01-23_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-sportsnet-2014-01-23-66">[66]</a></sup> On November 4, 2013, a jury found Eric Rivera, Jr. guilty of second-degree murder and armed burglary, which could carry a life imprisonment sentence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> On January 23, 2014, Miami-Dade Circuit Judge Dennis Murphy sentenced Rivera to 57​<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>⁄<sub>2</sub></span> years in prison.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sportsnet-2014-01-23_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-sportsnet-2014-01-23-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 10, 2014, Jason Scott Mitchell was convicted in Taylor's murder and sentenced to <a href="/wiki/Life_imprisonment" title="Life imprisonment">life imprisonment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p><p>Charles Wardlow, the fourth person charged with the death of Taylor, was sentenced to 30 years in prison on April 1, 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>The final defendant, Timmy Lee Brown, pled guilty on April 8, 2015, and will serve 18 years in prison under his plea agreement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sean_Taylor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Taylor was inducted posthumously into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins_Ring_of_Fame" title="Washington Redskins Ring of Fame">Washington Redskins Ring of Fame</a> on November 30, 2008. He joined 42 others and was the first player introduced to the ring since <a href="/wiki/Gary_Clark" title="Gary Clark">Gary Clark</a> was inducted in late 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tight end <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chris_Cooley_(American_football)" title="Chris Cooley (American football)">Chris Cooley</a>, tackle <a href="/wiki/Chris_Samuels" title="Chris Samuels">Chris Samuels</a>, and long snapper <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Albright" title="Ethan Albright">Ethan Albright</a> honored Taylor at the <a href="/wiki/2008_Pro_Bowl" title="2008 Pro Bowl">2008 Pro Bowl</a> by wearing his number 21 rather than their usual numbers. To honor Taylor in the first game following his death, the Redskins defense lined up ten players on the field against the <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Bills" title="Buffalo Bills">Buffalo Bills</a> leaving Taylor's traditional position of free safety vacant for the first defensive play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Multiple players have honored Taylor by donning his jersey numbers that he wore during his college and professional career. During his college career Taylor wore No. 26 for the Miami Hurricanes. As a rookie in 2004, he wore No. 36 before switching to No. 21 for the remainder of his career. Taylor's former teammate and fellow safety <a href="/wiki/Ryan_Clark_(American_football)" title="Ryan Clark (American football)">Ryan Clark</a> returned to the Washington Redskins in 2014 after an eight-year stint with the <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Steelers" title="Pittsburgh Steelers">Pittsburgh Steelers</a>. During practices, Clark wore No. 21 in Taylor's honor. The Washington Redskins' second round pick in the <a href="/wiki/2016_NFL_Draft" title="2016 NFL Draft">2016 NFL Draft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Su%27a_Cravens" title="Su'a Cravens">Su'a Cravens</a>, chose No. 36 upon joining the Redskins as a rookie in dedication to Taylor. Cravens switched to No. 39 in 2017 after safety <a href="/wiki/D._J._Swearinger" title="D. J. Swearinger">D. J. Swearinger</a>  requested No. 36 after signing with the Redskins as a free agent.
Swearinger wore No. 36 throughout four teams in his professional career as a way to honor Taylor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> On June 15, 2019, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Giants" title="New York Giants">New York Giants'</a> announced safety <a href="/wiki/Landon_Collins" title="Landon Collins">Landon Collins</a> would switch from No. 27 to No. 21 to honor Taylor. Collins also wore Taylor's college number (No. 26) during his collegiate career at Alabama as a way to pay tribute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p>
