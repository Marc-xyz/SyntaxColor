<p><b>Mark Fricker</b> was a mid-distance <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">Track and field</a> runner who achieved the sub-<a href="/wiki/Four-minute_mile" title="Four-minute mile">four-minute mile</a> in the 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Fricker failed to win many of the races he ran because he did not possess the fast finishing kick of his contemporaries like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steve_Scott_(athlete)" title="Steve Scott (athlete)">Steve Scott</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Walker_(athlete)" title="John Walker (athlete)">John Walker</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eamon_Coughlan" title="Eamon Coughlan">Eamon Coughlan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Maree" title="Sydney Maree">Sydney Maree</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ray_Flynn" title="Ray Flynn">Ray Flynn</a> and the other legends of 1980s mile racing.  His only actionable strategy to win was to run hard from the beginning of the race.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Context">Context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Fricker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the time when professionalism in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Track_and_field_athletics" title="Track and field athletics">Track and Field</a> was still relatively new and pacesetters were not written into the rule books, Fricker became a popular addition to major races because he set up the competition to run fast times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Fricker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was a high school star from <a href="/wiki/Hemet_High_School" title="Hemet High School">Hemet High School</a>.  In 1977, Fricker won the mile at the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Arcadia_Invitational" title="Arcadia Invitational">Arcadia Invitational</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and finished second in the <a href="/wiki/CIF_California_State_Meet" title="CIF California State Meet">CIF California State Meet</a>, losing on the last lap to kicker Mark Stillman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  He then ran for <a href="/wiki/Oregon_State_University" title="Oregon State University">Oregon State University</a>, where he still holds the school record for the 1500-meter run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_peak">Career peak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Fricker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career peak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>1982 was his peak year, when he managed to achieve the number 10 ranking amongst milers in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  He and his bright red hair got lots of TV time in the lead of big races, like the <a href="/wiki/USA_Outdoor_Track_and_Field_Championships" title="USA Outdoor Track and Field Championships">USA Outdoor Track and Field Championships</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> only to be engulfed by the fast finishers.  Steve Scott's training log credits Fricker.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Scott set the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_records_in_track_and_field" title="United States records in track and field">American Record</a>, Walker set the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_records_in_athletics" title="New Zealand records in athletics">New Zealand National Record</a>, and Flynn set the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irish_records_in_athletics" title="Irish records in athletics">Irish National Record</a> in the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Bislett_Games" title="Bislett Games">Bislett</a> <a href="/wiki/Dream_Mile#Dream_Mile_(Bislett)" title="Dream Mile">Dream Mile</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oslo" title="Oslo">Oslo</a>, Norway following Fricker's pacesetting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  To date, of those three records, only the American record has been improved upon <a href="/wiki/Alan_Webb_(runner)#Post-collegiate/professional" title="Alan Webb (runner)">just once</a>, over 25 years later.
</p><p>In 1984, Fricker finished ninth in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Olympic_Trials_(track_and_field)" title="United States Olympic Trials (track and field)">U.S. Olympic Trials</a> after leading the race with almost a quarter mile to go.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fricker's career extended for most of the 1980s based on his ability to set up great races<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fricker's record for a 1500-meter run has been ranked as the 658th best 1500m run time ever recorded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mark_Fricker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fricker's daughter, McKayla won the 2014 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NCAA_Women%27s_Division_II_Outdoor_Track_and_Field_Championships" title="NCAA Women's Division II Outdoor Track and Field Championships">NCAA Women's Division II Outdoor Track and Field Championships</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/800_meters" title="800 meters">800 meters</a> while representing <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Pacific_University" title="Seattle Pacific University">Seattle Pacific University</a>.  After college, she was the training partner of <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Efraimson" title="Alexa Efraimson">Alexa Efraimson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
