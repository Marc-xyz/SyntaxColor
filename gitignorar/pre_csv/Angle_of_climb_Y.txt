	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Angle of climb</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, <b>climb gradient</b> is the ratio between distance travelled over the ground and <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> gained, and is expressed as a percentage. The <b>angle of climb</b> can be defined as the angle between a horizontal plane representing the Earth's surface and the actual flight path followed by the aircraft during its ascent.
</p><p>The speed of an <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">aircraft type</a> at which the angle of climb is largest is called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V-speed" title="V-speed">V<sub>X</sub></a>. It is always slower than V<sub>Y</sub>, the speed for the best <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_climb" title="Rate of climb">rate of climb</a>.
As the latter gives the quickest way for gaining altitude levels, regardless of the distance covered during such a maneuver, it is more relevant to cruising. The maximum angle of climb on the other hand is where the aircraft gains the most altitude in a given distance, regardless of the time needed for the maneuver. This is important for clearing an obstacle, and therefore is the speed a pilot uses when executing a "short field" takeoff.
V<sub>X</sub> increases with altitude and V<sub>Y</sub> decreases with altitude until they converge at the airplane's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_ceiling" title="Absolute ceiling">absolute ceiling</a>.
</p><p><b> Best <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angle</a> of climb (BAOC) </b> airspeed for an airplane is the speed at which the maximum <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excess_thrust" title="Excess thrust">excess thrust</a> is available. Excess thrust is the difference between the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_drag" title="Aerodynamic drag">drag</a> of the aircraft, and the thrust output of the powerplant. For a jet aircraft, this speed is very close to the speed at which the total minimum drag occurs.
</p>
