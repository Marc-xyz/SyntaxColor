</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_McKay_(attorney)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>McKay, a member of a prominent Republican family in the state, attended <a href="/wiki/Seattle_Preparatory_School" title="Seattle Preparatory School">Seattle Preparatory School</a> and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a>, where he received a Bachelor of Arts degree in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a> in 1978. After working as an aide to Congressman <a href="/wiki/Joel_Pritchard" title="Joel Pritchard">Joel Pritchard</a> (R-WA) in 1978–79, McKay earned his law degree at <a href="/wiki/Creighton_University_School_of_Law" title="Creighton University School of Law">Creighton University School of Law</a> in <a href="/wiki/Omaha,_Nebraska" title="Omaha, Nebraska">Omaha, Nebraska</a> in 1982.
</p><p>He was admitted to the Washington State Bar and joined the Seattle law firm of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lane_Powell" title="Lane Powell">Lane Powell Spears Lubersky</a> in 1982, eventually becoming a litigation partner with that firm. He then joined Cairncross &amp; Hempelmann in Seattle, leading its litigation group and serving as a member of its management committee.
</p><p>He was admitted to practice before the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court" title="United States District Court">United States District Court</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ninth_Circuit_Court_of_Appeals" title="Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals">Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a>.
</p>

<p>McKay was one of eight <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._attorney" title="U.S. attorney">U.S. attorneys</a> fired by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_W._Bush_administration" title="George W. Bush administration">Bush administration</a> in 2006 which were, after the fact, publicly described as being for performance-related issues related to "policy, priorities and management."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment_as_U.S._Attorney">Appointment as U.S. Attorney</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_McKay_(attorney)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Appointment as U.S. Attorney">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McKay was appointed <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney" title="United States Attorney">United States Attorney</a> by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> in October 2001. He had received a positive evaluation seven months before he was dismissed. Among his noteworthy achievements the successful prosecution of terrorist <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Ressam" title="Ahmed Ressam">Ahmed Ressam</a>. Deputy Attorney General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_B._Comey" title="James B. Comey">James B. Comey</a> gave McKay the additional responsibility of overseeing a pilot program for a computer system (called Law Enforcement Information Exchange) for his success he earned the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>'s highest civilian honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle_Times" title="Seattle Times">Seattle Times</a></i> noted in February 2007, <i>"One of the most persistent rumors in Seattle legal circles is that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Justice Department</a> forced McKay, a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a>, to resign to appease <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington State</a> Republicans angry over the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_gubernatorial_election,_2004" title="Washington gubernatorial election, 2004">2004 governor's race</a>. Some believe McKay's dismissal was retribution for his failure to convene a federal <a href="/wiki/Grand_jury" title="Grand jury">grand jury</a> to investigate allegations of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voter_fraud" title="Voter fraud">voter fraud</a> in the race."</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 17, 2007, the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seattle_Times" title="Seattle Times">Seattle Times</a></i> reported,
"Former Republican congressman <a href="/wiki/Rick_White_(politician)" title="Rick White (politician)">Rick White</a>, one of three candidates the Republicans have submitted to replace John McKay as U.S. attorney for Western Washington, cannot practice law in the state. White's license was suspended by the state Supreme Court in August 2003 for failure to pay his bar dues. He was reinstated to the bar in 2005 after paying a small fee, but currently holds an 'inactive' status", which is common for attorneys that are not actively practicing law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In congressional testimony, <a href="/wiki/Kyle_Sampson" title="Kyle Sampson">Kyle Sampson</a>, chief of staff to Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Gonzales" title="Alberto Gonzales">Alberto Gonzales</a>, testified that McKay may have been fired at least in part because of his advocacy for a more-aggressive investigation into the <a href="/wiki/Cold_case" title="Cold case">cold case</a> murder of Assistant U.S. Attorney <a href="/wiki/Thomas_C._Wales" title="Thomas C. Wales">Thomas C. Wales</a> of Seattle.  His pursuit of the case and subsequent firing were the subjects of an article by <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Toobin" title="Jeffrey Toobin">Jeffrey Toobin</a> in the August 6, 2007 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a>.</i>  Toobin noted that Gonzales assured Wales's family that the investigation was still a top priority of the Justice Department.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-U.S._Attorney_career">Post-U.S. Attorney career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_McKay_(attorney)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-U.S. Attorney career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 21, 2007, <a href="/wiki/Getty_Images" title="Getty Images">Getty Images</a> announced that McKay would join the corporation as senior vice president and general counsel, based in their headquarters in Seattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 17, 2007, Jonathan Klein, CEO of Getty Images, announced that McKay would be stepping down as general counsel to return to the law faculty at Seattle University where he will teach full-time. McKay is currently a partner at Davis Wright Tremaine in Seattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>During his career as a U.S. Attorney, McKay was the prosecutor of Canadian marijuana activist <a href="/wiki/Marc_Emery" title="Marc Emery">Marc Emery</a> for marijuana seed distribution from Canada. Since that time, McKay has called for reform of marijuana laws such that its sale be legally regulated and taxed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
