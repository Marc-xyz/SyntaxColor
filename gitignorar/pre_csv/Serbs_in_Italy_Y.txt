<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Graziadio_Isaia_Ascoli" title="Graziadio Isaia Ascoli">Graziadio Isaia Ascoli</a>, the 19th century writer <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_de_Rubertis" title="Giovanni de Rubertis">Giovanni de Rubertis</a> considered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molise_Slavs" title="Molise Slavs"><i>Schiavoni</i> (Slavs) or <i>Dalmati</i> (Dalmatians) of Molise</a> in Italy to be Serbs that were brought there by <a href="/wiki/Skanderbeg" title="Skanderbeg">Skanderbeg</a> during his <a href="/wiki/Skanderbeg%27s_Italian_expedition" title="Skanderbeg's Italian expedition">Italian expedition</a> in 1460-1462 along with the <a href="/wiki/Albanians" title="Albanians">Albanians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1497 Italian court poet Rogeri de Pacienza di Nardo wrote about a group of Serbian refugees who left the Despotate of <a href="/wiki/%C4%90ura%C4%91_Brankovi%C4%87" title="Đurađ Branković">Đurađ Branković</a> to settle in the village of <a href="/wiki/Gioia_del_Colle" title="Gioia del Colle">Gioia del Colle</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bari" title="Bari">Bari</a>, Italy. He describes how they sang and danced the <a href="/wiki/Kolo_(dance)" title="Kolo (dance)">kolo (dance)</a> in honour of the Queen of Naples, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_del_Balzo" title="Isabella del Balzo">Isabella del Balzo</a>. The names of the singers that Pacienza wrote down are common Serbian names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1782 the first Serbian school opened in <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>, and in the 19th century the Serbian Orthodox "Saint Spyridon church" in Trieste was built near the Ponte Rosso square.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NSK1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-NSK1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some 40,000 Serbs live in northern Italy. In <a href="/wiki/Arzignano" title="Arzignano">Arzignano</a> there are thousands of Serbs from all over former Yugoslavia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>, 10,000-15,000 Serbs live in the city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trieste_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trieste-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><span data-sort-value="7004469580000000000♠">46,958</span> (2013 census), "Foreign citizens" from Serbia (excl. Kosovo) <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Triestine_Serbs">Triestine Serbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Triestine Serbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Triestine Serbs</b> (<a href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Serbian Cyrillic alphabet">Serbian Cyrillic</a>: <span lang="sr-Cyrl">Тршћански Срби /Tršćanski Srbi</span>; <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Serbi Triestini</i>) are the ethnic <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbs</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">Trieste</a>, present in the city since the 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medakovic_1987_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medakovic_1987-8">[8]</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_2">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Serb merchants and ship-owners established a community in Trieste at the start of the 18th century, most primarily originating from <a href="/wiki/Sarajevo" title="Sarajevo">Sarajevo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trebinje" title="Trebinje">Trebinje</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Kotor" title="Bay of Kotor">Bay of Kotor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorup_2006_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gorup_2006-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1751 <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa" title="Maria Theresa">Maria Theresa</a> of the Austro-Hungarian Empire proclaimed religious freedom in the city, and the Serbs and Greeks of Trieste built the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Spyridon_Church,_Trieste" title="Saint Spyridon Church, Trieste">Saint Spyridon church</a> that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peoplesaint_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-peoplesaint-10">[10]</a></sup> The most influential of the wealthy Serbian merchants of the time were the Kurtović, Gopčević, Vojnović, and Miletić families, who owned most the structures and dock area of the "Porto Vecchio" (Old Port).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medaković_1987_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Medaković_1987-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1766 Trieste's Serbs numbered 50, by 1780 they grew to 200.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbian_School">Serbian School</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Serbian School">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1782 the Serbian community of Trieste began expressing its desire for a <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian</a>-language day school, a place for their children to be passed down Serbian culture and language. Jovan Miletic, a wealthy Serb merchant in Trieste, donated 24,000 florins to build a Serbian Elementary School in 1787. On July 1, 1792, the local government approved its opening and the "Jovan Miletić" private school began official operation, located in the city center, right beside the Saint Spyridon Church. A night school and reading room were opened in 1911.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medakovic_1987_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Medakovic_1987-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1911 an asylum was added to the school, for Serbian political refugees, due to the constant warfare and bloodshed occurring between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian" title="Austro-Hungarian">Austro-Hungarian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> empires on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_Peninsula" title="Balkan Peninsula">Balkan Peninsula</a>. The school represented a pillar of the Serbian community of Trieste, where the children of the wealthy Serbian merchants went to school and integrated into the city's community. In 1973 the school was shut down due to lack of student enrollment and became a Sunday school for Serbian language and culture. Velimir Djerasimović, the school's principal and teacher since 1927, retired in 1973. Djerasimović is the father of Italian film actors <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Rassimov" title="Ivan Rassimov">Ivan Rassimov</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rada_Rassimov" title="Rada Rassimov">Rada Rassimov</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Spyridon_Church">Saint Spyridon Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Saint Spyridon Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1728" data-file-width="2304" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg/300px-Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg/450px-Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg/600px-Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trieste_Serb-orthodox_church_of_San-Spiridione3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Saint_Spyridon_Church,_Trieste" title="Saint Spyridon Church, Trieste">St. Spyridon Serbian Orthodox Church</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 1782 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox" title="Serbian Orthodox">Serbian Orthodox</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox" title="Greek Orthodox">Greek Orthodox</a> communities of Trieste split due to major disagreements concerning church rituals and language-usage, at which point the Greek community built its own church, dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Saint_Nicholas" title="Saint Nicholas">Saint Nicholas</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neoclassical</a> style, and the Serbs continued to use the original church of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Spyridon" title="Saint Spyridon">Saint Spyridon</a>. In 1861 the Serb community demolished the original church, and rebuilt it in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbo-Byzantine" title="Serbo-Byzantine">Serbo-Byzantine</a> style<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Serbs_in_Italy#Dubious" title="Talk:Serbs in Italy">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup>, in order to "stamp their identity architecturally in the midst of a baroque Austro-Italian city".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="Verification of this source citation failed to find it supporting or even relevant to the material that cites it (December 2014)">irrelevant citation</span></a></i>]</sup> The church's construction was completed seven years later, in 1868. With the added capacity for 1600 worshippers, it was for a long time the second largest Serbian church in the world.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs a reliable source by a historian of architecture. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The church is filled with liturgical masterpieces of the time, works in gold from the 17th and 18th centuries, antique Orthodox icons and handmade books, making it an important monument to Serbian history and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-peoplesaint_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-peoplesaint-10">[10]</a></sup> The church was designed by Italian architect <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Maciachini" title="Carlo Maciachini">Carlo Maciachini</a>, featuring four cupolas and one large main dome adorned in an off-blue color. In the 1800s the Serbian population in Trieste numbered around 200 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catalan_2011_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Catalan_2011-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Under_Fascist_Rule">Under Fascist Rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Under Fascist Rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1918, at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, Trieste became part of Italy and social life drastically changed for the Serbs and other minorities of Trieste. Due to the contentious national border with <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a>, Italian society became increasingly hostile towards all <a href="/wiki/Slavs" title="Slavs">Slavs</a> in Trieste including the Serbs, and anti-Slavic racism began to flourish in Italy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catalan_2011_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Catalan_2011-14">[14]</a></sup> The anti-Slavic feelings in Trieste were present already before the War, embodied by the local Liberal-National Party, led by Giuseppe Cuzzi, whose aim was to make Trieste completely <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> and Italian. The anti-Slavic propaganda focused on the idea that Slavic people were barbaric and could not integrate properly into a civilized society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Catalan_2011_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-Catalan_2011-14">[14]</a></sup> Tensions came to a hilt in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_War_Two" title="World War Two">World War Two</a>, when the Germans, who had occupied Northern Italy in September 1943, built the only Nazi Extermination Camp in Italy, "<a href="/wiki/Risiera_di_San_Sabba" title="Risiera di San Sabba">Risiera di San Sabba</a>", on the outskirts of Trieste. Three thousand Jews, Serbs and other Slavs were executed here in 1944 while thousands more were imprisoned awaiting transfer to other extermination camps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monuments">Monuments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Monuments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG/300px-Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG/450px-Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG/600px-Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trieste-Canal_Grande-DSCF1420.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Palazzo Gopcevich</div></div></div>
<p>Besides the Saint Spyridon Church and the Jovan Miletić Serbian School, the Serbs of Trieste contributed to several other important landmarks of the city. The Gopčević family built the "Palazzo Gopcevich" on the Canal Grande, near the Serbian Church, in 1850 in commemoration to the heroes who fought for the <a href="/wiki/Second_Serbian_Uprising" title="Second Serbian Uprising">independence of Serbia from the Ottoman Empire</a> (1814). Cristoforo Popovich owned many famous merchant ships in Trieste, some of the largest in the Adriatic: the "Tartana", "Il Feroce Dalmata", "La Forza" and the "Ripatriato", and was instrumental to the Russian-side in the Crimean War (1853-1856). Cristoforo Scuglievich (Skuljevic) built the Palazzo Scuglievich in the mid-1800s along the banks of the city, and donated the palace in his will to the Serbian community of Trieste; today it is owned by the Serb community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Medaković_1987_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Medaković_1987-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbian_Writers">Serbian Writers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Serbian Writers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Serbian community of Trieste had a deep and long-lasting relationship with Serbian writers from the 18th century to today. Iconic <a href="/wiki/Serbian_literature" title="Serbian literature">Serbian writers</a> and linguists such as <a href="/wiki/Vuk_Karad%C5%BEi%C4%87" title="Vuk Karadžić">Vuk Karadžić</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dositej_Obradovi%C4%87" title="Dositej Obradović">Dositej Obradović</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petar_II_Petrovi%C4%87-Njego%C5%A1" title="Petar II Petrović-Njegoš">Petar II Petrović-Njegoš</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zaharije_Orfelin" title="Zaharije Orfelin">Zaharije Orfelin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pavle_Solari%C4%87" title="Pavle Solarić">Pavle Solarić</a> were inspired by Trieste and its developed Serbian community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorup_2006_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gorup_2006-9">[9]</a></sup>  They wrote many works on Trieste's influence on Serbian "pre-Romanticism" and cultural development during a time when the Serbs lacked statehood and cultural expression. Serbian linguist and creator of the standardized Serbian language, <a href="/wiki/Vuk_Karad%C5%BEi%C4%87" title="Vuk Karadžić">Vuk Karadžić</a>, kept in constant contact with Trieste's Serb community from his home in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. In 1813 Vuk called upon the Triestan Serbs to subscribe to the Serbian-language newspaper, "Novine Serbske" promoting the newly standardized Serbian language, and more than sixty copies of the standard Serbian dictionary were bought in 1814.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorup_2006_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gorup_2006-9">[9]</a></sup> The "father of contemporary Serbian literature" <a href="/wiki/Lukijan_Mu%C5%A1icki" title="Lukijan Mušicki">Lukijan Mušicki</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2014)">according to whom?</span></a></i>]</sup>, wrote an ode to Serbian merchants of Trieste in 1835, and revolutionary writer Dositej Obradović tutored the children of the wealthy Serb merchants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gorup_2006_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gorup_2006-9">[9]</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Serbian_epic_poetry" title="Serbian epic poetry">Serbian poets</a> and folklorists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2014)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> even worked in the Saint Spyridon Church for a time. An almost universal feeling of inspiration and admiration of Trieste's wealthy Serb community was shared by their Balkan-Serb contemporaries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2014)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Day">Modern Day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modern Day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, Serbian-born Serbs represent the largest foreign-born community in Trieste, numbering around 10,000, although some figures state anywhere up to 18,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISTAT_2010_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-ISTAT_2010-16">[16]</a></sup> The overwhelming majority of Serbs in Trieste today descend from the immigration wave following the <a href="/wiki/Yugoslav_Wars" title="Yugoslav Wars">Yugoslav Wars</a> of the 90s. Recently the Serbian Orthodox Society in Trieste led by Bogoljub Stojičević has called on the local government to grant the Serb Community of Trieste cultural autonomy and reinstate the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jovan_Mileti%C4%87&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jovan Miletić (page does not exist)">Jovan Miletić</a> Serbian school as a full-time school since it downgraded to a Sunday school due to inactivity in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Since 2009 the Serbian community of Trieste, namely the Serbian Association "Vuk Karadzic", has been organizing an annual Balkan-style trumpet festival on the outskirts of Trieste called "Guca na Krasu" (Guca in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karst_Plateau" title="Karst Plateau">Karst</a>), modeled after the famous <a href="/wiki/Gu%C4%8Da_Trumpet_Festival" title="Guča Trumpet Festival">Guča Trumpet Festival</a> in Serbia. Since its beginnings, the festival has managed to gain recognition and popularity, succeeding in getting famous musicians like <a href="/wiki/Goran_Bregovi%C4%87" title="Goran Bregović">Goran Bregović</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boban_Markovi%C4%87" title="Boban Marković">Boban Marković</a>, as well as many popular Italian acts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The oldest active Serbian organization in Trieste is the soccer club "Serbia Sport",<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2014)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> – <a href="/wiki/Talk:Serbs_in_Italy#Dubious" title="Talk:Serbs in Italy">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> started in 1992, and since then has won a multitude of championships in the local Trieste soccer league, making it today one of the top soccer teams in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Trieste" title="Province of Trieste">Province of Trieste</a>. Together with the Serbian Association "Vuk Karadzic", Serbia Sport organizes an annual Serbian-Diaspora Soccer Tournament in Trieste on the Serbian holiday Spasovdan, which occurs 40 days after Easter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organizations">Organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbs_in_Italy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several community organizations of Serbs in Italy. The Association of Serbs of Italy was established in April 2015 by the combined organizations meeting in Trieste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Rada_Rassimov" title="Rada Rassimov">Rada Rassimov</a> (born 1941), actress born in Trieste to Serbian parents as Rada Djerasimović; sister of <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Rassimov" title="Ivan Rassimov">Ivan Rassimov</a>.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Sportspeople</dt></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Raicevich" title="Giovanni Raicevich">Giovanni Raicevich</a> was a famous Italian wrestler in the early 20th century.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Milo%C5%A1_Malivojevi%C4%87" title="Miloš Malivojević">Miloš Malivojević</a> (born 1993), active footballer born in Scandiano, <a href="/wiki/Reggio_Emilia" title="Reggio Emilia">Reggio Emilia</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marino_Nicolich" title="Marino Nicolich">Marino Nicolich</a> (born 1910), late footballer born in Monfalcone, whose name was italianized in "Marino Nicoli" by the Italian Fascist government.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dragan_Travica" title="Dragan Travica">Dragan Travica</a> (born 1986), volleyball player born in Croatia, son of the Serbian coach <a href="/wiki/Ljubomir_Travica" title="Ljubomir Travica">Ljubomir Travica</a>; plays for the Italian national team</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ljubomir_Travica" title="Ljubomir Travica">Ljubomir Travica</a> (born 1954), volleyball coach and retired player; has residence in Italy since he played for <a href="/wiki/Modena" title="Modena">Modena</a> in 1983. He is the father of <a href="/wiki/Dragan_Travica" title="Dragan Travica">Dragan Travica</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marko_Stanojevic" title="Marko Stanojevic">Marko Stanojevic</a> (born 1979), rugby union player born in Birmingham to a Serbian father and an Italian mother. He played for the Italian national team.</li></ul>
