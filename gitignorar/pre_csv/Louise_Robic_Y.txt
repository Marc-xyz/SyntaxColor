	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Louise Robic</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Louise Robic</b> (born January 25, 1935) is a former politician in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>. She represented <a href="/wiki/Bourassa_(provincial_electoral_district)" title="Bourassa (provincial electoral district)">Bourassa</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quebec_National_Assembly" title="Quebec National Assembly">Quebec National Assembly</a> from 1985 to 1994 as a <a href="/wiki/Quebec_Liberal_Party" title="Quebec Liberal Party">Liberal</a>.
</p><p>The daughter of Jean-Baptiste Goyer and Berthe Trudeau, she was born <b>Louise Goyer</b> in <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>. She was educated at the Notre-Dame-des-Ange convent and then took a commercial course at the Alexander Business College and a course in human relations at the <a href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_du_Qu%C3%A9bec_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al" title="Université du Québec à Montréal">Université du Québec à Montréal</a>. She also took course in real estate and various management courses and studied at <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill University</a>. She worked for <a href="/wiki/Air_Canada" title="Air Canada">Air Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Trust_(Canada)" title="Royal Trust (Canada)">Royal Trust</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Desjardins_Group" title="Desjardins Group">Caisse populaire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mna_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-mna-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Robic was an organizer for the NO campaign during the <a href="/wiki/1980_Quebec_referendum" title="1980 Quebec referendum">1980 Quebec referendum</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and helped organize the "<a href="/wiki/1980_Quebec_referendum#Brunch_des_Yvettes" title="1980 Quebec referendum">Yvette</a>" gathering at the Montreal forum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> From 1982 to 1985, she was president of the Quebec Liberal Party. She was elected to the Quebec assembly in 1985 and was reelected in 1989. She served in the Quebec cabinet as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Immigration,_Diversity_and_Inclusion" title="Ministry of Immigration, Diversity and Inclusion">Minister of Cultural Communities and Immigration</a>. She was Minister responsible for Health and Social Services and Minister responsible for financial institutions. She resigned her seat in the assembly in April 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mna_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-mna-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1997 to 2007, she was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Immigration_and_Refugee_Board_of_Canada" title="Immigration and Refugee Board of Canada">Immigration and Refugee Board of Canada</a>.
</p><p>She was founding president of the Fondation pour le Refuge des femmes de l'Ouest-de-l'Île de Montréal from 1982 to 1984. Robic was named a Dame Commander of Merit in the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Military_Order_of_Malta" title="Sovereign Military Order of Malta">Sovereign Military Order of Malta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mna_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-mna-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
