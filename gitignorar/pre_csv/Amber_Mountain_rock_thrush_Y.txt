<p>The <b>Amber Mountain rock thrush</b> (<i>Monticola sharpei erythronotus</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Songbird" title="Songbird">songbird</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muscicapidae" title="Muscicapidae">Muscicapidae</a>, formerly placed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turdidae" title="Turdidae">Turdidae</a> together with the other <a href="/wiki/Chat_(bird)" title="Chat (bird)">chats</a>. It is now usually considered a subspecies of the <a href="/wiki/Forest_rock_thrush" title="Forest rock thrush">forest rock thrush</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amber_Mountain_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Amber Mountain rock thrush is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> where it occurs only on the Amber Mountain massif in the north of the island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amber_Mountain_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This is a small forest-dwelling thrush. Males have blue hoods, chestnut upperparts, bright orange tail with brown central feathers and orange underparts. Females are mostly brown with an orange wash on the underparts and lack the blue hood. Males are distinguished from other rock-thrushes by the dark rufous back, while the females have bright orange tails and lack white streaking on the breast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-birdlife_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-birdlife-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_ecology">Habitat and ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amber_Mountain_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Habitat and ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It inhabits mid-altitude and montane humid, evergreen forest from 800â1,300Â m (2,600â4,300Â ft), and forages inconspicuously in the understorey and on the ground. The species nests in tree hollows or in crevices under overhangs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation_status">Conservation status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amber_Mountain_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conservation status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Amber Mountain rock thrush is listed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature_and_Natural_Resources" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources">International Union for Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources</a> as Endangered because it has a very small range and its forest habitat is declining in both area and quality. The population is small and believed to be declining, albeit slowly. The total population of this species is estimated to number less than 5,000 individuals, which occur in a single block of forest in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amber_Mountain_National_Park" title="Amber Mountain National Park">Amber Mountain National Park</a>. It may be declining, although so far there has been relatively low levels of habitat loss in the area of its occurrence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomic_notes">Taxonomic notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amber_Mountain_rock_thrush&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Taxonomic notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Amber Mountain rock thrush has been regarded as a separate species but this view is in doubt. It had been split from the <a href="/wiki/Forest_rock_thrush" title="Forest rock thrush">forest rock thrush</a> because of a variety of morphological differences and in spite of an absence of genetic distinctiveness. As the forest rock thrush and <a href="/wiki/Benson%27s_rock_thrush" title="Benson's rock thrush">Benson's rock thrush</a> have been lumped into <i>Monticola sharpei</i> this taxon has followed suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iucn_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-iucn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
