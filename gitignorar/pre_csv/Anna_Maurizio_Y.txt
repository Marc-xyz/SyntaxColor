<p><b>Anna Maurizio</b> (26 November 1900<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> – 24 July 1993)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> was a Swiss biologist who studied <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bees" title="Bees">bees</a>. She worked for more than three decades in the Department of Bees at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liebefeld" title="Liebefeld">Liebefeld</a> Federal Dairy Industry and Bacteriological Institute, where she developed new methods for determining the amount of <a href="/wiki/Pollen" title="Pollen">pollen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Honey" title="Honey">honey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anna_Maurizio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anna Maurizio was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich" title="Zurich">Zurich</a>, the daughter of the botanist and cultural historian <a href="/wiki/Adam_Maurizio" title="Adam Maurizio">Adam Maurizio</a>. She studied at a gymnasium in Lviv, then graduated from <a href="/wiki/Lviv_National_Agrarian_University" title="Lviv National Agrarian University">High school of agriculture</a> in <a href="/wiki/Dubliany" title="Dubliany">Dubliany</a> (near Lviv) in 1923. She began work at the Federal Station of Dairy and Bacteriology in Liebefeld-Bern in 1928 and retired in 1966.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She died in Switzerland, aged about 93.
</p>
