<p><b>KCLU</b> (1340 <a href="/wiki/AM_broadcasting" title="AM broadcasting">AM</a>) is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-commercial_educational" title="Non-commercial educational">non-commercial</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_station" title="Radio station">radio station</a> licensed to and serving <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The station airs a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_radio" title="Public radio">public radio</a> format simulcasting <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a> sister station <a href="/wiki/KCLU-FM" title="KCLU-FM">KCLU-FM</a>. KCLU is rebroadcast on FM <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_relay_station" title="Broadcast relay station">translator station</a> <b>K272DT</b> (102.3 <a href="/wiki/FM_broadcasting" title="FM broadcasting">FM</a>) in Santa Barbara. The two stations form part of a five-signal network owned by <a href="/wiki/California_Lutheran_University" title="California Lutheran University">California Lutheran University</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=KCLU_(AM)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The station first signed on in 1946 as <b>KIST</b> under the ownership of <a href="/wiki/Harry_C._Butcher" title="Harry C. Butcher">Harry C. Butcher</a>. It was affiliated with the <a href="/wiki/NBC_Radio_Network" title="NBC Radio Network">NBC Radio Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BC_YB_1948_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BC_YB_1948-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1958, Butcher sold KIST to Western States Radio — a group consisting of A.R. Ellman, A.C. Morici, and station manager Karl A. Rembe — for $197,500.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 
</p><p>For many years, especially in the 1960s and 1970s, KIST was a premier <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">top 40</a> music station. Under the ownership of Joseph Patterson "Patt" Wardlaw, Jr., who purchased Western States Radio in 1960,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> KIST not only played the popular music of the day but also claimed one of the finest news broadcasting teams in the Santa Barbara area. The station won awards from the Southern California Broadcasters Association for its coverage of the Sycamore Fire in 1977. Each on-air disc jockey and many of the support staff carried two-way mobile radios in their vehicles; these enabled instant on-scene news reporting. The KIST staff from this era included program director Hal Bates, music director Dick Williams (since deceased), news director Patrick C. Riley, and chief engineer Doug Allan. On-air personalities included morning drive host Baron Ron Herron, reporter Ed Foley, and disc jockeys Tom Payne, Jack Kinney, Mike Hennie, Jim Cordes (aka Jim Evans), Frank Catalano, and Steve Dezormo (since deceased). Station <a href="/wiki/Bumper_sticker" title="Bumper sticker">bumper stickers</a> of the time read, "Get KIST 1340!"
</p><p>In the early 1980s (1983-1984), KIST began broadcasting in <a href="/wiki/C-QUAM" title="C-QUAM">C-QUAM</a> AM Stereo. By the early 1990s, KIST had changed its format to <a href="/wiki/Oldies" title="Oldies">oldies</a>. In March 1993, RSB Communications sold KIST and KMGQ to Channel Islands Broadcasting for $850,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Three years later, in September 1996, Channel Islands Broadcasting sold the combo to Engles Enterprises for $3.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 1997, Engels sold KIST to <a href="/wiki/Jacor" title="Jacor">Jacor Communications</a> for $850,000;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Jacor subsequently was absorbed by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clear_Channel_Communications" title="Clear Channel Communications">Clear Channel Communications</a>. The new owner changed the station's call letters to <b>KLDZ</b>. Soon it became <a href="/wiki/Sports_radio" title="Sports radio">all-sports</a> outlet <b>KXXT</b> branded "XTRA Sports 1340". One of the station's on-air hosts was <a href="/wiki/Jim_Rome" title="Jim Rome">Jim Rome</a>, a graduate of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara" title="University of California, Santa Barbara">University of California, Santa Barbara</a>. On March 29, 2000, the call sign was changed back to <b>KIST</b>.
</p><p>On August 8, 2003, the call sign was again changed, this time to <b>KTLK</b>. This change accompanied a format flip to <a href="/wiki/Progressive_talk_radio" title="Progressive talk radio">progressive talk radio</a>, part of national rollout of the format by Clear Channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Those call letters remained in place until February 3, 2005, when they were changed back to <b>KIST</b> for a third time.
</p><p>On January 11, 2007, Clear Channel sold all of its radio stations in Santa Barbara, including KIST, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rincon_Broadcasting" title="Rincon Broadcasting">Rincon Broadcasting</a>, headed by John Hearne, for $17.3 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The new owner immediately donated KIST to the Santa Barbara Community Broadcasting Company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 19, 2008, R &amp; R Radio, LLC announced it had sold KIST to <a href="/wiki/California_Lutheran_University" title="California Lutheran University">California Lutheran University</a> for $1.44 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> On October 7, the university, owner of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Public_Radio" title="National Public Radio">National Public Radio</a> affiliate <a href="/wiki/KCLU-FM" title="KCLU-FM">KCLU-FM</a>, converted the station to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-commercial_educational" title="Non-commercial educational">non-commercial educational</a> status and changed its call letters to <b>KCLU</b>. On October 28, Rincon Broadcasting picked up the KIST call and format on KBKO (1490 AM, now <a href="/wiki/KOSJ" title="KOSJ">KOSJ</a>).
</p>
