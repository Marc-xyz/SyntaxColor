<p><b>Searoad Ferries</b> (formerly known as <b>Peninsula Searoad Transport</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> company that operates a <a href="/wiki/Roll-on/roll-off" title="Roll-on/roll-off">roll-on/roll-off</a> vehicle and passenger <a href="/wiki/Ferry" title="Ferry">ferry</a> service between the heads of <a href="/wiki/Port_Phillip" title="Port Phillip">Port Phillip</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.
</p><p>The route operates between terminals at <a href="/wiki/Queenscliff,_Victoria" title="Queenscliff, Victoria">Queenscliff</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Bellarine_Peninsula" title="Bellarine Peninsula">Bellarine Peninsula</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sorrento,_Victoria" title="Sorrento, Victoria">Sorrento</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Mornington_Peninsula" title="Mornington Peninsula">Mornington Peninsula</a>. It is serviced by two ships, currently the <a href="/wiki/MV_Queenscliff_(1992)" title="MV Queenscliff (1992)">MV <i>Queenscliff</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/MV_Sorrento_(2001)" title="MV Sorrento (2001)">MV <i>Sorrento</i></a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolphins" title="Dolphins">Dolphins</a> are often seen following the ferries during their crossing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The ferry service runs every hour during the day, and makes a crossing in approximately 40 minutes. The single journey cost for a car and driver is <a href="/wiki/Australian_dollar" title="Australian dollar">A$</a>62,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with an additional charge for passengers. The alternative drive from Queenscliff to Sorrento via road is approximately three hours during non-peak traffic conditions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Searoad_Ferries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three sea pilots, Paul Ringe, Keith Finnemore and Maurie Cobal founded Peninsula Searoad Transport Pty Ltd (PST) in 1983. They believed that a vehicular ferry should run between Queenscliff and Sorrento. There were mixed opinions about this from the public. Some people thought that the novelty would wear off and then business would fail. However, the various tourism boards in Victoria were excited about the prospect of having a vehicular ferry, linking the two peninsulas and would also create tourism jobs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twelvemil_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-twelvemil-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peninsula_Princess">Peninsula Princess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Searoad_Ferries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Peninsula Princess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peninsula_Princess.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="556" data-file-width="825" decoding="async" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Peninsula_Princess.jpg/220px-Peninsula_Princess.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Peninsula_Princess.jpg/330px-Peninsula_Princess.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Peninsula_Princess.jpg/440px-Peninsula_Princess.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peninsula_Princess.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <i>Peninsula Princess</i> in 2008</div></div></div>
<p>After various planning, on 19 June 1987 the <i><a href="/wiki/Peninsula_Princess_(Australia)" title="Peninsula Princess (Australia)">Peninsula Princess</a></i> was launched in <a href="/wiki/Carrington,_New_South_Wales" title="Carrington, New South Wales">Carrington</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>. She underwent sea trials before moving to Port Phillip Bay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamp_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-4">[4]</a></sup> Her crew boarded her in <a href="/wiki/Geelong" title="Geelong">Geelong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a> to get a feel for the vessel. She had to wait there for the Queenscliff berth to be completed.
</p><p>The first sailing was intended to be on 7 September 1987,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamp_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-4">[4]</a></sup> but the berths specially designed for this vessel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> at Queenscliff and Sorrento were not yet complete.
</p><p>On 13 September 1987, she entered service.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> On the first arrival at Sorrento, the skipper encountered a problem with the ahead/astern controls. She hit the concrete wharf and whilst tyres around the wharf buffered the impact there was still considerable damage done to the <a href="/wiki/Transom_(nautical)" title="Transom (nautical)">transom</a>. Commercial operations started on the weekend commencing 19 September 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stamp_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-stamp-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>A few weeks later, during low tide at Queenscliff the crew encountered problems because of the depth of the water and the strong winds. As one of the deckhands attempted to take control by winching the boat in to its berth, the rope slipped and jumped and he was thrown to the deck. By the time he attempted to regain his footing the <i>Peninsula Princess</i> had been taken hold of by the wind. She was thrown against the edge of the creek and broke a propeller and had a bent shaft. The ferry had to be <a href="/wiki/Dry_dock" title="Dry dock">dry docked</a> for repairs. The business had financial issues and could not afford for this to happen again.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>After the new ferry was introduced, the <i>Peninsula Princess</i> was frequently docked at the former Queenscliff ferry berth. In 2004 the ferry was sighted docked in the <a href="/wiki/Tamar_River" title="Tamar River">Tamar River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Tasmania" title="Launceston, Tasmania">Launceston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MV_Queenscliff">MV <i>Queenscliff</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Searoad_Ferries&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: MV Queenscliff">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/MV_Queenscliff_(1992)" title="MV Queenscliff (1992)">MV Queenscliff (1992)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="1920" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg/220px-Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg/330px-Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg/440px-Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Queenscliff_ferry_terminal.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ferry terminal at <a href="/wiki/Queenscliff,_Victoria" title="Queenscliff, Victoria">Queenscliff</a>, with <a href="/wiki/MV_Queenscliff_(1992)" title="MV Queenscliff (1992)"><i>MV Queenscliff</i></a> unloading cars</div></div></div>
<p>By the early 1990s traffic using the ferry had increased, and an increase in capacity was required to cope. A new and much larger ferry, the <a href="/wiki/MV_Queenscliff_(1992)" title="MV Queenscliff (1992)">MV <i>Queenscliff</i></a> was purchased, and work commenced on the ferry berths to enable them to  handle the new ferry. At Sorrento the existing berth was altered, while at Queenscliff a new berth was built to the south.
</p><p>These works were not without controversy, and Peninsula Searoad Transport was required to attend the Administrative Appeals Tribunal to resolve the issues.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The new ferry cost $5 million, and was put into service on 22 December 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 12 October 2005 the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Defence_Force" title="Australian Defence Force">Australian Defence Force</a> staged an anti-terrorism exercise on the MV <i>Queenscliff</i>. Two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UH-60_Black_Hawk" title="UH-60 Black Hawk">Black Hawk</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helicopters" title="Helicopters">helicopters</a> were used to fast rope members of the Tactical Assault Group onto the roof of the ferry, and members of the Boat Assault Force boarded the ferry from inflatable dinghies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Traffic using the ferry service continued to grow though the 1990s, carrying about 110,000 cars/trucks/coaches/motorcycles and 600,000 passengers each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twelvemil_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-twelvemil-3">[3]</a></sup> As a result, in 2000 it was decided to purchase a second ferry, enabling a doubling in the service frequency.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/MV_Sorrento_(2001)" title="MV Sorrento (2001)"><i>MV Sorrento</i></a> was built in <a href="/wiki/Launceston,_Tasmania" title="Launceston, Tasmania">Launceston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> at a cost of $12 million, and was similar in size and appearance to the existing ferry. Minor differences between the two include an elevator from the car deck to the top deck, a new lounge at the front called the 'Portsea Lounge', and the number of exterior windows at the top would be in groups of three, not in fours.
</p><p>The increased frequency resulted in the closure of the <a href="/wiki/Queenscliff,_Victoria" title="Queenscliff, Victoria">Queenscliff</a> - <a href="/wiki/Portsea,_Victoria" title="Portsea, Victoria">Portsea</a> - <a href="/wiki/Sorrento,_Victoria" title="Sorrento, Victoria">Sorrento</a> passenger ferry service in the early 2000s.
</p>
