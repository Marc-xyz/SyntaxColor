<p><b>Bacharuddin Jusuf Habibie</b> (<small>Indonesian: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Malay" title="Help:IPA/Malay">[baxaˈrudːin ˈjusuf haˈbibi]</a></span> <span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Id-Habibie_Full.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" decoding="async" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/></a></span><a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/42/Id-Habibie_Full.ogg" title="Id-Habibie Full.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</span>; 25 June 1936 – 11 September 2019) was an <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> engineer and politician who was the third <a href="/wiki/President_of_Indonesia" title="President of Indonesia">president of Indonesia</a> from 1998 to 1999. Less than three months after his inauguration as the seventh <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_Indonesia" title="Vice President of Indonesia">vice president</a> in March 1998, he succeeded <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a> who <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Suharto" title="Fall of Suharto">resigned</a> after 31 years in office. His presidency is seen as a landmark and transition to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Suharto_era" title="Post-Suharto era">Reformation era</a>. Upon becoming president, he liberalized Indonesia's press and political party laws, and held an <a href="/wiki/1999_Indonesian_legislative_election" title="1999 Indonesian legislative election">early democratic election</a> three years sooner than scheduled, which resulted in the end of his presidency. His 517-day presidency and 71-day vice presidency are the shortest in the country's history.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="544" data-file-width="970" decoding="async" height="67" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png/120px-Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png/180px-Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png/240px-Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png 2x" width="120"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Patung_Habibie_-_Isimu_-_Gorontalo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habibie's monument in Gorontalo, 2018.</div></div></div>
<p>Habibie was a native of <a href="/wiki/Parepare" title="Parepare">Parepare</a>, in <a href="/wiki/South_Sulawesi" title="South Sulawesi">South Sulawesi</a>. Habibie is an ethnic <a href="/wiki/Gorontaloan_people" title="Gorontaloan people">Gorontalese</a>-<a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> descent from Kabila, <a href="/wiki/Gorontalo" title="Gorontalo">Gorontalo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yogyakarta" title="Yogyakarta">Yogyakarta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salam1986_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Salam1986-1">[1]</a></sup> His parents, Alwi Abdul Jalil Habibie,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salam1986_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Salam1986-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agriculturist" title="Agriculturist">agriculturist</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gorontaloan_people" title="Gorontaloan people">Gorontalese</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> descent, and R. A. Tuti Marini Puspowardojo,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Priyayi" title="Priyayi">noblewoman</a> from <a href="/wiki/Yogyakarta" title="Yogyakarta">Yogyakarta</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> met while studying in <a href="/wiki/Bogor" title="Bogor">Bogor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Habibie's family comes from Kabila, a village now sits in the eastern part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorontalo_Province" title="Gorontalo Province">Gorontalo Province</a>. He was the fourth of eight children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-eb-7">[7]</a></sup> Habibie's father died when he was 14 years old.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-eb-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studies_and_career_in_Europe">Studies and career in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Studies and career in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Habibie went to <a href="/wiki/Delft" title="Delft">Delft</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, to study aviation and aerospace at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technische_Hogeschool_Delft" title="Technische Hogeschool Delft">Technische Hogeschool Delft</a> (Delft University of Technology), but for political reasons (the <a href="/wiki/West_New_Guinea_dispute" title="West New Guinea dispute">West New Guinea dispute</a> between the Netherlands and Indonesia), he had to continue his study at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Technische_Hochschule_Aachen" title="Technische Hochschule Aachen">Technische Hochschule Aachen</a> (RWTH Aachen University) in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aachen,_Germany" title="Aachen, Germany">Aachen, Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 1960, Habibie received an <a href="/wiki/Engineer%27s_degree" title="Engineer's degree">engineer's degree</a> in Germany with the title <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science_in_Engineering" title="Master of Science in Engineering">Diplom-Ingenieur</a></i>. He remained in Germany as a <a href="/wiki/Research_assistant" title="Research assistant">research assistant</a> under Hans Ebner at the Lehrstuhl und Institut für Leichtbau, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RWTH" title="RWTH">RWTH</a> <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a> to conduct research for his doctoral degree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1962, Habibie returned to Indonesia for three months on <a href="/wiki/Sick_leave" title="Sick leave">sick leave</a>. During this time, he was reacquainted with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hasri_Ainun" title="Hasri Ainun">Hasri Ainun</a>, the daughter of R. Mohamad Besari. Habibie had known Hasri Ainun in childhood, junior high school and in senior high school at SMA Kristen Dago (Dago Christian Senior High School), <a href="/wiki/Bandung" title="Bandung">Bandung</a>. The two married on 12 May 1962, returning to Germany shortly afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
Habibie and his wife settled in <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a> for a short period before moving to Oberforstbach. In May 1963 they had a son, Ilham Akbar Habibie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakka200872–75_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakka200872–75-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Habibie later found employment with the railway stock firm <a href="/wiki/Waggonfabrik_Talbot" title="Waggonfabrik Talbot">Waggonfabrik Talbot</a>, where he became an advisor in designing train wagons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakka200879–80_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakka200879–80-12">[12]</a></sup> Due to his work with Makosh, the head of train construction offered his position to Habibie upon retirement three years later, but Habibie refused the position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakka200879–80_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakka200879–80-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1965, Habibie delivered his dissertation in aerospace engineering and received the grade of "very good," giving him the title <i><a href="/wiki/Doktoringenieur" title="Doktoringenieur">Doktoringenieur</a></i> (Dr.-Ing.). The same year, he accepted Hans Ebner's offer to continue his research on <i>Thermoelastisitas</i> and work toward his <a href="/wiki/Habilitation" title="Habilitation">Habilitation</a>, but he declined the offer to join RWTH as a professor. His thesis about light construction for supersonic or hypersonic states also attracted offers of employment from companies such as <a href="/wiki/Boeing" title="Boeing">Boeing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a>, which Habibie again declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Habibie did accept a position with <a href="/wiki/Messerschmitt-B%C3%B6lkow-Blohm" title="Messerschmitt-Bölkow-Blohm">Messerschmitt-Bölkow-Blohm</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>. There, he developed theories on <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, construction, and aerodynamics known as the Habibie Factor, Habibie Theorem, and Habibie Method, respectively. He worked for Messerschmitt on the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Airbus_A-300" title="Airbus A-300">Airbus A-300</a>B aircraft. In 1974, he was promoted to vice president of the company.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Rourke140_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Rourke140-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_in_Indonesia">Career in Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career in Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1974, Suharto recruited Habibie to return to Indonesia as part of his drive to industrialize and develop the country. Habibie initially served as a special assistant to <a href="/wiki/Ibnu_Sutowo" title="Ibnu Sutowo">Ibnu Sutowo</a>, Chief Executive Officer of the state oil company <a href="/wiki/Pertamina" title="Pertamina">Pertamina</a>. Two years later, Habibie was made CEO of the new state-owned enterprise <i>Industri Pesawat Terbang <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurtanio" title="Nurtanio">Nurtanio</a></i> (IPTN; Nurtanio Aircraft Industry),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Rourke140_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-O'Rourke140-15">[15]</a></sup> which in 1985 changed its name to <i>Industri Pesawat Terbang <a href="/wiki/Nusantara" title="Nusantara">Nusantara</a></i> (Nusantara Aircraft Industry; also abbreviated as IPTN) and is known as <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Aerospace" title="Indonesian Aerospace">Indonesian Aerospace (PT. Dirgantara Indonesia)</a> since 2000). In 1978, he was appointed as Minister of Research and Technology. He continued to play an important role in IPTN other "strategic" industries in this post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Rourke140_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-O'Rourke140-15">[15]</a></sup> By the 1980s, IPTN had grown considerably, specializing in the manufacture of  helicopters and small passenger planes. Under Habibie's leadership, IPTN became a manufacturer of aircraft including <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale_SA_330_Puma" title="Aérospatiale SA 330 Puma">Puma helicopters</a> and <a href="/wiki/CASA/IPTN_CN-235" title="CASA/IPTN CN-235">CASA planes</a>. It pioneered a small passenger airplane, the <a href="/wiki/IPTN_N-250" title="IPTN N-250">N-250 Gatotkaca</a>, in 1995, but the project was a commercial failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In developing Indonesia's aviation industry, he adopted an approach called "Begin at the End and End at the Beginning".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In this method, elements such as basic research became the last things upon which to focus, whilst actual manufacturing of the planes was placed as the first objective.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1092" data-file-width="1380" decoding="async" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg/220px-Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg/330px-Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg/440px-Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Golongan_Karya_rally_1997.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habibie (center) as <a href="/wiki/Golkar" title="Golkar">Golkar</a> Board of Patron member during the 1997 campaign in Tanah Abang, <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>By 1991, Habibie oversaw ten state-owned industries including ship- and train-building, steel, arms, communications, and energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Rourke140_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-O'Rourke140-15">[15]</a></sup> A 1993 estimate determined that the estimates used nearly $2 billion a year in state funding, although the government's opaque accounting practices meant that the size of the industries was not completely known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>As minister, Habibie created the OFP (Overseas Fellowship Program), STMDP (Science Technology and Manpower Development Program) and STAID (Science and Technology for Industrial Development). These three programs provided scholarships to thousands of high school graduates to earn their bachelor's degrees in the STEM fields and for other technical professionals to continue their study for master's and doctorate program in the United States, Europe, Japan, and other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>Habibie was, continuously, a member of six Indonesian cabinets for over 20 years. After his initial appointment in 1978, he served in another five cabinets (including the <a href="/wiki/Development_Reform_Cabinet" title="Development Reform Cabinet">Development Reform Cabinet</a> which, as president he formed after the resignation of Suharto in May 1998):
</p>
<ul><li>1978–1983: State Minister of Research and Technology in the <a href="/wiki/Third_Development_Cabinet" title="Third Development Cabinet">Third Development Cabinet</a></li>
<li>1983–1988: State Minister of Research and Technology and Chair of the Research and Technology Implementation Board in the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Development_Cabinet" title="Fourth Development Cabinet">Fourth Development Cabinet</a></li>
<li>1988–1993: State Minister of Research and Technology and Chair of the Research and Technology Implementation Board in the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Development_Cabinet" title="Fifth Development Cabinet">Fifth Development Cabinet</a></li>
<li>1993–1998: State Minister of Research and Technology and Chair of the Research and Technology Implementation Board in the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Development_Cabinet" title="Sixth Development Cabinet">Sixth Development Cabinet</a></li>
<li>1998: Vice-president in the <a href="/wiki/Seventh_Development_Cabinet" title="Seventh Development Cabinet">Seventh Development Cabinet</a></li>
<li>1998–1999: President in the <a href="/wiki/Development_Reform_Cabinet" title="Development Reform Cabinet">Development Reform Cabinet</a></li></ul>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a>'s regime, as was expected of senior government executives, Habibie became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Golkar" title="Golkar">Golkar</a> organisation. Suharto appointed him as deputy daily coordinator for the chairman of the executive board in 1992, and the following year he became the daily coordinator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMakka2008190_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMakka2008190-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>While serving in cabinet, Habibie was also elected as the first Chair of the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Association_of_Muslim_Intellectuals" title="Indonesian Association of Muslim Intellectuals">Indonesian Association of Muslim Intellectuals</a> (ICMI) in 1990. This modernist Muslim organization provided him with a political base, linked to but independent of the Suharto administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vice_presidency">Vice presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vice presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1383" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="231" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg/200px-Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg/300px-Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg/400px-Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bacharuddin_Jusuf_Habibie_official_portrait.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habibie's official vice-presidential portrait, <a href="/wiki/Star_of_the_Republic_of_Indonesia" title="Star of the Republic of Indonesia">BRI</a> 2nd Class featured.</div></div></div>
<p>In January 1998, after accepting nomination for a seventh term as President, Suharto announced the selection criteria for the nomination of a vice president. Suharto did not mention Habibie by name, but his suggestion that the next vice president should have a mastery of science and technology made it obvious he had Habibie in mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In that year, in the midst of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asian_Financial_Crisis" title="Asian Financial Crisis">Asian Financial Crisis</a>, this suggestion was received badly, causing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rupiah" title="Rupiah">rupiah</a> to fall. Despite this, Habibie was elected as Vice President in March 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidency">Presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Post-Suharto_era" title="Post-Suharto era">Post-Suharto era</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="461" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg/220px-Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg/330px-Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg/440px-Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Habibie_presidential_oath.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habibie taking his presidential oath on 21 May 1998.</div></div></div>
<p>On 21 May 1998, just two months into Habibie's vice presidency, Suharto announced his resignation. Habibie, as the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Indonesia" title="Constitution of Indonesia">Constitution</a> says, succeeded him as president. The following day, Habibie announced the <a href="/wiki/Development_Reform_Cabinet" title="Development Reform Cabinet">Development Reform Cabinet</a>, which removed some of the most controversial ministers in Suharto's last cabinet while maintaining others - with no major figures from the opposition. Within days of his appointment, he asked his relatives to resign from government positions, promised an early election, repealed some legislation, and ordered the release of political prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Timor">East Timor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: East Timor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Habibie was opposed to <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timorese Independence</a> but did offer East Timor special autonomy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Timorese independence forces led by the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Maubere_Resistance" title="National Council of Maubere Resistance">National Council of Timorese Resistance</a> had been calling for a referendum in the territory for some time. Its chief diplomat, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ramos-Horta" title="José Ramos-Horta">Jose Ramos Horta</a>, proposed a transitional period of autonomy leading up to a referendum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>  In late 1998, <a href="/wiki/John_Howard" title="John Howard">John Howard</a>, the Prime Minister of Australia, sent a letter to Habibie suggesting that Indonesia defuse the East Timorese issue by providing autonomy to be followed by the promise of a referendum in the long run, following the method used by France to settle <a href="/wiki/1987_New_Caledonian_independence_referendum" title="1987 New Caledonian independence referendum">New Caledonian demands for independence</a>. Wishing to avoid the impression that Indonesia ruled East Timor as a colony, Habibie surprised some by announcing that a <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a>, offering a choice between special autonomy and independence, would be held immediately in East Timor. Leaders of the Indonesian armed forces (<a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Armed_Forces" title="Indonesian National Armed Forces">ABRI</a>) were not consulted on this decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 30 August 1999, the referendum was held and the East Timorese people overwhelmingly chose Independence. Subsequently, pro-Indonesia militias killed and displaced large numbers of people during the <a href="/wiki/1999_East_Timorese_crisis" title="1999 East Timorese crisis">1999 East Timorese crisis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> On 10 September, General <a href="/wiki/Wiranto" title="Wiranto">Wiranto</a> allegedly threatened to stage a military coup if Habibie allowed in peacekeeping forces, causing Habibie to back down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> On 12 September, however, Habibie accepted a <a href="/wiki/International_Force_East_Timor" title="International Force East Timor">UN-mandated peacekeeping force</a> to halt violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-27">[27]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Transitional_Administration_in_East_Timor" title="United Nations Transitional Administration in East Timor">UN administration</a> followed and East Timor became independent in 2002.
</p>
<h3><span id="Suharto.27s_corruption_charge"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Suharto's_corruption_charge">Suharto's corruption charge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Suharto's corruption charge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Consultative_Assembly" title="People's Consultative Assembly">MPR</a> Special Session in November 1998 decried the presence of corruption in Indonesia, focusing particularly on Suharto.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In response to this, Habibie then appointed <a href="/wiki/Andi_Muhammad_Ghalib" title="Andi Muhammad Ghalib">Andi Muhammad Ghalib</a> as <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Indonesia" title="Attorney General of Indonesia">Attorney General</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-23">[23]</a></sup> A tape of a telephone conversation between Habibie and Ghalib was made public. It raised concerns about the veracity of the investigation by suggesting that the interrogation of Suharto was intended only for public appearances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under Habibie, the Indonesian government also began investigating and prosecuting Suharto's youngest son, Hutomo Mandala Putra (commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Suharto" title="Tommy Suharto">Tommy Suharto</a>). Ghalib charged Tommy in December 1998 in conjunction with the Goro scandal, where the government, under pressure from Tommy, allegedly gave him a desirable parcel and below-market loan for the construction of a Goro supermarket. However, Tommy was found innocent in the case after several key witnesses, including Habibie aide Rahardi Ramelan, changed their testimony and declared that the deal did not cause losses to the state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Habibie's government stabilized the economy in the face of the Asian financial crisis and the chaos of the last few months of Suharto's presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Habibie's government began to make conciliatory gestures towards <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesians" title="Chinese Indonesians">Chinese-Indonesians</a> who, because of their elite status, were targeted in the <a href="/wiki/May_1998_riots_of_Indonesia" title="May 1998 riots of Indonesia">riots of 1998</a>. In September 1998, Habibie issued a 'Presidential Instruction' forbidding use of the terms <i><a href="/wiki/Native_Indonesians" title="Native Indonesians">pribumi</a></i> and <i>non-pribumi</i> to differentiate indigenous and non-indigenous Indonesians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 1999, Habibie directed that an ID card was sufficient proof of Indonesian citizenship, revoking the previous requirement for a 'Letter of Evidence of Republic of Indonesia Citizenship' (SBKRI). Additionally, he lifted restrictions on the teaching of <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_reform">Political reform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Political reform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under Habibie, Indonesia made significant changes to its political system that expanded competition and <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">freedom of speech</a>. Shortly after taking office, in June 1998, Habibie's government lifted the Suharto-era restriction on political parties and ended censorship by dissolving the Information Ministry. He also quickly committed to holding democratic elections, albeit on an initially vague timetable. In December, he proposed political reform laws that were passed by the legislature and MPR. These laws set elections for December 1999, reduced the number of seats in parliament held by the military, and barred political activity by civil servants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Rourke145_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Rourke145-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, political opponents criticized Habibie for allowing the military to retain some seats in parliament, and taking little action on other military and judicial reforms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Habibie's government also passed laws which granted significant autonomy to regional governments, namely at the <a href="/wiki/Regency_(Indonesia)" title="Regency (Indonesia)">regency</a> and city level. The laws resulted in indirect elections for mayors and regents, and allowed local legislatures to hold said executives accountable, though it was not implemented until after his presidency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBohlken2016220_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBohlken2016220-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_presidency">End of presidency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: End of presidency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although he had been viewed as leading a transitional government, Habibie seemed determined to continue as president. He was initially unclear about whether he would seek a full term as president when he announced parliamentary elections in June 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Rourke145_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-O'Rourke145-36">[36]</a></sup> Habibie faced opposition from many within the government party, Golkar; in July 1998, he struggled to win control of Golkar by appointing <a href="/wiki/Akbar_Tandjung" title="Akbar Tandjung">Akbar Tandjung</a> as chair of the party, but was ultimately able to defeat a rival camp including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Vice_Presidents_of_Indonesia" title="List of Vice Presidents of Indonesia">former Vice President</a> <a href="/wiki/Try_Sutrisno" title="Try Sutrisno">Try Sutrisno</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(Indonesia)" title="Ministry of Defence (Indonesia)">Defence Minister</a> Edi Sudrajat, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siswono_Yudhohusodo" title="Siswono Yudhohusodo">Siswono Yudhohusodo</a>, and Sarwono Kusumaatmadja.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> Habibie began to lose support from Akbar Tandjung and a faction in Golkar, composed of both reformers and hardliners, that wanted to oust him. In March 1999, Golkar put forth five presidential nominees: Habibie, Tandjung, Wiranto, <a href="/wiki/Hamengkubuwono_X" title="Hamengkubuwono X">Hamengkubuwono X</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ginandjar_Kartasasmita" title="Ginandjar Kartasasmita">Ginandjar Kartasasmita</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> In May 1999, after extensive lobbying, Golkar announced that Habibie would be their presidential candidate, but a large faction in the party remained loyal to Tandjung and opposed to Habibie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the 1999 MPR General Session in October, Habibie delivered an accountability speech. MPR members then began voting to decide if they would accept or reject his speech. Habibie attempted to win the support of the military by offering the vice-presidency to General Wiranto, but his offer was declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Tandjung's Golkar faction broke ranks and voted against him, and his accountability speech was rejected by 355 votes to 322, and Habibie withdrew his nomination as President.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Abdurrahman_Wahid" title="Abdurrahman Wahid">Abdurrahman Wahid</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Post-presidency.2C_final_years_and_death"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-presidency,_final_years_and_death">Post-presidency, final years and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post-presidency, final years and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie,_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="602" data-file-width="1074" decoding="async" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie%2C_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg/300px-State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie%2C_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie%2C_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg/450px-State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie%2C_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie%2C_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg/600px-State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie%2C_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:State_Funeral_Procession_of_President_B._J._Habibie,_Jakarta_-_12_September_2019.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>State funeral procession of Habibie at the <a href="/wiki/Kalibata_Heroes_Cemetery" title="Kalibata Heroes Cemetery">Kalibata Heroes Cemetery</a> in Jakarta, 12 September 2019</div></div></div>
<p>After relinquishing the presidency, Habibie spent more time in Germany than in Indonesia, though he was active during <a href="/wiki/Susilo_Bambang_Yudhoyono" title="Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono">Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono</a>'s presidency as a presidential adviser. During this time, he established the Habibie Centre, an independent think tank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2006, he released a book called <i>Detik-Detik Yang Menentukan: Jalan Panjang Indonesia Menuju Demokrasi</i> (Decisive Moments: Indonesia's Long Road Towards Democracy). The book recalled the events of May 1998 which led to his rise to the Presidency. In the book, he controversially accused <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> <a href="/wiki/Prabowo_Subianto" title="Prabowo Subianto">Prabowo Subianto</a>, Suharto's son-in-law (at that time) and the <a href="/wiki/Kostrad" title="Kostrad">Kostrad</a> Commander, of planning a coup d'état against him in May 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>In early September 2019, he was admitted to <a href="/wiki/Gatot_Soebroto_Army_Hospital" title="Gatot Soebroto Army Hospital">Gatot Soebroto Army Hospital</a>, where he was undergoing treatments for heart problems,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> namely <a href="/wiki/Cardiomyopathy" title="Cardiomyopathy">cardiomyopathy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> and died on 11 September 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> He was buried the next afternoon at the <a href="/wiki/Kalibata_Heroes_Cemetery" title="Kalibata Heroes Cemetery">Kalibata Heroes Cemetery</a>, next to his wife's grave.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to his death, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Indonesia" title="Government of Indonesia">Government of Indonesia</a> announced a three-day national mourning period starting on 12 September, and announced that the Indonesian flag is to be flown at half-staff during the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 12 September 2019, video was released showing <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a>'s former <a href="/wiki/President_of_East_Timor" title="President of East Timor">President</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xanana_Gusm%C3%A3o" title="Xanana Gusmão">Xanana Gusmão</a>, visiting Habibie in hospital on 22 July 2019. Gusmao is shown talking briefly to Habibie while crying, then kissing Habibie on the forehead and lowering his face to Habibie’s chest, with the other holding his head. A wreath in Gusmao’s name was at the funeral, with a sign reading: "Profound Condolences – With heartfelt sympathy for the loss of Big Brother President B.J. Habibie – Timorese people will remember you forever – Rest in Peace - Xanana Gusmão".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2019-09-13_ABC_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-2019-09-13_ABC-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="905" decoding="async" height="109" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png/220px-Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png/330px-Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png/440px-Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Foto_Pernikahan_Habibie_-_Ainun_Adat_Gorontalo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Habibie and Ainun wedding attire in Gorontalonese culture</div></div></div>
<p>Habibie was married to <a href="/wiki/Hasri_Ainun_Habibie" title="Hasri Ainun Habibie">Hasri Ainun Besari</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medical_doctor" title="Medical doctor">medical doctor</a>, from 12 May 1962 until her death on 22 May 2010. Their wedding was held in <a href="/wiki/Javanese_culture" title="Javanese culture">Javanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gorontaloan_people" title="Gorontaloan people">Gorontalese</a> culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> The couple had two sons, Ilham Akbar Habibie and Thareq Kemal Habibie.
</p><p>B. J. Habibie's brother, <a href="/wiki/Junus_Effendi_Habibie" title="Junus Effendi Habibie">Junus Effendi Habibie</a>, was Indonesian ambassador to the United Kingdom and the Netherlands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> After his wife's death, Habibie published a book titled <i><a href="/wiki/Habibie_%26_Ainun" title="Habibie &amp; Ainun">Habibie &amp; Ainun</a></i> which recounts his relationship with Hasri Ainun from their courtship until her death. The book was adapted into a film of the same name which was released on 20 December 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Honours.2C_decorations.2C_and_distinctions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours,_decorations,_and_distinctions">Honours, decorations, and distinctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Honours, decorations, and distinctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Habibie received several honorary degrees for his contributions in the fields of technology and science, e.g. he was awarded an Honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Science" title="Doctor of Science">DSc</a> degree from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cranfield_Institute_of_Technology" title="Cranfield Institute of Technology">Cranfield Institute of Technology</a> (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctor_Honoris_Causa" title="Doctor Honoris Causa">Dr.h.c.</a> degrees from <a href="/wiki/Chungbuk_National_University" title="Chungbuk National University">Chungbuk National University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hankuk_University_of_Foreign_Studies" title="Hankuk University of Foreign Studies">Hankuk University of Foreign Studies</a> (<a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>) for his services to aircraft technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> In 2010, Habibie was honored with an Honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD</a> degree in Technology by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Indonesia" title="University of Indonesia">University of Indonesia</a> for his contribution to science in practice as a technocrat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>
</p><p>Habibie was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering">Fellow of the Royal Academy of Engineering</a> (FREng) in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> In 1993, he was awarded an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Aeronautical_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Aeronautical Society">Honorary Fellow of the Royal Aeronautical Society</a> (HonFRAeS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> He was also named an honorary member of several professional bodies, including:
</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul><li>Malaysian Engineers Association (IEM)</li>
<li>Japanese Academy of Engineering</li>
<li>Fellowship of Engineering of the United Kingdom, London</li>
<li>National Academy of Engineering, USA</li>
<li>Academie Nationale de l'Air et de l'Espace, France</li>
<li>Royal Aeronautical Society, UK</li>
<li>Royal Swedish Academy of Engineering Science</li>
<li>Gesselschaft Fuer Luft und Raumfarht (Institute for Aviation &amp; Space), Germany</li>
<li>American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics, USA</li></ul>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">Timor-Leste</a> named a bridge and park after Habibie shortly before his death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-51">[51]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Movies">Movies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._J._Habibie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Movies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Habibie has been portrayed in several biopic movies based on both his political and personal life. In the first installment of <i><a href="/wiki/Habibie_%26_Ainun" title="Habibie &amp; Ainun">Habibie &amp; Ainun</a></i> (2012) and its sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Rudy_Habibie" title="Rudy Habibie">Rudy Habibie</a></i> (2016), Habibie is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Reza_Rahadian" title="Reza Rahadian">Reza Rahadian</a>, while <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bima_Azriel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bima Azriel (page does not exist)">Bima Azriel</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bastian_Bintang_Simbolon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bastian Bintang Simbolon (page does not exist)">Bastian Bintang Simbolon</a> portrayed Habibie<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> during his childhood, and teenage years in <i>Rudy Habibie</i> respectively. The third installment of the movies, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Habibie_%26_Ainun_3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Habibie &amp; Ainun 3 (page does not exist)">Habibie &amp; Ainun 3</a></i> (2019), young Habibie was portrayed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jefri_Nichol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jefri Nichol (page does not exist)">Jefri Nichol</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Di_Balik_98" title="Di Balik 98">Di Balik 98</a></i>, Habibie was portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Agus_Kuncoro" title="Agus Kuncoro">Agus Kuncoro</a>.
</p>
