<p><b>Messenger</b> (1780 – January 28, 1808) was an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred" title="Thoroughbred">Thoroughbred</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stallion_(horse)" title="Stallion (horse)">stallion</a> bred by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Grosvenor,_1st_Earl_Grosvenor" title="Richard Grosvenor, 1st Earl Grosvenor">Richard Grosvenor</a>, and imported into the newly-formed <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> of America just after the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messenger_(horse)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with three other stallions, (Medley, Sharp, and <a href="/wiki/Diomed" title="Diomed">Diomed</a>), Messenger provided the type of foal that was needed for the era of long distance (stamina and speed) racing popular in the early days of the American sport.
</p><p>Messenger was a grey by <a href="/wiki/Mambrino_(horse)" title="Mambrino (horse)">Mambrino</a> out of an unnamed mare (1774) by Turf. He was most likely foaled at Oxford Stud in <a href="/wiki/Balsham" title="Balsham">Balsham, Cambridgeshire</a>. Messenger was inbred to Cade in the third and fourth generations of his pedigree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  Mambrino traced straight back to Blaze, the father of trotters. Messenger has crosses to all three of the Thoroughbred foundation sires, particularly <a href="/wiki/Godolphin_Arabian" title="Godolphin Arabian">Godolphin Arabian</a>.  Although his sire was a trotter, Messenger never ran a trot race.  While still in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, he started in 16 flat races and won ten of them.  Messenger's races, usually less than two and half miles, were mainly "match" races in which the side bets far exceeded the purse.
</p><p>Messenger's appearance gave an impression of solidity and power. He had large and always active ears, a large and bony head; its nose had a decided Roman shape, the nostrils large and flexible. He had a large windpipe and short neck, but not coarse or thick, low withers and around the shoulders, heavy and upright. Messenger had superior hips and quarters. The bones of the limbs were strong and large. He always stood prompt and upright on all four legs. He was 15.3 <a href="/wiki/Hand_(unit)" title="Hand (unit)">hands</a> (63 inches, 160 cm) high.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_in_America">Arrival in America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messenger_(horse)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arrival in America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 1788 Sir Thomas Benger imported Messenger to <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>. In 1793, Messenger was sold to Henry Astor.  Messenger was once advertised in a Philadelphia newspaper as: <i>Available for service: Inquiries to be made to a certain Alexander Clay at the sign of the Black Horse in Market Street</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TbH_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-TbH-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Offspring_and_Legacy">Offspring and Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messenger_(horse)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Offspring and Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like the other three English stallions, and as was the custom of the day, Messenger was bred throughout <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. The mares he was bred with were not of the highest quality, but even so he proved himself a superior stallion, siring a great many successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Racehorse" title="Racehorse">racehorses</a>.
</p><p>Messenger's daughter, Miller's Damsel, also known as "Queen of the American Turf", gave birth to the horse his breeder named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_Eclipse_(racehorse)" title="American Eclipse (racehorse)">American Eclipse</a> in the belief the foal would be as great as the famous English <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_(horse)" title="Eclipse (horse)">Eclipse</a>. By Duroc, a fine son of <a href="/wiki/Diomed" title="Diomed">Diomed</a>, American Eclipse did indeed turn out to be a champion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montgomery_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Montgomery-4">[4]</a></sup> 
</p><p>He was not only a great sire of Thoroughbreds, he was also the founding father of the harness breed, or modern-day American <a href="/wiki/Standardbred" title="Standardbred">Standardbred</a> through his great grandson, Rysdyk's (<a href="/wiki/Hambletonian_10" title="Hambletonian 10">Hambletonian 10</a>). His genes have also contributed to the <a href="/wiki/American_Saddlebred" title="American Saddlebred">American Saddlebred</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Walking_Horse" title="Tennessee Walking Horse">Tennessee Walking Horse</a> breeds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TbH_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-TbH-3">[3]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Messenger died on January 8, 1808 at the age of 28.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TbH_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-TbH-3">[3]</a></sup>  He is buried under a boulder memorialized with an inscribed plaque on Duck Pond Road in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locust_Valley" title="Locust Valley">Locust Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, New York.
</p>
