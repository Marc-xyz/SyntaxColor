<p>"<b>Marco</b>" is the tenth and final episode of the <a href="/wiki/Better_Call_Saul_(season_1)" title="Better Call Saul (season 1)">first season</a> of the American television drama series <i><a href="/wiki/Better_Call_Saul" title="Better Call Saul">Better Call Saul</a></i>, the spinoff series of <i><a href="/wiki/Breaking_Bad" title="Breaking Bad">Breaking Bad</a></i>. Written and directed by series co-creator <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gould_(writer)" title="Peter Gould (writer)">Peter Gould</a>, "Marco" aired on <a href="/wiki/AMC_(TV_channel)" title="AMC (TV channel)">AMC</a> in the United States on April 6, 2015. Outside of the United States, the episode premiered on streaming service <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> in several countries. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening">Opening</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Opening">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a flashback, <a href="/wiki/Saul_Goodman" title="Saul Goodman">Jimmy</a> has been <a href="/wiki/Nacho_(Better_Call_Saul)" title="Nacho (Better Call Saul)">released from jail</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chuck_McGill" title="Chuck McGill">Chuck's</a> help. He goes to a favorite bar in his hometown of <a href="/wiki/Cicero,_Illinois" title="Cicero, Illinois">Cicero, Illinois</a> to say goodbye to Marco, his friend and partner in crime. Jimmy has agreed to Chuck's demand that he move to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albuquerque" title="Albuquerque">Albuquerque</a> to live an honest life and give up his cons, which disappoints Marco.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_story">Main story</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Main story">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jimmy hands the Sandpiper Crossing case over to HHM and reveals that he figured out Chuck was sabotaging his career. <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hamlin" title="Howard Hamlin">Howard</a> apologizes for his involvement and <a href="/wiki/Pimento_(Better_Call_Saul)" title="Pimento (Better Call Saul)">gives Jimmy his</a> $20,000 <a href="/wiki/Of_counsel" title="Of counsel">of counsel</a> fee. Jimmy requests that Howard take over caring for Chuck, including buying his groceries and daily newspapers, and Howard is impressed with the amount of time and effort Jimmy has devoted to Chuck's well-being.
</p><p>Jimmy hosts <a href="/wiki/Bingo_(U.S.)" title="Bingo (U.S.)">bingo</a> at the local senior center. After an improbable string of numbers beginning with the letter "B" are drawn at the start of a game, he fixates on words beginning with that letter which remind him of Chuck, including "betrayal" and "brother". Jimmy breaks down and rants about taking revenge against Chet, who "may have" owed him money or cheated with his wife, by defecating through the sunroof of Chet's car (a move he calls the "Chicago Sunroof") without realizing Chet's children were in the backseat. Since Chet had connections to law enforcement officials, he had the District Attorney file charges for <a href="/wiki/Indecent_exposure" title="Indecent exposure">indecent exposure</a>, which could force Jimmy to register as a sex offender if he's convicted. Chuck managed <a href="/wiki/Nacho_(Better_Call_Saul)" title="Nacho (Better Call Saul)">to have the charges dropped</a>, but Jimmy attributes his current situation to that event. He quits hosting and storms out.
</p><p>After returning to Cicero, Jimmy reunites with Marco. They run a scam on an unsuspecting businessman, which involves the sale of a "rare, valuable <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_half_dollar" title="Kennedy half dollar">JFK half-dollar</a>". Jimmy and Marco spend several days running cons. At the end of the week, Jimmy has multiple messages from his elderly clients asking where he is. Jimmy explains to Marco that he is a budding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elder_law" title="Elder law">elder law</a> attorney, and that he must return to Albuquerque to see to his clients. Marco is envious that Jimmy has a legitimate career, pointing out that he has a dead end job thanks to his brother in law and nothing to live for but his cons. Marco convinces Jimmy to stay for one last con, to which Jimmy reluctantly agrees. While running the "fake <a href="/wiki/Rolex" title="Rolex">Rolex</a>" scam, Marco suffers a heart attack and collapses. Jimmy rushes to his aid, and before dying Marco thanks Jimmy for providing the greatest week of his life. Jimmy inherits Marco's <a href="/wiki/Pinky_ring" title="Pinky ring">pinky ring</a>, which he begins to wear even though it's too big, and which Saul Goodman wears throughout the events of <i><a href="/wiki/Breaking_Bad" title="Breaking Bad">Breaking Bad</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kim_Wexler" title="Kim Wexler">Kim</a> calls Jimmy to report that the Sandpiper case has grown too big for HHM to handle, requiring them to partner with Davis &amp; Main, a <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_New_Mexico" title="Santa Fe, New Mexico">Santa Fe</a> firm. Because of his detailed knowledge of the case and rapport with the clients, D&amp;M is interested in hiring Jimmy as an associate and placing him on track to become a partner. Jimmy arranges to meet his prospective employer at the courthouse, but as he walks through the parking lot, he looks at Marco's pinky ring and stops. As he's about to drive away, he stops at the attendant's booth and asks Mike why they didn't keep the $1.6 million they <a href="/wiki/Bingo_(Better_Call_Saul)" title="Bingo (Better Call Saul)">took from the Kettlemans</a>. Mike recalls Jimmy didn't take the money because he wanted to do the "right thing", and says he didn't take it because he was "hired to do a job and he did it." Jimmy assures Mike he will not make the same mistake again, and drives off while humming "<a href="/wiki/Smoke_on_the_Water" title="Smoke on the Water">Smoke on the Water</a>", the same song Marco was humming just before he died.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This episode was written and directed by series co-creator <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gould_(writer)" title="Peter Gould (writer)">Peter Gould</a>, who also wrote "<a href="/wiki/Uno_(Better_Call_Saul)" title="Uno (Better Call Saul)">Uno</a>" and "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mijo_(Better_Call_Saul)" title="Mijo (Better Call Saul)">Mijo</a>" earlier this season.
</p><p>According to Gould, all the scams depicted in the episode are based on real-life cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scams_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-scams-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The fictitious Equatorial Uqbar Orbis that Jimmy mentions during a scam is a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a> story <a href="/wiki/Tl%C3%B6n,_Uqbar,_Orbis_Tertius" title="Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius">Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius</a>.
</p><p>During the montage of scams, Marco speaks the line, "<a href="/wiki/Repo_Man_(film)" title="Repo Man (film)">Hey, kid, help me get my wife's car out of this bad neighborhood.</a>" 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratings">Ratings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ratings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Upon airing, the episode received 2.53 million American viewers, and an 18-49 rating of 1.2;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> including Live+7 ratings, the finale was watched by 5.76 million viewers, and had a 2.8 18-49 rating overall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marco_(Better_Call_Saul)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The episode received a highly positive reception from critics. On <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, based on 22 reviews, it received an 86% approval rating with an average score of 8.6 out of 10. The site's consensus reads, "The investment into Better Call Saul's first season pays off with a finale that is at once comic and tragic, as Jimmy's conversion to Saul nears completion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Roth Cornet of <a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gave the episode a 9.0 rating, concluding, "Better Call Saul gave us an exemplary first season of television. The finale brought us to the moment where Jimmy made the decision to abandon his attempt to walk a righteous path and, instead, give over to his baser drives and desires. A more open-ended conclusion may have felt more emotionally impactful. On the whole, though, this was a gorgeously designed and executed episode; one that leaves enough room for – what we hope will be - an equally extraordinary second season."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cornet_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cornet-5">[5]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Telegraph</a></i> rated the episode 4 out of 5 stars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Power_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Power-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
