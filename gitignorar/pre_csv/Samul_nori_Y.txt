	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Samul nori</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox" style="width: 22em"><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size: 125%; <p><b>Samul nori</b> (사물놀이) is a genre of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Percussion" title="Percussion">percussion</a> music that originated in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>. The word <i>samul</i> means "four objects", while <i>nori</i> means "play". Samul nori is performed with four <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Korean_musical_instruments" title="Traditional Korean musical instruments">traditional Korean musical instruments</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Pungmul" title="Pungmul">pungmul</a></i>. These are <i><a href="/wiki/Kkwaenggwari" title="Kkwaenggwari">Kkwaenggwari</a></i> (꽹과리), a small gong; <i><a href="/wiki/Jing_(instrument)" title="Jing (instrument)">Jing</a></i> (징), a larger gong; <i><a href="/wiki/Janggu" title="Janggu">Janggu</a></i> (장구), an hourglass-shaped <a href="/wiki/Drum" title="Drum">drum</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Buk_(drum)" title="Buk (drum)">Buk</a></i> (북), a <a href="/wiki/Barrel_drum" title="Barrel drum">barrel drum</a> similar to the <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a>.
</p><p>Samul nori's roots are in <i><a href="/wiki/Pungmul" title="Pungmul">Pungmul nori</a></i> (풍물놀이), meaning "playing Korean traditional percussion instruments", which is a Korean folk genre comprising music, <a href="/wiki/Acrobatics" title="Acrobatics">acrobatics</a>, folk dance, and rituals. Samul nori was traditionally performed in <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>-farming villages in order to ensure and to celebrate good harvests. Until modern times, nine-tenths of Korea's people were employed in agricultural work, and this genre defined Korean music.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Pungmul nori</i> is also called "Nong-ak nori" (농악놀이): "nong" meaning farm and "ak" meaning music. This name was instituted by the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">Japanese imperial government's</a> cultural colonization policies regarding Korean culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Samul nori</i> is the formalized, more modern version of <i>Pungmul nori</i>.
</p><p><i>Samul nori</i> started by adapting music from <i>Utdari pungmul</i> (the <i>gut</i>, or shaman ceremony rhythm of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeonggi-do" title="Gyeonggi-do">Gyeonggi-do</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chungcheong" title="Chungcheong">Chungcheong</a> provinces of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>), as well as the genres of <a href="/wiki/Yeongnam" title="Yeongnam">Yeongnam</a> folk music and <a href="/wiki/Honam" title="Honam">Honam</a> <i>udo gut</i>, combined with more contemporary improvisations, elaborations, and compositions.<sup><a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.lifeinkorea.com/culture/samul/samul.cfm?Subject=history" rel="nofollow">[3]</a></sup> The original music of these local rhymes is steeped in traditional <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korean_shamanism" title="Korean shamanism">shamanism</a> but also shows influences from Korean military music and <a href="/wiki/Korean_Buddhism" title="Korean Buddhism">Korean Buddhism</a>. While full <i>Pungmul nori</i> often features the use of wind instruments, <i>Samul nori</i> only features the four aforementioned percussion instruments.
</p><p>After seeing a <i>Samul nori</i> performance, a poet once described each of the four instruments as a different element of weather: the <i>janggu</i> represents rain; the <i>kkwaenggwari,</i> thunder; the <i>jing,</i> the sounds of the wind; and the <i>buk,</i> clouds.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Korean philosophy of Chun-Ji-In ("Chun" meaning heaven, "Ji" meaning Earth, and "In" meaning people) is also reflected in these instruments: the <i>buk</i> and <i>janggu</i> (leather) represent the sounds of the earth, while the <i>jing</i> and <i>kkwaenggwari</i> (metal) represent sounds of the heavens and the people playing. For this reason, <i>Samul nori</i> without the sound of people is considered incomplete.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Although it is generally performed indoors as a staged genre, <i>Samul nori</i> depicts the traditional Korean culture, an agricultural society rooted in the natural environment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Samul nori</i> is characterized by strong, accented rhythms, vibrant body movements, and an energetic spirit.
</p><p><i>Samul nori</i> has gained international popularity, with many <i>Samul nori</i> bands and camps performing worldwide. Since the 1980s in South Korea, there has been a marked increase in the amount of fusion music, which combines <i>Samul nori</i> and Western instruments.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>Samul nori</i> was also extensively used in the Korean musical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanta_(musical)" title="Nanta (musical)">Nanta</a>.
</p><p>The most famous <i>Samul nori</i> ensemble is the internationally famous South Korean ensemble styled SamulNori, which is credited for bringing the music from a rural folk genre to the contemporary stage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The group was established in February 1978 by <i>janggu</i> player and former <a href="/wiki/Namsadang" title="Namsadang">Namsadang</a> star performer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kim_Duk_Soo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kim Duk Soo (page does not exist)">Kim Duk Soo</a> (<span lang="ko-Hang" title="Korean language text">김덕수</span><a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.art.go.kr/vli_dir/vli_dir08_pop_detail.jsp?ar_vvm_cd_seq=8754" rel="nofollow">[4]</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> along with Kim Young Bae (<i>kkwaenggwari</i>), Choi Tae Hyun (<i>jing</i>), and Lee Jong Dae (<i>buk</i>). Following Kim Young Bae's death in 1985, he was replaced by Choi Jong Sil, and Lee Kwang Soo replaced Lee Jong Dae on the <i>buk</i>.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.lifeinkorea.com/culture/samul/samul.cfm?Subject=history" rel="nofollow">[5]</a>  The group has collaborated and recorded with a number of non-Korean ensembles, most notably in 1987 with the Red Sun jazz band, with one Samul Nori/Red Sun CD selling 70,000 copies.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.asiaweek.com/asiaweek/magazine/nations/0,8782,165862,00.html" rel="nofollow">[6]</a><a class="external autonumber" href="http://times.hankooki.com/lpage/culture/200610/kt2006101117513511690.htm" rel="nofollow">[7]</a> They have also performed in August 2000 at the Earth Celebration International Arts Festival on <a href="/wiki/Sado,_Niigata" title="Sado, Niigata">Sado Island</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> with the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Taiko" title="Taiko">taiko</a> group <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kodo_(taiko_group)" title="Kodo (taiko group)">Kodo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="external autonumber" href="https://www.allmusic.com/album/r254657" rel="nofollow">[8]</a>
</p><p>Regarding his choice to move from the more traditional outdoor performances to indoor venues, Kim Duk Soo states that at the time he established SamulNori, during the last years of the administration of former South Korean <a href="/wiki/President_of_South_Korea" title="President of South Korea">president</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Park_Chung_Hee" title="Park Chung Hee">Park Chung Hee</a>, Korean traditional music was associated with the student movement, and anyone playing such instruments outdoors could be arrested. Thus, he developed the current version of the genre, which is generally presented indoors on concert hall stages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, SamulNori expanded to include twenty performers and changed its name to SamulNori Hanullim, Inc. ("Hanullim" meaning "big bang").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
