	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">International trade in fine art</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>international trade of fine art</b> is most precisely defined as the trade across nations of unique, non-reproducible works by an artist.  The art trade contradicts typical international trade models since it is a culturally significant good.  It is not treated by consumers the same way any other commodity would because of the aesthetic value that is unique to each piece.  Despite existing as a finite physical piece, unique art is still considered intellectual property. This sparks the debate as to whether art exports should be restricted for nationalistic and cultural reasons, or liberalized for the sake of a healthier international market.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decorative_art_as_a_trade_commodity">Decorative art as a trade commodity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_trade_in_fine_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Decorative art as a trade commodity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The trade commodities included in the definition of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visual_art" title="Visual art">visual art</a>" include the following: <a href="/wiki/Painting" title="Painting">painting</a>, drawing, <a href="/wiki/Sculpture" title="Sculpture">sculpture</a> in various materials, <a href="/wiki/Printmaking" title="Printmaking">printmaking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">photography</a>, maps, <a href="/wiki/Performance_art" title="Performance art">performance art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Installation_art" title="Installation art">installation art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mail_art" title="Mail art">mail art</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assemblage_art" title="Assemblage art">assemblage art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Textile_arts" title="Textile arts">textile arts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">fashion design</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_art" title="Video art">video art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Digital_art" title="Digital art">digital art</a>, and product design.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-1">[1]</a></sup> These works are non-functional, emotional, social, political, traditional, and cultural statements, and in comparison to other goods, are not greatly affected by commercial-sector constraints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-one-1">[1]</a></sup> Though visual art is a physical, hand-made good, it is often culturally rooted and created for aesthetic appeal.  Therefore, art is considered <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">intellectual property</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-one-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 4-digit <a href="/wiki/Standard_International_Trade_Classification" title="Standard International Trade Classification">Standard International Trade Classification</a> (SITC) classifies "Works of Art, Collectors Pieces and Antiques" under category 8960, which includes paintings, drawings, pastels, original sculptures, original prints, stamps, and antiques over 100 years old. This is the only SITC category that consists of unique, non-reproducible art, which is typically thought of as "fine" art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup> The 4-digit <a href="/wiki/Harmonized_System" title="Harmonized System">harmonized commodity</a> description and coding system, otherwise known as the harmonized system (HS) code for "fine" art is 9701, which is classified as "Paintings, Drawings and Pastels, Executed Entirely By Hand".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-three-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_international_art_trade">History of international art trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_trade_in_fine_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of international art trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest known regulation of cultural property dates to 1464, when <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_II" title="Pope Pius II">Pope Pius II</a> prohibited the exportation of works of art from the Papal States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-four-4">[4]</a></sup> It wasn’t until the mid-1500s that any sizable amount of formal artwork was transferred between nations in licit markets.  Previously, local demand had satisfied the supply of artwork, but it couldn’t keep up as the number of artists increased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-five-5">[5]</a></sup>  Consequently, artists exported their works to foreign markets.  Between 1540 and 1670, an average of 144 paintings per year were transported between the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">New Spain</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-five-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Throughout much of the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>, if an artist could not domestically sell his art, he sold it instead to dealers who exported the works abroad.  Starting in the 17th Century, however, most art was traded at the massive auction houses of <a href="/wiki/Christie%27s" title="Christie's">Christie's</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">Sotheby's</a> of London, which both still survive today.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-five-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like any other traded good, art has been historically subjected to import duties.  For example, in the more enlightened years of the 19th Century, art escaped high tariffs in America because the government viewed art as an important cultural good.   At other times, though, tariff revenue was considered more important than free intellectual property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-six-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, neutral <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> became the primary trafficker of art on the European continent.  Most "degenerate" works of art that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nazi" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> government purged from German museums were sold there, where they largely entered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_markets" title="Black markets">black markets</a>.  Since the war ended, there has been a massive, ongoing effort to recover all of these works.  For fifteen years following the war, 45,000 pieces were returned to France, mostly to Jewish owners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-seven-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, almost every country in the world has restrictions and regulations on the export of cultural property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-four-4">[4]</a></sup>  Currently, most art auctions are facilitated on online sites such as <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">eBay</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://Lauritz.com/" rel="nofollow">Lauritz.com</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-five-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_theory_of_art_trade">Economic theory of art trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_trade_in_fine_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic theory of art trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fine art proves complicated for economists to analyze, mostly because trade in unique art is in large part trade between consumers – the "secondary market" – rather than the "primary market" trade between the producer (artist) and the consumer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup>  For example, when a museum buys a sculpture from a private collector, the exchange is between two consumers and considered a transaction in the secondary market because neither of them produced the sculpture.  It is more complicated for economists, then, to capture these transactions in their data.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Comparative_advantage" title="Comparative advantage">Comparative advantage</a> is also more difficult to pinpoint in the case of cultural goods.   There is a certain degree of cultural nationalism of art,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eight_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-eight-8">[8]</a></sup> making some nations reluctant to part with their cultural property.  Additionally, relative advantage cannot simply be calculated by the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_cost" title="Marginal cost">marginal cost</a> of producing a unit of art, since aesthetic value plays so heavily into its price.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Trade theory demonstrates how much and at what price countries trade goods if they have different endowments or different preferences.  But this model is not very useful because of retentive nationalism:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nine-9">[9]</a></sup> a country that is relatively less endowed with art-producing resources will not stop producing simply because they can import art from abroad at cheaper prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Determination_of_prices">Determination of prices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_trade_in_fine_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Determination of prices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditional trade theory treats art as a homogenous, non-differentiated good, which is where it fails to reliably predict trade trends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup> Unique art is valued precisely because of its uniqueness.  Since each piece of art is different, and because each piece does not appear on the market very often, the determination of changes in market value prove difficult to determine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-two-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Economists use the <a href="/wiki/Hedonic_regression" title="Hedonic regression">hedonic regression</a> (HR) estimation method to calculate prices in art.  This is used to predict prices based on various attributes of the artwork such as its dimensions, the artist, and the subject matter attended to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ten_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-ten-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protectionism_vs._Liberalization">Protectionism vs. Liberalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=International_trade_in_fine_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protectionism vs. Liberalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Protectionism" title="Protectionism">Protectionism</a> is a nationalistic viewpoint that contends that a healthy cultural industry is necessary to assert national sovereignty and identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-11">[11]</a></sup>  Countries with small domestic markets are often overwhelmed by imports from larger markets in which producers can make up their costs of production by dumping content abroad.  America is the largest exporter of artwork in the world, and English speaking countries are especially vulnerable to American imports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-three-3">[3]</a></sup> Protectionists view this as a modern form of American <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">imperialism</a>, which reduces cultural diversity when national industries are unable to compete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Protectionism is less concerned with direct economic interest as it is with preserving cultural integrity.  Australian economist <a href="/wiki/David_Throsby" title="David Throsby">David Throsby</a> argues that investment in "cultural capital" may be necessary for the sustainability of a culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-twelve_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-twelve-12">[12]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_M._Bator" title="Paul M. Bator">Paul M. Bator</a>, who helped negotiate and draft the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> convention on the International Trade of Art, argues that larger countries are responsible for the cultural interests of smaller ones.  He explains in <i>The International Trade in Art</i> that "art-rich countries should create tax and other financial and psychological incentives to persuade those with important antiquities and archaeological objects to keep them at home".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-four-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><br/>
Proponents of <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalization" title="Economic liberalization">liberalization</a> in international art markets stress that open markets contribute to efficient production and distribution of cultural products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-11">[11]</a></sup> Reliance on competitive market forces also frees creative individuals from the hindrance of government oversight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-11">[11]</a></sup>   Many special characteristics of cultural products – the lack of rivalry in content, <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">economies of scale</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_agglomeration" title="Economies of agglomeration">agglomeration economies</a> – enhance the case for opening markets.  Protection raises rather than lowers domestic prices, which detracts from consumer welfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The "<a href="/wiki/Commodification" title="Commodification">commodification</a> objection" is the reluctance to classify an important cultural good as a simple trade commodity, and is frequently cited by cultural protectionists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nine-9">[9]</a></sup>  Those who advocate openness believe that free-market nations can provide the most effective political force for development of an active market.  This argument diametrically opposes Bator’s: free-trade advocates contend that large countries are better suited to help smaller nations finance organization of their cultural resources for more effective participation in international trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eleven_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-eleven-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The issue of the illicit art trade also factors into the debate.  In terms of value transferred, the illegal trafficking of art ranks in black market activities second only to narcotics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-thirteeen_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-thirteeen-13">[13]</a></sup> More problematic than the actual theft of the work is its subsequent transport, which proves difficult to prosecute since most paintings are easily concealable. By maintaining an open market, liberalists argue that much of the illicit trade of art can be eliminated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nine_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-nine-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
