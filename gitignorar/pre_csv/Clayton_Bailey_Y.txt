<p><b>Clayton George Bailey</b> (born 9 March 1939 in <a href="/wiki/Antigo,_Wisconsin" title="Antigo, Wisconsin">Antigo, Wisconsin</a>), is an American artist who works primarily in the mediums of ceramic and metal sculpture.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clayton_Bailey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bailey attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Madison" title="University of Wisconsin–Madison">University of Wisconsin–Madison</a>, where he received a B.S. degree in 1961, followed by an M.S. in Art and Art Education in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1962, Bailey served as a technical assistant to <a href="/wiki/Harvey_Littleton" title="Harvey Littleton">Harvey Littleton</a>, who was conducting glassblowing seminars at the <a href="/wiki/Toledo_Museum_of_Art" title="Toledo Museum of Art">Toledo Museum of Art</a>.
</p><p>Over the next five years, Bailey will travel the country accepting invitations to teach, from the People's Art Center in St. Louis, Missouri to positions with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_South_Dakota" title="University of South Dakota">University of South Dakota</a>. During this period Bailey receives a Louis Comfort Tiffany grant (1963), and will be appointed artist-in-residence at <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin%E2%80%93Whitewater" title="University of Wisconsin–Whitewater">University of Wisconsin–Whitewater</a>, where he will teach ceramics for the next three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the bequest of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Arneson" title="Robert Arneson">Robert Arneson</a>, Bailey covers Arneson's classes at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Davis" title="University of California, Davis">University of California, Davis</a> while Arneson is on sabbatical in 1967. In 1968, Bailey relocates to northern California, where he becomes a leading figure in the ceramic vein of the regional <a href="/wiki/Funk_art" title="Funk art">Funk art</a> movement, as had been pioneered by Arneson. Much of the <a href="/wiki/Funk_art" title="Funk art">Funk art</a> activity is centered around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Davis" title="UC Davis">UC Davis</a>, where other prominent figures in the movement (<a href="/wiki/Roy_De_Forest" title="Roy De Forest">Roy De Forest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Neri" title="Manuel Neri">Manuel Neri</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_T._Wiley" title="William T. Wiley">William T. Wiley</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Gilhooly" title="David Gilhooly">David Gilhooly</a>, Chris Unterseher, Margaret Dodd) either taught, or attended classes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A leading venue for exhibiting <a href="/wiki/Funk_art" title="Funk art">Funk art</a> was at the <a href="/wiki/Candy_Store_Gallery" title="Candy Store Gallery">Candy Store Gallery</a>, located in nearby <a href="/wiki/Folsom,_California" title="Folsom, California">Folsom, California</a>, where Bailey would regularly present work in the context of both solo and group shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1968, Bailey began teaching at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/California_State_University,_Hayward" title="California State University, Hayward">California State University, Hayward</a> (now <a href="/wiki/California_State_University,_East_Bay" title="California State University, East Bay">California State University, East Bay</a>). He retired from this position in June 1996, with the title of Professor Emeritus of Ceramics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1970, Bailey established a home-studio space in rural <a href="/wiki/Port_Costa,_California" title="Port Costa, California">Port Costa, California</a>, where he became neighbors with fellow artist <a href="/wiki/Roy_De_Forest" title="Roy De Forest">Roy De Forest</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup> The two collaborated on numerous projects,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup> and remained friends until De Forest's death in 2007.
</p><p>Overlapping the <a href="/wiki/Funk_art" title="Funk art">Funk art</a> movement was <a href="/wiki/Nut_art" title="Nut art">Nut art</a>, a term coined by De Forest,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Linhares_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Linhares-4">[4]</a></sup> which brought together many of the same practitioners including Arneson, De Forest, and Gilhooly, along with <a href="/wiki/Peter_Saul" title="Peter Saul">Peter Saul</a>, Jerry Gooch, Victor Cicanski, Richard Shaw, David Zack. In 1972, Bailey co-curated the first-ever Nut art show at California State University, Hayward.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Stylistically, Bailey's work bridges several different categories and styles including Funk art, Nut art, ceramic and metallic sculpture, and Performance art. A recurring thread through all his work is humor,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup> along with a high degree of craftsmanship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another track for artistic expression developed with the creation of Bailey's alter ego Dr. George Gladstone, beginning in 1969. Initially, the works blended performance and creations based on pseudo-science and personal mythologies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Albright_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Albright-6">[6]</a></sup> Works included the creation of fossilized remains (usually ceramic or from earth materials) and the classification of a new time period, the Pre-Credulous Era, the source of such Kaolithic curiosities as a cyclops skull and a <a href="/wiki/Bigfoot" title="Bigfoot">Bigfoot</a> skeleton.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup> Dressed in a lab coat and pith helmet,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup> Bailey as Dr. Gladstone performed excavations and staged performances and pranks. The World of Wonders, a traveling museum dedicated to Dr. Gladstone's life and work was developed, and presented in various venues, beginning with the Richmond Art Center. In 1976, The World of Wonders Museum opened in downtown Port Costa, where it became a local tourist attraction until its closure in 1978. The collection is now split between Bailey's home-studio, and the Bailey Art Museum in downtown <a href="/wiki/Crockett,_California" title="Crockett, California">Crockett, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Over the years, the Dr. Gladstone character evolved into something more akin to a mad scientist, allowing Bailey to explore other forms of art with pseudo-scientific origins and associations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup> Most notable have been sculptures based on robots, beginning in the late 1970s and continuing on to today, although this work is now presented, and credited as Bailey's, not Dr. Gladstone's. The latest offshoot of the metallic sculpture (built from found materials and objects) has been a series of Space Guns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DePaoli_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-DePaoli-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1974, Bailey was profiled in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esquire_Magazine" title="Esquire Magazine">Esquire Magazine</a> as Dr. Gladstone. In the article written by <a href="/wiki/Susan_Subtle" title="Susan Subtle">Susan Subtle</a>, "Their Arts Belong to Dada",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esq_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-esq-7">[7]</a></sup> Bailey was featured alongside artists <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Darling" title="Lowell Darling">Lowell Darling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anna_Banana" title="Anna Banana">Anna Banana</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ant_Farm" title="Ant Farm">Ant Farm (group)</a>, Futzie Nutzle, Dr. Brute &amp; <a href="/wiki/Kate_Craig" title="Kate Craig">Lady Brute</a>, Mr. Peanut, Irene Dogmatic, <a href="/wiki/AA_Bronson" title="AA Bronson">AA Bronson</a>, Captain Video, Flakey Rose Hip, Henry Humble, The Gluers<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and Dickens Bascom, Don and Rae Davis, and T.R. Uthco.
</p><p>Presently, Bailey continues to work in a variety of mediums and styles. Throughout his career as an artist, Bailey has exhibited regularly throughout the United States, Europe, and Asia in both solo and group exhibitions. In 2011, Bailey was honored with a 50-year retrospective exhibition (Clayton Bailey's World of Wonders) at the <a href="/wiki/Crocker_Art_Museum" title="Crocker Art Museum">Crocker Art Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sacramento,_California" title="Sacramento, California">Sacramento, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1970, Bailey and his wife (fellow artist Betty Bailey), have resided in Port Costa, California.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daniels_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Daniels-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bailey_Art_Museum">Bailey Art Museum</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clayton_Bailey&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bailey Art Museum">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2013, Bailey and his wife, artist <a href="/wiki/Betty_G._Bailey" title="Betty G. Bailey">Betty G. Bailey</a>, founded the Bailey Art Museum in <a href="/wiki/Crockett,_California" title="Crockett, California">Crockett, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> The Bailey Art Museum is near the Bailey's residence and studio in Port Costa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The 3,200 square feet (300 m<sup>2</sup>) space brings together works from across the artist's five decades plus career featuring examples of <a href="/wiki/Funk_art" title="Funk art">Funk art</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nut_art" title="Nut art">Nut art</a>, ceramics, and metal sculpture (including robots and <a href="/wiki/Space_gun" title="Space gun">space guns</a>), as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-scientific" title="Pseudo-scientific">pseudo-scientific</a> curiosities by the artist's alter-ego Dr. Gladstone. The museum also includes narrative watercolor drawings by Betty G. Bailey and a gift shop.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>

<p><i>Selected Public Collections</i>
