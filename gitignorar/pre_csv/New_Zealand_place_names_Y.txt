	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">New Zealand place names</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

Most <b>New Zealand place names</b> have <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> or <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> origins. Both groups used names to commemorate notable people, events, places from their homeland, and their ships, or to describe the surrounding area. It is unknown whether Māori had a name for the whole of <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> before the arrival of Europeans, but post-colonisation the name <i><a href="/wiki/Aotearoa" title="Aotearoa">Aotearoa</a></i> (commonly translated as 'long white cloud') has been used to refer to the whole country. Dutch cartographers named the islands <i>Nova Zeelandia,</i> the Latin translation of the Dutch <i>Nieuw Zeeland</i> (after the Dutch province of <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a>).  British explorer <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> subsequently <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicised" title="Anglicised">anglicised</a> the name to <i>New Zealand</i>.
</p><p>Many of the early <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">Māori names</a> were replaced by Europeans during the late 18th and early 19th centuries. Government <a href="/wiki/Amend_(motion)" title="Amend (motion)">amendments</a> in 1894 and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Geographic_Board" title="New Zealand Geographic Board">New Zealand Geographic Board</a> in the mid-1940s led to the encouragement of original Māori names, although differing spellings and anglicised pronunciations persisted. Many names now have alternative or <a href="/wiki/Dual_naming" title="Dual naming">dual</a> English and Māori names or, in a few rare cases, dual Māori names or dual English names. Most names have never been made official, but if they are mentioned in authoritative publications they are considered recorded names. Colloquial names in New Zealand result from an ironic view of the place's entertainment value, or plays on advertising mottos, or are shortened versions of the full name. Some places tried to capitalise on the success of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> films by linking themselves to the movies.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Country_and_main_islands">Country and main islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_place_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Country and main islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius,_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png"><img alt="brown square paper with Dutch writing and a thick red, curved line" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="532" data-file-width="684" decoding="async" height="171" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png/220px-Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png/330px-Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png/440px-Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius%2C_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Detail_of_1657_map_Polus_Antarcticus_by_Jan_Janssonius,_showing_Nova_Zeelandia.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dutch map of 1657 showing western coastline of "Nova Zeelandia"</div></div></div>
<p>No known pre-contact Māori name for New Zealand as a whole survives, although the Māori had several names for the North and South Islands, including <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Te_Ika-a-M%C4%81ui" title="Te Ika-a-Māui">Te Ika-a-Māui</a></i> (the fish of <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ui_(M%C4%81ori_mythology)" title="Māui (Māori mythology)">Māui</a>) for the <a href="/wiki/North_Island" title="North Island">North Island</a> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Te_Waipounamu" title="Te Waipounamu">Te Waipounamu</a></i> (the waters of <a href="/wiki/Pounamu" title="Pounamu">greenstone</a>) and <i>Te Waka o Aoraki</i> (the canoe of Aoraki) for the <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMein_Smith20056_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMein_Smith20056-1">[1]</a></sup> Until the early 20th century, Māori also referred to the North Island as <i><a href="/wiki/Aotearoa" title="Aotearoa">Aotearoa</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKing200341_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing200341-2">[2]</a></sup> (commonly translated as 'long white cloud'); in modern Māori usage this has become the name for the whole country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHay,_Maclagan,_Gordon72_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHay,_Maclagan,_Gordon72-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first European visitor to New Zealand, Dutch explorer <a href="/wiki/Abel_Tasman" title="Abel Tasman">Abel Tasman</a>, named the islands <i>Staten Landt</i>, believing they were part of the <a href="/wiki/Isla_de_los_Estados" title="Isla de los Estados">Staten Landt</a> that <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Le_Maire" title="Jacob Le Maire">Jacob Le Maire</a> had seen off the southern end of South America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_Brouwer" title="Hendrik Brouwer">Hendrik Brouwer</a> proved that the South American land was an island in 1643, and Dutch cartographers subsequently renamed Tasman's discovery <i>Nova Zeelandia,</i> from Latin, after the Dutch province of <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StatenLandt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-StatenLandt-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <i>Nova Zeelandia</i> later became <i>Nieuw Zeeland</i> in <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, and British explorer <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> subsequently called the archipelago <i>New Zealand</i>, an anglicised form of the Dutch name.
</p><p>The 1840 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Letters_Patent" title="Letters Patent">Letters Patent</a> established New Zealand as a British colony and claimed the "principal islands" of New Zealand, identified by their commonly known names at the time: the Northern Island (the North Island), the Middle Island (the South Island) and Stewart's Island or "South Island".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The letters patent attempted to rename the islands <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Ulster" title="New Ulster">New Ulster</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Munster_Province" title="New Munster Province">New Munster</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Leinster" title="New Leinster">New Leinster</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Ireland" title="Provinces of Ireland">provinces in Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Paterson_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Paterson-7">[7]</a></sup> New Ulster, New Munster and New Leinster were also used for the initial <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_New_Zealand" title="Provinces of New Zealand">provinces of New Zealand</a>, but the names did not endure. In the 1830s the South Island was used as an alternative to Middle Island and by 1907 it became the common name. The North and South Island names arose through common usage rather than official declaration<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NZ_name_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NZ_name-9">[9]</a></sup> and in 2009 it was revealed that they had never been formalised.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, alternative names were formalised for the two main islands, as North Island or Te Ika-a-Māui, and South Island or Te Waipounamu,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> with either English or Māori name being used or a combination of both.
</p>
<h2><span id="M.C4.81ori_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Māori_names">Māori names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_place_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Māori names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cook_new_zealand.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="601" data-file-width="499" decoding="async" height="361" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Cook_new_zealand.jpg/300px-Cook_new_zealand.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Cook_new_zealand.jpg/450px-Cook_new_zealand.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/48/Cook_new_zealand.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cook_new_zealand.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Captain James Cook's map of New Zealand, showing a mixture of Māori names and names Cook bestowed himself</div></div></div>
<p>Many Māori place names possess either historical or mythological significance. Their meaning is not always apparent from literal translations, but some interpretations have passed down through oral tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maori_Names_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Maori_Names-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIG_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FIG-14">[14]</a></sup> Before the arrivals of Europeans, place names often commemorated notable or historical incidents, described features of the location or  derived from traditional <i><a href="/wiki/Hawaiki" title="Hawaiki">Hawaiki</a></i> names or myths. After European arrival many locations became known under names representing Māori versions of European words or poorly-pronounced contractions of the original Māori names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maori_Names_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-Maori_Names-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early Māori explorers such as <a href="/wiki/Kupe" title="Kupe">Kupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ngahue" title="Ngahue">Ngahue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Toi_(name)" title="Toi (name)">Toi</a> named many of New Zealand's coastal features. Like later European explorers, they named things after themselves, their family members and events that occurred at the newly-discovered locations. <a href="/wiki/Kahumatamomoe" title="Kahumatamomoe">Kahumatamomoe</a> named <a href="/wiki/Manukau_Harbour" title="Manukau Harbour">Manukau Harbour</a> after a <a href="/wiki/Leptospermum_scoparium" title="Leptospermum scoparium">manuka</a> stake that he used to claim ownership of the area, and <a href="/wiki/Kaipara_Harbour" title="Kaipara Harbour">Kaipara Harbour</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Ptisana_salicina" title="Ptisana salicina">para fern</a> he ate there (<i>kai</i> means food).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Māori name for <a href="/wiki/Wellington_Harbour" title="Wellington Harbour">Wellington Harbour</a>, <i>Te Whanganui a Tara</i> (the great harbour of Tara), derives from Tara, a grandson of Kupe and ancestor of several local <i><a href="/wiki/Iwi" title="Iwi">iwi</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Names from other islands visited during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maori_migration_canoes" title="Maori migration canoes">Polynesian migrations</a> have become attached to some New Zealand landmarks, for example <a href="/wiki/Raratoka_Island" title="Raratoka Island">Raratoka Island</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Rarotonga" title="Rarotonga">Rarotonga</a>) and Tawhiti (from <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a>). <a href="/wiki/Whakatane" title="Whakatane">Whakatane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rangitoto_Island" title="Rangitoto Island">Rangitoto Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taupo" title="Taupo">Taupo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Te_Urewera" title="Te Urewera">Urewera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ngongotaha" title="Ngongotaha">Ngongotaha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Tikitapu" title="Lake Tikitapu">Tikitapu</a> all commemorate incidents that occurred during the early arrivals, many of which are now forgotten.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maori_Names_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-Maori_Names-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Maketu" title="Maketu">Maketu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moehau_Range" title="Moehau Range"> Mount Moehau</a> are two of the few remaining names connected to places in <i>Hawaiki</i>. The use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynesian_mythology" title="Polynesian mythology">Polynesian mythology</a> in names is more apparent, with <a href="/wiki/T%C4%81ne" title="Tāne">Tāne</a> (the forest god) lending his name to <a href="/wiki/Otane" title="Otane">Otane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taneatua" title="Taneatua">Taneatua</a>, among others.
</p><p>Descriptive words often occur as part of a place name: widely-used elements include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>ara</i> (path, road)</li>
<li><i>hau</i> (wind)</li>
<li><i>maunga</i> (mountain)</li>
<li><i>moana" (sea, ocean)</i></li>
<li><i>nui</i> (big)</li>
<li><i>one</i> (mud, sand, beach)</li>
<li><i>puke</i> (hill)</li>
<li><i>roto</i> (lake)</li>
<li><i>wai</i> (water)</li>
<li><i>whanga</i> (bay)</li>
<li><i>whenua</i> (land, country)</li></ul>
<p>Thus <a href="/wiki/Whanganui" title="Whanganui">Whanganui</a> means "wide river-mouth" and <a href="/wiki/Waikanae" title="Waikanae">Waikanae</a> indicates good waters for catching <i>kanae</i>, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flathead_mullet" title="Flathead mullet">flathead mullet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>European arrivals exposed Māori to Christianity, leading to the settlements of <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem,_New_Zealand" title="Jerusalem, New Zealand">Hiruharama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay_View,_New_Zealand" title="Bay View, New Zealand">Petane</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallett%27s_Bay,_New_Zealand" title="Hallett's Bay, New Zealand">Hamaria</a> - named after the biblical <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bethany" title="Bethany">Bethany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samaria" title="Samaria">Samaria</a> respectively. The names of the <a href="/wiki/Ranana" title="Ranana">Rānana</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atene" title="Atene">Ātene</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Karaponia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Karaponia (page does not exist)">Karaponia</a> settlements represent Māori-language <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transliterations" title="Transliterations">transliterations</a> of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> and <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maori_Names_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-Maori_Names-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moriori_people" title="Moriori people"> Moriori</a>, descendants of Māori,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Islands" title="Chatham Islands">Chatham Islands</a> (off the eastern coasts of mainland New Zealand) <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 1500 CE and named them Rēkohu (Misty Sun). Sometime before 1835 mainland Māori settled at Rēkohu and confused the name of the settlement, Wharekauri, with the name of the main island. It has been known as Wharekauri to Māori ever since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-colonial_recognition">Post-colonial recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_place_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post-colonial recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early Europeans replaced most of the original Māori names with their own, although they retained some Māori names, especially in the north and central regions of the North Island. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Geographical_Society_of_London" title="Royal Geographical Society of London">Royal Geographical Society of London</a> was responsible for place names until 1894 when authority was given to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_Zealand" title="Governor of New Zealand">Governor of New Zealand</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIG_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-FIG-14">[14]</a></sup> The future <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Prime Minister of New Zealand"> Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Ward" title="Joseph Ward">Joseph Ward</a> moved an <a href="/wiki/Amend_(motion)" title="Amend (motion)">amendment</a> in 1894 that gave preference to Māori names for any new features and allowed the alteration of misspelt names (although this did not always occur). In 1924 the Honorary Geographic Board of New Zealand was set up<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to advise on place names, and in 1946 the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Parliament" title="New Zealand Parliament">New Zealand Parliament</a> established the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Geographic_Board" title="New Zealand Geographic Board">New Zealand Geographic Board</a> (NZGB) and gave it power to change or implement Māori and English names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIG_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-FIG-14">[14]</a></sup> Anyone can propose a geographical name to the Board, which consults local Māori and allows public submissions before determining if a name should be made official.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Names can also be made official through an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a>. The NZGB is required to keep a <a class="external text" href="https://www.linz.govt.nz/regulatory/place-names/find-place-name/new-zealand-gazetteer-place-names" rel="nofollow">public list</a> of all the official New Zealand geographical names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FIG_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-FIG-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Help_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Help-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The NZGB encourages the use of original Māori names and has given some places official double names. These can be expressed as alternative names where officially either name could be used (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Taranaki_or_Mount_Egmont" title="Mount Taranaki or Mount Egmont">Mount Taranaki or Mount Egmont</a>) or dual names where both names should be used together (<a href="/wiki/Matiu_/_Somes_Island" title="Matiu / Somes Island">Matiu / Somes Island</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> In 1998, as a result of the settling of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ngai_Tahu" title="Ngai Tahu">Ngai Tahu</a> <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi_claims_and_settlements" title="Treaty of Waitangi claims and settlements">Treaty claim</a>, New Zealand's tallest mountain officially became <a href="/wiki/Aoraki_/_Mount_Cook" title="Aoraki / Mount Cook">Aoraki / Mount Cook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> There are also a few English dual names, with <a href="/wiki/Wellington_Harbour" title="Wellington Harbour">Wellington Harbour</a> also officially known as Port Nicholson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Māori groups have campaigned to correct inaccurately-spelled or -pronounced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori-language" title="Māori-language">Māori-language</a> place names. In 2000 the Geographic Board declined a local <i>iwi</i> suggestion to rename the Wellington suburb of <a href="/wiki/Hataitai" title="Hataitai">Hataitai</a> to <i>Whataitai</i> (the name of a <i><a href="/wiki/Taniwha" title="Taniwha">taniwha</a></i> (sea-monster) which legend says lived in Wellington Harbour).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proposed_name_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Proposed_name-26">[26]</a></sup> In 2009 the NZGB recommended renaming the city of Wanganui to <a href="/wiki/Whanganui" title="Whanganui">Whanganui</a> as the town was originally named after the <a href="/wiki/Whanganui_River" title="Whanganui River">Whanganui River</a> and the word  <i>wanga</i> is not in the Māori <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Proposed_name_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-Proposed_name-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title=" Dead link since December 2018">dead link</span></a></i>]</span></sup>The government decided later in 2009 to accept both "Whanganui" and "Wanganui" as alternative official names.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
The spelling "Wanganui" may have originated to reflect the <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialectal</a> pronunciation of local Māori, who pronounce 'wh' (an "f"-like sound in other dialects) as <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">[ˀw]</span> – a <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop"> glottalised</a> "w".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_names">European names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_place_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: European names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:George_Edward_Grey.jpg"><img alt="Black and White photograph of Sir George Grey" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1064" data-file-width="559" decoding="async" height="419" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/George_Edward_Grey.jpg/220px-George_Edward_Grey.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/George_Edward_Grey.jpg/330px-George_Edward_Grey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/George_Edward_Grey.jpg/440px-George_Edward_Grey.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:George_Edward_Grey.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Grey_River" title="Grey River">Grey River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Grey_/_Maukatere" title="Mount Grey / Maukatere">Mount Grey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greytown,_New_Zealand" title="Greytown, New Zealand">Greytown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greymouth" title="Greymouth">Greymouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grey_Lynn" title="Grey Lynn">Grey Lynn</a> all derive their name from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_George_Grey" title="Sir George Grey">Sir George Grey</a></div></div></div>
<p>Tasman named a few geographic features as he sailed along New Zealand's west coast. Many such as <a href="/wiki/Golden_Bay" title="Golden Bay">Murderers Bay</a> did not last, while some like <a href="/wiki/Cape_Maria_van_Diemen" title="Cape Maria van Diemen">Cape Maria van Diemen</a> (the wife of the governor of <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Batavia</a>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Kings_Islands" title="Three Kings Islands">Three Kings Islands</a> are still in use. Cook named many more on his voyage, including <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Islands" title="Bay of Islands">Bay of Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Plenty" title="Bay of Plenty">Bay of Plenty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poverty_Bay" title="Poverty Bay">Poverty Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Farewell,_New_Zealand" title="Cape Farewell, New Zealand">Cape Farewell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Charlotte_Sound_(New_Zealand)" title="Queen Charlotte Sound (New Zealand)">Queen Charlotte Sound</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Egmont" title="Mount Egmont">Mount Egmont</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banks_Peninsula" title="Banks Peninsula">Banks Peninsula</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cape_Kidnappers" title="Cape Kidnappers">Cape Kidnappers</a> commemorates a Māori attempt to kidnap one of Cook's crew members. <a href="/wiki/Jules_Dumont_d%27Urville" title="Jules Dumont d'Urville">Jules Dumont d'Urville</a> named <a href="/wiki/French_Pass" title="French Pass">French Pass</a> after sailing through it. The explorers lent their names to many places, including <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tasman" title="Mount Tasman">Mount Tasman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tasman_Glacier" title="Tasman Glacier">Tasman Glacier</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tasman_Region" title="Tasman Region">Tasman Region</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cook_Strait" title="Cook Strait">Cook Strait</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Cook" title="Mount Cook">Mount Cook</a> and <a href="/wiki/D%27Urville_Island_(New_Zealand)" title="D'Urville Island (New Zealand)">d'Urville Island</a>. The <a href="/wiki/HMS_Malabar_(1804)" title="HMS Malabar (1804)"><i>Coromandel</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Chatham_(1788)" title="HMS Chatham (1788)"><i>Chatham</i></a> and <i>Pegasus</i> ships visited New Zealand's shores between 1791 and 1820 and were used to name <a href="/wiki/Coromandel_Peninsula" title="Coromandel Peninsula">Coromandel Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Islands" title="Chatham Islands">Chatham Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_Bay" title="Pegasus Bay">Pegasus Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_Pegasus" title="Port Pegasus">Port Pegasus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Island" title="Stewart Island">Stewart Island</a> was named after the first officer aboard the <i>Pegasus</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Most European names came during the 1840s to the 1910s and were given by surveyors who worked for colonising associations or provincial governments. Names acknowledged the colonising associations sponsors (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hutt_Valley,_New_Zealand" title="Hutt Valley, New Zealand">Hutt Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield,_New_Zealand" title="Wakefield, New Zealand">Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_Chalmers" title="Port Chalmers">Port Chalmers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ashburton,_New_Zealand" title="Ashburton, New Zealand">Ashburton</a>), ships (<a href="/wiki/Bombay_Hills" title="Bombay Hills">Bombay</a>), government officials (<a href="/wiki/Featherston,_New_Zealand" title="Featherston, New Zealand">Featherston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rolleston,_New_Zealand" title="Rolleston, New Zealand">Rolleston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invercargill" title="Invercargill">Invercargill</a>), politicians (<a href="/wiki/Foxton,_New_Zealand" title="Foxton, New Zealand">Foxton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fox_Glacier" title="Fox Glacier">Fox Glacier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gisborne,_New_Zealand" title="Gisborne, New Zealand">Gisborne</a>) and church leaders (<a href="/wiki/Selwyn_District" title="Selwyn District">Selwyn</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> Military heroes and famous battles were popular place names, with <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napier,_New_Zealand" title="Napier, New Zealand">Napier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hastings,_New_Zealand" title="Hastings, New Zealand">Hastings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Havelock,_New_Zealand" title="Havelock, New Zealand">Havelock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Picton,_New_Zealand" title="Picton, New Zealand">Picton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_Region" title="Marlborough Region">Marlborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nelson,_New_Zealand" title="Nelson, New Zealand">Nelson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blenheim,_New_Zealand" title="Blenheim, New Zealand">Blenheim</a>. British politicians and royalty lent their names to <a href="/wiki/Russell,_New_Zealand" title="Russell, New Zealand">Russell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Palmerston,_New_Zealand" title="Palmerston, New Zealand">Palmerston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cromwell,_New_Zealand" title="Cromwell, New Zealand">Cromwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queenstown,_New_Zealand" title="Queenstown, New Zealand">Queenstown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexandra,_New_Zealand" title="Alexandra, New Zealand">Alexandra</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Franz_Josef,_New_Zealand" title="Franz Josef, New Zealand">Franz Josef</a> is named after the Austrian emperor. Royal names are more popular for streets than towns or geographical features, although both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Victoria,_Auckland" title="Mount Victoria, Auckland">Auckland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Victoria,_Wellington" title="Mount Victoria, Wellington">Wellington have Mount Victoria</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gore,_New_Zealand" title="Gore, New Zealand">Gore</a> is named after governor <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gore_Browne" title="Thomas Gore Browne">Thomas Gore Browne</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Grey" title="George Grey">George Grey</a>'s name is used for <a href="/wiki/Greytown,_New_Zealand" title="Greytown, New Zealand">Greytown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greymouth" title="Greymouth">Greymouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grey_Lynn" title="Grey Lynn">Grey Lynn</a> and many natural features. Relatively few names are derived from British towns, with <a href="/wiki/Dunedin" title="Dunedin">Dunedin</a> (after <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>—<i>Dùn Éideann</i> in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>), <a href="/wiki/New_Plymouth" title="New Plymouth">New Plymouth</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Province" title="Canterbury Province">Canterbury Province</a> among the most notable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many inland locations (including <a href="/wiki/Helensville" title="Helensville">Helensville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dargaville" title="Dargaville">Dargaville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morrinsville" title="Morrinsville">Morrinsville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulls,_New_Zealand" title="Bulls, New Zealand">Bulls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masterton" title="Masterton">Masterton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Levin,_New_Zealand" title="Levin, New Zealand">Levin</a>) were named after the early settlers. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mackenzie_Country" title="Mackenzie Country">Mackenzie Country</a> takes its name from a <a href="/wiki/James_Mckenzie_(outlaw)" title="James Mckenzie (outlaw)">sheep stealer</a> and <a href="/wiki/King_Country" title="King Country">King Country</a> references the region where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_king" title="Māori king">Māori king</a> defied colonial government for many years. Mountains and <a href="/wiki/Mountain_pass" title="Mountain pass">passes</a> were named after their discoverers (<a href="/wiki/Haast_Pass" title="Haast Pass">Haast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Pass" title="Lewis Pass">Lewis</a>) or scientists (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Newton,_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Newton, New Zealand (page does not exist)">Newton</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mount_Lyell,_New_Zealand&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mount Lyell, New Zealand (page does not exist)">Lyell</a>). Other names were descriptive, such as <a href="/wiki/Woodville,_New_Zealand" title="Woodville, New Zealand">Woodville</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_Bay" title="Island Bay">Island Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riverton,_New_Zealand" title="Riverton, New Zealand">Riverton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Whitecliffs" title="Whitecliffs">Whitecliffs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bluff,_New_Zealand" title="Bluff, New Zealand">Bluff</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Local_Naming_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-Local_Naming-32">[32]</a></sup> A few derive their names from descriptions in languages other than English or Māori, for example: <a href="/wiki/Miramar,_New_Zealand" title="Miramar, New Zealand">Miramar</a>, which means "sea view" in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Inchbonnie" title="Inchbonnie">Inchbonnie</a>, which means "beautiful" (<i>bonnie</i> in Lallands) "island" (<i>innis</i> in Scottish Gaelic).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_patterns">Regional patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_place_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regional patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Scottish names occur in <a href="/wiki/Otago" title="Otago">Otago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southland,_New_Zealand" title="Southland, New Zealand">Southland</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Lammermoor_Range" title="Lammermoor Range">Lammerlaws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invercargill" title="Invercargill">Invercargill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wedderburn,_New_Zealand" title="Wedderburn, New Zealand">Wedderburn</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glenfalloch" title="Glenfalloch">Glenfalloch</a>) and Scottish settlers also named <a href="/wiki/Riccarton,_New_Zealand" title="Riccarton, New Zealand">Riccarton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Avon_River_(Canterbury)" title="Avon River (Canterbury)">Avon River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>. <a href="/wiki/Canterbury,_New_Zealand" title="Canterbury, New Zealand">Canterbury</a> has an English flavour to its nomenclature with Christchurch and <a href="/wiki/Oxford,_New_Zealand" title="Oxford, New Zealand">Oxford</a>, but also contains <a href="/wiki/Belfast,_New_Zealand" title="Belfast, New Zealand">Belfast</a> and French names throughout the <a href="/wiki/Akaroa" title="Akaroa">Akaroa</a> area. Scandinavian migrants left their mark in the <a href="/wiki/Seventy_Mile_Bush" title="Seventy Mile Bush">Seventy Mile Bush</a> with <a href="/wiki/Dannevirke" title="Dannevirke">Dannevirke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norsewood" title="Norsewood">Norsewood</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Local_Naming_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-Local_Naming-32">[32]</a></sup> and some of the suburbs and streets of Auckland and Wellington reflect Australian immigration (Footscray and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botany_Downs,_New_Zealand" title="Botany Downs, New Zealand">Botany Downs</a>). There is very little Asian influence in New Zealand place naming, with <a href="/wiki/Khandallah" title="Khandallah">Khandallah</a> in Wellington and <a href="/wiki/Cashmere,_New_Zealand" title="Cashmere, New Zealand">Cashmere</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kashmir" title="Kashmir">Kashmir</a>) in Christchurch, reflecting British imperial connections rather than Indian influence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unofficial_names">Unofficial names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Zealand_place_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unofficial names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of New Zealand's place names have never been officially approved by the NZGB. They either fall outside the board's jurisdiction (homesteads, lighthouses) or were common names before the board was established and have never been officially formalised. Unofficial recorded names are defined as "names that have appeared in at least two publicly available authoritative publications or databases".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Help_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Help-22">[22]</a></sup> Recorded names include major cities (Wellington, Auckland, Hamilton, Christchurch), mountains (<a href="/wiki/Mount_Tasman" title="Mount Tasman">Mount Tasman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Dampier" title="Mount Dampier">Mount Dampier</a>), islands (<a href="/wiki/Auckland_Islands" title="Auckland Islands">Auckland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Island" title="Great Barrier Island">Great Barrier Island</a>) and many other geographical features.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[n 1]</a></sup>
</p><p>New Zealand and various parts of it have acquired a range of colloquial names over the years. Unofficial late 19th century names for New Zealand included "Maoriland" and "<a href="/wiki/God%27s_Own_Country" title="God's Own Country">God's Own Country</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup> The former occurred widely in the <a href="/wiki/Labour_movement" title="Labour movement">labour movement</a> and an early labour newspaper was named the <i><a href="/wiki/Maoriland_Worker" title="Maoriland Worker">Maoriland Worker</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_New_Zealand" title="Prime Minister of New Zealand">Premier</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_John_Seddon" title="Richard John Seddon">Richard John Seddon</a> (in power 1893–1906) popularised "God's Own Country". Both names fell out of popularity in the 20th century, although "God's Own Country" (or "Godzone") still occasionally appears.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> Latinate names for the country have included "Zealandia" and "Nova Zealandia".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many cities and towns have nicknames based on a prominent feature or one which promoters wish to emphasise. Christchurch is promoted as the "Garden City"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[40]</a></sup> and Auckland is commonly referred to as the "city of sails".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> Various councils have come up with mottos to advertise their cities, with Hamilton going from "Where It's Happening" to "More Than You Expect" in 2000<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> and Dunedin using the "<a href="/wiki/I_am_Dunedin" title="I am Dunedin">I am Dunedin</a>" slogan from 2001 until 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> Hamilton acquired the nickname "the Tron" after "Hamiltron: City of the Future" was suggested for a city slogan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> Wellington is also known as the "windy city" due to its strong and unpredictable winds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg/220px-EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg/330px-EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg/440px-EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EN-Hobbiton-Matamata-NZ-Attribution-Share-Alike25.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Matamata" title="Matamata">Matamata</a>'s 'Welcome to Hobbiton' sign</div></div></div>
<p>Following the release of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(film_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (film series)"><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> films</a>, New Zealand has sometimes been called "Middle-earth" after the <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">fictional setting</a> of the films. <a href="/wiki/Wellington" title="Wellington">Wellington</a>, the centre of the films' production, was sometimes called Middle-earth, and for about a week around the release of the first movie the local newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Evening_Post_(New_Zealand)" title="The Evening Post (New Zealand)">The Evening Post</a></i> renamed itself to <i>The Middle Earth Post</i>. The town of <a href="/wiki/Matamata" title="Matamata">Matamata</a>, near the location of many of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hobbiton" title="Hobbiton">Hobbiton</a> scenes, unofficially renamed itself "Hobbiton".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> The flourishing of the film-industry in Wellington has led to the nickname "<a href="/wiki/Wellywood" title="Wellywood">Wellywood</a>", and a proposal to erect a "<a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>" style sign near the airport was overturned due to public resistance and possible copyright infringements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of towns have acquired ironic nicknames comparing them to the "more exciting" city of <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_Strip" title="Las Vegas Strip">Las Vegas</a>; "Rotovegas" for <a href="/wiki/Rotorua" title="Rotorua">Rotorua</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> "Ashvegas" for <a href="/wiki/Ashburton,_New_Zealand" title="Ashburton, New Zealand">Ashburton</a> and "Invervegas" for <a href="/wiki/Invercargill" title="Invercargill">Invercargill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHay,_Maclagan_and_Gordon81_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHay,_Maclagan_and_Gordon81-50">[49]</a></sup> Other areas have nicknames or popular designations based on abbreviations or mangling of the Māori name. New Zealanders will sometimes refer to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taranaki_Region" title="Taranaki Region">Taranaki Region</a> as "The Naki",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Palmerston_North" title="Palmerston North">Palmerston North</a> as "Palmy", Gisborne as "Gizzie", <a href="/wiki/Cardrona,_New_Zealand" title="Cardrona, New Zealand">Cardrona</a> as "Cardie",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paraparaumu" title="Paraparaumu">Paraparaumu</a> as "Paraparam", <a href="/wiki/Waimakariri_District" title="Waimakariri District">Waimakariri</a> as "Waimak" and <a href="/wiki/Paekakariki" title="Paekakariki">Paekakariki</a> as "Pie-cock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHay,_Maclagan_and_Gordon72_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHay,_Maclagan_and_Gordon72-53">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Waikikamukau" title="Waikikamukau">Waikikamukau</a> (/waɪkɪkɑːmuːkaʊ/, as if saying "Why kick a moo-cow") is a generic name for a small rural town or locality in New Zealand.
</p>
