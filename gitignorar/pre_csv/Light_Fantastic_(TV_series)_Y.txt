<p><i><b>Light Fantastic</b></i> is the title of a television <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Documentary_series" title="Documentary series">documentary series</a> that explores the phenomenon of light and aired in December 2004<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">BBC Four</a>. The series comprised four programmes respectively titled: "Let There Be Light"; "The Light of Reason"; "The Stuff of Light"; and "Light, the Universe and Everything." The material was presented by <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a> academic <a href="/wiki/Simon_Schaffer" title="Simon Schaffer">Simon Schaffer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC4_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC4-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Let_There_Be_Light">Let There Be Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Let There Be Light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first episode shows how the desire, by Greek, Arab and Christian scholars to penetrate the divine nature of light led to modern science's origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC4_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC4-3">[3]</a></sup> The programme explores the contributions of <a href="/wiki/Empedocles" title="Empedocles">Empedocles</a>;  <a href="/wiki/Euclid" title="Euclid">Euclid</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Hazen" title="Al Hazen">Al Hazen</a>; <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descartes" title="Descartes">Descartes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ep1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_scholars">Greek scholars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Greek scholars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nature of light, and how we see, was first <a href="/wiki/Light#Greek_and_Hellenistic_theories" title="Light">investigated by the early Greek philosophers</a>. Light seemed to fill all space while allowing a kind of penetration of the world thus offering a clue to the structure of the whole universe. The world was bathed in light but, to bring it within the world of reason, it was necessary to abstract and choose phenomena where light behaved in a special or strange way: Why do faraway objects appear smaller and why do objects change their position and shape when placed underwater?.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ep1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Empedocles#Perception_and_knowledge" title="Empedocles">Empedocles' idea</a>, that we see objects because <a href="/wiki/Emission_theory_(vision)" title="Emission theory (vision)">light streams out of our eyes</a> and touches them, became the fundamental basis on which mathematicians would construct some of the most important theories on light and vision. <a href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Optics" title="Euclid's Optics">Euclid's Optics</a> expanded this idea to make an important breakthrough: We know in our minds that a faraway building is bigger, yet it is possible to position a finger such that our <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">eye tells us they are of similar size</a>. Euclid's elegant solution was that the eye and both the tops of finger and building must lie on the same line – thus the rays from the eye must follow straight lines; the new discipline of geometry could thus make predictions and solve problems of light and optics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep1_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ep1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Al_Hazen">Al Hazen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Al Hazen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Hazen" title="Al Hazen">Al Hazen</a> earned a living selling his copies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euclid%27s_Geometry" title="Euclid's Geometry">Euclid's Geometry</a> before obtaining the patronage of <a href="/wiki/Al-Hakim_bi-Amr_Allah" title="Al-Hakim bi-Amr Allah">Al Hakim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate" title="Fatimid Caliphate">6th Fatimid Caliph</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">Cairo</a>. Al Hazen was unable to fulfill his task of stopping the <a href="/wiki/Flooding_of_the_Nile" title="Flooding of the Nile">flooding of the Nile</a> and was imprisoned. Here he noted a problem with Empedocles's theory: having been in darkness and then suddenly exposed to light, his eyes felt intense pain. It seemed improbable that, if rays were indeed emitted by the eye, this would happen; instead Al Hazen <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intromission_theory" title="Intromission theory">postulated that light rays travelled through space</a> in straight lines and entered our eyes by bouncing off objects. He studied <a href="/wiki/Refraction#Explanation" title="Refraction">refraction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)#Reflection_of_light" title="Reflection (physics)">symmetry of reflection</a>, producing a  <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">seven-volume work</a> which became the new standard text.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep1_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ep1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Christian_science">Christian science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Christian science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the centuries following Al Hazen's death, the Catholic Church determined to demonstrate its Divine authority and produce a "Christian" knowledge of light. The translation of the work of the Islamic scholars allowed <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">Roger Bacon</a>, in the 13thC, to study and <a href="/wiki/Opus_Majus" title="Opus Majus">develop Al Hazen's work</a> through experimentation with the distortion and colour effects of light through glass and water <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep1_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ep1-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Light_of_Reason">The Light of Reason</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Light of Reason">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The second episode explores the link between the development of practical tools that manipulate light and the emergence of new ideas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC4_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC4-3">[3]</a></sup> The subject is examined through the work of <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">Tycho Brahe</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">Galileo</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vermeer" title="Vermeer">Vermeer</a>; <a href="/wiki/Robert_Hooke" title="Robert Hooke">Robert Hooke</a>; <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ole_R%C3%B8mer" title="Ole Rømer">Ole Rømer</a>; <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ep2-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Brahe was granted the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_of_Hven" title="Island of Hven">island of Hven</a> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denmark%27s_Frederick_II" title="Denmark's Frederick II">Denmark's Frederick II</a>. From here he observed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comet_in_1577" title="Comet in 1577">comet in 1577</a>. Tycho's measurements proved it was further away than supposed thus challenging the Church's traditional view that God had created the Earth at the centre of the <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">Universe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ep2-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Stuff_of_Light">The Stuff of Light</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Stuff of Light">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The third episode charts the discovery of the true nature of light and the subsequent development of modern technology such as electricity and mobile phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC4_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC4-3">[3]</a></sup> The pioneers are credited as <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a>; <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Swan" title="Joseph Swan">Joseph Swan</a> <a href="/wiki/William_Armstrong,_1st_Baron_Armstrong" title="William Armstrong, 1st Baron Armstrong">William Armstrong</a>; <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>; <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">Wilhelm Röntgen</a>; <a href="/wiki/J._J._Thomson" title="J. J. Thomson">J. J. Thomson</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck" title="Max Planck">Max Planck</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ep3-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1847, as a sixteen-year-old, Maxwell was taken to see one of the minor scientific wonders of the Victorian world: A <a href="/wiki/Prism" title="Prism">prism</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicol_prism" title="Nicol prism">made from a special Icelandic crystal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep3_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ep3-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Light.2C_the_Universe_and_Everything"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Light,_the_Universe_and_Everything">Light, the Universe and Everything</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_Fantastic_(TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Light, the Universe and Everything">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The final episode explores the relationship between light, the eye and the mind and the development of technologies such as photography and cinema.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC4_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-BBC4-3">[3]</a></sup> The achievements of <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a>; <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Thompson" title="Benjamin Thompson">Benjamin Thompson</a>; <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist)" title="Thomas Young (scientist)">Thomas Young</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh" title="John Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a>; <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Wedgwood_(1771-1805)" title="Thomas Wedgwood (1771-1805)">Thomas Wedgwood</a>; <a href="/wiki/Eadweard_Muybridge" title="Eadweard Muybridge">Eadweard Muybridge</a>; <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne-Jules_Marey" title="Étienne-Jules Marey">Étienne-Jules Marey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> are discussed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep4_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ep4-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>From their knowledge of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour_blindness" title="Colour blindness">colour blindness</a>, some Victorian scientists believed they could prove the perceived cultural supremacy of the English by measuring differences of colour perception in different races. The idea was that animals were lower down the evolutionary scale but had better attuned senses than humans. If it could be proved that <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">black people</a> had better responses to light and colour this would be evidence of their inferiority. In 1898 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rivers" title="William Rivers">William Rivers</a>, together with a group of Cambridge academics, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Rivers#Torres_Straits_Expedition" title="William Rivers">set off for the Torres Straits</a> to prove exactly this. Rivers used a <a href="/wiki/Tintometer" title="Tintometer">tintometer</a> but found his original hypothesis was false and that the range of "colour difference perception" of the islanders was little different from that of the English. When Rivers returned to England he spearheaded dissemination of the fact that there was no scientific evidence to support <a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">white supremacy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep4_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ep4-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The programme continues and describes Priestley's discovery of <a href="/wiki/Photosynthesis" title="Photosynthesis">photosynthesis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ep4_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ep4-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
