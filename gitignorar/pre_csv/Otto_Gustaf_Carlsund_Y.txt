	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Otto Gustaf Carlsund</h1>

<p><b>Otto Gustaf Carlsund</b> (1897–1948) was a <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">avant-garde</a> artist and art critic, connected to <a href="/wiki/Cubism" title="Cubism">Cubism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purism_(arts)" title="Purism (arts)">Purism</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neo-Plasticism" title="Neo-Plasticism">Neo-Plasticism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Concrete_art" title="Concrete art">Concrete art</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Gustaf_Carlsund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1924 he moved to <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> to study under <a href="/wiki/Fernand_L%C3%A9ger" title="Fernand Léger">Fernand Léger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9d%C3%A9e_Ozenfant" title="Amédée Ozenfant">Amédée Ozenfant</a> at their newly founded Académie Moderne, where he developed a style of painting at first resembling Léger's, though soon became greatly influenced by Ozenfant's Purist style.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1925 he met <a href="/wiki/Le_Corbusier" title="Le Corbusier">Le Corbusier</a> and was commissioned to create murals for a cinema he was working on, though the project was eventually shelved, and in the same year he was recommended by Ozenfant to create murals for the planned library annex to <a href="/wiki/Erich_Mendelsohn" title="Erich Mendelsohn">Erich Mendelsohn's</a> <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Tower" title="Einstein Tower">Einstein Tower</a>. Although Mendelsohn eventually abandoned this later project, an example of the designs from the series of paintings that Carlsund had prepared for the Einstein Library - the suite <i>Rapidité</i> - was realized in a mural at the Stockholm restaurant Parkrestaurangen Lilla Paris, in connection with the <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Exhibition_(1930)" title="Stockholm Exhibition (1930)">Stockholm Exhibition</a> in 1930. In 1926 he was invited by <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Sophie_Dreier" title="Katherine Sophie Dreier">Katherine Dreier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_Anonyme_(art)" title="Société Anonyme (art)">Société Anonyme</a> to participate in her exhibition <i>International Exhibition of Modern Art</i> at the Brooklyn Museum in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> featuring both his mural projects, and over the following years he exhibits several times in Paris, also again in New York at <a href="/wiki/Jane_Heap" title="Jane Heap">Jane Heap's</a> Little Review Gallery (1928), the Arts Council (1928), and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rand_School" title="Rand School">Rand School</a> (1931). As he met <a href="/wiki/Piet_Mondrian" title="Piet Mondrian">Piet Mondrian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Theo_van_Doesburg" title="Theo van Doesburg">Theo van Doesburg</a> and the circle of radical artists working in the Neo-Plasticist aesthetic, he came to adopt an increasingly reduced vocabulary of abstract rectilinear form, as well as being more and more affected by <a href="/wiki/Constructivism_(art)" title="Constructivism (art)">Constructivism</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_concret">Art concret</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Gustaf_Carlsund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Art concret">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carlsund_Rapid_(1930).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2508" data-file-width="4000" decoding="async" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Carlsund_Rapid_%281930%29.jpg/220px-Carlsund_Rapid_%281930%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Carlsund_Rapid_%281930%29.jpg/330px-Carlsund_Rapid_%281930%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Carlsund_Rapid_%281930%29.jpg/440px-Carlsund_Rapid_%281930%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carlsund_Rapid_(1930).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mural "Rapid" at the restaurant <i>Parkrestaurangen Lilla Paris</i>, Stockholm, 1930.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1929 he and van Doesburg, <a href="/wiki/Jean_H%C3%A9lion" title="Jean Hélion">Jean Hélion</a>, <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Arthur_Tutundjian" title="Léon Arthur Tutundjian">Léon Arthur Tutundjian</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcel_Wantz" title="Marcel Wantz">Marcel Wantz</a> (1911-79) founded the group <a href="/wiki/Art_Concret" title="Art Concret">Art Concret</a>, and the following year they published their joint <a href="/wiki/Manifesto" title="Manifesto">manifesto</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> "Their proclamation called for a universal art composed of planes and colours executed crisply, precisely, mechanically. Form and rhythm were governed by mathematical principles, while abstract cinema provided a model of combining space and time in a single work of art"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The group was short lived and only exhibited together at three occasions in 1930 as part of larger group exhibitions, the first being at <i>Salon des Surindépendents</i> in Paris in June, followed by <i>Production Paris 1930</i> in Zürich, and in August at the exhibition <i>AC: Internationell utställning av postkubistisk konst</i> (International exhibition of post-cubist art) in Stockholm, curated by Carlsund himself. In the catalog to the latter, Carlsund states that the group's "programme is clear: absolute Purism. Neo-Plasticism, Purism and Constructivism combined".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Stockholm exhibition was largely a failure, getting harsh judgement from unsupportive critics, and ultimately led to Carlsund's decision to move back to Stockholm. Following van Doesburg's death in 1931, the Art Concret group united with the larger association Abstraction-Création, founded in 1932, though was never formally dissolved.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Otto_Gustaf_Carlsund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After 1932, Carlsund was primarily active as an art critic, still painting and creating murals but not exhibiting his art. Following increased domestic attention to geometric abstraction in the 1940s, a retrospective exhibition was held in Stockholm in 1947 to great acclaim.
</p>
