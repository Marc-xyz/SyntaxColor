<p>Major <b>Ronald Saint Clair McClintock</b> <span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Military_Cross" title="Military Cross">MC</a></span> (13 July 1898 – 22 June 1922) was a British <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> <a href="/wiki/Flying_ace" title="Flying ace">flying ace</a> credited with five aerial victories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theaerodrome_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-theaerodrome-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATT_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ATT-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_background">Family background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_McClintock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McClintock was born in County Carlow, Ireland, the fifth and youngest son of Arthur George Florence McClintock <span class="noexcerpt nowraplinks" style="font-size:85%;"><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace#United_Kingdom" title="Justice of the Peace">JP</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deputy_Lieutenant" title="Deputy Lieutenant">DL</a></span>, and first wife Susan Heywood-Collins. His grandfather was Lieutenant-Colonel George Augustus Jocelyn McClintock, of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/52nd_Regiment_of_Foot" title="52nd Regiment of Foot">52nd Regiment of Foot</a> and the Sligo Rifles, and his great-grandfather, <a href="/wiki/John_McClintock_(1770%E2%80%931855)" title="John McClintock (1770–1855)">John McClintock</a>, was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serjeant-at-Arms" title="Serjeant-at-Arms">Serjeant-at-Arms</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Commons" title="Irish House of Commons">Irish House of Commons</a> and <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Louth" title="High Sheriff of Louth">High Sheriff of Louth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turtlebunbury_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-turtlebunbury-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I_service">World War I service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_McClintock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War I service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McClintock first served in Egypt from November 1914, as a private in the <a href="/wiki/Ceylon_Planters%27_Rifle_Corps" title="Ceylon Planters' Rifle Corps">Ceylon Planters' Rifle Corps</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theaerodrome_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-theaerodrome-1">[1]</a></sup> however he was soon commissioned as a second lieutenant in the West Lancashire Brigade, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Field_Artillery" title="Royal Field Artillery">Royal Field Artillery</a> (<a href="/wiki/Territorial_Force" title="Territorial Force">Territorial Force</a>), and was promoted to temporary lieutenant on 5 July 1915.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was seconded for duty with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Flying_Corps" title="Royal Flying Corps">Royal Flying Corps</a> on 4 April 1916,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Air_observer" title="Air observer">flying officer (observer)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He first served as an observer/gunner in <a href="/wiki/No._2_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 2 Squadron RAF">No. 2 Squadron RFC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATT_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-ATT-2">[2]</a></sup> before training as a pilot, and being appointed a flying officer on 4 August.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> On 1 January 1917 he was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Flight_commander" title="Flight commander">flight commander</a> with the temporary rank of captain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> On 28 June his promotion to lieutenant in the Royal Field Artillery was made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substantive_rank" title="Substantive rank">substantive</a>, while remaining seconded to the RFC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July he was posted to <a href="/wiki/No._64_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 64 Squadron RAF">No. 64 Squadron RFC</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATT_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-ATT-2">[2]</a></sup> flying the <a href="/wiki/Airco_DH.5" title="Airco DH.5">Airco DH.5</a> fighter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The squadron moved to France in October, and took part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cambrai_(1917)" title="Battle of Cambrai (1917)">battle of Cambrai</a>, flying low-level ground attack missions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The squadron replaced their DH.5s with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Aircraft_Factory_S.E.5" title="Royal Aircraft Factory S.E.5">SE.5a</a> in March 1918.
</p><p>McClintock gained his first aerial victory on 10 March 1918, driving down an <a href="/wiki/LVG" title="LVG">LVG</a> C reconnaissance aircraft over <a href="/wiki/Marquion" title="Marquion">Marquion</a>. On the afternoon of 23 March he destroyed a <a href="/wiki/Pfalz_D.III" title="Pfalz D.III">Pfalz D.III</a> over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pronville" title="Pronville">Pronville</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Albatros_D.V" title="Albatros D.V">Albatros D.V</a> over <a href="/wiki/Biache-Saint-Vaast" title="Biache-Saint-Vaast">Biache</a>. The following day he destroyed another Type C over <a href="/wiki/Le_Transloy" title="Le Transloy">Le Transloy</a>, and finally on 2 April another D.V over <a href="/wiki/Fricourt" title="Fricourt">Fricourt</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theaerodrome_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-theaerodrome-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATT_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-ATT-2">[2]</a></sup> On 20 April he was appointed a temporary major,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> to serve as commander of <a href="/wiki/No._3_Squadron_RAF" title="No. 3 Squadron RAF">No. 3 Squadron RAF</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ATT_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-ATT-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>McClintock was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Military_Cross" title="Military Cross">Military Cross</a> which was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gazetted" title="Gazetted">gazetted</a> on 21 June 1918. His citation read:
</p>
<dl><dd>Lieutenant (Temporary Captain) Ronald Sinclair McClintock, RFA and RFC.
<dl><dd>"For conspicuous gallantry and devotion to duty. On one occasion he shot down two enemy machines, and on the following day he attacked and shot down a hostile two-seater machine at a height of 100 feet. He has led upwards of forty patrols and has performed much valuable work on low-flying reconnaissance and bombing patrols. As a flight commander he has been untiring in his care of personnel and machines, and as a patrol leader he has displayed the greatest courage and resource."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_career">Post-war career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_McClintock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post-war career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McClintock was granted a permanent commission in the RAF, with the rank of captain, on 1 August 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> From 1920 he served at the No. 3 School of Technical Training (Men) at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Manston" title="RAF Manston">RAF Manston</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> while living in <a href="/wiki/Birchington-on-Sea" title="Birchington-on-Sea">Birchington-on-Sea</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightKilled_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FlightKilled-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 22 June 1922 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flight_Lieutenant" title="Flight Lieutenant">Flight Lieutenant</a> McClintock was flying a <a href="/wiki/Sopwith_Snipe" title="Sopwith Snipe">Sopwith Snipe</a> at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Northolt" title="RAF Northolt">RAF Northolt</a>, practising for a relay race to be held at the RAF's Annual Aerial Pageant at <a href="/wiki/Hendon_Aerodrome" title="Hendon Aerodrome">Hendon Aerodrome</a>, when his aircraft crashed and he was killed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FlightKilled_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FlightKilled-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_McClintock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>McClintock married Mary Gordon Laird at the Church of the Holy Trinity, <a href="/wiki/Kensington_Gore" title="Kensington Gore">Kensington Gore</a>, London, on 20 December 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> They had two children: John Arthur Peter McClintock (1920–1940), who served as a flight lieutenant in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Auxiliary_Air_Force" title="Royal Auxiliary Air Force">Auxiliary Air Force</a>, and was killed in action during World War II, and Pamela Mary McClintock, who was less than four months old at the time of her father's death.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-turtlebunbury_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-turtlebunbury-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
