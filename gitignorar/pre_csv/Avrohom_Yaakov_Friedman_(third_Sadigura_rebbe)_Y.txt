	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Avrohom Yaakov Friedman (third Sadigura rebbe)</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

<p><b>Avrohom Yaakov Friedman</b>, in English also spelled <b>Abraham Jacob Friedman</b> (July 30, 1884<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried35_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-fried35-1">[1]</a></sup> – December 24, 1961<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried46_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-fried46-2">[2]</a></sup>) was the third <a href="/wiki/Rebbe" title="Rebbe">Rebbe</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Sadigura_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Sadigura (Hasidic dynasty)">Sadigura</a> <a href="/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism" title="Hasidic Judaism">Hasidic</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_Hasidic_dynasties" title="List of Hasidic dynasties">dynasty</a>. He was a prominent Jewish leader in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">interwar period</a> and in the nascent State of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, where he established his court in <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>. He was one of the first members of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agudat_Israel" title="Agudat Israel">Agudat Israel</a> and occupied a seat on the <a href="/wiki/Moetzes_Gedolei_HaTorah" title="Moetzes Gedolei HaTorah">Moetzes Gedolei HaTorah</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(third_Sadigura_rebbe)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="331" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg/300px-Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg/450px-Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sadigura_rebbe%27s_palace.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Palatial home of the Sadigura Rebbe in Sadigura</div></div></div>
<p>Avrohom Yaakov Friedman was the fifth child of Rabbi Yisrael Friedman of Sadigura (1853–1907).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He was the grandson of Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(first_Sadigura_rebbe)" title="Avrohom Yaakov Friedman (first Sadigura rebbe)">Avrohom Yaakov Friedman</a>, the first Sadigura Rebbe; and the great-grandson of Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israel_Friedman_of_Ruzhyn" title="Israel Friedman of Ruzhyn">Yisrael Friedman of Ruzhyn</a>, founder of the <a href="/wiki/Ruzhin_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Ruzhin (Hasidic dynasty)">Ruzhiner</a> Hasidic dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried20_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-fried20-4">[4]</a></sup> He was born within the year of mourning for his paternal grandfather and was named after him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried35_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-fried35-1">[1]</a></sup> He had four brothers: Rabbi Aharon of Sadigura (the <i>Kedushas Aharon</i>) (1877–1913), Rabbi Shlomo Yosef of Chernovitz, Rabbi Yitzhak of Rimanov, and Rabbi Shlomo Chaim (Reb Shlomenu) of Sadigura (1887–1972).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is said that his father loved him the most of all his sons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup> He assiduously spent many hours in <a href="/wiki/Torah_study" title="Torah study">Torah study</a>. He was engaged at the age of 11<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup> to Bluma Raizel, daughter of Rabbi Yitzhak Meir Heschel of <a href="/wiki/Kopychyntsi" title="Kopychyntsi">Kopychyntsi</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Apta_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Apta (Hasidic dynasty)">Kopshitzer</a> Rebbe; they married when he was 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sadigura_kloiz.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="343" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="172" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Sadigura_kloiz.jpg/300px-Sadigura_kloiz.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Sadigura_kloiz.jpg/450px-Sadigura_kloiz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Sadigura_kloiz.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sadigura_kloiz.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Sadigura <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sadhora" title="Sadhora">Sadhora</a></div></div></div>
<p>Upon his father's death in 1907, he and his brothers all became Rebbes, making their courts and conducting their <i><a href="/wiki/Tish_(Hasidic_celebration)" title="Tish (Hasidic celebration)">tishen</a></i> in different halls in the great Sadigura synagogue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman,_p._37_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman,_p._37-8">[8]</a></sup> Rabbi Yisrael's eldest son, the <i>Kedushas Aharon</i>, died six years later, in 1912,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup> and was succeeded by his 16-year-old son, Rabbi Mordechai Sholom Yosef Friedman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1914, with the outbreak of World War I, Rabbi Avrohom Yaakov fled to <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a> together with his younger brother, Rabbi Shlomo Chaim, and his orphaned nephew, Rabbi Mordechai Sholom Yosef. Rabbi Avrohom Yaakov established his court in Vienna and led the Sadigura Hasidim from that city for the next 24 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The relocation of the Sadigura Rebbes to Vienna effectively put an end to the once-flourishing Jewish community in Sadigura, which comprised more than 5,000 Jews before World War I. The remaining Jews of the town were decimated by the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Move_to_Israel">Move to Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(third_Sadigura_rebbe)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Move to Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the <i><a href="/wiki/Anschluss" title="Anschluss">Anschluss</a></i> of 1938, the Nazis entered Vienna and subjected leading rabbis to public humiliation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup> The Sadigura Rebbe was seized and forced to sweep the streets in front of laughing Germans. The Rebbe vowed that if he could escape Austria and get to the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>, he would gladly sweep the streets there. His Hasidim obtained a visa for him that year and for many months after he arrived in <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>, he swept the streets around his house early each morning. He stopped this practice only after one of his Hasidim noticed what he was doing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He established his court on Nachmani Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-marks-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thousands of Sadigura Hasidim were murdered in <a href="/wiki/The_Holocaust" title="The Holocaust">the Holocaust</a>, leaving the Rebbe with only a few dozen followers. Nonetheless, he continued to lead his Hasidim with dignity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried41_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-fried41-14">[14]</a></sup> He conducted his court in Tel Aviv for 22 years until his death in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamodia_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-hamodia-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rebbe was actively involved in Jewish communal life in the new state of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. He was one of the first members of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agudat_Israel" title="Agudat Israel">Agudat Israel</a> and occupied a seat on the <a href="/wiki/Moetzes_Gedolei_HaTorah" title="Moetzes Gedolei HaTorah">Moetzes Gedolei HaTorah</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried41_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-fried41-14">[14]</a></sup> He served as the "conscience" of Agudat Israel, steering the organization away from politics and toward its mission of religious advocacy. An epitome of honesty and truthfulness, he did not use polemics or protests to get his point across, but sincerity and straightforwardness. He always acceded to the majority view, even if it did not agree with his own.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamodia_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-hamodia-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">Personal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(third_Sadigura_rebbe)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Rebbe was considered a <a href="/wiki/Tzadik" title="Tzadik">tzadik</a> and great Jewish leader. He was known for his wisdom and clear-cut, decisive answers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman,_p._37_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Friedman,_p._37-8">[8]</a></sup> He was particular not to inconvenience others or accept favors from others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Like other Rebbes of the Ruzhiner dynasty, he was humble and modest on the outside, while on the inside he burned with love of God and fierce allegiance to <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitzvah" title="Mitzvah">mitzvahs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hamodia_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-hamodia-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rebbe and his wife were childless.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marks_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-marks-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years_and_legacy">Final years and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(third_Sadigura_rebbe)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Final years and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(II)_gravestone.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_%28II%29_gravestone.jpg/220px-Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_%28II%29_gravestone.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_%28II%29_gravestone.jpg/330px-Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_%28II%29_gravestone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_%28II%29_gravestone.jpg/440px-Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_%28II%29_gravestone.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(II)_gravestone.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The third Sadigura Rebbe's gravestone in the <a href="/wiki/Nahalat_Yitzhak_Cemetery" title="Nahalat Yitzhak Cemetery">Nahalat Yitzhak Cemetery</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giv%27atayim" title="Giv'atayim">Givatayim</a>, Israel.</div></div></div>
<p>The Rebbe's wife died three years before him. He died on December 24, 1961 (5 <a href="/wiki/Tevet" title="Tevet">Tevet</a> 5721) and was buried in the Ruzhiner section of the <a href="/wiki/Nahalat_Yitzhak_Cemetery" title="Nahalat Yitzhak Cemetery">Nahalat Yitzhak Cemetery</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giv%27atayim" title="Giv'atayim">Givatayim</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fried46_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-fried46-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>His <a href="/wiki/Torah_study#D'var_Torah" title="Torah study">divrei Torah</a> were collected in a volume under the name <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="he">אביר יעקב</span>‎ (<i>Avir Yaakov</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-7"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>His Hasidim asked his younger brother, Rabbi Shlomo Chaim, to succeed him, but the latter demurred. He did agree to sit in his brother's place at <i><a href="/wiki/Tish_(Hasidic_celebration)" title="Tish (Hasidic celebration)">tishen</a></i> held on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_holiday" title="Jewish holiday">Jewish holidays</a> and on the <a href="/wiki/Bereavement_in_Judaism#Yahrtzeit,_Nahala" title="Bereavement in Judaism">yahrtzeits</a> of his Ruzhiner and Sadigura ancestors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Meanwhile, the Sadigura dynasty continued through the Rebbe's nephew, Rabbi Mordechai Sholom Yosef Friedman (1897–1979), who led Sadigura Hasidim in Sadigura and <a href="/wiki/Przemy%C5%9Bl" title="Przemyśl">Przemyśl</a> before <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">emigrating</a> to Tel Aviv in 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tog_6-8"><a href="#cite_note-tog-6">[6]</a></sup> Upon his death in 1979, Rabbi Mordechai Sholom Yosef was succeeded by his son, Rabbi Avrohom Yaakov Friedman (1928–2013), who moved the Sadigura court from Tel Aviv to <a href="/wiki/Bnei_Brak" title="Bnei Brak">Bnei Brak</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haaretz_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-18">[18]</a></sup> He was succeeded by his only son, the present Sadigura Rebbe, Rabbi Tzvi Yisrael Moshe Friedman of Bnei Brak.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lineage_of_Sadigura_dynastic_leadership">Lineage of Sadigura dynastic leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(third_Sadigura_rebbe)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lineage of Sadigura dynastic leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israel_Friedman_of_Ruzhyn" title="Israel Friedman of Ruzhyn">Yisrael Friedman of Ruzhyn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruzhin_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Ruzhin (Hasidic dynasty)">Ruzhiner</a> <a href="/wiki/Rebbe" title="Rebbe">Rebbe</a> (1797–1850). Re-established his court in <a href="/wiki/Sadhora" title="Sadhora">Sadigura</a> in 1842.
<ul><li>Sholom Yosef Friedman (1813–1851), son of Yisrael Friedman of Ruzhyn. Rebbe from 1850 to 1851.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(first_Sadigura_rebbe)" title="Avrohom Yaakov Friedman (first Sadigura rebbe)">Avrohom Yaakov Friedman</a> (1820–1883), son of Yisrael Friedman of Ruzhyn. Rebbe from 1851 to 1883.
<ul><li>Yisrael Friedman of Sadigura (1852–1907), son of Avrohom Yaakov Friedman of Sadigura. Rebbe from 1883 to 1907.
<ul><li>Aharon of Sadigura (1877–1913), son of Yisrael Friedman of Sadigura. Rebbe from 1907 to 1913.
<ul><li><b>Avrohom Yaakov Friedman</b> (1884–1961), son of Yisrael Friedman of Sadigura. Rebbe from 1913 to 1961.
<ul><li>Mordechai Sholom Yosef Friedman (1897–1979), son of Aharon of Sadigura. Rebbe from 1961 to 1979.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Avrohom_Yaakov_Friedman_(fifth_Sadigura_rebbe)" title="Avrohom Yaakov Friedman (fifth Sadigura rebbe)">Avrohom Yaakov Friedman</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bnei_Brak" title="Bnei Brak">Bnei Brak</a> (1928–2013). Rebbe from 1979 to 2013.
<ul><li>Yisrael Moshe Friedman of Bnei Brak (b. 1955). Rebbe from 2013.</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
