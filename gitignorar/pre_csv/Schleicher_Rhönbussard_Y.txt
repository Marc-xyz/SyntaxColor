<p>The <b>Schleicher Rhönbussard</b>, otherwise known as the <b>DFS Rhönbussard</b> was intended as an intermediate <a href="/wiki/Glider_(sailplane)" title="Glider (sailplane)">glider</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Training_aircraft" title="Training aircraft">trainer</a> which could also fly competitively.  It was designed by <a href="/wiki/Hans_Jacobs" title="Hans Jacobs">Hans Jacobs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in the early 1930s.  More than 200 were built.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_and_development">Design and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schleicher_Rh%C3%B6nbussard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the early 1930s a large performance, size and cost gap had been opened between the kind of glider in which people learned to <a href="/wiki/Lift_(soaring)" title="Lift (soaring)">soar</a> and make cross country flights, like the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grunau_Baby" title="Grunau Baby">Grunau Baby</a>, and the best sailplanes like the <a href="/wiki/Schleicher_Rh%C3%B6nadler" title="Schleicher Rhönadler">Schleicher Rhönadler</a>.  In 1932 the glider manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Schleicher" title="Alexander Schleicher">Alexander Schleicher</a> went to <a href="/wiki/Hans_Jacobs" title="Hans Jacobs">Hans Jacobs</a>, then at the RRG (<a href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B6n-Rossitten_Gesellschaft" title="Rhön-Rossitten Gesellschaft">Rhön-Rossitten Gesellschaft</a>) on the <a href="/wiki/Wasserkuppe" title="Wasserkuppe">Wasserkuppe</a>, to seek a design for a glider more advanced than the Baby but smaller, cheaper and easier to fly than Jacobs' Rhönadler.  The result was the Rhönbussard (in English, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B6n" title="Rhön">Rhön</a> <a href="/wiki/Buzzard" title="Buzzard">Buzzard</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonsI_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SimonsI-1">[1]</a></sup> Since it was later produced at the Schleicher works, it is often attributed to them<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardy_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hardy-2">[2]</a></sup> but in 1933 the RRG was replaced by the state owned DFS (<a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Forschungsanstalt_f%C3%BCr_Segelflug" title="Deutsche Forschungsanstalt für Segelflug">Deutsche Forschungsanstalt für Segelflug</a>) at <a href="/wiki/Darmstadt" title="Darmstadt">Darmstadt</a>, to which Jacobs moved and continued to refine the Rhönbussard, hence the alternative name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zuerl_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zuerl-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rhönbussard is an all wood and <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_fabric_covering" title="Aircraft fabric covering">fabric</a> aircraft, with a span 1.43 m (4 ft 8 in) greater than the Grunau Baby 1, a <a href="/wiki/Wing_loading" title="Wing loading">wing loading</a> 50% higher and considerably more refined aerodynamically, with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantilever_wing" title="Cantilever wing">cantilever wing</a> and a smooth, oval section <a href="/wiki/Fuselage" title="Fuselage">fuselage</a>.  The wing, lacking <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dihedral_(aircraft)" title="Dihedral (aircraft)">dihedral</a>, has a parallel <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chord_(aircraft)" title="Chord (aircraft)">chord</a> centre section and straight tapered outer panels ending in semi-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical" title="Elliptical">elliptical</a> <a href="/wiki/Wing_tip" title="Wing tip">tips</a>.  It is built around a single <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spar_(aviation)" title="Spar (aviation)">spar</a> with <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a> covering forward to form a torsion resisting D-box.  Behind the spar the wing is fabric covered.  The whole span of the <a href="/wiki/Trailing_edge" title="Trailing edge">trailing edge</a> of the outer panels carries <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ailerons" title="Ailerons">ailerons</a>.  The earliest Rhönbussards had no lift losing or drag increasing surfaces but later examples followed the development of these at the DFS: first with spoilers deployed above the upper wing surface, then with DFS-type <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_brake_(aircraft)" title="Air brake (aircraft)">airbrakes</a> rotating out of both surfaces on a common span-wise axis at about  mid-chord and finally with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_ruler" title="Parallel ruler">parallel ruler</a> action <a href="/wiki/Schempp-Hirth" title="Schempp-Hirth">Schempp-Hirth</a> brakes mounted just behind the spar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonsI_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-SimonsI-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The wing is mounted on a low pedestal, faired aft into the main fuselage. This is slightly shorter than that of the Baby and for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_of_gravity" title="Centre of gravity">centre of gravity</a> reasons the Rhönbussard's open <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a> is under the <a href="/wiki/Leading_edge" title="Leading edge">leading edge</a> of the wing, restricting the pilot's upward and rear view.  The fuselage tapers to the rear and is ply covered all over apart from a metal nose cone, including the <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fin</a> and tail bumper.  The fin is narrow and straight edged but the fabric covered <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a>, extending down to the keel, is full and more rounded.  The fabric covered, straight tapered and square tipped horizontal tail is placed on top of the fuselage, with its trailing edge forward of the rudder hinge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonsI_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-SimonsI-1">[1]</a></sup> The Rhönbussard took off on a jettisonable two wheeled <a href="/wiki/Dolly_(trailer)" title="Dolly (trailer)">dolly</a>, leaving a long skid for landing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hardy_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hardy-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rhönbussard first flew in 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonsI_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-SimonsI-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schleicher_Rh%C3%B6nbussard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As intended the Rhönbussard proved popular, providing good performance at a moderate cost, and more than two hundred were built by Schleicher.  Sixteen of them competed at the 15th <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh%C3%B6n" title="Rhön">Rhön</a> International meeting in 1934, where they were only outnumbered by Grunau Babys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonsI_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-SimonsI-1">[1]</a></sup> Two years later at the same meeting Eugen Wagner managed a 325 km (202 mi) flight and many other flights of 200–300 km (124–186 mi) were made over the years. At competitions it regularly did well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brütting_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brütting-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>One Rhönbussard almost set a sailplane world altitude record in 1936.  Piloted by Hermann Seeler, it reached more than 5,000 m (16,400 ft), but he lost control in cloud and the aircraft broke up.  Seeler escaped by parachute but his sealed barometer was not so fortunate, leaving his feat unrecorded. Despite this failure, the Rhönbussard's structure was strong and the type was used by several pioneers of glider aerobatics in displays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SimonsI_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-SimonsI-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Three Rhönbussards remained on European civil aircraft registers in 2010, two in Germany and one in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>.  One of the German aircraft had at one time served with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> under civil registration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EuReg_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-EuReg-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications">Specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Schleicher_Rh%C3%B6nbussard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0;"><i>Data from</i> Die Beruhmtesten Segelflugzeuge<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brütting_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brütting-4">[4]</a></sup></p><p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span>
</p><ul><li><b>Crew:</b> One</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 5.80 m (19 ft 0 in)</li>
<li><b>Wingspan:</b> 14.30 m (46 ft 11 in)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 14.10 m<sup>2</sup> (151.8 sq ft)</li>
<li><b>Aspect ratio:</b> 14.50</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">Airfoil</a>:</b> Göttingen 535</li>
<li><b>Empty weight:</b> 150 kg (331 lb)</li>
<li><b>Gross weight:</b> 245 kg (540 lb)</li></ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Stall speed:</b> 50 km/h (31 mph, 27 kn)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/V_speeds#Vne" title="V speeds">Never exceed speed</a>:</b> 130 km/h (81 mph, 70 kn)</li>
<li><b>Maximum glide ratio:</b> 19.8:1</li>
<li><b>Rate of sink:</b> 0.75 m/s (148 ft/min)</li>
<li><b>Wing loading:</b> 17.4 kg/m<sup>2</sup> (3.6 lb/sq ft)</li></ul>
