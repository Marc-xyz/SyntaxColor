	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Department of Veterans Affairs Management Accountability Act of 2014</h1>

<p>The <b>Department of Veterans Affairs Management Accountability Act of 2014</b> (<a class="external text" href="https://www.congress.gov/bill/113th-congress/house-bill/4031" rel="nofollow">H.R. 4031</a>) is a bill that would give the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Secretary of Veterans Affairs">United States Secretary of Veterans Affairs</a> the authority to remove or demote any individual from the Senior Executive Service upon determining that such individual's performance warrants removal or demotion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031sum_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-4031sum-1">[1]</a></sup> The bill was written in response to a scandal indicating that some VA hospitals were keeping secret waiting lists for care, the length of which may have led to the deaths of some veterans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamGehrke17_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamGehrke17-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The bill was introduced into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> during the <a href="/wiki/113th_United_States_Congress" title="113th United States Congress">113th United States Congress</a>. It passed the House on May 21, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031allactions_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-4031allactions-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Veterans_Affairs_Management_Accountability_Act_of_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Health_Administration_scandal_of_2014" title="Veterans Health Administration scandal of 2014">Veterans Health Administration scandal of 2014</a></div>
<p>In May 2014, questions involving substandard timely care and false records covering up related timelines came to light, involving treatment of veterans in a number of VA hospitals. <a href="/wiki/Eric_Shinseki" title="Eric Shinseki">Eric Shinseki</a>, then <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="Secretary of Veterans Affairs">Secretary of Veterans Affairs</a>, resigned on May 30, 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Senior Veterans Affairs officials reportedly kept secret waiting lists in order to hide the high number of veterans that were forced to wait for months to receive important medical care.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup> As many as 450 senior officials may have been involved, including VA hospital managers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup> Some veterans died while they were still awaiting treatment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamGehrke17_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamGehrke17-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>A VA department handbook on administrative policies indicates that employees have 30 days to appeal any disciplinary action before it can be taken and have all disciplinary actions or reprimands removed from their records after two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup> The Department has to meet high standards for doing things like separately proving an employee was absent each and every day if the employee quits showing up to work and proving that there is a direct relationship between the misconduct and their own efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Data provided by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Office_of_Personnel_Management" title="Office of Personnel Management">Office of Personnel Management</a> shows that only 7,349 federal employees were fired in 2013 out of a total of almost 2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-4"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisions_of_the_bill">Provisions of the bill</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Veterans_Affairs_Management_Accountability_Act_of_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Provisions of the bill">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>This summary is based largely on the summary provided by the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Research_Service" title="Congressional Research Service">Congressional Research Service</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> source.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031sum_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-4031sum-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Department of Veterans Affairs Management Accountability Act of 2014 would authorize the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Secretary of Veterans Affairs">United States Secretary of Veterans Affairs</a> (VA) to: (1) remove any individual from the Senior Executive Service upon determining that such individual's performance warrants removal, and (2) remove such individual from federal service or transfer the individual to a General Schedule position at any grade that the Secretary deems appropriate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031sum_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-4031sum-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The bill would require: (1) the Secretary to notify the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Veterans%27_Affairs" title="United States House Committee on Veterans' Affairs">United States House Committee on Veterans' Affairs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Veterans%27_Affairs" title="United States Senate Committee on Veterans' Affairs">United States Senate Committee on Veterans' Affairs</a> within 30 days after removing such an individual, and (2) such removal to be done in the same manner as the removal of a professional staff member employed by a Member of Congress.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031sum_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-4031sum-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedural_history">Procedural history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Veterans_Affairs_Management_Accountability_Act_of_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Procedural history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Department of Veterans Affairs Management Accountability Act of 2014 was introduced into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> on February 11, 2014 by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Miller_(Florida_politician)" title="Jeff Miller (Florida politician)">Rep. Jeff Miller (R, FL-1)</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031allactions_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-4031allactions-3">[3]</a></sup> The bill was referred to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Veterans%27_Affairs" title="United States House Committee on Veterans' Affairs">United States House Committee on Veterans' Affairs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Veterans%27_Affairs_Subcommittee_on_Economic_Opportunity" title="United States House Veterans' Affairs Subcommittee on Economic Opportunity">United States House Veterans' Affairs Subcommittee on Economic Opportunity</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Oversight_and_Government_Reform" title="United States House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform">United States House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031allactions_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-4031allactions-3">[3]</a></sup> The House voted in <a class="external text" href="http://clerk.house.gov/evs/2014/roll229.xml" rel="nofollow">Roll Call Vote 229</a> on May 21, 2014 to pass the bill 390-33.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4031allactions_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-4031allactions-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Debate_and_discussion">Debate and discussion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Veterans_Affairs_Management_Accountability_Act_of_2014&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Debate and discussion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Department of Veterans' Affairs released a statement opposing the bill, arguing that the bill "would generate serious unintended consequences that would prove counterproductive."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVAstatementMay5_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVAstatementMay5-7">[7]</a></sup> One alleged unintended consequence might be "any change that would single out VA employees for punishment or discharge could have a chilling effect on VA's ability to recruit and retain high-quality employees."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVAstatementMay5_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-DVAstatementMay5-7">[7]</a></sup> Their statement also indicated that they feared anyone fired could sue, leading to "lengthy litigation."
</p><p>The House members who sponsored the bill argue that, although federal workers can be fired, the process is extremely lengthy, sometimes taking years, and that the officials who are "under scrutiny for neglecting veterans actually received tens of thousands of dollars in bonuses and positive performance reviews."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-5"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup> Rep. <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Miller_(Florida_politician)" title="Jeff Miller (Florida politician)">Jeff Miller</a>, who sponsored the bill, said that "this bill would simply give the VA Secretary the authority to fire or demote VA Senior Executive Service employees based on performance, similar to the authority the Secretary of Defense already has to remove military general officers from command or how I am able to fire someone who works for me on my staff."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashExamMay21_6-6"><a href="#cite_note-WashExamMay21-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The organization <a href="/wiki/AMVETS" title="AMVETS">AMVETS</a> supported the legislation. National Commander John H. Miller Jr. said that "under the current, antiquated and morbidly dysfunctional civil service system, it's nearly impossible to dismiss or do more than slap the wrists of incompetent, ineffective and wasteful Senior Executive Service employees."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMVETSmiller_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-AMVETSmiller-8">[8]</a></sup> John Miller argued that the Secretary of Veterans Affairs needed "the ability to remove deadweight executives."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMVETSmiller_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-AMVETSmiller-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
