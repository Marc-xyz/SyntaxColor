<p><b>Catherine Hickson</b> (born 1955) is a Canadian volcanologist, formerly with the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Survey_of_Canada" title="Geological Survey of Canada">Geological Survey of Canada</a>, part of <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>. Since 2014, she has been the Chief Operating Officer for Dajin Resources Corp. and President, Tuya Terra Geo Corp. Hickson studied at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a> and received her PhD in Geology and <a href="/wiki/Volcanology" title="Volcanology">Volcanology</a> in 1987.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_and_Research">Career and Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Hickson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career and Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_Early_Career">Education and Early Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Hickson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and Early Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hickson grew up in <a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> During her childhood, family trips to <a href="/wiki/Banff_National_Park" title="Banff National Park">Banff National Park</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Yellowstone_National_Park" title="Yellowstone National Park">Yellowstone National Park</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> exposed her to the natural world, including to geysers and volcanoes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> As a student in 1980, Hickson was camping 14 km east of <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">Mount St. Helens when the volcano erupted</a>; she credited this event with changing the course of her career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> She completed her undergraduate degree in 1982 and her PhD in 1987, both at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">University of British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> She undertook PhD related field work in <a href="/wiki/Wells_Gray_Provincial_Park" title="Wells Gray Provincial Park">Wells Gray Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Hickson's early career focused on assessing past and future eruptions and hazard impacts of young <a href="/wiki/Volcanology_of_Canada" title="Volcanology of Canada">volcanoes in Canada</a> and globally.
</p><p>Hickson spent 25 years with <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Canadian federal government</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Survey_of_Canada" title="Geological Survey of Canada">Geological Survey of Canada</a>, part of the Earth Sciences Sector of <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>. In 1998, she worked with the Province of British Columbia on Thunderbird III, a civil protection exercise focused on a hypothetical eruption of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Baker" title="Mount Baker">Mount Baker, Washington state, USA</a>. 
</p><p>In 2003, Hickson was one of three scientists to report on a hypothetical eruption at the Canadian volcano, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Cayley" title="Mount Cayley">Mount Cayley</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  She also worked closely with the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">International Civil Aviation Organization</a> creating pilot and airspace regulations for flight in regions impacted by volcanic ash. Furthermore, she formulated and implemented Canada's volcanic eruption response plan (Canada's <a href="/wiki/Interagency_Volcanic_Event_Notification_Plan" title="Interagency Volcanic Event Notification Plan">Interagency Volcanic Event Notification Plan</a>, IVENP) in the event of a volcanic event within Canada or in a neighbouring jurisdiction.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Hickson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Catherine moved to the private sector and joined <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magma_Energy_Corp" title="Magma Energy Corp">Magma Energy Corp</a>., a global energy company in 2008. In 2010, she became the VP Exploration &amp; Chief Geologist at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magma_Energy_Corp" title="Magma Energy Corp">Magma Energy Corp</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Working at Magma, and subsequently Alterra, she expanded her geothermal expertise to Chile, Iceland, Italy, Peru and the USA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> After leaving Alterra in 2008, her work continues to involve harnessing electricity and heat from the hot water and steam released by volcanic activity and earth's internal heat engine.
</p><p>In 2014, Catherine founded her own company, Tuya Terra Geo Corp., a geological and management services company specializing in supporting geothermal development, <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a> exploration and public engagement in the earth sciences.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Combining her management and volcanological expertise as well as her global experience, she became a Director and Chief Operating Officer of Dajin Resources Corp., an energy metals company surveys lithium brines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Lithium brines are found in geological environments that host frequent volcanic activity and geothermal systems.
</p><p>In February 2018, she and several other researchers resurrected the Canadian Geothermal Association, as <a class="external text" href="http://www.geothermalcanada.org" rel="nofollow">Geothermal Canada</a>. The Association was founded in 1974 and has been inactive for several decades.  With increasing interest in geothermal development in Canada, the group felt the time was ripe to reestablish the organization to assist Canadian researchers and developers pushing geothermal project to fruition.  The organization is officially called the "Pan Canadian Society for Geothermal Research, Innovation &amp; Collaboration" and is registered as a not-for-profit society under the <i>Societies Act</i> of British Columbia.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Work">International Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Hickson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: International Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hickson's work has taken her to many parts of the world, including a significant amount of time in South America where some of the largest and highest volcanoes form the mountainous spine of South America. She was the creator and leader of the Multinational Andean Project (MAP), Projecto Multinacional Andeno (PMA), and Geoscience for Sustainability. This Project ran from 1996 - 2002, in partnership with Argentina, Bolivia, Chile, and Peru and was co-funded by the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_International_Development_Agency" title="Canadian International Development Agency">Canadian International Development Agency</a> and the participating countries. The project focus was creating high quality geoscience data for resource development. A second project, Geoscience for Andean Communities (MAP: GAC), ran from 2002 – 2006, and focused on natural hazard mitigation (volcanoes, earthquakes and landslides) to create safer communities. It was expanded from the initial project to include 3 more participating countries: Ecuador, Colombia, and Venezuela. This work garnered Hickson the prestigious C.J. Westerman Memorial Award (2010) presented by the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Professional_Engineers_and_Geoscientists_of_British_Columbia" title="Association of Professional Engineers and Geoscientists of British Columbia">Association of Professional Engineers and Geoscientists of BC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Other awards include the Head of the Public Service Award (1999), given by the Clerk of the Privy Council, and an Honorary Doctorate of Technology from the University of the Fraser Valley in June 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_contributions">Other contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Hickson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to her scientific contributions, she has made contributions to many publications for young people and the public, as well as being a sought-after lecturer, field trip leader and supporter of science for the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> She was an eyewitness to the <a href="/wiki/1980_eruption_of_Mount_St._Helens" title="1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens">1980 eruption of Mount St. Helens</a> and wrote a book in 2005 describing her experience and explaining the mechanism of the eruption for the public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Hickson is one of a few female leaders in earth science and is an adjunct professor at The University of British Columbia where she mentors women and young managers and sits on thesis committees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="888" data-file-width="1337" decoding="async" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png/220px-Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png/330px-Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png/440px-Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Taking_Geology_Notes_of_the_Cave.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Catherine Hickson at the <a href="/wiki/Wells_Gray_Park_Cave_discovery" title="Wells Gray Park Cave discovery">Wells Gray Park Cave discovery</a>, 2018</div></div></div>
<p>As noted above, Hickson has been very involved in increasing the understanding of the public in the natural world. One of her key focuses has been <a href="/wiki/Wells_Gray_Provincial_Park" title="Wells Gray Provincial Park">Wells Gray Provincial Park</a>. The park formed part of her PhD field work area in the early 1980s and she has been associated with it ever since. Leading field trips and lecturing about the geological heritage of the park, she brought the park to national and international attention through a bid to have it recognized as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_site" title="World Heritage site">UNESCO World Heritage site</a>.  With colleague and Lichen specialist, Trevor Goward, they presented a <a class="external text" href="http://www.wellsgrayworldheritage.ca/" rel="nofollow">comprehensive application</a> for its addition to Canada's tentative list of World Heritage sites.  Although unsuccessful, they will continue to support the park with its rich geology and abundance of flora and fauna as well as critical habitat for the endangered Mountain Caribou. She is president of the Wells Gray Wilderness Society. This Society was established to increase understanding of the park through field studies, education seminars, public lectures and workshops. It is partnered with <a href="/wiki/Thompson_Rivers_University" title="Thompson Rivers University">Thompson Rivers University</a> who have a field camp in the park. She is also a board member of the "<a class="external text" href="http://www.protectwellsgray.ca/about.html" rel="nofollow">Wells Gray Gateway Protection Society</a>". In 2018, Hickson led the first expedition to explore <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sarlacc%27s_Pit_cave" title="Sarlacc's Pit cave">Sarlacc's Pit cave</a>, a newly discovered <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wells_Gray_Park_Cave_Discovery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wells Gray Park Cave Discovery (page does not exist)">karst cave in the Park</a> which is the largest cave of its type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbc_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbc-22">[22]</a></sup> Catherine was contacted about the unusual feature due to her long-standing association with Wells Gray Park – first as a geology student who did her PhD on the geological young volcanoes found mostly in the southern part of the park, and later on as a scientist who continued to work in the park. She co-authored a book on Wells Gray Park – “Nature Wells Gray” with Trevor Goward. In addition, she wrote “Wells Gray Rocks” a companion book to Trevor's “Treasure Wells Gray” in celebration of the Parks’ 75th anniversary. These books for the public are in addition to technical field guides and scientific papers on the geology of the park. In 2017 Trevor and Catherine submitted an application for Wells Gray Park to be added to Canada's tentative list for UNESCO world heritage status. The application was part of a process to add sites to Canada's tentative list in celebration of Canada’ 150th birthday celebrations in 2017. Unfortunately Wells Gray was not among the eight sites named. Hopefully, this latest discovery will provide new impetus for Wells Gray Park to achieve UNESCO world heritage status in the future. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wells_Grey_Cave_discovery">Wells Grey Cave discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Catherine_Hickson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wells Grey Cave discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Catherine was instrumental in recognizing the potential significance of a <a href="/wiki/Wells_Gray_Park_Cave_discovery" title="Wells Gray Park Cave discovery">newly discovered cave in Wells Gray Park</a> first reported to her in early May 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> She realized that it was unlikely to be a volcanic feature (crater or lava tube), but most likely to be a karst feature, despite it not occurring in a thick limestone sequence. Catherine quickly reached out to John Pollack, an expert on caves, and an archaeological surveyor. Catherine and John Pollack are longtime associates through the Royal Canadian Geographical Society and the Explorers Club. She knew that John would either know himself what the feature was, or know who to ask about the feature. Catherine also reached out to BC Parks and her local Clearwater contacts to organize the reconnaissance work. John and Catherine co-wrote the permit application (for exploration) and John sought additional support from the caving community – he brought in Lee Hollis and Chas Yonge. At the cave site exploration in September 2018, Catherine evaluated the geology and geomorphology of the site in an effort to understand more about the cave's formation and its age. She will continue to work alongside John and Lee to support the actual exploration of the cave in the near future.
</p>
