<p><b>Vadstena Castle</b> (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">Vadstena slott</i>) is a former Royal Castle in <a href="/wiki/Vadstena" title="Vadstena">Vadstena</a>, the province of <a href="/wiki/%C3%96sterg%C3%B6tland" title="Östergötland">Östergötland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vadstena_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vadstena Castle was originally built by <a href="/wiki/Gustav_I_of_Sweden" title="Gustav I of Sweden">King Gustav I</a> in 1545 as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fortress" title="Fortress">fortress</a> to protect <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a> from enemies approaching from the south. The fortress consisted of three smaller stone buildings facing <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4ttern" title="Vättern">Lake Vättern</a>, three 31-meter wide <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">ramparts</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Courtyard" title="Courtyard">courtyard</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a> and four circular cannon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turrets" title="Turrets">turrets</a>. The original ramparts were torn down in the 19th century and the present ramparts were inaugurated in 1999. The stone buildings later formed the ground floor of the castle.
</p><p>On August 22, 1552, King Gustav I married his third wife, <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Stenbock" title="Catherine Stenbock">Catherine Stenbock</a>, in Vadstena. One of the castle banqueting halls is called The Wedding Hall (<a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>: <i lang="sv">Bröllopssalen</i>), although its construction wasn't finished in time for the wedding.
</p><p>The reconstruction from fortress into a habitable <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> began in the 1550s, when <a href="/wiki/Magnus,_Duke_of_%C3%96sterg%C3%B6tland" title="Magnus, Duke of Östergötland">prince Magnus</a> became <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Duke</a> of Östergötland. Duke Magnus suffered from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_illness" title="Mental illness">mental illness</a> and was the only son of Gustav I who didn't eventually become king of Sweden. Magnus died in 1595 and is buried in the church of nearby <a href="/wiki/Vadstena_Abbey" title="Vadstena Abbey">Vadstena Abbey</a>.
</p><p>By 1620, when the castle was completed,  all the kings of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Vasa" title="House of Vasa">House of Vasa</a> had contributed to its construction. Since 1620, the castle has been very well preserved, and is one of Sweden's best examples of <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance architecture</a>.
</p><p>Vadstena Castle was a royal palace until 1716, when the royal family lost interest in it; after which it became a storage barn for grain.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vadstena_Castle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1899, the castle has housed the Provincial Archives and today visitors can also find a Castle Museum with 16th and 17th century furniture, portraits and paintings. The castle is also the seat of the International Vadstena Academy, Sweden's smallest opera house, commissioning new operas and reviving lost operas from archival scores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> During summers the courtyard plays host to many concerts; both classical and pop music.
</p>
