	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Geauga Lake</h1>

<p><b>Geauga Lake</b> was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">theme park</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bainbridge_Township,_Geauga_County,_Ohio" title="Bainbridge Township, Geauga County, Ohio">Bainbridge Township</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Ohio" title="Aurora, Ohio">Aurora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, United States. Established in 1887 in what had been a local recreation area adjacent to a <a href="/wiki/Geauga_Lake_(lake)" title="Geauga Lake (lake)">lake of the same name</a>, the first amusement ride was added in 1889, and the park's first roller coaster –  later known as the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper_(Geauga_Lake)" title="Big Dipper (Geauga Lake)">Big Dipper</a> –  was built in 1925. In 1969, the park was sold to <a href="/wiki/Funtime,_Inc." title="Funtime, Inc.">Funtime, Inc.</a>, and was expanded over the years with additional rides and amenities. Further expansion occurred in the mid and late 1990s after Funtime's acquisition by <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags#History_of_Premier_Parks" title="Six Flags">Premier Parks</a> in 1995. Prior to the 2000 season, soon after Premier Parks acquired <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags" title="Six Flags">Six Flags</a>, the park was re-branded as <b>Six Flags Ohio</b> and four new roller coasters were added. A year later, Six Flags bought the adjacent <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Ohio" title="SeaWorld Ohio">SeaWorld Ohio</a> and combined the two parks under the name <b>Six Flags Worlds of Adventure</b>.
</p><p>The park changed ownership again in 2004 after a purchase by <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Fair" title="Cedar Fair">Cedar Fair</a>. The park's SeaWorld portion was transformed into a water park in 2005, and together they became known as <b>Geauga Lake and Wildwater Kingdom</b>. On September 21, 2007, Cedar Fair announced the closing of the amusement park in 2008, and that the property would operate solely as a water park under the title <a href="/wiki/Wildwater_Kingdom_(Ohio)" title="Wildwater Kingdom (Ohio)">Wildwater Kingdom</a>. Cedar Fair announced Wildwater Kingdom would not reopen after the 2016 season.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-amusement_park_era">Pre-amusement park era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre-amusement park era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geauga Lake was originally known as "Picnic Lake" or "Giles Pond."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Geauga Lake area was home to early <a href="/wiki/Settler" title="Settler">settlers</a> such as the Staffords, Mark Patterson, Capt. Simon Henry with his wife Rhoda Parsons and their children, Charles Swires, the Brewsters, and Bohan Blair. There is a city park and ballfields on East Boulevard in Aurora, named after this lake. Sullivan Giles chose this area for his log cabin in 1817. He later built a large frame home on the spot behind Geauga Lake depot on the north side of the lake. When the railroad came to town in 1856, it made a stop at "pond station". Giles took advantage of his scenic lake location and, in the last half of the 19th century, established picnic grounds, a dance hall, and other entertainment near his home for the all-day pleasure of residents and those taking the train to the country.
</p><p>Geauga Lake opened for picnics and swimming in 1872. An 1880 history of Geauga County reported the Giles residence "being easy of access by rail, has become, within a few years, a very popular place of resort during the summer months, for fishing, picnic, and excursion parties" and "for the convenience of such parties, Mr. Giles has recently erected a hall of considerable size near the lake. The surrounding grounds are kept clean and attractive, and, without exception, this is the most charming place to spend a leisure day to be found in this section."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At the time, a full-sized <a href="/wiki/Steamboat" title="Steamboat">steamboat</a> circled the lake, towing a large <a href="/wiki/Scow" title="Scow">scow</a>, topped with a dance floor. The boat, first owned by William Banford and Rowe Fuller, was later purchased by the Kents. In 1907, the boat was shipped by rail to <a href="/wiki/Brady_Lake_(Ohio)" title="Brady Lake (Ohio)">Brady Lake</a> near <a href="/wiki/Kent,_Ohio" title="Kent, Ohio">Kent</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="1887.E2.80.931969:_Geauga_Lake_amusement_park"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1887–1969:_Geauga_Lake_amusement_park">1887–1969: Geauga Lake amusement park</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1887–1969: Geauga Lake amusement park">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geauga Lake park was established in 1887. Three major league baseball games were played on Sundays at Geauga Lake in 1888 (plus a Thursday exhibition game) by the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Spiders" title="Cleveland Spiders">Cleveland Forest Citys</a> of the major league <a href="/wiki/American_Association_(19th_century)" title="American Association (19th century)">American Association</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> By 1889, the park installed its first ride, a steam-powered carousel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> More rides would follow.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2112" data-file-width="2816" decoding="async" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG/300px-Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG/450px-Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG/600px-Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Big_Dipper_Geauga_Lake.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Big Dipper from across the lake.</div></div></div>
<p>William J. Kuhlman expanded the park in 1925. At that time, Geauga Lake built the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dipper_(Geauga_Lake)" title="Big Dipper (Geauga Lake)">Big Dipper</a>, the largest wooden roller coaster of its time, 2,800 feet (850 m) long and 65 feet (20 m) high. Geauga Lake's Olympic-sized swimming pool was built, and it stayed in operation until the mid-1960s. On Sunday, July 11, 1926, Olympic medalist and Tarzan actor <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Weissmuller" title="Johnny Weissmuller">Johnny Weissmuller</a> set a new world record in the 220-yard free style swim in the pool in front of 3,000 spectators.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Lake swimming also continued over the coming decades. Many amusement parks at the time had race tracks, dance halls, and sometimes a theater and bowling alley, making them year round attractions. The race track was added in 1931, although it closed in 1969. The theater, dance hall, and bowling alley were also added around the same time. In 1937, the park's 1926 hand-carved Marcus Illions Carousel was added, after having been located in Philadelphia and Birmingham, at a cost of $35,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>At that point, the park's dance hall and ballroom were major draws, with big band music performed by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Lombardo" title="Guy Lombardo">Guy Lombardo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fred_Waring" title="Fred Waring">Fred Waring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artie_Shaw" title="Artie Shaw">Artie Shaw</a>, and other big names of the time.
</p><p>In 1942, a tornado hit the park, injuring six, destroying buildings, and damaging the Big Dipper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The park reported $50,000 in damages, but it quickly rebuilt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In July 1944, Viola Schryer ("Vi") took over management of the park after the death of her uncle William Kuhlman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1952, a fire destroyed the park's bowling alley, theater, dance hall and roller rink with damages estimated at $500,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> At that time the park became strictly a seasonal amusement park, beach, and swimming area. The pool was closed and razed in the early 1960s, but lake swimming continued.
</p>
<h3><span id="1969.E2.80.932000:_Geauga_Lake_amusement_park_.28Funtime_era.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1969–2000:_Geauga_Lake_amusement_park_(Funtime_era)">1969–2000: Geauga Lake amusement park (Funtime era)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1969–2000: Geauga Lake amusement park (Funtime era)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Funtime,_Inc." title="Funtime, Inc.">Funtime Incorporated</a> purchased the park. Funtime was formed to purchase Geauga Lake by former managers of <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Point" title="Cedar Point">Cedar Point</a>, Earl Gascoigne, Gaspar Lococo, and M.P. Jacobson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The focus continued to be rides and swimming. The racetrack closed and was razed in 1969. In 1970 a marine life park, <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Ohio" title="SeaWorld Ohio">SeaWorld Ohio</a>, was built across the lake from the amusement park after Funtime persuaded SeaWorld to build the marine park on the other side of the lake. SeaWorld and Geauga Lake were friendly neighbors for 30 years working together to become a regional destination. SeaWorld focused on marine life and shows, while Geauga Lake focused on thrill rides and swimming. SeaWorld was purchased by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harcourt_Brace_Jovanovich" title="Harcourt Brace Jovanovich">Harcourt Brace Jovanovich</a> in 1976 and later by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busch_Entertainment_Corp" title="Busch Entertainment Corp">Busch Entertainment Corp</a> in late 1989.
</p><p>In 1972, the Gold Rush log flume water ride was added, and two years later Geauga Lake added the Skyscraper, which took passengers up 21 stories for views of the park. Admission to the park was free until 1972. Until then, rides on various attractions were purchased on a pay-as-you-go basis. Beginning in 1973, the park converted to an admission charge with a pay-one-price for all the rides and attractions. The Geauga Dog became the park's mascot and would remain so until 1999. In 1976, the park added the Wildcat compact steel roller coaster, and a year later the park added the <a href="/wiki/Double_Loop_(Geauga_Lake)" title="Double Loop (Geauga Lake)">Double Loop</a>, a looping steel coaster.  For a time, the park ran a short-lived series of TV commercials featuring Geauga Dog and a singing, dancing adolescent boy performing a song about the park. The boy's off-key singing and awful dancing were deliberate, a means of getting viewers to notice the ad. It succeeded.
</p><p>Corkscrew coaster made its debut in 1978, making Geauga Lake the first amusement park in Ohio and one of the first amusement parks anywhere to have two looping coasters. Swimming in the lake continued to be a feature at the park, and in 1983, the park added Boardwalk Shores, which featured a paddleboat marina, a new bath house, a children's swimming pool area and water slides. A year later, The Wave, the only authentic tsunami wave pool in the Midwest at the time, opened to rave reviews.
</p><p>In 1985, Harcourt Brace Jovanovich, owner of SeaWorld, announced their intent to purchase Funtime and combine the two parks, but the deal fell through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1986, more children's rides were added and themed as Rainbow Island, a children's dry ride area. Stingray water slides and the Euroracer Grand Prix rides were added.
</p><p>In 1988, Geauga Lake celebrated its centennial by introducing the <a href="/wiki/Raging_Wolf_Bobs" title="Raging Wolf Bobs">Raging Wolf Bobs</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Wooden_roller_coaster" title="Wooden roller coaster">wooden roller coaster</a> with a hybrid twister/out and back design modeled after the original <i>Bobs</i> roller coaster at Chicago's defunct <a href="/wiki/Riverview_Park_(Chicago)" title="Riverview Park (Chicago)">Riverview Park</a>. Two years later, the park re-themed the children's water area as Turtle Beach, which was advertised as the ultimate children's water playground. Geauga Lake expanded its midway with The Mirage and the $2.1 million Texas Twister in the early 1990s.
</p><p>A corporate deal in 1995 saw Premier Parks acquiring Funtime, giving Geauga Lake a new owner. Premier Parks invested $9 million in new rides, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_Spin_(roller_coaster)" title="Head Spin (roller coaster)">Mind Eraser</a>, a steel looping shuttle coaster designed by <a href="/wiki/Vekoma" title="Vekoma">Vekoma</a>, and Grizzly Run, a water rapids ride designed by <a href="/wiki/Intamin" title="Intamin">Intamin</a>. These attractions opened in 1996, and the Corkscrew was closed and sold and moved to <a href="/wiki/MGM_Dizzee_World" title="MGM Dizzee World">Dizzee World</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. The next year, the park expanded its water area by 32,000 square feet (3,000 m<sup>2</sup>) with Hook's Lagoon. Several new water slides were also added.
</p><p>In 1998, <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags#History_of_Premier_Parks" title="Six Flags">Premier Parks</a> purchased <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags" title="Six Flags">Six Flags</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Time_Warner" title="Time Warner">Time Warner</a>. Serial Thriller, later known as <a href="/wiki/Thunderhawk_(Michigan%27s_Adventure)" title="Thunderhawk (Michigan's Adventure)">Thunderhawk</a>, was added. The next year, Americana, Time Warp, and an up-charge attraction Skycoaster were added.  Premier Parks re-branded Geauga Lake in 2000 as Six Flags Ohio.
</p>
<h3><span id="2000.E2.80.932004:_Six_Flags_era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–2004:_Six_Flags_era">2000–2004: Six Flags era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2000–2004: Six Flags era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sfwoalogo.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="247" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/Sfwoalogo.gif/200px-Sfwoalogo.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/55/Sfwoalogo.gif/300px-Sfwoalogo.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/55/Sfwoalogo.gif 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sfwoalogo.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The logo when it was known as <i>Six Flags Worlds of Adventure</i></div></div></div>
<p>In 2000, Geauga Lake received a $40 million expansion and became Six Flags Ohio. As part of that expansion, the park received 20 new rides, including four new roller coasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20_new_rides,_four_roller_coaster_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-20_new_rides,_four_roller_coaster-13">[13]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Vekoma_Junior_Coaster" title="Vekoma Junior Coaster">junior roller coaster</a> called <a href="/wiki/Beaver_Land_Mine_Ride" title="Beaver Land Mine Ride">Road Runner Express</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Wooden_roller_coaster" title="Wooden roller coaster">wooden roller coaster</a> called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Villain_a_Rocky_Mountain_Construction_Wooden_Coaster_(roller_coaster)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Villain a Rocky Mountain Construction Wooden Coaster (roller coaster) (page does not exist)">Villain</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Floorless_coaster" title="Floorless coaster">Floorless roller coaster</a> called <a href="/wiki/Dominator_(roller_coaster)" title="Dominator (roller coaster)">Batman: Knight Flight</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Impulse_roller_coaster" title="Impulse roller coaster">Inverted impulse roller coaster</a> called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel_Venom_(Geauga_Lake)" title="Steel Venom (Geauga Lake)">Superman: Ultimate Escape</a>. Also added was a new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shoot_the_chute" title="Shoot the chute">shoot the chute</a> water ride named <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck_Falls" title="Shipwreck Falls">Shipwreck Falls</a> and a new wave pool in the water park. The old wave pool was razed, filled, and used for a new <a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a> themed kids' area known as Looney Tunes Boomtown.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Busch_Entertainment" title="Busch Entertainment">Busch Entertainment</a> determined that its <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld" title="SeaWorld">SeaWorld</a> parks should feature roller coasters, water rides, and other attractions to supplement the marine displays and shows, and the company began de-emphasizing the educational aspects of its parks. They began modifying their <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Orlando" title="SeaWorld Orlando">Orlando</a>, <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_San_Antonio" title="SeaWorld San Antonio">San Antonio</a>, and to a lesser extent their <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_San_Diego" title="SeaWorld San Diego">San Diego</a> parks to reflect this. Due to Six Flags Ohio's close proximity, as well as the fact that the SeaWorld side of the lake had height restrictions, Busch approached Six Flags about buying the Six Flags park. Six Flags then made a counter offer to instead buy <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Ohio" title="SeaWorld Ohio">SeaWorld Ohio</a>. That winter, Six Flags purchased SeaWorld for $110 million in cash, merging the two complexes into one, and changing the entire complex's name to Six Flags Worlds of Adventure. By combining the parks, Six Flags created the largest theme park in the world to date, at 700 acres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-world's_largest_theme_park_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-world's_largest_theme_park-14">[14]</a></sup> The SeaWorld side became known as the "Wild Life" area and remained primarily marine life shows, with a few portable children's rides placed throughout. In 2002, <a href="/wiki/Shamu_(SeaWorld_show)" title="Shamu (SeaWorld show)">Shamu</a> was replaced by Shouka, who came on a breeding loan from Marineland in Antibes, France. The original amusement park area became known as the "Wild Rides" area and continued expansion with a <a href="/wiki/Vekoma" title="Vekoma">Vekoma</a> <a href="/wiki/Flying_roller_coaster" title="Flying roller coaster">Flying roller coaster</a> called <a href="/wiki/Firehawk_(roller_coaster)" title="Firehawk (roller coaster)">X-Flight</a>. The small water park area also continued, so the park was marketed as "Three Parks for One Price".
</p><p>In hopes to expand the water park area, the addition of Hurricane Mountain, the then-largest water slide complex in North America, occurred in 2003 and the water park area was later renamed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Harbor" title="Hurricane Harbor">Hurricane Harbor</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="2004.E2.80.932007:_Cedar_Fair_era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2004–2007:_Cedar_Fair_era">2004–2007: Cedar Fair era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2004–2007: Cedar Fair era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Thunderhawk06.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1531" data-file-width="1927" decoding="async" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Thunderhawk06.png/225px-Thunderhawk06.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Thunderhawk06.png/338px-Thunderhawk06.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Thunderhawk06.png/450px-Thunderhawk06.png 2x" width="225"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Thunderhawk06.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of <i>Thunderhawk</i> (yellow), <i>Dominator</i> (blue), and <i>Raging Wolf Bobs</i> (white) with the ferry boats (then unused) in the background in 2006</div></div></div>
<p>Facing financial difficulties across its chain and high debt, Six Flags considered selling the park. Two months before the 2004 season, a sale to Cedar Fair, owner of <a href="/wiki/Cedar_Point" title="Cedar Point">Cedar Point</a> located 85 miles (137 km) away, was announced. The deal was finalized less than a month later for $145 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The park was immediately "unflagged", "unbranded", and reverted to the name Geauga Lake. The Six Flags <a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a> characters and <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC</a> superhero branding was removed. To conform with copyright laws, the names of many of the rides and roller coasters were changed. The Looney Tunes Boomtown kids area was renamed Kidsworks. The Hurricane Harbor water park area was renamed Hurricane Hannah's Waterpark. The marine life side was shut down immediately before opening. The animals were retained by Six Flags and relocated to other parks such as <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags_Discovery_Kingdom" title="Six Flags Discovery Kingdom">Six Flags Discovery Kingdom</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Six_Flags_Wild_Safari" title="Six Flags Wild Safari">Wild Safari</a>. While most of the marine area was razed, the amusement park area attractions and rides remained the same except for name changes.
</p><p>As part of Cedar Fair's 2004 purchase of Geauga Lake, many of the coasters received new names as Cedar Fair does not own the rights to <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a> characters. Below is a list of renamed rides:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Batman: Knight Flight</i> lost the Batman theming and opened in 2004 under the name of <i><a href="/wiki/Dominator_(roller_coaster)" title="Dominator (roller coaster)">Dominator</a></i></li>
<li><i>Mind Eraser</i> was renamed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head_Spin_(roller_coaster)" title="Head Spin (roller coaster)">Head Spin</a></i></li>
<li><i>Serial Thriller</i> was renamed <i><a href="/wiki/Thunderhawk_(Michigan%27s_Adventure)" title="Thunderhawk (Michigan's Adventure)">Thunderhawk</a></i></li>
<li><i>Superman: Ultimate Escape</i> was renamed <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel_Venom_(Geauga_Lake)" title="Steel Venom (Geauga Lake)">Steel Venom</a></i></li>
<li><i>Road Runner Express</i> was renamed the <i><a href="/wiki/Beaver_Land_Mine_Ride" title="Beaver Land Mine Ride">Beaver Land Mine Ride</a></i></li></ul>
<p>In 2005, Cedar Fair invested $26 million in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wildwater_Kingdom_(Aurora,_Ohio)" title="Wildwater Kingdom (Aurora, Ohio)">Wildwater Kingdom</a>, a new water park on the former <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Ohio" title="SeaWorld Ohio">SeaWorld</a> site, which resulted in the name being altered slightly to Geauga Lake &amp; Wildwater Kingdom. The Wildwater Kingdom side had about six water slides and a children's water play area. The Hurricane Hannah area remained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkmaps_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-parkmaps-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, Wildwater Kingdom was expanded to include Tidal Wave Bay. The Hurricane Hannah area was then shut down, leaving Wildwater Kingdom as the remaining water park. The season was also scaled back, eliminating the spring and fall weekend operations and opening strictly between Memorial Day and Labor Day with one last weekend in mid-September. At the end of the season, the X-Flight roller coaster was removed, as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel_Venom_(Geauga_Lake)" title="Steel Venom (Geauga Lake)">Steel Venom</a> (formerly Superman The Ultimate Escape). X-Flight was relocated to <a href="/wiki/Kings_Island" title="Kings Island">Kings Island</a> and opened as <a href="/wiki/Firehawk_(roller_coaster)" title="Firehawk (roller coaster)">Firehawk</a> in 2007. Steel Venom was relocated to Dorney Park, where it opened for the 2008 season as <i>Voodoo,</i> until 2009 when it was renamed <i>Possessed.</i>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Combined attendance at both parks reached an estimated 2.7 million visitors in 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attendance-Horton_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Attendance-Horton-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attendance-MorningJournal_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Attendance-MorningJournal-18">[18]</a></sup> By 2004, total park attendance had fallen to approximately 700,000 despite a $40 million investment on rides in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmusementShutdown1-ChicagoTribune_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmusementShutdown1-ChicagoTribune-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmusementShutdown2-Cleveland.com_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-AmusementShutdown2-Cleveland.com-20">[20]</a></sup> Citing the Cleveland area as their "most difficult market", Six Flags sold Worlds of Adventure to Cedar Fair in March 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sale_to_Cedar_Fair_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sale_to_Cedar_Fair-21">[21]</a></sup> Speculation that the amusement park side would eventually close began after Cedar Fair relocated two major roller coasters – Steel Venom and X-Flight – to other parks prior to the 2007 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AmusementShutdown2-Cleveland.com_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-AmusementShutdown2-Cleveland.com-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coaster_Relocation_-_CrainCleveland_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Coaster_Relocation_-_CrainCleveland-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Closing_and_land_redevelopment">Closing and land redevelopment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Closing and land redevelopment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" decoding="async" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg/200px-Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg/300px-Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg/400px-Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geauga_Lake_Ripcord_2011.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the last standing rides, Ripcord, pictured in 2011</div></div></div>
<p>In 2007, the summer-only operation of Geauga Lake continued. The annual Oktoberfest festival held on September 14–16, 2007, was the final weekend for the amusement ride area of the park. On Friday, September 21, 2007, Cedar Fair announced that it was permanently closing the ride side of Geauga Lake, and that the Wildwater Kingdom water park would reopen the following season as Geauga Lake's Wildwater Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> This led to efforts to save Geauga Lake, especially landmarks such as the Big Dipper and the Carousel, including an online petition and letters to public officials.
</p><p>Cedar Fair placed the land of the amusement park side of the park up for sale. The remaining rides and remnants were auctioned separately on June 17, 2008. Many returned to the park for one last visit preview and auction days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>From 2012-2013, Cleveland-based photographer and artist <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Joo" title="Johnny Joo">Johnny Joo</a> visited Geauga Lake to capture the park in a state of decay.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> These photos brought heavy local attention from families who had remembered the park in its heyday.
</p><p>As late as January 2013, the Geauga Lake side was still for sale and projects similar to <a href="/wiki/Crocker_Park" title="Crocker Park">Crocker Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Westlake,_Ohio" title="Westlake, Ohio">Westlake, Ohio</a> were being considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Crocker_Park_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Crocker_Park-26">[26]</a></sup> Bainbridge Township and Cedar Fair hoped to have it resolved by the end of 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jan_2013_complaints_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jan_2013_complaints-27">[27]</a></sup> In March 2013, Cedar Fair announced that they were putting Geauga Lake's property up for sale again. Unlike before, they were willing to sell the land in parcels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Geauga_Lake_land_will_be_sold_--_in_pieces_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-Geauga_Lake_land_will_be_sold_--_in_pieces-28">[28]</a></sup> Several companies showed interest in the land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meijer_GL_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meijer_GL-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Developers_sizing_up_GL_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Developers_sizing_up_GL-30">[30]</a></sup> On September 17, 2017, a plaque was unveiled in memory of the park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Fate_of_Geauga_Lake.27s_coasters"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fate_of_Geauga_Lake's_coasters">Fate of Geauga Lake's coasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fate of Geauga Lake's coasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_names_and_management">Previous names and management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geauga_Lake&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Previous names and management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is not uncommon for amusement parks to be sold and this property has changed hands a number of times, although there were only four ownership changes in the 124-year span from 1872 to 1996.
</p><p>The park was originally two parks- Geauga Lake and <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Ohio" title="SeaWorld Ohio">SeaWorld Ohio</a>. Geauga Lake became <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags" title="Six Flags">Six Flags</a> Ohio in 2000; before the 2001 season SeaWorld was purchased by Six Flags and the entire complex was combined and renamed Six Flags Worlds of Adventure.
