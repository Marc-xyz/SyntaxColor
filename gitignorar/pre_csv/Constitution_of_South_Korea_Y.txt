<p>The <b>Constitution of the Republic of Korea</b> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko-Hang" title="Korean language text">대한민국 헌법</span>; <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a>: <span lang="ko-Hani" title="Korean language text">大韓民國憲法</span>) is the supreme law of <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>. It was promulgated on July 17, 1948, and was last revised on October 29, 1987.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Provisional_Charter_of_Korea">The Provisional Charter of Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Provisional Charter of Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Provisional Charter of Korea is the first constitution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_Provisional_Government" title="Korean Provisional Government">Korean Provisional Government</a>, which was promulgated in 1919. It made the name of the country as ‘Republic of Korea’, and it is made up of the preamble of 10 articles. The provisional charter of Korea became the primary spirit of current Constitution, which is stipulated in the preamble of Constitution of South Korea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Provisional Charter of Korea</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The Republic of Korea is a democratic republic country.</li>
<li>The Republic of Korea should be governed by the provisional people of the provisional government.</li>
<li>All citizens of the Republic of Korea are equal without gender, wealth and stratum.</li>
<li>All citizens of the Republic of Korea have the rights to be free of religion, media, writing, publishing, association, assembly, the charge of address, body and ownership.</li>
<li>The citizens who have the qualification of the citizen of the Republic of Korea have a right to vote and to be elected.</li>
<li>The citizens of the Republic of Korea have a duty to education, taxation, and military service.</li>
<li>The Republic of Korea will join the League of Nations in order to exert its founding spirit in the world and to contribute to human culture and peace by the will of the citizens.</li>
<li>The Republic of Korea gives preference to the old imperial family.</li>
<li>The Republic of Korea forbids the punishment of life, body, and licensed prostitution.</li>
<li>The Provisional Government convenes the National Assembly within one year after the restoration of the country.</li>
<li class="mw-empty-elt"></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>South Korea's first 1948 Constitution, drafted by Dr. Chin-O Yu (Hangul: 유진오; Hanja 兪鎭午), framed a presidential system mixed with a parliamentary system. It gave the president to act as the head of state, be elected directly by the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(South_Korea)" title="National Assembly (South Korea)">National Assembly</a>, and share executive power with the cabinet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Constitutional Charter of the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Government_of_the_Republic_of_Korea" title="Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea">Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea</a> of 1919 became the forerunner of 1948 Constitution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1949 Constitution has first amended in 1952 ahead of <a href="/wiki/Syngman_Rhee" title="Syngman Rhee">Syngman Rhee</a>'s re-election, providing for direct presidential elections and a bicameral legislature. It was passed with procedural irregularities after fierce debate. In 1954, Rhee again forced an amendment, removing term limits for himself and emphasizing a capitalistic economic model.
</p><p>Rhee was overthrown in 1960 following widespread protests against his increasingly authoritarian rule. Partly in response to Rhee's abuses, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Second Republic of South Korea">Second Republic</a> turned to a parliamentary system. The 1960 Constitution provided for a figurehead president, a bicameral legislature, a cabinet headed by a prime minister, an election commission, and a constitutional commission. It also provided for elections for supreme court justices and provincial governors, as well as <a href="/wiki/Natural_law" title="Natural law">natural law</a>-based individual rights.
</p><p>With the <a href="/wiki/May_16_coup" title="May 16 coup">May 16 coup</a> of <a href="/wiki/Park_Chung-hee" title="Park Chung-hee">Park Chung-hee</a> in 1961, the 1960 version was nullified, and in 1962, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Third Republic of South Korea">Third Republic</a>'s Constitution was passed. This document returned to a presidential system. It had a number of similarities to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>, such as nominal <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a> functions. In 1972, Park extended his rule with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Fourth Republic of South Korea">Fourth Republic</a> constitution, called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yusin_Constitution" title="Yusin Constitution">Yusin Constitution</a>, which gave the president sweeping (almost dictatorial) powers and permitted him to run for an unlimited number of six-year terms.
</p><p>After Park was assassinated in 1979, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fifth_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Fifth Republic of South Korea">Fifth Republic</a> began with the 1980 Constitution under President <a href="/wiki/Chun_Doo-hwan" title="Chun Doo-hwan">Chun Doo-hwan</a>.  The president's powers were curbed somewhat, and he was barred from reelection after his seven-year term.  It also provided for a unicameral legislature and a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">cabinet</a> system.
</p><p>With the pro-democratic protests of 1987 (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/June_Democracy_Movement" title="June Democracy Movement">June Democracy Movement</a>), the 1988 Constitution of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sixth_Republic_of_South_Korea" title="Sixth Republic of South Korea">Sixth Republic</a> was passed. The constitutional bill was passed by the National Assembly on October 12, 1987, and approved by 93 percent in a national referendum on October 28, taking effect on February 25, 1988, when Roh Tae-Woo was inaugurated as president.

</p>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spirit_of_4._19_revolution">Spirit of 4. 19 revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spirit of 4. 19 revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The spirit of April nineteenth and <a href="/wiki/March_1st_Movement" title="March 1st Movement">March 1st movement</a> is stipulated in the preamble of Constitution of South Korea. But it took a long time to be established. The contents of the <a href="/wiki/April_Revolution" title="April Revolution">April revolution</a> were removed on the fifth amendment, and these were included in the preamble on the sixth amendment, identified with <a href="/wiki/May_16_coup" title="May 16 coup">May 16 coup</a>. After the ninth amendment, the spirit of the <a href="/wiki/April_Revolution" title="April Revolution">April revolution</a> was excepted from the preamble, and it was included for the ‘Resistance ideology for protection of democratic constitution’ on the 10th amendment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Consisting of a preamble, 130 articles, and supplementary provisions, the Constitution provides for an <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive branch</a> headed by a <a href="/wiki/President_(government_title)" title="President (government title)">president</a> and an appointed <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">prime minister</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Unicameralism" title="Unicameralism">unicameral</a> <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a> called the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_South_Korea" title="National Assembly of South Korea">National Assembly</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">judiciary</a> consisting of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court" title="Constitutional Court">Constitutional Court</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">Supreme Court</a> and lower courts.
</p><p>The President is elected by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Past_the_Post" title="First Past the Post">First Past the Post</a> vote and limited to a single five-year term. The Prime Minister is appointed by the President with the consent of the National Assembly.  Although not required by the Constitution, the President also appoints members of the cabinet. President <a href="/wiki/Kim_Dae-jung" title="Kim Dae-jung">Kim Dae-jung</a> changed to the cabinet system.
</p><p>The National Assembly consists of at least 200 (presently 300) members elected to four-year terms. The Supreme Court's chief justice is appointed by the president and up to 13 other justices appointed by the president on the recommendation of the chief justice with the approval of the National Assembly. The President serves a six-year term.
</p><p>The Constitution declares South Korea a "<a href="/wiki/Democratic_republic" title="Democratic republic">democratic republic</a>" (taken from Article 1 of Constitutional Charter of the Provisional Government of the Republic of Korea of 1919),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-auto-6">[6]</a></sup> its territory consisting of "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean Peninsula</a> and its adjacent islands," and that "The Republic of Korea shall seek <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unification_of_Korea" title="Unification of Korea">unification</a> and shall formulate and carry out a policy of peaceful unification based on the principles of freedom and democracy." There are disputes over what "freedom and democracy" mean in Korea, but the direct translation of the Korean word used in the constitution (자유민주적 기본질서) would be <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_rights">Individual rights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Individual rights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Korean Bill of Rights (or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fundamental_right" title="Fundamental right">fundamental right</a>) is Constitution CHAPTER 2. RIGHTS AND DUTIES OF CITIZENS (4-687)
Individuals may not be punished, placed under preventive restrictions, or subjected to involuntary labor except as provided by law. Those detained or arrested must be informed of the reason and of their right to an attorney, and family members must be informed. Warrants must be issued by a judge "through due procedures," and accused persons may sue for wrongful arrest in certain cases.
</p><p>However, individual rights are qualified by other constitutional provisions and pre-existing laws, including the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Act_(South_Korea)" title="National Security Act (South Korea)">National Security Act</a>, which restricts due process rights.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_provisions">Economic provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economic provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Article 119, stable and balanced growth rates, "proper distribution of income", and preventing "abuse of economic power" are explicitly listed as goals of the government. The regulatory goal to "democratize the economy through harmony among economic agents" in the same article reflects the strong prevalence of traditional Korean values and the close relationship between politics and the economy. Article 125 designates foreign trade as a strategic area to be fostered, regulated and coordinated by the government. <a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20061213041106/http://www.oefre.unibe.ch/law/icl/ks__indx.html" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>
</p><p>The Constitution affirms both the right and the duty to work, requiring regulation of minimum wages and working conditions.  Workers have the right to independent association, <a href="/wiki/Collective_bargaining" title="Collective bargaining">collective bargaining</a>, and collective action.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_neutrality">Political neutrality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Political neutrality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Political neutrality is a constitutional convention which provides that public servants should avoid activities likely to impair, or seem to impair, their political impartiality or the political impartiality of the public service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The political neutrality of South Korea's Constitution is guaranteed in the area of military, administration, and education. In the form of the guarantee of 'political neutrality', the constitution provides an objective legal system to guarantee political neutrality as an essential element of the system, unlike the form of guarantee of basic rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 5 (2) of the Constitution stipulates that "the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ROK_Military" title="ROK Military">ROK military</a> shall fulfill the sacred duty of national security and defense of the nation, and its political neutrality shall be obeyed" .
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Education promotes the potential of individuals so that individuals can develop their personality in each area of life. In view of the important functions of education, Article 31(6) of the Constitution stipulates to specify in the law about the basic laws and regulations about the education system and its operation, education finances and the status of teachers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Political neutrality of public officials is specified in the law. Article 6(2) of the Constitution stipulates that "The status and political neutrality of public officials shall be guaranteed by law". Also, Article 9(1) of the Public Service Elections act regulates that Public servants or any other person (including any organization or organization) that is required to be in a political neutrality shall not engage in any unfair influence on the election or otherwise act on the election results. Article 65 of the Public Officials Act(Prohibition of Political Movement) Section 2 regulates that Public officials shall not engage in the following activities to support or oppose a specific political party or a particular person in an election.
</p><p>1. To make an invitation to vote or not
</p><p>2. Pray, preside, or recommend the signatory movement.
</p><p>3. To publish or post documents or books to public facilities
</p><p>4. To make a donation recruitment, or to use public funds
</p><p>5. To encourage others to join or not to join a political party or other political organizations
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_with_other_constitutions">Differences with other constitutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Differences with other constitutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S.">U.S.</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: U.S.">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The difference between the constitutions of South Korea and the U.S. is shown in many constitution provisions. For example, Article 66 to 85 in the constitution of South Korea prescribes the law about the president. The constitution of the U.S. prescribes the law about the president in <a href="/wiki/Article_Two_of_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Article Two of the United States Constitution">Article Two</a>. In South Korea, the president is voted by <a href="/wiki/Direct_election" title="Direct election">Direct election</a>. He or she serves a single five-year term, and no additional terms are allowed. His or her major roles include (1) Head of State, (2) Chief administrator, (3) Commander-in-chief, (4) Top diplomat and foreign policy maker, (5) Chief policy maker and key lawmaker. The Prime Minister is the replacement in the event of presidential disability. However, in the US, the President is elected by indirect election. The President can only serve for up to 8 years in 2 4-year terms, the second term contingent upon re-election. His or her major roles include (1) Head of State, (2) Chief administrator, (3) Commander-in-chief, and (4) has the final veto power on bills passed by the legislature. The Vice president is the replacement in the event of presidential disability.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_Court">Constitutional Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Constitutional Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following the 1987<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> revision, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_South_Korea" title="Constitutional Court of South Korea">Constitutional Court</a> was established in September 1988. Based on the European model, it is a specialized court that determines the Constitutionality of laws, disputes between governmental entities, Constitutional complaints filed by individuals, <a href="/wiki/Impeachment" title="Impeachment">impeachments</a>, and dissolution of political parties. Earlier constitutions provided for various forms of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a>, but the judiciary did not exercise actual independence.
</p><p>The Court's nine Justices serve six-year renewable terms. As of December 2004, the Court has declared 418 laws unconstitutional and revoked about 214 governmental actions. <a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20070504000338/http://www.ccourt.go.kr/home/english/index.jsp" rel="nofollow">[2]</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (November 2007)">failed verification</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Related_Articles_Of_the_Constitution_Court.5B12.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_Articles_Of_the_Constitution_Court[12]">Related Articles Of the Constitution Court<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Related Articles Of the Constitution Court[12]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Articles 111 through 113 of the Constitution of the Republic of Korea refer to the Constitutional Court.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_111">Article 111</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Article 111">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 111 of the Constitution stipulates the enforcement of the Constitutional Court and the qualifications and appointments of the Constitutional Court Judges.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_111_.281.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_111_(1)">Article 111 (1)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Article 111 (1)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Section 1 sets out five judgments ruled by the Constitutional Court.
</p>
<ul><li>The constitutionality of a law upon the request of the courts: Judge whether the law enacted by the National Assembly violates the Constitution.</li>
<li>Impeachment referee: If a public official in a high position, such as the president, violates the Constitution and the law, he may withdraw from office.</li>
<li>Political party dissolution: A political party that violates the purpose or basic order of a political party can dissolve the political party.</li>
<li>Competence disputes between State agencies, between State agencies and local governments, and between local governments: It plays a role of solving the case of disputes between national institutions, national institutions, local autonomous bodies, and local autonomous entities.</li>
<li>Constitutional appeal: Judgment of the constitutional wishes such as whether the state violates the rights of the people.</li></ul>
<p>Paragraphs 2 to 4 refer to the judges of the Constitutional Court.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_111_.282.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_111_(2)">Article 111 (2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Article 111 (2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Paragraph 2 states that the judge of the Constitutional Court is qualified as a judge and that a total of nine people are appointed by the President.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_111_.283.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_111_(3)">Article 111 (3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Article 111 (3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In paragraph 3, three of the judges of the Constitutional Court shall be selected by the National Assembly, and the three shall be appointed by the Chief Justice of the Supreme Court. This means the division of three government powers between the executive, judicial and legislative branches.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_111_.284.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_111_(4)">Article 111 (4)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Article 111 (4)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The President of the Republic, with the consent of the National Assembly, appoints the President of the Constitutional Court as the Constitutional Court judge.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_112">Article 112</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Article 112">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 112 of the Constitution stipulates the term of the members of the Constitutional Court, its limitations, and the things to be observed.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_112_.281.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_112_(1)">Article 112 (1)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Article 112 (1)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Clause 1 states the term of the judge of the Constitutional Court. The term of the members of the Constitutional Court is six years and it is stipulated that it can be renewed by the law.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_112_.282.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_112_(2)">Article 112 (2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Article 112 (2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Section 2 specifies what the judges of the Constitutional Court must observe. The judges of the Constitutional Court declare that they can not join political parties and can not engage in politics.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_112_.283.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_112_(3)">Article 112 (3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Article 112 (3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Section 3 sets out the terms and conditions of the Constitutional Court's jurisdiction. The judges of the Constitutional Court declare that they are not excluded unless they are impeached or sentenced to a sentence of more than an imprisonment without prison labor.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_113">Article 113</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Article 113">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Article 113 of the Constitution specifies the powers of the Constitutional Court.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_113_.281.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_113_(1)">Article 113 (1)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Article 113 (1)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Paragraph 1 stipulates the judgment of the Constitutional Court. It is stipulated that there should be more than 6 judges in case of the unconstitutional decision of law, the decision of impeachment, the decision of dissolution of the political party, or constitutional appeal decision.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_113_.282.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_113_(2)">Article 113 (2)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Article 113 (2)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Paragraph 2 of the Constitution provides for the rule-making powers of the Constitutional Court. The Constitutional Court may establish rules on judicial procedures, internal discipline, and administrative proceedings, as long as they do not conflict with the law.
</p>
<h4><span id="Article_113_.283.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Article_113_(3)">Article 113 (3)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_South_Korea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Article 113 (3)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Paragraph 3 stipulates that the law requires the organization and operation of the Constitutional Court and other necessary matters. This law is the Constitutional Court Act.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
