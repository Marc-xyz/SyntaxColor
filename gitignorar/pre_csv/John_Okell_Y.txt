<p><b>John William Alan Okell</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">OBE</a> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'k' in 'kind'">k</span><span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">ɛ</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span></span>/</a></span></span>; born 1934) is a British linguist notable for his expertise in the field of <a href="/wiki/Burma_studies" title="Burma studies">Burma studies</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Okell&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was educated at <a href="/wiki/The_Queen%27s_College,_Oxford" title="The Queen's College, Oxford">The Queen's College</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>, where he read <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literae_Humaniores" title="Literae Humaniores">Literae Humaniores</a> ("Greats").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOAS-OBE_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOAS-OBE-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959, an inquiry about language courses through the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office" title="Foreign and Commonwealth Office">British Foreign Office</a> led to his joining the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SOAS,_University_of_London" title="SOAS, University of London">School of Oriental and African Studies</a> as a trainee lecturer in <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a>: "They were looking for someone to be taught Burmese. I applied to the program as I was interested in languages and they chose me and trained me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loveofthelingo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-loveofthelingo-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOAS-OBE_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SOAS-OBE-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>At SOAS, he studied Burmese with <a href="/wiki/Hla_Pe" title="Hla Pe">Hla Pe</a> and Anna J. Allott, phonetics with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Keith_Sprigg" title="Richard Keith Sprigg">R. K. Sprigg</a> and N. C. Scott, and general linguistics with <a href="/wiki/R._H._Robins" title="R. H. Robins">R. H. Robins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A9nie_Henderson" title="Eugénie Henderson">Eugénie Henderson</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOAS-OBE_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SOAS-OBE-2">[2]</a></sup> After eighteen months of study, he travelled to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">Burma</a>, where he spent a year (1960–1961) immersing himself in the country's language and culture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loveofthelingo_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-loveofthelingo-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBS_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBS-4">[4]</a></sup> He returned for another year-long visit in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBS_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBS-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He retired from SOAS in 1999, although he remains a research associate in the Department of South East Asia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Since his retirement, he has continued his teaching of Burmese through short courses convened in a variety of places, most notably <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Mai" title="Chiang Mai">Chiang Mai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yangon" title="Yangon">Yangon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loveofthelingo_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-loveofthelingo-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOAS-OBE_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-SOAS-OBE-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, in the <a href="/wiki/2014_Birthday_Honours" title="2014 Birthday Honours">Queen's Birthday Honours</a>, he was made an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Officer of the Order of the British Empire</a> for services to UK/Burma relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOAS-OBE_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-SOAS-OBE-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, SOAS awarded him an honorary doctorate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOAS-D.Litt._7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOAS-D.Litt.-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>He is the creator of the <i>Avalaser</i> Burmese computer font and the Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Britain%E2%80%93Burma_Society" title="Britain–Burma Society">Britain–Burma Society</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBS_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBS-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
