<p>The <b>Deep Ecliptic Survey</b> (<b>DES</b>) is a project to find <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">Kuiper belt</a> objects (KBOs), using the facilities of the <a href="/wiki/National_Optical_Astronomy_Observatory" title="National Optical Astronomy Observatory">National Optical Astronomy Observatory</a> (NOAO). The principal investigator is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_L._Millis" title="Robert L. Millis">Robert L. Millis</a>.
</p><p>Since 1998 through the end of 2003, the survey covered 550 <a href="/wiki/Square_degree" title="Square degree">square degrees</a> with <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">sensitivity</a> of 22.5, which means an estimated 50% of objects of this magnitude have been found.
</p><p>The survey has also established the mean Kuiper Belt plane and introduced new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubewano#Toward_a_formal_definition" title="Cubewano">formal definitions</a> of the dynamical classes of Kuiper belt objects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Elliot-2005_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Elliot-2005-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The remarkable first observations and/or discoveries include:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie-2003_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buie-2003-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/28978_Ixion" title="28978 Ixion">28978 Ixion</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Plutino" title="Plutino">plutino</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/19521_Chaos" title="19521 Chaos">19521 Chaos</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubewano" title="Cubewano">cubewano</a>)</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/1998_WW31" title="1998 WW31">1998 WW<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">31</span></span></a></span>, the first <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">binary</a> <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">trans-Neptunian object</a> (TNO)</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(148209)_2000_CR105" title="(148209) 2000 CR105">(148209) 2000 CR<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">105</span></span></a></span>, the first object with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">perihelion</a> too far to be affected (scattered) by <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> and a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">semi-major axis</a></li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/(87269)_2000_OO67" title="(87269) 2000 OO67">(87269) 2000 OO<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">67</span></span></a></span>, remarkable for its semi-major axis of more than 500 <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">AU</a> and extreme <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">eccentricity</a> (0.96) taking the object from the inside of the Neptune's <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> to more than 1000 AU</li>
<li><span class="nowrap"><a href="/wiki/2001_QR322" title="2001 QR322">2001 QR<span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;"><span style="font-size:80%;">322</span></span></a></span>, the first <a href="/wiki/Neptune_trojan" title="Neptune trojan">Neptune trojan</a></li>
<li><span class="nowrap">2002 XU<sub>93</sub></span>, with one of the most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">inclined</a> orbits (&gt;68°)</li></ul>
