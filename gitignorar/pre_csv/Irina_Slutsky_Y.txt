<p><b>Irina Slutsky</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_blog" title="Video blog">vlogger</a>, and early pioneer in <a href="/wiki/Web_television" title="Web television">web television</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irina_Slutsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Irina Slutsky was born in <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which was, at the time, part of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Speaking of her childhood in 2007, Slutsky said "When I lived in the Soviet Union... we were told what we could read, what we could write, what we <i>couldn't</i> read, what we couldn't watch.... Needless to say, the Soviet Union isn't around any more; that stuff doesn't work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>At a young age, Slutsky and her family emigrated to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, settling in the <a href="/wiki/Parkchester,_Bronx" title="Parkchester, Bronx">Parkchester</a> section of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronx" title="Bronx">Bronx</a>, where she learned to speak English.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irina_Slutsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Red_Herring">Red Herring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irina_Slutsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Red Herring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the demise of the <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a> magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Herring_(magazine)" title="Red Herring (magazine)">Red Herring</a></i>, founder Tony Perkins sold the name and trademarks to <a href="/wiki/Alex_Vieux" title="Alex Vieux">Alex Vieux</a> in 2003, who then revived the brand as a technology-news website with a short-lived companion magazine. In 2004 Vieux paid recent graduates of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_Graduate_School_of_Journalism" title="Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism">Columbia School of Journalism</a> $2000 each to move to San Francisco to work as reporters, among them Irina Slutsky.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superstar_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Superstar-4">[4]</a></sup> Although Slutsky represented <i>Red Herring</i> as a featured speaker at the 2005 <a href="/wiki/South_by_Southwest" title="South by Southwest">South by Southwest</a> conference,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> she did not enjoy her time with the magazine; in her words, "it totally sucked".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superstar_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Superstar-4">[4]</a></sup> Editors did not allow <a href="/wiki/Byline" title="Byline">bylines</a>, as Vieux was allegedly worried that crediting the authors would give the young journalists "egos". Female employees were also not treated the same as their male counterparts; for <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> 2004, Vieux reportedly gave the male staff each a <a href="/wiki/Charvet_Place_Vend%C3%B4me" title="Charvet Place VendÃ´me">Charvet</a> <a href="/wiki/Necktie" title="Necktie">necktie</a>, while female staff (including the current managing editor) received nothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Slutsky quickly moved on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Superstar_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Superstar-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GETV_and_PodTech">GETV and PodTech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irina_Slutsky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: GETV and PodTech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue,_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue%2C_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg/220px-Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue%2C_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue%2C_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg/330px-Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue%2C_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue%2C_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg/440px-Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue%2C_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geek_Entertainment_TV_-_Violet_Blue,_Eddie_Codel_and_Irina_Slutsky.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slutsky with Geek Entertainment co-founder <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eddie_Codel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eddie Codel (page does not exist)">Eddie Codel</a> and occasional guest-host <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Violet_Blue_(author)" title="Violet Blue (author)">Violet Blue</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In November 2005, Slutsky teamed with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eddie_Codel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eddie Codel (page does not exist)">Eddie Codel</a> to form Geek Entertainment TV (GETV), a "snarky"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Web_television" title="Web television">web television</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_blog" title="Video blog">video blog</a> (or vlog) initially focusing on <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a>, "reporting from deep inside <a href="/wiki/Dot-com_bubble" title="Dot-com bubble">the bubble</a> as it re-inflates."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Codel produced the series, and the majority of episodes featured Slutsky performing interviews. Occasionally episodes would feature guest reporters, such as noted sex <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogger</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Violet_Blue_(author)" title="Violet Blue (author)">Violet Blue</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> By February, the blog celebrated its 1000th subscriber.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2006, Slutsky and Codel signed with <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=PodTech&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="PodTech (page does not exist)">PodTech</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcasting</a> company), to work under <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogging</a> celebrity <a href="/wiki/Robert_Scoble" title="Robert Scoble">Robert Scoble</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> who had been hired-away from <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> the month before.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> GETV became the company's first branded content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-podtech_geek_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-podtech_geek-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>While with PodTech, Slutsky and Codel continued to produce weekly episodes similar to their independent work, only with an increased budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-podtech_geek_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-podtech_geek-15">[15]</a></sup> Slutsky helped sign independent vloggers to the company, as well as helped develop new content.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup> She also spearheaded and co-hosted the first ever online video awards show, the Vloggies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> She then starred in a new PodTech series (in addition to the other programs) called The Vloggies Show, focusing on video blogs and video bloggers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>Slutsky and Codel worked on yet another series called LunchMeet, interviewing <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">Bay Area</a> internet companies during lunchtime. Early interviews included <a href="/wiki/Wetpaint" title="Wetpaint">Wetpaint</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wesabe" title="Wesabe">Wesabe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dogster" title="Dogster">Dogster</a> and <a href="/wiki/Instructables" title="Instructables">Instructables</a>, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Unlike the other series, Codel appeared in front of the camera with Slutsky in addition to producing the video. Scoble credited the two with first getting him to use <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a>, as LunchMeet had one of the earliest significant interviews with the original Twitter team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although PodTech had raised $5.5 million in funding in 2006, and had developed a reputation for hiring respected bloggers, it was unclear to those outside the company how PodTech would raise the return on that investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> When PodTech was unsuccessful in their attempt at attracting a second round of funding, they started making cutbacks. In early 2007 they released GETV back to Slutsky and Codel. Codel was let go as a full-time employee, though he continued producing videos and hosting LunchMeet on a contracted basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="270" data-file-width="480" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png/220px-Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png/330px-Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png/440px-Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Irina_Slutsky_and_Randi_Jayne_Zuckerberg_music_video_still.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slutsky and Zuckerberg</div></div></div>
<p>On July 18, 2007, Slutsky appeared (as MC Slutsky) in and produced a GETV parody music video in the style of <i><a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Cha" title="Don't Cha">Don't Cha</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/The_Pussycat_Dolls" title="The Pussycat Dolls">The Pussycat Dolls</a>, celebrating the newly released <a href="/wiki/IPhone_(1st_generation)" title="IPhone (1st generation)">iPhone</a>. Titled "Dontcha (iphone remix)", the song was sung by <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Randi_Zuckerberg" title="Randi Zuckerberg">Randi Zuckerberg</a> (as Randi Jayne), with assistance from <a href="/wiki/David_Prager" title="David Prager">David Prager</a> (as MC Prager).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The video also features <a href="/wiki/Nerdcore" title="Nerdcore">nerdcore</a> rapper <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doctor_Popular&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doctor Popular (page does not exist)">Doctor Popular</a>, performing <a href="/wiki/Yo-yo" title="Yo-yo">yo-yo</a> tricks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> An advertisement for <a href="/wiki/Motorola" title="Motorola">Motorola</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Droid_Pro" title="Droid Pro">Droid Pro</a> later used a similar idea, though it was unrelated to Slutsky's video.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was later learned that the same day that she had published the music video, Slutsky had been let go from PodTech. PodTech founder John Furrier explained that he had been unable to monetize GETV as he had hoped, and that most of the company's revenue was coming from contracted work for corporations, rather than their original <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property" title="Intellectual property">IP</a>. He had hoped to continue working with Slutsky through contracted work, as had happened with Codel. He admitted that he had spent over $500,000 on GETV alone. Slutsky said that she understood the move, that her projects had been increasingly de-emphasized by PodTech, and that the company was spending too much of its limited resources on her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
