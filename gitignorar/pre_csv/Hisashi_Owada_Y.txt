<p><b>Hisashi Owada</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">小和田 恆</span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>Owada Hisashi</i>, born 18 September 1932)</span> is a former <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese</a> jurist, diplomat and law professor, and served as a judge on the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a> from 2003 until June 7, 2018, and was President of the Court from 2009 to 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> He is the <a href="/wiki/Parent-in-law" title="Parent-in-law">father-in-law</a> of the incumbent <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naruhito" title="Naruhito">Naruhito</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Owada was born in <a href="/wiki/Shibata,_Niigata" title="Shibata, Niigata">Shibata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niigata_Prefecture" title="Niigata Prefecture">Niigata Prefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  the third of seven children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.35_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.35-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After earning a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a> in 1955, Owada passed the civil service examinations to join the Diplomatic and Consular service, now known as the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Japan)">Foreign Service</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Educator">Educator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Educator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because of sponsorship from his Foreign Ministry, Owada was sent to study at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> where he earned a law degree in 1959<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and later a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doctorate_of_philosophy" title="Doctorate of philosophy">doctorate of philosophy</a>. Overall he served as a law professor for three decades at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University Law School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Law_School" title="Columbia Law School">Columbia Law School</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hague_Academy_of_International_Law" title="Hague Academy of International Law">Hague Academy of International Law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Waseda_University" title="Waseda University">Waseda University</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>. In between postings he took semesters as a visiting professor in international treaty law, his specialty, at Harvard and at Oxford.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He also served as the Bright International Jurist-in-Residence at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">University of Hawaii</a>'s <a href="/wiki/William_S._Richardson_School_of_Law" title="William S. Richardson School of Law">William S. Richardson School of Law</a> in 2010. He has received honorary degrees from <a href="/wiki/Keiwa_College" title="Keiwa College">Keiwa College</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banaras_Hindu_University" title="Banaras Hindu University">Banaras Hindu University</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Waseda_University" title="Waseda University">Waseda University</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diplomat">Diplomat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diplomat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Owada's first foreign assignment was <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>, serving from 1963 to 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Immediately following, from 1969 to 1971, was a post at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York,_New_York" title="New York, New York">New York, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.44_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.44-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Returning to Japan, Owada was chosen to accompany Emperor <a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito</a> on his first postwar trip outside Japan to Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> From 1976 to 1978, he served as Private Secretary to <a href="/wiki/Takeo_Fukuda" title="Takeo Fukuda">Takeo Fukuda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Japan" title="Prime Minister of Japan">Prime Minister of Japan</a>.
</p><p>From 1979 to 1981, while serving as visiting professor at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a>, Owada "remain[ed] on the Foreign Ministry payroll with the title of Minister at the Embassy in Washington, and would resume his career with another plum posting the following year."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> After this, however, the Owadas would move again to <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> save for Masako, who would stay behind to enroll as a student at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">Harvard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1988, Owada was appointed Japanese <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassador</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> (OECD) in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He served for a year before returning to Japan, working first as Deputy Minister and from 1991 to 1993 as Vice-Minister.
</p><p>From 1994 to 1998, he served as Japanese ambassador to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, where he served twice as <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> President.
</p><p>From 1999 to 2000 Owada then served as senior adviser to the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_World_Bank_Group" title="President of the World Bank Group">President of the World Bank</a>. Until 2003 Owada was president of the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Institute_of_International_Affairs" title="Japan Institute of International Affairs">Japan Institute of International Affairs</a> and adviser to the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Japan)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Japan)">Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Diplomat">Post-Diplomat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-Diplomat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Having been <a href="/wiki/2011_International_Court_of_Justice_judges_election" title="2011 International Court of Justice judges election">re-elected to the ICJ in 2011</a>, Owada's term was set to expire on 5 February 2021 prior to his retirement in June, 2018.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GA11171_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GA11171-13">[13]</a></sup> He received 170 out of 192 votes in the General Assembly on the first round, more than any other candidate,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GA11171_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-GA11171-13">[13]</a></sup> and 14 out of 15 votes in the Security Council on the first round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SC10444_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-SC10444-14">[14]</a></sup> Owada had been nominated by the Japanese national group of the Permanent Court of Arbitration (as well as the national groups of 32 other countries).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wife_and_children">Wife and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wife and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1962, at age 30, Hisashi married twenty-five-year-old Yumiko Egashira, introduced to him by a mutual friend and later employer <a href="/wiki/Takeo_Fukuda" title="Takeo Fukuda">Takeo Fukuda</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  A year later, their eldest daughter <a href="/wiki/Empress_Masako" title="Empress Masako">Masako</a> was born at Toranomon Hospital in Tokyo,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> followed by twins Reiko and Setsuko in the summer of 1966 in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.44_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.44-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1993, Hisashi's daughter Masako Owada, a diplomat in her own right, married <a href="/wiki/Naruhito" title="Naruhito">Crown Prince Naruhito</a>, the heir to the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Chrysanthemum_Throne" title="Chrysanthemum Throne">Chrysanthemum Throne</a>. Following <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Emperor Akihito</a>'s <a href="/wiki/2019_Japanese_imperial_transition" title="2019 Japanese imperial transition">2019 abdication</a>, she became <a href="/wiki/Empress_of_Japan" title="Empress of Japan">empress consort</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancestry">Ancestry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hisashi_Owada&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ancestry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Owada's father Takeo is descended from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Owada_clan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Owada clan (page does not exist)">Owada clan</a>, whose head Shinroku—Masako's 4th-great-grandfather<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.33_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.33-19">[19]</a></sup>—was called to <a href="/wiki/Murakami_Domain" title="Murakami Domain">Murakami</a> in 1787 to serve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naito_clan" title="Naito clan">Naito clan</a> that the <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa</a> shōgun had installed as the city's rulers sixty-seven years earlier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.33_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.33-19">[19]</a></sup> After the fall of the shogunate, the Owadas participated in a <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a>-<a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">cooperative</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.35_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.35-3">[3]</a></sup> the proceeds of which provided schooling for many local children, including Takeo.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.35_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.35-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Takeo became principal of a prefectural high school in modern-day <a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Detsu,_Niigata" title="Jōetsu, Niigata">Joetsu</a> city and head of its board of education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.35_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.35-3">[3]</a></sup> Takeo and his wife would have seven children, all of whom survived infancy to graduate from university or teaching college.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.35_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.35-3">[3]</a></sup> His five sons all graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Tokyo" title="University of Tokyo">University of Tokyo</a>—Akira, who would become a lecturer of Chinese literature at <a href="/wiki/Senshu_University" title="Senshu University">Senshu University</a>; Takashi, who would become a lawyer; Hisashi; Osamu, who would become head of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_National_Tourist_Organization" title="Japan National Tourist Organization">Japan National Tourist Organization</a>; and Makoto, who would become an inspector at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Transportation" title="Ministry of Transportation">Ministry of Transportation</a>'s Ports and Harbors Bureau.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> His two daughters Yasuko and Toshiko would marry highly, the former to managing director of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Krosaki-Harima&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Krosaki-Harima (page does not exist)">Krosaki-Harima</a> Tadashi Katada and the latter to one-time managing director of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Bank_of_Japan" title="Industrial Bank of Japan">Industrial Bank of Japan</a> (IBJ) Kazuhide Kashiwabara.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hills,_Ben_pp.35_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Hills,_Ben_pp.35-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
