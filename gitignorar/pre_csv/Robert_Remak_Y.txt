<p><b>Robert Remak</b> (26 July 1815 – 29 August 1865) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Polish</a>-<a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> embryologist, physiologist, and neurologist, born in <a href="/wiki/Pozna%C5%84" title="Poznań">Posen, Prussia</a>, who discovered that the origin of cells was by the <a href="/wiki/Cell_division" title="Cell division">division</a> of post-existing cells.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> as well as several other key discoveries.
</p><p>According to historian <a href="/wiki/Paul_Weindling" title="Paul Weindling">Paul Weindling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Virchow" title="Rudolf Virchow">Rudolf Virchow</a>, one of the founders of modern cell theory, plagiarized the notion that all cells come from pre-existing cells from Remak. Remak had concluded after observing red blood cells from chicken embryos in various stages of division. He then confirmed that the phenomenon existed in the cell of every frog's egg immediately after fertilization, proving that this was a universal phenomenon and finally explaining the reason for the results of tests by <a href="/wiki/Louis_Pasteur" title="Louis Pasteur">Louis Pasteur</a> which had previously proved that there exists no spontaneous generation of life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dr. Remak obtained his medical degree from Friedrich Wilhelm University in Berlin in 1838 specializing in neurology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He is best known for reducing <a href="/wiki/Karl_Ernst_von_Baer" title="Karl Ernst von Baer">Karl Ernst von Baer</a>'s four <a href="/wiki/Germ_layer" title="Germ layer">germ layers</a> to three: the <a href="/wiki/Ectoderm" title="Ectoderm">ectoderm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesoderm" title="Mesoderm">mesoderm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Endoderm" title="Endoderm">endoderm</a>. He also discovered unmyelinated nerve fibres and the nerve cells in the heart sometimes called <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Remak%27s_ganglia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Remak's ganglia (page does not exist)">Remak's ganglia</a>. He studied under <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Peter_M%C3%BCller" title="Johannes Peter Müller">Johannes Muller</a> at the University of Berlin.
</p><p>Despite his accomplishments, because of his Jewish faith, he was repeatedly denied full professor status, and finally late in life was appointed assistant professor, being the first Jew to teach in that institute. Even then he was never fully recognized for his discoveries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>His son <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernst_Julius_Remak" title="Ernst Julius Remak">Ernst Julius Remak</a> was also a Geologist and his grandson was the mathematician <a href="/wiki/Robert_Remak_(mathematician)" title="Robert Remak (mathematician)">Robert Remak</a> who died in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auschwitz" title="Auschwitz">Auschwitz</a> in 1942.
</p>
