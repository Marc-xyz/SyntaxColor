	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ballymun Flats</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="333" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg/220px-Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg/330px-Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ballymun_tower_2007.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An April 2007 image of Joseph Plunkett Tower, the last remaining tower, in Ballymun. In the background the Sillogue Road flats can be seen.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Ballymun Flats</b> refers to a number of <a href="/wiki/Apartment" title="Apartment">flats</a>—including the <b>Ballymun tower blocks</b>, seven landmark residential towers built in the 1960s—in <a href="/wiki/Ballymun" title="Ballymun">Ballymun</a>, Dublin. In total there were 36 blocks of Ballymun flats (7 fifteen-storey, 19 eight-storey and 10 four-storey blocks). While nothing unusual by global standards, and small compared to Eastern European or Swedish norms, they were experimental for Ireland.
</p><p>They were all demolished in the early 21st century; from October 2013 all three remaining blocks were empty, and last one was demolished in September 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 15-storey blocks actually had 17 storeys including the entrance floor and the plant room on the roof.
</p><p>The Plunkett tower, one of the original named after the signatories of the Proclamation, was a 42-metre, 8,500-tonne building. It housed 90 families in 30 three, two and one-bed units.
</p><p>There were also four-storey and eight-storey blocks.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballymun_Flats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ballymun Flats were built in the 1960s to accommodate the rising population, particularly to accommodate former residents of inner-city areas which were being cleared in the process of 1960s '<a href="/wiki/Urban_renewal" title="Urban renewal">urban slum clearances</a>'. Whilst suffering from a lack of sufficient public amenities, several schools served the area (Holy Spirit N.S. and Ballymun Comprehensive), as well as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_Board_(Ireland)" title="Health Board (Ireland)">Eastern Health Board</a> medical centre and a purpose built shopping centre. The area suffered from many social problems such as drugs and rampant crime. The causes of these social problems, and the subsequent discrimination faced by many people with Ballymun addresses when seeking employment outside the suburb, have been disputed, but Ballymun generally paralleled the experience of many working-class people in the 1960 and 1970s when placed in high-rise locations.
</p><p>Despite the negative perceptions of many non-residents of Ballymun, many of the residents insist that there is a strong sense of pride and community in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Lynn Connolly, whose 2006 memoir <i>The Mun: Growing Up in Ballymun</i> detailed her raising there in the 1970s and 1980s, readily acknowledged the problems there and wanted to get out at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-connolly_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-connolly-3">[3]</a></sup> But she later came to realise that there had been much that was good at the towers – in terms of a collective wit among residents and a helping sense of community – which had been ignored by the media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-connolly_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-connolly-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mun-review_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-mun-review-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ballymun Flats were the first place in Ireland to receive cable television. RTE Relays Ltd, a subsidiary of the national broadcaster RTE installed cable television into the flats in 1963, giving each flat the ability to watch the main UK television channels BBC One, BBC Two, ITV (from 1982 Channel 4) as well as RTE Television via cable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Four-storey_flats">Four-storey flats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballymun_Flats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Four-storey flats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The  four-storey flats consisted of Sandyhill Avenue, Sillogue Avenue and Shangan Avenue, the height of all three were not always as visible as the higher storey flats. They were the earliest complexes to be demolished. The former flats of Sillogue Avenue are now just land whilst the former flats of Shangan Avenue have been replaced by new complexes.  A new area called Marewood, consisting of houses and apartments, is now situated where the Sandyhill Avenue flats once stood.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eight-storey_flats">Eight-storey flats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballymun_Flats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eight-storey flats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eight-storey flats consisted of Balbutcher Lane (which only consisted of two blocks), Shangan Road (three blocks), Coultry Road (four blocks),  Balcurris Road and Sillogue Road (five blocks each). 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg/220px-BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg/330px-BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg/440px-BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BallymunFlatsDifferentDesigns.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The eight-storey flats contained two different designs, one where the lift was located opposite the stairwell (pictured right) and an alternative design where the lift was located on the same side as the stairwell, as seen here on Sillogue Road, the four-storey Sillogue Avenue can be seen in the middle.</div></div></div>
<p>The flats had two different designs, the more common being with the lift on the opposite side of the stairwell, Balbutcher and Shangan were the only ones to contain just this design. Balcurris contained one of the alternative blocks, Sillogue (pictured) had two of the alternative blocks and three of the other blocks, three of the four Coultry blocks were made contained the lift on the side of the stairwell. Balcurris was the only row of flats to have its blocks separated by a road, the latter three blocks faced a different direction to the first two blocks and were the most visible from <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a>.
</p><p>Balbutcher Lane was upgraded in the 1990s where only residents were allowed to enter and had to buzz their way in, visitors needed permission from residents in order to enter. Windows were placed on the balconies and post would be delivered similar to how an apartment block would receive their mail. Railings were also placed around the flats and a playground was built at the back of the complex.
</p><p>Sections of Coultry and Balcurris were demolished first, with the latter becoming the first complex to be completely demolished by 2009, it was originally noted that the Ballymun Shopping Centre and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dublin_Metro#Metro_North" title="Dublin Metro">Metro North</a> would be situated on the old Balcurris site, although these plans failed to materialize. Many of the complexes were demolished by 2012, after the removal of the Sillogue flats. Balbutcher Lane were the last eight-storey flats to go in 2015.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fifteen-story_flats">Fifteen-story flats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballymun_Flats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fifteen-story flats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ballymun tower blocks were seven landmark residential towers built in the 1960s in <a href="/wiki/Ballymun" title="Ballymun">Ballymun</a>, Dublin, Republic of Ireland. The seven towers were named after the seven leaders of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1916_rising" title="1916 rising">1916 rising</a>; <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Pearse" title="Patrick Pearse">Patrick Pearse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_MacDonagh" title="Thomas MacDonagh">Thomas MacDonagh</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sean_MacDermott" title="Sean MacDermott">Sean MacDermott</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eamonn_Ceannt" title="Eamonn Ceannt">Eamonn Ceannt</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Clarke_(Irish_republican)" title="Thomas Clarke (Irish republican)">Thomas Clarke</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Connolly" title="James Connolly">James Connolly</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Plunkett" title="Joseph Plunkett">Joseph Plunkett</a>.
</p><p>In 2004 demolition of the first tower began. The Patrick Pearse tower was demolished slowly by mechanical means, whilst MacDermott and MacDonagh Towers were demolished by <a href="/wiki/Building_implosion" title="Building implosion">controlled implosion</a>. Ceannt, Plunkett, Clarke and Connolly towers were demolished by mechanical means.
</p><p>The red <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_warning_lights" title="Aircraft warning lights">aircraft warning lights</a> on these structures were not connected to any form of <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">back-up power</a> for many years, leaving the towers completely dark in a power outage.
</p>
<h3><span id="Construction.2FDemolition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction/Demolition">Construction/Demolition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballymun_Flats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Construction/Demolition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Patrick Pearse tower</b> (1966–2004) was the first of the Ballymun towers to go up in 1966. Pearse Tower was halfway through construction when the construction of MacDonagh Tower started. Pearse Tower was the first tower to be demolished in 2004.</li>
<li><b>Thomas MacDonagh tower</b> (1966–2005) was the second tower to go up in 1966 and in 2005 was demolished by controlled implosion.</li>
<li><b>Eamonn Ceannt tower</b> (1966–2005) was the third tower built in 1966; in 2005 it was demolished.</li>
<li><b>James Connolly tower</b> (1966–2007) was the fourth tower built in 1966. In 2007 it was demolished.</li>
<li><b>Sean MacDermott tower</b> (1966–2005) was the fifth tower to be built, in 1966. In 2005 it was demolished by controlled implosion and went down in less than 8 seconds.</li>
<li><b>Thomas Clarke tower</b> (1966–2008) was the 6th tower built in 1966. Before it was demolished in 2008, the top floor was turned into a short-stay hotel.</li>
<li><b>Joseph Plunkett tower</b> (1967–2015) was the last built in 1967. The tower's demolition began on 22 September 2015.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ballymun_Flats&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The line "I see seven towers, but I only see one way out" from <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>'s 1987 song "<a href="/wiki/Running_to_Stand_Still" title="Running to Stand Still">Running to Stand Still</a>" (on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Joshua_Tree" title="The Joshua Tree">The Joshua Tree</a></i> album) refers to these towers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The link between the towers and the song was pervasive enough to be mentioned in some tourist books about Dublin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guide_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-guide-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>Ballymun flats feature in <a href="/wiki/M._J._Hyland" title="M. J. Hyland">M. J. Hyland</a>'s Booker-shortlisted novel <i><a href="/wiki/Carry_Me_Down" title="Carry Me Down">Carry Me Down</a></i> (2006), symbolising John's family's descent into poverty.</li>
<li>The Ballymun Trilogy is a series of three plays about the process of change in Ballymun, written by <a href="/wiki/Dermot_Bolger" title="Dermot Bolger">Dermot Bolger</a> and staged by the Axis Art Centre located close to the original site of McDonagh Tower. They are <i>From These Green Heights</i>, <i>The Townlands of Brazil</i>, and <i>The Consequences of Lightning</i>.</li>
<li>Lynn Connolly followed up her memoir with a 2012 mystery set in the Ballymun flats entitled <i>Elizabeth III'</i>.</li>
<li>On an episode of the podcast <a href="/wiki/Song_Exploder" title="Song Exploder">Song Exploder</a>, <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>'s Bono detailed the track "Cedarwood Road" focuses on the violence that youth faced in the Ballymun Flats community during the '70s.</li>
<li>Ballymun also appears in the popular Irish film <i>The Committments</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> where some of its residents become members of the titular band. This <a href="/wiki/The_Commitments_(novel)#Film" title="The Commitments (novel)">film</a> was voted the best Irish film of the decade, favoured mostly for its wry humour. Some if the quips in the film directly reference <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> this part of North Dublin.</li></ul>
