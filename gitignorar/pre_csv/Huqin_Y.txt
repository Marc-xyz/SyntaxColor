<p><i><b>Huqin</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh"><a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%83%A1%E7%90%B4" title="wiktionary:胡琴">胡琴</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">húqin</span></i>) is a family of bowed <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">string instruments</a>, more specifically, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spike_fiddle" title="Spike fiddle">spike fiddle</a> popularly used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_music" title="Chinese music">Chinese music</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The instruments consist of a round, hexagonal, or octagonal <a href="/wiki/Sound_box" title="Sound box">sound box</a> at the bottom with a neck attached that protrudes upwards. They also usually have two strings, and their soundboxes are typically covered with either <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snakeskin</a> (most often <a href="/wiki/Pythonidae" title="Pythonidae">python</a>) or thin wood. Huqin instruments usually have two tuning pegs, one peg for each string. The pegs are attached horizontally through holes drilled in the instrument's neck. Most huqin have the bow hair pass in between the strings. Exceptions to having two strings and pegs include variations of huqin with three, four, and sometimes even more than five. These include the zhuihu, a three stringed huqin, the sihu, a huqin of Mongolian origin, and the sanhu, a lesser-known three-stringed variation.
</p><p>The most common huqin are the <i><a href="/wiki/Erhu" title="Erhu">erhu</a></i>, which are tuned to a middle range; <i><a href="/wiki/Zhonghu" title="Zhonghu">zhonghu</a></i>, which is tuned to a lower register, and <i><a href="/wiki/Gaohu" title="Gaohu">gaohu</a></i>, which is tuned to a higher pitch. The lowest pitched huqins include the dahu and gehu. The highest pitched huqin is the jinghu, used in the Beijing opera. Over eighty types of <i>huqin</i> instruments have been documented.
</p><p><i>Huqin</i> instruments are believed to have come from the nomadic <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Chinese_history" title="Ethnic groups in Chinese history">Hu people</a>, who lived on the extremities of ancient Chinese kingdoms, possibly descending from an instrument called the <i><a href="/wiki/Xiqin_(instrument)" title="Xiqin (instrument)">Xiqin</a></i> (<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A5%9A%E7%90%B4" title="wiktionary:奚琴">奚琴</a>), originally played by the Mongolic <a href="/wiki/Kumo_Xi" title="Kumo Xi">Xi</a> tribe. Like the people of China, Mongolian people also have cultural and ethnic heritage of the ancient Hu nomads, and the Mongol version of the <i>xiqin</i>, known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Khuuchir" title="Khuuchir">khuuchir</a></i>, is testament to this shared heritage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 20th century, large bass huqin such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Dihu" title="Dihu">dihu</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Gehu" title="Gehu">gehu</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Diyingehu" title="Diyingehu">diyingehu</a></i> were developed for use in modern <a href="/wiki/Chinese_orchestra" title="Chinese orchestra">Chinese orchestras</a>. Of these, the <i>gehu</i> and <i>diyingehu</i> would be analogous to Occidental cellos and double basses respectively, and were designed to have a timbre that would blend in with the sound of traditional <i>huqin</i>. These instruments generally have four strings and fingerboards, and are played in a similar manner to cellos and double basses, and are very different from the traditional <i>huqin</i>.
</p><p>Similar instruments also feature in the music traditions of neighboring countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Mongolia" title="Music of Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_music" title="Korean music">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Japan" title="Music of Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Kyrgyzstan" title="Music of Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Vietnam" title="Music of Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Thailand" title="Music of Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Cambodia" title="Music of Cambodia">Cambodia</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laotian_music" title="Laotian music">Laos</a>.
</p>
