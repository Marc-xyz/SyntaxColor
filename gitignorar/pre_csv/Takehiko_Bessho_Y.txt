<p><b>Takehiko Bessho</b> (別所 毅彦<span style="font-weight: normal"></span> <i>Bessho Takehiko</i>, October 1, 1922 -June 24, 1999), born <b>Akira Bessho</b> (別所昭<span style="font-weight: normal"></span>, <i>Bessho Akira</i>), was a Japanese <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baseball_player" title="Baseball player">baseball player</a> whose professional career as a player lasted from 1942 until 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robs_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-robs-1">[1]</a></sup> Bessho first achieved fame as a <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_professional_baseball" title="Japanese professional baseball">Japanese professional baseball</a>; later, he served as a <a href="/wiki/Nippon_Professional_Baseball" title="Nippon Professional Baseball">Nippon Professional Baseball</a> (NPB) <a href="/wiki/Manager_(baseball)" title="Manager (baseball)">manager</a>.
</p><p>Bessho spent his first five <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Season_(sport)" title="Season (sport)">seasons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_League" title="Japanese Baseball League">Japanese Baseball League</a> (the predecessor of NPB) with the <a href="/wiki/Fukuoka_SoftBank_Hawks" title="Fukuoka SoftBank Hawks">Nankai franchise</a> (1942–43, 1946–48) and his final 12 seasons with the <a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Giants" title="Yomiuri Giants">Yomiuri Giants</a> (1949–1960).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yshp_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-yshp-2">[2]</a></sup>  He quickly established himself as a top pitcher and went on to earn two <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sawamura_Award" title="Sawamura Award">Sawamura Awards</a>, the Japanese equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Cy_Young_Award" title="Cy Young Award">Cy Young Award</a>, and six <a href="/wiki/Best_Nine_Award" title="Best Nine Award">Best Nine Awards</a>. In 1947, Bessho set the NPB record for most <a href="/wiki/Complete_game" title="Complete game">complete games</a> in a single season (47).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guru_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-guru-3">[3]</a></sup>  In addition, Bessho earned the NPB <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="Most Valuable Player Award">Most Valuable Player Award</a> in 1952 and 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robs_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-robs-1">[1]</a></sup>  Bessho <a href="/wiki/Retirement" title="Retirement">retired</a> after the 1960 season with 310 wins, a 2.18 <a href="/wiki/Earned_run_average" title="Earned run average">earned run average</a>, and 1,932 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strikeouts" title="Strikeouts">strikeouts</a>.
</p><p>After he retired from the sport as a player and a manager, Bessho became a sports <a href="/wiki/Broadcasting" title="Broadcasting">broadcaster</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robs_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-robs-1">[1]</a></sup>  In recognition of his accomplishments, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame">Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame</a> elected Bessho in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-robs_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-robs-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raised solely by his mother, Besho grew up in a in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kobe,_Hy%C5%8Dgo" title="Kobe, Hyōgo">Kobe, Hyōgo</a>, Japan<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-daily-4">[4]</a></sup> and went by the name Akira Bessho. Bessho started his athletic career by playing <i>nanshiki</i> (rubber baseball) while he was in the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_grade" title="Fifth grade">fifth grade</a> in elementary school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-daily-4">[4]</a></sup>  By the time he started his high school career at Takikawa Junior High School, Bessho had transitioned from nanshiki to baseball.
</p><p>In 1940 and 1941, Bessho appeared in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_High_School_Baseball_Championship" title="Japanese High School Baseball Championship">Koshien</a> tournament.  During the 1941 spring edition, he pitched all fourteen innings of a game despite his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broken_arm" title="Broken arm">broken arm</a>; unfortunately, he also lost the decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-daily-4">[4]</a></sup>  After his graduation, Bessho took the entrance exam for <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> but ultimately enrolled in vocational classes at <a href="/wiki/Nihon_University" title="Nihon University">Nihon University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-daily-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_career">Professional career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Professional career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nankai">Nankai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nankai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1942, Besho joined the Nankai <a href="/wiki/Professional_sports_league_organization" title="Professional sports league organization">franchise</a>, an Osaka-based team in the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_League" title="Japanese Baseball League">Japanese Baseball League</a> (JBL).  Bessho quickly established himself as a quality player, both as a pitcher and a hitter. In fact, he was so good at batting that the manager had him play in the field (either at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_base" title="First base">first base</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Outfield" title="Outfield">outfield</a>) when he didn't pitch. On May 26, 1943, Bessho pitched a <a href="/wiki/No-hitter" title="No-hitter">no-hitter</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Yamato_Baseball_Club" title="Yamato Baseball Club">Yamato</a> team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-daily-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In December of that year, the Japanese army <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscripted</a> Bessho and sent him to <a href="/wiki/Manchuria" title="Manchuria">Manchuria</a> because of World War II.  However, in 1944, Bessho moved to the officer's school in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matsudo,_Chiba" title="Matsudo, Chiba">Matsudo, Chiba</a> before the army finally moved him to the <a href="/wiki/K%C5%8Dchi_Prefecture" title="Kōchi Prefecture">Kōchi Prefecture</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-daily-4">[4]</a></sup> After the war had ended, Bessho rejoined Nankai in 1946.
</p><p>In 1947 he won and completed 47 games, still a Nippon Pro Baseball record, of which he was proud for many years.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Yomiuri_Giants">Yomiuri Giants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Yomiuri Giants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the late part of 1948 he moved to the <a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Giants" title="Yomiuri Giants">Yomiuri Giants</a>. This became a big scandal in Japanese sports journalism, known as the <i>Bessho head-hunting Incident</i> (ja: <a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E5%88%A5%E6%89%80%E5%BC%95%E3%81%8D%E6%8A%9C%E3%81%8D%E4%BA%8B%E4%BB%B6" title="ja:別所引き抜き事件">別所引き抜き事件</a>) which led the league to put sanctions on him which prohibit him playing for two months. For the Yomiuri Giants, he was still a star.
</p><p>In 1956, the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Dodgers" title="Los Angeles Dodgers">Los Angeles Dodgers</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Major_League_Baseball" title="Major League Baseball">Major League Baseball</a> franchise, decided to play a series of games in Japan.  On October 23, 1956, the Dodgers faced the Yomiuri Giants at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maruyama_Stadium" title="Maruyama Stadium">Maruyama Stadium</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sapporo,_Japan" title="Sapporo, Japan">Sapporo</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Carl_Erskine" title="Carl Erskine">Carl Erskine</a> started for the Dodgers, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sho_Horiuchi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sho Horiuchi (page does not exist)">Sho Horiuchi</a> started for the Giants.  In the seventh inning, Bessho replaced Horiuchi and pitched the rest of the game.  Unfortunately, Bessho surrendered a solo-<a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> to <a href="/wiki/Duke_Snider" title="Duke Snider">Duke Snider</a> in the top of the ninth inning; it was the only run of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dodgers_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-dodgers-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960 he was a player and pitching coach for the Yomiuri Giants. At the end of season he retired and remained on the team as coach. His 310 victories were the NPB all-time record when he retired. During his career, Bessho won six Best Nine Awards, more than any other pitcher in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guru_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-guru-3">[3]</a></sup> In addition, he won two MVP awards, led the league in strikeouts from 1950 until 1952, and won 20 or more games eight different seasons. He led the NPB in ivcotires three times and in ERA, strikeouts, and <a href="/wiki/Winning_percentage" title="Winning percentage">winning percentage</a> once each.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guru_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-guru-3">[3]</a></sup> With 72 career <a href="/wiki/Shutouts_in_baseball" title="Shutouts in baseball">shutouts</a>, he ranks fourth all-time in Japanese professional baseball.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-playing_career">Post-playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Post-playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1964 till 1966 he was the pitching coach of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taiy%C5%8D_Whales" title="Taiyō Whales">Taiyō Whales</a>. He managed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sankei_Atoms" title="Sankei Atoms">Sankei Atoms</a> from 1968 until mid-1970.
</p><p>From then he give commentaries at <a href="/wiki/Fuji_TV" title="Fuji TV">Fuji TV</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunka_H%C5%8Ds%C5%8D" title="Bunka Hōsō">Bunka Hōsō</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikkan_Sports" title="Nikkan Sports">Nikkan Sports</a>. As a commentator, he was known for his cheerful loud laughter, grayed hair, and apparent but innocent favoritism toward the <a href="/wiki/Yomiuri_Giants" title="Yomiuri Giants">Yomiuri Giants</a> (he often couldn't remember the names of non-Giants players).
</p><p>In 1979 he was nominated to <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Baseball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame">Japanese Baseball Hall of Fame</a>.
</p><p>In 1992 he surmounted to the presidency of the Yomiuri Giants Old Boy Club, succeeding <a href="/wiki/Tetsuharu_Kawakami" title="Tetsuharu Kawakami">Tetsuharu Kawakami</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Takehiko_Bessho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1999, he died at his home at the age of 76.
</p>
