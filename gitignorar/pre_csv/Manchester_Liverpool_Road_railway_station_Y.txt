<p><b>Liverpool Road</b> is a former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway_station" title="Railway station">railway station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway" title="Liverpool and Manchester Railway">Liverpool and Manchester Railway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, England that opened on 15 September 1830.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The station was the Manchester <a href="/wiki/Train_station#Terminus" title="Train station">terminus</a> of the world's first <a href="/wiki/Inter-city_rail" title="Inter-city rail">inter-city</a> passenger railway in which all services were hauled by timetabled <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a>. It is the world's oldest surviving terminal railway station.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HG_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HG-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The station closed to passenger services on 4 May 1844 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> when the line was extended to join the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_and_Leeds_Railway" title="Manchester and Leeds Railway">Manchester and Leeds Railway</a> at Hunt's Bank. Liverpool Road was superseded by <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Victoria_station" title="Manchester Victoria station">Manchester Victoria station</a> for passenger services. Like its counterpart at <a href="/wiki/Crown_Street_railway_station" title="Crown Street railway station">Liverpool Crown Street</a> the station was converted to a goods yard.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Since Liverpool Road ceased operation, the oldest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway_station" title="Railway station">railway station</a> in use is <a href="/wiki/Broad_Green_railway_station" title="Broad Green railway station">Broad Green railway station</a> in Liverpool which opened on 15 September 1830.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEButt199545_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEButt199545-6">[6]</a></sup> The Liverpool and Manchester Railway opened starting from Liverpool Crown Street, hence the older stations start from Liverpool.
</p><p>The station, a Grade I <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>, is part of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_Science_and_Industry_in_Manchester" title="Museum of Science and Industry in Manchester">Museum of Science and Industry in Manchester</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manchester_Liverpool_Road_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street,_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway,_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="819" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="176" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street%2C_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway%2C_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg/220px-Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street%2C_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway%2C_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street%2C_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway%2C_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg/330px-Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street%2C_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway%2C_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street%2C_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway%2C_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg/440px-Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street%2C_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway%2C_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Entrance_into_Manchester_across_Water_Street,_from_Bury%27s_Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway,_1831_-_artfinder_267571.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1833 view of the bridges over the Irwell and Water Street</div></div></div>
<p>As a consequence of opposition from the proprietors of the <a href="/wiki/Mersey_and_Irwell_Navigation" title="Mersey and Irwell Navigation">Mersey and Irwell Navigation</a>, it had been intended to terminate the railway on the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Salford" title="City of Salford">Salford</a> side of the <a href="/wiki/River_Irwell" title="River Irwell">Irwell</a>. A last minute agreement brought about a change in plan. The river would be spanned by a two arched stone bridge, incorporating a cart road for the use of the Navigation company. The real problem was how to cross Water Street. The levels would not permit an arched bridge to the dimensions demanded by the Manchester Highway Commissioners. The only alternative was a flat span across a distance judged to be too wide. <a href="/wiki/William_Fairbairn" title="William Fairbairn">William Fairbairn</a>, in the quest to perfect fireproof mill flooring, had developed a parabolic 'T' section <a href="/wiki/Girder" title="Girder">girder</a> that could span such a distance. Thus, the Water Street bridge might be said to be the first modern girder bridge.
</p><p>The station itself comprised a slightly curved brick viaduct that terminated in the slope that led up from Water Street to <a href="/wiki/Deansgate" title="Deansgate">Deansgate</a>, alongside Liverpool Road. The viaduct fronted a solid brick warehouse, a construction that owed much to canal warehouses, beyond which was a low-level yard that was excavated into the hillside. There was a wooden transit shed at the viaduct end, on the corner of Liverpool Road and Byrom Street and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turnplate" title="Turnplate">turnplates</a> gave access to coal drops at the end of the yard. Of course, at that time, goods traffic was considered the most important potential source of income, and for this, the terminus was well placed in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Castlefield" title="Castlefield">Castlefield</a> canal and warehousing complex.
</p><p>Provision for the passengers was something of an afterthought. An existing house on the corner of Liverpool Road and Water Street was purchased for the use of the 'Station Agent'. A brick structure was erected next to it, incorporating elaborate door and window surrounds, the whole being finished in <a href="/wiki/Stucco" title="Stucco">stucco</a> rendering, scored to imitate stonework. The most curious feature was the positioning of a sundial over the first class entrance.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Liverpool_Road.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" decoding="async" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Liverpool_Road.jpg/250px-Liverpool_Road.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Liverpool_Road.jpg/375px-Liverpool_Road.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Liverpool_Road.jpg/500px-Liverpool_Road.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Liverpool_Road.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Liverpool Road railway station, Manchester</div></div></div>
<p>The railway only carried <a href="/wiki/First_class_travel" title="First class travel">first</a> and <a href="/wiki/Travel_class#Great_Britain" title="Travel class">second class</a> passengers, and each class had its own booking hall and waiting room. As the station was some distance from the centre of Manchester, most passengers purchased a handwritten ticket from an agent at an inn or hotel. Several routes of horse <a href="/wiki/Bus" title="Bus">omnibuses</a> then conveyed them to the station. A clerk in the booking hall exchanged the ticket for a counterfoil, similar to a modern airline <a href="/wiki/Boarding_pass" title="Boarding pass">boarding pass</a>, and made up a waybill from the ticket information for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Train_guard" title="Train guard">train guard</a>. The train guard thus had a passenger list indicating class of travel and destination, the only check against fraud. 
</p><p>The passengers proceeded upstairs to the waiting room, and licensed <i>"outdoor porters"</i> took charge of their luggage, being paid a set scale of fees. <i>"Indoor porters"</i> took charge of the luggage on the station platform and strapped it to the carriage roofs. The ringing of the station bell then announced that the passengers could go through the door on to the platform and board the train. The bell was also rung as a warning of departure, but the actual signal was given by the guard's trumpet or horn. The original bell is still exhibited in the station building. The planning of railway stations was clearly not so advanced at this time as supporting pillars of the building prevented certain carriage doors from opening fully.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manchester_Liverpool_Road_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The station buildings were extended in 1831 by the construction of a simple two-road carriage shed on top of a range of rooms along Liverpool Road. These were intended as shops but, due to the insalubrious surroundings, were only used as offices. Beyond this range was a gate and a ramp leading to <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaduct</a> level. This was used to load and unload gentlemen's carriages, which were conveyed on flat wagons, a popular form of travel for those who could afford it.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Grand_Junction_Railway" title="Grand Junction Railway">Grand Junction Railway</a>, Britain's first trunk line, was completed between <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birmingham_Curzon_Street_railway_station_(1838-1966)" title="Birmingham Curzon Street railway station (1838-1966)">Curzon Street railway station</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warrington_Bank_Quay_railway_station" title="Warrington Bank Quay railway station">Warrington Bank Quay railway station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warrington" title="Warrington">Warrington</a>, on 4 July 1837.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Through trains began to convey passengers from the station to Birmingham, and a separate booking office and waiting room were provided. From 17 September 1838 there were through carriages to <a href="/wiki/Euston_railway_station" title="Euston railway station">London Euston</a> by some trains after completion of the <a href="/wiki/London_and_Birmingham_Railway" title="London and Birmingham Railway">London and Birmingham Railway</a> in that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This increase in long-distance services resulted in one of the first private railway hotels opening in Liverpool Road. Congestion required the opening of a separate arrival station across Water Street, on the site of a former dyeworks. Further goods warehouses were erected.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goods_yard">Goods yard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manchester_Liverpool_Road_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Goods yard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Manchester_RJD_47.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1916" data-file-width="2861" decoding="async" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Manchester_RJD_47.JPG/350px-Manchester_RJD_47.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Manchester_RJD_47.JPG/525px-Manchester_RJD_47.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Manchester_RJD_47.JPG/700px-Manchester_RJD_47.JPG 2x" width="350"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Manchester_RJD_47.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A 1910 <a href="/wiki/Railway_Clearing_House" title="Railway Clearing House">Railway Clearing House</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Manchester" title="Central Manchester">central Manchester</a> showing Liverpool Road goods station served by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_%26_North_Western_Railway" title="London &amp; North Western Railway">London &amp; North Western Railway</a>. The 1844 line from Ordsall to Victoria is shown in red.</div></div></div>
<p>Passenger services ended with the opening of an extension of the L&amp;M line from <a href="/wiki/Ordsall,_Greater_Manchester" title="Ordsall, Greater Manchester">Ordsall Lane</a> to Hunt's Bank. All passenger services were then transferred to the new Victoria Station from 4 May 1844.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Liverpool Road, which was turned into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goods_yard" title="Goods yard">goods yard</a>, was developed under the ownership of the <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Western_Railway" title="London and North Western Railway">London and North Western Railway</a>. An iron viaduct (an early girder-frame structure) was constructed to provide access to Byrom Street Warehouse; it was soon followed by the Grape Street Warehouse. The goods complex remained in operation <a href="/wiki/Railways_Act_1921" title="Railways Act 1921">post grouping</a> under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Midland_and_Scottish_Railway" title="London Midland and Scottish Railway">London Midland and Scottish Railway</a> from 1923–1948. In addition to LMS <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goods_train" title="Goods train">goods trains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> locomotives worked their goods trains to Liverpool Road from <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> via <a href="/wiki/Warrington" title="Warrington">Warrington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 1948 the site was taken over by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Railways" title="British Railways">British Railways</a> (BR) following the nationalisation of the UK railway system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Closure">Closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manchester_Liverpool_Road_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the station and warehouses were abandoned in 1975 by <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HG_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HG-3">[3]</a></sup> the Grape Street Warehouse and surroundings buildings were purchased by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granada_Television" title="Granada Television">Granada Television</a>. Part of it was used for the <a href="/wiki/Granada_Studios_Tour" title="Granada Studios Tour">Granada Studios Tour</a> theme attraction, and other parts were used for the set of long-running British <a href="/wiki/Soap_opera" title="Soap opera">TV soap</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Coronation_Street" title="Coronation Street">Coronation Street</a></i>.
</p><p>The station building and adjacent historic railway warehouses now form part of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Museum_of_Science_and_Industry_in_Manchester" title="Museum of Science and Industry in Manchester">Museum of Science and Industry in Manchester</a> which is located in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Castlefield_Urban_Heritage_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Castlefield Urban Heritage Park (page does not exist)">Castlefield Urban Heritage Park</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainline_connection">Mainline connection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manchester_Liverpool_Road_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mainline connection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout its history, Liverpool Road station has retained a working rail connection to the operational mainline, allowing heritage trains to be run directly from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manchester_to_Preston_Line" title="Manchester to Preston Line">Manchester to Preston Line</a> into the museum station. However, the planned <a href="/wiki/Ordsall_Chord" title="Ordsall Chord">Ordsall Chord</a> rail link — a Network Rail project to create a new connection between Piccadilly and Victoria stations — will involve the construction of a new bridge over the <a href="/wiki/River_Irwell" title="River Irwell">River Irwell</a> which will sever the 180-year-old link to Liverpool Road station. After this project is completed, it will no longer be possible to run trains into or out of Liverpool Road station. The MOSI management had initially objected to the scheme and an inquiry was set up in 2014 to investigate the potential damage to the historic structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a> reached an agreement with MOSI, in which the museum was to receive an undisclosed sum in compensation, and the objection was dropped.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MEN-27032015_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-MEN-27032015-12">[12]</a></sup> In March 2015, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_McLoughlin" title="Patrick McLoughlin">Patrick McLoughlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Transport" title="Secretary of State for Transport">Secretary of State for Transport</a>, announced that the Ordsall Chord project was to go ahead with an estimated completion date of 2017. It was confirmed that the operational rail link into Liverpool Road station would be severed as a result of this project,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and later in 2015 the main line connection was removed.
</p>
