<p><b>Gideon Gono</b> (born 29 November 1959) was the Governor of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Zimbabwe" title="Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe">Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe</a> (RBZ) from 2003 to 2013<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is the former CEO of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewel_Bank" title="Jewel Bank">Jewel Bank</a>, formerly known as the Commercial Bank of Zimbabwe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Gono became known internationally due to his connection to the <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation_in_Zimbabwe" title="Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe">hyperinflation in Zimbabwe</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Earlier_career_and_education">Earlier career and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Earlier career and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gono started his career as a tea boy at National Breweries in <a href="/wiki/Kwekwe" title="Kwekwe">Que Que</a> in 1977. He put himself through various correspondence courses from O-Level through A-Level and moved on to the Zimbabwe Fertiliser Company as a bookkeeper. After working at Van Leer as an accountant, he was appointed finance manager at the Zimbabwe Development Bank (ZDB) in 1987. When he left the Zimbabwe Development Bank in 1995, he had risen to the post of general manager.
</p><p>He went on to become managing director of the Bank of Credit and Commerce of Zimbabwe (BCCZ). Under his direction BCCZ became the largest and most successful bank in Zimbabwe, The Commercial Bank of Zimbabwe, or as he coined it, 'Jewel Bank'. He was subsequently appointed as governor of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Zimbabwe" title="Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe">Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe</a>.
</p><p>He obtained a master's degree in Business Administration at the University of Zimbabwe and went on to lecture there, and was also appointed to head the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Zimbabwe" title="University of Zimbabwe">University of Zimbabwe</a> Council. He was awarded an honorary degree at the same institution. He then went on to earn another PhD in Strategic Management in 2007 from <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_International_University" title="Atlantic International University">Atlantic International University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reserve_Bank_governorship">Reserve Bank governorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reserve Bank governorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gono was first appointed as governor of the Reserve Bank in November 2003 because of his reputation as a turnaround specialist, particularly because of his work at the Commercial Bank of Zimbabwe. In November 2008, Gono was reappointed to a new five-year term as governor, beginning on 1 December 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Gono stepped down on 30 November 2013 after a 10-year term at the helm of the Reserve Bank.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Results_of_policies">Results of policies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Results of policies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After taking over the governorship of the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Zimbabwe" title="Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe">Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe</a>, Gono implemented a host of highly criticized policies in a bid to try to keep the Zimbabwe afloat. There were a host of problems before he became governor, hence the appointment of turnaround strategies but they seemed to be too much for him to tackle, hence the decline of the economy continued:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li>Cash shortages</li>
<li>There were fuel and shortages in agriculture<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVNZ_837163_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TVNZ_837163-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li>Corrupt businessmen were arrested and banks not adhering to banking laws were penalized<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li>The highest inflation in the world and unemployment and the collapse of the health, education and agriculture sectors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zimbabwe_Dollar_and_inflation">Zimbabwe Dollar and inflation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Zimbabwe Dollar and inflation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hyperinflation_in_Zimbabwe" title="Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe">Hyperinflation in Zimbabwe</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Zimbabwe" title="Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe">RBZ</a> printed large quantities of money to keep the economy afloat against the backdrop of economic sanctions placed upon the Zimbabwe since 2000. This went against the advice of global economists, but with full support from President Robert Mugabe. As predicted by the textbook <a href="/wiki/Quantity_theory_of_money" title="Quantity theory of money">quantity theory of money</a>, this practice devalued the <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwean_dollar" title="Zimbabwean dollar">Zimbabwean dollar</a> and caused hyperinflation.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Zimbabwe" title="Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe">RBZ</a> demonetized old bank notes on 1 August 2006 and introduced a new currency. Each new Zimbabwe dollar was worth 1000 old Zimbabwe dollars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The highest denominations for the new currency were 1, 10, and 100 thousand revalued dollars. A year later on 1 August 2007, he authorized a 200 thousand dollar denomination. This marked the start of a series of new denominations issued in rapid succession, including 250, 500, and 750 thousand dollars (20 December 2007); 1, 5, and 10 million dollars (16 January 2008); 25 and 50 million dollars (4 April 2008); 100 and 250 million dollars (5 May 2008); 500 million and 5, 25, and 50 billion dollars (20 May 2008); and 100 billion dollars (21 July 2008). From the time of currency revaluation to the beginning of June 2008 the money supply in the country increased from  billion to more than  quadrillion, or a 20,000,000 fold increase.
</p><p>Gono denied media claims that he had opposed price cuts that the government instituted to arrest inflation. As time went by, it became apparent that the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Bank_of_Zimbabwe" title="Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe">RBZ</a> had instituted price cuts that saw bare shelves in shops and many businesses closing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> He sent in the police to arrest businessmen for failing to reduce their prices. On one occasion, he personally visited shop owners in <a href="/wiki/Harare" title="Harare">Harare</a> to demand they lower prices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Despite these efforts, inflation in Zimbabwe remains the world's highest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> (Note: after <a href="/wiki/Currency_substitution" title="Currency substitution">dollarization</a> in 2009, Zimbabwe's inflation rate has become deflation since 2014. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>)
</p><p>Critics have noted that most of Gono's monetary policy statements in the past have had biblical references. Notably, he usually ends in policy statements to the Parliament of Zimbabwe thus: "In the Lord's hands, I commit this Monetary Policy Framework for our economic turnaround."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sokwanele_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-sokwanele-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Banks_and_currency_exchange">Banks and currency exchange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Banks and currency exchange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of banks that were skirting banking laws had their operating licenses cancelled and/or were placed under curatorship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Critics blame Gono for closing 16 money transfer agencies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture_sector">Agriculture sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Agriculture sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the shortages of inputs in the agriculture the RBZ came to the forefront to try to bail the sector out by helping with fertilizer and machinery procurement.
</p><p>Backed by vice president <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joyce_Mujuru" title="Joyce Mujuru">Joyce Mujuru</a>, Gono has several times called for an end to the farm invasions sanctioned by the ZANU-PF party, as these destroy the Zimbabwean economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Gono's criticism of farm takeovers is in sharp contrast to statements made by other ministers, including Lands Minister <a href="/wiki/Didymus_Mutasa" title="Didymus Mutasa">Didymus Mutasa</a>.
</p><p>In an interview with the state-controlled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Herald_(Zimbabwe)" title="The Herald (Zimbabwe)">The Herald</a></i> newspaper, he said, "I have openly condemned such retrogressive acts as destruction of <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticultural</a> greenhouses, decimation of <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a> barns, institution of fresh farm invasions".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
"There are too many subdivisions among us, too many contradictions, too much infighting among ourselves, incredible suspicion and mistrust of one another," Gono said.
</p><p>Gono admitted that his efforts to rescue and improve the economy of Zimbabwe were being thwarted and risked failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Gono said there were several factors that were outside the central bank's control, which made it difficult to reign in inflation.
</p><p>"Some of those factors are within the governor's control and influence while others such as politics, <a href="/wiki/International_sanctions" title="International sanctions">sanctions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a>, under-utilisation of farms, disruptions at those farms, rampant <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">corruption</a>, indiscipline, law and order are factors outside the governor's control," he said in an interview with <i>The Herald</i> newspaper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> He has also blamed the failure of the economy on sanctions imposed on the country, a charge critics dispute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics allege that Gono has kept his job as the governor mainly through Mugabe's patronage saying Mugabe has not only shielded Gono from critics, but has commended him in his activities as governor despite the extreme deterioration of the Zimbabwean economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> He had been Mugabe's banker for a long period before becoming governor of the RBZ.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many of Mugabe's inner circle, Gono also owns a farm (in <a href="/wiki/Norton,_Zimbabwe" title="Norton, Zimbabwe">Norton</a>). However, unlike others, he is said to have bought and paid for it in the late 1990s before the land redistribution program in which other farms were confiscated from localised white farmers.
</p><p>In November 2008, Gono published a book titled <i>Zimbabwe's Casino Economy: Extra-ordinary Measures for Extra-ordinary Challenges</i> describing the post-colonial economy of Zimbabwe particularly during the first five years (2003–2008) of his term as Governor of the Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe (RBZ).
</p><p>On 24 October 2010, the South-African newspaper <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(South_Africa)" title="The Sunday Times (South Africa)">Sunday Times</a></i> ran a story falsely claiming that he had an affair with <a href="/wiki/Grace_Mugabe" title="Grace Mugabe">Amai Grace Mugabe</a>, the wife of President Robert Mugabe. It was later reported that the story could have been made up as a part of internal fighting inside the ruling party or the RBZ.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_sanctions">Personal sanctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gideon_Gono&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal sanctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gono is banned from travelling to the United States and EU member states because of his position in the government of Zimbabwe. He was added to the EU's list of individuals subject to personal sanctions—a ban on travel to the EU and the freezing of any assets there—in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
