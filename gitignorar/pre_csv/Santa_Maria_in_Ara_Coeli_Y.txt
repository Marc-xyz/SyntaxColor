<p>The <b>Basilica of St. Mary of the Altar of Heaven</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Basilica Sanctae Mariae de Ara coeli in Capitolium</i>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Basilica di Santa Maria in Ara coeli al Campidoglio</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Titular_church" title="Titular church">titular</a> <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">basilica</a> in <a href="/wiki/Churches_of_Rome" title="Churches of Rome">Rome</a>, located on the highest summit of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Campidoglio" title="Campidoglio">Campidoglio</a>. It is still the designated Church of the city council of Rome, which uses the ancient title of <i><a href="/wiki/SPQR" title="SPQR">Senatus Populusque Romanus</a></i>. The present <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Priest" title="Cardinal Priest">Cardinal Priest</a> of the <i>Titulus Sanctae Mariae de Aracoeli</i> is <a href="/wiki/Salvatore_De_Giorgi" title="Salvatore De Giorgi">Salvatore De Giorgi</a>.
</p>

<p>The shrine is known for housing relics belonging to <a href="/wiki/Helena_(empress)" title="Helena (empress)">Saint Helena</a>, mother of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Constantine" title="Emperor Constantine">Emperor Constantine</a>, various minor relics from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Sepulchre" title="Holy Sepulchre">Holy Sepulchre</a>, both the canonically crowned images of <i>Nostra Signora di Mano di Oro di Aracoeli</i> (1636) on the high altar and the <a href="/wiki/Santo_Bambino_of_Aracoeli" title="Santo Bambino of Aracoeli">Santo Bambino of Aracoeli</a> (1897).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_in_Ara_Coeli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Originally the church was named <i>Sancta Maria in Capitolio</i>, since it was sited on the <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Hill" title="Capitoline Hill">Capitoline Hill</a> (Campidoglio, in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>) of Ancient Rome; by the 14th century it had been renamed. A medieval legend included in the mid-12th-century guide to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mirabilia_Urbis_Romae" title="Mirabilia Urbis Romae">Mirabilia Urbis Romae</a></i>, claimed that the church was built over an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar_Augustus" title="Caesar Augustus">Augustan</a> <i>Ara primogeniti Dei</i>, in the place where the <a href="/wiki/Tiburtine_Sibyl" title="Tiburtine Sibyl">Tiburtine Sibyl</a> prophesied to Augustus the coming of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">Christ</a>. "For this reason the figures of Augustus and of the Tiburtine sibyl are painted on either side of the arch above the high altar" (Lanciani chapter 1). A later legend<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> substituted an <a href="/wiki/Marian_apparition" title="Marian apparition">apparition of the Virgin Mary</a>.  In the Middle Ages, condemned criminals were executed at the foot of the steps; there the self-proclaimed Tribune and reviver of the Roman Republic <a href="/wiki/Cola_di_Rienzo" title="Cola di Rienzo">Cola di Rienzo</a> met his death, near the spot where his statue commemorates him.
</p><p>In <i>The History of Money</i>, anthropologist Jack Weatherford goes into some detail about the church's previous incarnation as the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Juno_Moneta" title="Temple of Juno Moneta">temple of Juno Moneta</a>—on the <a href="/wiki/Arx_(Roman)" title="Arx (Roman)">Arx</a>—after whom <a href="/wiki/Money" title="Money">Money</a> is named.
</p>
<blockquote><p>According to Roman historians, in the fourth century B.C., the irritated honking of the sacred geese around Juno's temple on Capitoline Hill warned the people of an impending night attack by the Gauls, who were secretly scaling the walls of the citadel. From this event, the goddess acquired [the] surname-Juno Moneta, from Latin monere (to warn). . .As patroness of the state, Juno Moneta presided over various activities of the state, including the primary activity of issuing money.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>. . . from Moneta came the modem English words mint and money and, ultimately, from the Latin word meaning warning.</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>Today, the site of the Temple of Juno Moneta, the source of the great stream of Roman currency, has given way to the ancient . . . brick church of Santa Maria in Aracoeli. Centuries ago, church architects incorporated the ruins of the ancient temple into the new building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The church is also thought to have replaced the <i><a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#auguraculum" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">auguraculum</a></i>, the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Augur" title="Augur">augurs</a>.
</p><p>The foundation of the church was laid on the site of a <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> abbey mentioned in 574. Many buildings were built around the first church; in the upper part they gave rise to a cloister, while on the slopes of the hill a little quarter and a market grew up. Remains of these buildings - such as the little church of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Biagio_de_Mercato&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Biagio de Mercato (page does not exist)">San Biagio de Mercato</a> and the underlying "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insula_Romana_(Capitoline_Hill)" title="Insula Romana (Capitoline Hill)">Insula Romana</a>") - were discovered in the 1930s.
At first the church followed the Greek rite, a sign of the power of the Byzantine exarch. Taken over by the papacy by the 9th century, the church was given first to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">Benedictines</a>, then, by papal bull to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscans</a> in 1249–1250; under the Franciscans it received its <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a>-<a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> aspect. The arches that divide the <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> from the aisles are supported on columns, no two precisely alike, scavenged from Roman ruins. 
</p><p>During the Middle Ages, this church became the centre of the religious and civil life of the city. in particular during the republican experience of the 14th century, when <a href="/wiki/Cola_di_Rienzo" title="Cola di Rienzo">Cola di Rienzo</a> inaugurated the <b>monumental stairway of 124 steps</b> in front of the church, designed in 1348 by Simone Andreozzi, on the occasion of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>.
</p>

<p>In 1571, Santa Maria in Aracoeli hosted the celebrations honoring <a href="/wiki/Marcantonio_Colonna" title="Marcantonio Colonna">Marcantonio Colonna</a> after the victorious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lepanto_(1571)" title="Battle of Lepanto (1571)">Battle of Lepanto</a> over the Turkish fleet. Marking this occasion, the compartmented ceiling was gilded and painted (finished 1575), to thank the Blessed Virgin for the victory. In 1797, with the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic_(18th_century)" title="Roman Republic (18th century)">Roman Republic</a>, the basilica was deconsecrated and turned into a stable.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interior">Interior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_in_Ara_Coeli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Interior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original unfinished façade has lost the mosaics and subsequent frescoes that originally decorated it, save a mosaic in the <a href="/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)" title="Tympanum (architecture)">tympanum</a> of the main door, one of three doors that are later additions. The Gothic window is the main detail that tourists can see from the bottom of the stairs, but it is the sole truly Gothic detail of the church.
</p><p>The church is built as a nave and two aisles that are divided by Roman columns, all different, taken from diverse antique monuments. Among its numerous treasures are <a href="/wiki/Pinturicchio" title="Pinturicchio">Pinturicchio</a>'s 15th-century frescoes depicting the life of Saint <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_of_Siena" title="Bernardino of Siena">Bernardino of Siena</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bufalini_Chapel" title="Bufalini Chapel">Bufalini Chapel</a>, the first chapel on the right. Other features are the wooden ceiling, the inlaid <a href="/wiki/Cosmatesque" title="Cosmatesque">cosmatesque</a> floor, a <i>Transfiguration</i>  painted on wood by <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Siciolante_da_Sermoneta" title="Girolamo Siciolante da Sermoneta">Girolamo Siciolante da Sermoneta</a>, the tombstone of Giovanni Crivelli by <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">Donatello</a>, the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Cecchino_dei_Bracci" title="Cecchino dei Bracci">Cecchino dei Bracci</a>, designed by his friend <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> (who also wrote love <a href="/wiki/Sonnet" title="Sonnet">sonnets</a> in his dedication), and works by other  artists like <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Cavallini" title="Pietro Cavallini">Pietro Cavallini</a> (of his frescoes only one survives), <a href="/wiki/Benozzo_Gozzoli" title="Benozzo Gozzoli">Benozzo Gozzoli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Romano" title="Giulio Romano">Giulio Romano</a>. It houses also <i>Madonna Aracoeli</i> (Our Lady of the Golden Hands), (<a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantian</a> icon, the 10-11th-century) in the <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">Altar</a>. This Marian image was Pontifically <a href="/wiki/Canonical_coronation" title="Canonical coronation">crowned</a>  on 29 March 1636 by <a href="/wiki/Pope_Urban_VIII" title="Pope Urban VIII">Pope Urban VIII</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> <a href="/wiki/Consecration_and_entrustment_to_Mary" title="Consecration and entrustment to Mary">consecrated</a> the people of Rome to the <a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">Most Blessed Virgin Mary</a> and her <a href="/wiki/Immaculate_Heart_of_Mary" title="Immaculate Heart of Mary">Immaculate Heart</a> in front of this image in 1948, May 30. In the <a href="/wiki/Transept" title="Transept">transept</a> there is a sepulchral monument by <a href="/wiki/Arnolfo_di_Cambio" title="Arnolfo di Cambio">Arnolfo di Cambio</a>. 
The church was also famous in Rome for the wooden statue of the <a href="/wiki/Santo_Bambino_of_Aracoeli" title="Santo Bambino of Aracoeli">Santo Bambino of Aracoeli</a>, carved in the 15th century of olive wood coming from the <a href="/wiki/Gethsemane" title="Gethsemane">Gethsemane</a> garden and covered with valuable <i><a href="/wiki/Ex-voto" title="Ex-voto">ex-votos</a></i>. Many people of Rome believed in the power of this statue. It had been taken by the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> in 1797, was later recovered, yet stolen again in February 1994. A copy was made from <a href="/wiki/Gethsemane" title="Gethsemane">Gethsemane</a> wood,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which is now displayed in its own chapel by the sacristy. At midnight Mass on <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a> the image is brought out to a throne before the high altar and unveiled at the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloria_in_Excelsis_Deo" title="Gloria in Excelsis Deo">Gloria</a></i>. Until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epiphany_(Christian)" title="Epiphany (Christian)">Epiphany</a> the jewel-encrusted image resides in the Nativity crib in the left nave.
</p><p>The relics of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helena_of_Constantinople" title="Helena of Constantinople">Saint Helena</a>, mother of <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">Constantine the Great</a> are housed at Santa Maria in Aracoeli, as are the remains of <a href="/wiki/Juniper_(friar)" title="Juniper (friar)">Saint Juniper</a>, one of the original followers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Francis_of_Assisi" title="Saint Francis of Assisi">Saint Francis of Assisi</a>. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Honorius_IV" title="Pope Honorius IV">Pope Honorius IV</a> and Queen <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Bosnia" title="Catherine of Bosnia">Catherine of Bosnia</a> are also buried in the church. The tablet with the <a href="/wiki/Christogram" title="Christogram">monogram of Jesus</a> that Saint <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_of_Siena" title="Bernardino of Siena">Bernardino of Siena</a> used to promote <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Holy_Name_of_Jesus" title="Feast of the Holy Name of Jesus">devotion to the Holy Name of Jesus</a> is kept in Aracoeli.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Curiosities">Curiosities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Santa_Maria_in_Ara_Coeli&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Curiosities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The church also contains the marble tomb of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cecchino_Bracci" title="Cecchino Bracci">Cecchino Bracci</a>, pupil and lover of artist <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">Michelangelo</a> who had dedicated a number of poems in his name. The tomb's design (not the carving) is by Michelangelo.</li>
<li>A part of the last mission of the videogame <a href="/wiki/Assassin%27s_Creed:_Brotherhood" title="Assassin's Creed: Brotherhood">Assassin's Creed: Brotherhood</a> takes place in this basilica.</li>
<li>In this church, football player <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Totti" title="Francesco Totti">Francesco Totti</a> and Ilary Blasi celebrated their marriage in 2005, followed by thousands of fans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>It was this church where Edward Gibbon was struck with the idea to write his “Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire”.  "It was at Rome, on the 15th of October 1764," he wrote in his "Autobiography", "as I sat musing amid the ruins of the capitol, while the bare-footed friars were singing vespers in the temple of Jupiter [Gibbon was mistaken; this church was actually the former Temple of Juno Moneta], that the idea of writing the decline and fall of the city first started to my mind.” <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li></ul>
