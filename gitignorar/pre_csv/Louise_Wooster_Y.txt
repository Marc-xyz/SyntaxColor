<p><b>Louise Catharine Wooster</b> (1842–1913),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup> better known as <b>Lou Wooster</b>, was a famous <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pimp" title="Pimp">madam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> Her colorful character and her care for the sick and dying during the <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> epidemic of 1873 endeared her to the Birmingham community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> The shocking true story of the Birmingham Madam The "Lou Wooster Public Health Award" is named in her honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-al2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louise_Wooster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Louise Wooster was born in 1842 in <a href="/wiki/Tuscaloosa,_Alabama" title="Tuscaloosa, Alabama">Tuscaloosa, Alabama</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritage_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-heritage-5">[5]</a></sup> to William Wooster and Mary Chism Wooster.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-al2-3">[3]</a></sup>  Her father died in 1851 and Mary Wooster remarried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> A few years later, Lou's stepfather abandoned the family and took their money with him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> Mary Wooster died a few years later virtually destitute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>By her middle teens, Lou was an orphan with nothing to rely on but the mercy of relatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup> During this time, she was abused, attempted suicide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup> and her older sister became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prostitute" title="Prostitute">prostitute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritage_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-heritage-5">[5]</a></sup> She later wrote that she "fell, step by step, until at last I was beyond redemption".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1873, Lou was a well paid lady of the evening when a deadly cholera epidemic swept through Birmingham. Several thousand people fled the city, but Lou stayed to nurse the sick,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-al2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup> feed the hungry, and prepare the dead for funerals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> After the epidemic, few of Lou's clients remained in Birmingham and she moved to <a href="/wiki/Montgomery,_Alabama" title="Montgomery, Alabama">Montgomery, Alabama</a> to open a brothel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1880, she had returned to Birmingham operating multiple brothels<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heritage_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-heritage-5">[5]</a></sup> near City Hall where she could attract the wealthiest patrons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> Lou made a fortune, donated heavily to charities<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup> and frequently came to the aid of fallen women.
</p><p>Lou was a master at storytelling and self-promotion. She wrote a book chronicling her life titled <i>Autobiography of a Magdalene</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louise_Wooster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lou died in 1913 and was buried in Birmingham's <a href="/wiki/Oak_Hill_Cemetery_(Birmingham,_Alabama)" title="Oak Hill Cemetery (Birmingham, Alabama)">Oak Hill Cemetery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al2_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-al2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_recognition">Honors and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Louise_Wooster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honors and recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lou Wooster Public Health Award is presented annually by the University of Alabama School of Public Health to recognize individuals, groups, or organizations who are unconventional public health heroes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup> The award is named in honor of Lou Wooster,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al2_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-al2-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup> the 19th-century Birmingham madam who risked her own death by staying in the city to care for the sick and dying during the 1873 cholera epidemic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup>  Lou Wooster organized the women in her brothel "determined to stay and help nurse the poor sick and suffering ones who needed me."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup>  Her courage went a long way toward assuring there was a Birmingham for the leadership to come back to.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup>  When she died in May 1913, hundreds of empty black carriages drove by the funeral home to pay respects to a local hero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pioneers_1-18"><a href="#cite_note-pioneers-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-al_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-al-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup> Recipients are driven by a horse-drawn carriage from the School of Public Health to Oak Hill Cemetery where Lou Wooster is buried.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first award was presented in 2007 Representative Patricia Todd, MPA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uab_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-uab-6">[6]</a></sup> who is also known as the first openly gay State Representative in Alabama.  In 2010 the award was presented to American Electric Power<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uab_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-uab-6">[6]</a></sup> for their efforts to work with environmental groups and other stakeholders to reduce pollution from power production.  Master Chef Frant Stitt<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uab_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-uab-6">[6]</a></sup> was recognized in 2011 for his work in improving nutrition by working on urban farms and creating new menus for University Hospital.  In 2012 the VF Corporation<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vulcan_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-vulcan-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uab_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-uab-6">[6]</a></sup> was recognized for its decision to rebuild the Hackleburg, Alabama Wrangler Distribution Center destroyed by the April 27, 2011 tornado.  Rebuilding this facility will have profound impacts on the long term health of the community.
</p>
