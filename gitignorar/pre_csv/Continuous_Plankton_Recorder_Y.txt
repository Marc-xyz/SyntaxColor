	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Continuous Plankton Recorder</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Continuous Plankton Recorder</b> (<b>CPR</b>) Survey is one of the longest running <a href="/wiki/Marine_biology" title="Marine biology">marine biological</a> monitoring programmes in the world. Started in 1931 by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Alister_Hardy" title="Sir Alister Hardy">Sir Alister Hardy</a> and Sir <a href="/wiki/Cyril_Lucas" title="Cyril Lucas">Cyril Lucas</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> the Survey has provided marine scientists with their only measure of <a href="/wiki/Plankton" title="Plankton">plankton</a> communities on a pan-oceanic scale. Today the CPR Survey is operated by the <a class="external text" href="http://www.mba.ac.uk" rel="nofollow">Marine Biological Association</a>(MBA), located in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>, UK. Uniquely, the CPR Survey’s methods of sampling and plankton analysis remain unchanged since 1948, providing a spatio-temporally comprehensive &gt; 70 year record of marine plankton dynamics.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sampling_and_analysis">Sampling and analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Continuous_Plankton_Recorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sampling and analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The CPR is a plankton sampling instrument designed to be towed from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merchant_ships" title="Merchant ships">merchant ships</a>, or ships of opportunity, on their normal sailings. As of December 31, 2007, CPRs have been towed a total of 5,460,042 nmi (10,111,998 km) by 278 ships since the survey’s inception. Recorders have been towed in all oceans of the world, the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">Baltic</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Seas</a> and in freshwater lakes. However, MBA CPR sampling primarily focuses on the northwest European shelf and the Northeast and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Atlantic" title="Northwest Atlantic">Northwest Atlantic</a>, with these regions undergoing monthly sampling; regular sampling is also now carried out in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Pacific" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a>. Additionally, sister CPR surveys, not conducted by the MBA but using similar methodology, are operated from the United States, Australia, India, Cyprus, Brazil, South Africa, France and Japan, as part of the Global Alliance of Continuous Plankton Recorder Surveys (www.globalcpr.org)
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sahfos_cpr.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="567" decoding="async" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Sahfos_cpr.gif/400px-Sahfos_cpr.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Sahfos_cpr.gif 1.5x" width="400"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sahfos_cpr.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram showing a cutaway view of the CPR, the plankton filtering mechanism, and a photograph of the instrument</div></div></div>
<p>The CPR is towed at a depth of approximately 10 metres. Water passes through the CPR and plankton are filtered onto a slow-moving band of <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">silk</a> (270 micrometre mesh size) and covered by a second silk. The silks and plankton are then spooled into a storage tank containing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formalin" title="Formalin">formalin</a>. On return to the laboratory, the silk is removed from the mechanism and divided into samples representing 10 nautical miles (19 km) of tow.
</p><p>CPR samples are analyzed in two ways. Firstly, the Phytoplankton Colour Index (PCI) is determined for each sample. The colour of the silk is evaluated against a standard colour chart and given a 'green-ness' value based on the visual discoloration of the CPR silk produced by green <a href="/wiki/Chlorophyll" title="Chlorophyll">chlorophyll</a> pigments; the PCI is a semi-quantitative estimate of phytoplankton biomass. In this way the PCI takes into account the chloroplasts of broken cells and small <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> which cannot be counted during the microscopic analysis stage. After determination of the PCI, microscopic analysis is undertaken for each sample, and individual <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zooplankton" title="Zooplankton">zooplankton</a> taxa are identified and counted. Nearly 500 phyto- and zooplankton taxa have been identified on CPR samples since 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecological_research">Ecological research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Continuous_Plankton_Recorder&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecological research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to its long time-series, comprehensive spatial coverage and methodological consistency, the Continuous Plankton Recorder Survey is a unique ecological dataset which has provided invaluable insights into numerous aspects of plankton dynamics and ecology. Key areas of research include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">Eutrophication</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Harmful_algal_bloom" title="Harmful algal bloom">Harmful Algal Blooms</a> (HABs)</li>
<li>Fisheries investigations</li>
<li>Plankton ecology</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology)" title="Taxonomy (biology)">Taxonomy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Regime_shift" title="Regime shift">Regime shifts</a></li>
<li>Non-indigenous species</li></ul>
<p>Their research results show that warmer water species of plankton are moving northwards towards the colder North Atlantic at a rate of about 23 km (14 mi) per year and that some plankton species have moved 1,000 km (620 mi) north over the course of 50 years due to regional climate warming. But they are not replacing the cold water species in similar abundance. The open oceans are not yet subject to eutrophication or changes due to <a href="/wiki/Ocean_acidification" title="Ocean acidification">acidification</a> though the number of <a href="/wiki/Microplastics" title="Microplastics">microplastics</a> collected on CPR samples is increasing. It has detected a new species in the North Atlantic which may be the first evidence of Trans-Arctic species migration in modern times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
