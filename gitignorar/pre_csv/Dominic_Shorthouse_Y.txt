	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dominic Shorthouse</h1>

<p><b>Dominic Shorthouse</b> is a British private investor, founder of <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> business Englefield Capital LLP and a former partner at Warburg Pincus. His notable investments include the creation of the UK terrestrial TV channel <a href="/wiki/Channel_5_(UK)" title="Channel 5 (UK)">Channel 5</a> and Cognita, a UK-based private schools operator. He is a member of the advisory board of ManoCap,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> which invests in post-conflict African states, and a member of the Advisory Board of Lennox Investment Management, investing in prime Central London residential property.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominic_Shorthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shorthouse graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> with a master's degree in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_literature" title="Greek literature">Greek literature</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, and holds an MBA from the <a href="/wiki/Stanford_Graduate_School_of_Business" title="Stanford Graduate School of Business">Stanford Graduate School of Business</a>. Prior to Stanford, Shorthouse worked in the investment-banking group at <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Stanley" title="Morgan Stanley">Morgan Stanley</a> and in 1987 interned at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thoma_Cressey_Bravo" title="Thoma Cressey Bravo">Golder, Thoma &amp; Cressey</a> in Chicago.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Warburg_Pincus">Warburg Pincus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominic_Shorthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Warburg Pincus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before founding Englefield, Shorthouse was a partner at global private equity firm <a href="/wiki/Warburg_Pincus" title="Warburg Pincus">Warburg Pincus</a>.  From the mid-1990s, he served as co-head of Warburg's London office and made several notable deals, including a founding investment in Channel 5, the sale of mortgage broker <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_Charcol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John Charcol (page does not exist)">John Charcol</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bradford_and_Bingley" title="Bradford and Bingley">Bradford and Bingley</a> for £100m <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and deals in Aegis, London Weekend Television and Esprit Telecom.
</p><p>Shorthouse was a non-executive director of several Warburg portfolio companies. Overall, he was involved in the investment of over $2 billion in the UK and Europe, in sectors such as financial services, manufacturing, real estate, sport, and technology, media and telecommunications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Channel_5">Channel 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominic_Shorthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Channel 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While at Warburg, Shorthouse participated in the winning bid to create <a href="/wiki/Channel_5_(UK)" title="Channel 5 (UK)">Channel 5</a>, the final <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_television" title="Terrestrial television">terrestrial television</a> network to launch on British TV. Shorthouse worked on the bid alongside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Hollick" title="Lord Hollick">Lord Hollick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Dyke" title="Greg Dyke">Greg Dyke</a> and Ferd Kayser.
</p><p>Channel 5's licence to broadcast was awarded under a controversial auction process conducted by the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Television_Commission" title="Independent Television Commission">Independent Television Commission</a> (ITC). The winning bid vehicle, named Channel 5 Broadcasting Ltd, offered £22,002,000 for the licence, exactly the same amount as a rival bid from <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Media" title="Virgin Media">Virgin Television</a>. The ITC decision was later contested in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court</a> by Virgin and other rival bidders. Appellants claimed that the ITC had been "...guilty of procedural impropriety", particularly that the ITC had unfairly allowed Channel 5 Broadcasting Ltd to increase its programme funding by £100m four months after the deadline for bids had closed, and that the ITC had "...irrationally failed" Virgin's application on programme quality grounds. The High Court dismissed all claims and denied Virgin further leave to appeal.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1999 Warburg Pincus sold its 18% shareholding to Channel 5’s three other shareholders, Pearson, CLT-ufa and United News &amp; Media, for a reported £180m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Englefield_Capital">Englefield Capital</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominic_Shorthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Englefield Capital">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002 Shorthouse founded Englefield Capital, a European mid-market private equity house. Englefield’s initial partner was <a href="/wiki/Etienne_de_Villiers" title="Etienne de Villiers">Etienne de Villiers</a>, a former president at Walt Disney Television International.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The firm's inaugural fund closed at €706 million, including a €650 million commitment from Bregal Investments LP, an investment vehicle owned by the <a href="/wiki/Brenninkmeijer_family" title="Brenninkmeijer family">Brenninkmeijer family</a>, owners of the <a href="/wiki/C%26A" title="C&amp;A">C&amp;A</a> retail chain. The balance came from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AXA" title="AXA">AXA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Shell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Lloyd_Group" title="Delta Lloyd Group">Delta Lloyd Group</a> and around 150 individual investors. Englefield’s advisory board included former head of NATO <a href="/wiki/George_Robertson,_Baron_Robertson_of_Port_Ellen" title="George Robertson, Baron Robertson of Port Ellen">Lord Robertson</a>, former CEO of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_plc" title="Rolls-Royce plc">Rolls-Royce</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Rose_(businessman)" title="John Rose (businessman)">Sir John Rose</a>,  CEO of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reed_Elsevier" title="Reed Elsevier">Reed Elsevier</a> <a href="/wiki/Crispin_Davis" title="Crispin Davis">Sir Crispin Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Myners,_Baron_Myners" title="Paul Myners, Baron Myners">Paul Myners</a>, former UK Treasury Minister.
</p><p>Under Shorthouse, Englefield's investments included the creation of international schools business <a href="/wiki/Cognita" title="Cognita">Cognita</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the purchase of insurance company Canopius and an investment in renewable energy company Zephyr.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Following the investment in Zephyr, Englefield set up a separate €200m fund raised from Bregal for renewable energy investments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009 the Brenninkmeijer family negotiated with Shorthouse to acquire Englefield's investments for its private equity subsidiary, Bregal Investments. Shorthouse retained the rights to the Englefield name and continues as a private investor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominic_Shorthouse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shorthouse works with a number of charities. He serves on the board of the Amber Foundation, a charity which seeks to reduce the impact of unemployment among the 17-30 age group; he is also a trustee of the <a href="/wiki/Anna_Freud" title="Anna Freud">Anna Freud</a> Centre, a research charity dealing with child and adolescent mental health issues; and a trustee of Tommy’s, which funds medical research into miscarriage, stillbirth and premature birth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  He is also a member of the advisory board of Lennox Investment Management.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Debrett%27s" title="Debrett's">Debrett's</a> <i>People of Today</i> lists Shorthouse’s interests as golf, skiing, reading and <a href="/wiki/Equitation" title="Equitation">equitation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> He is a managing partner in an organic farm in Tillac, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
