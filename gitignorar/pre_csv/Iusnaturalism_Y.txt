	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Iusnaturalism</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="839" decoding="async" height="238" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg/200px-Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg/300px-Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg/400px-Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Michiel_Jansz_van_Mierevelt_-_Hugo_Grotius.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">jurist</a> and scholar, is an iusnaturalist author, who maintained that rights are innate <i>potestas</i> of the human being and do not arise out of law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><b>Iusnaturalism</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Jurisprudence" title="Jurisprudence">theory of law</a>, which holds that legal norms follow a human universal knowledge on justice and harmony of relations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Thus, it views enacted laws that contradict such universal knowledge as unjust and illegitimate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Modern theorists considered as iusnaturalists include <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">Immanuel Kant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz" title="Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz">Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz</a>, and Franz von Zeiller, among others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>This school of thought became popular during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a> amid the attempt to identify "the relation, if any, of the <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_gentium" title="Jus gentium">ius gentium</a></i> to natural law in the <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotelian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Thomism" title="Thomism">Thomistic</a> sense."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This issue had covered the role of God and the adequacy of reason without divine grace, among others particularly in the field of legal theory as it established the notion that natural law could exist without religion. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iusnaturalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iusnaturalism is associated with the notion of <a href="/wiki/Natural_law" title="Natural law">natural law</a> proposed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hobbes" title="Thomas Hobbes">Thomas Hobbes</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Locke" title="John Locke">John Locke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_von_Pufendorf" title="Samuel von Pufendorf">Samuel von Pufendorf</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> It emerged from the view that emphasizes how the ideas of nature and divinity or reason are the sources of the validity of natural and positive laws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> It is also linked to the general theory of law, which has been viewed as one that has positivist character, aiming to be objective and non-normative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> For iusnaturalists, in order for a law to be accorded the status of legal or one that has a binding effect on people, it must be fair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>  
</p><p>Some scholars distinguish iusnaturalism and legal positivism but it is noted that both are concerned with the <i>good</i> law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Iusnaturalism subordinates power to law as well as positive law to higher laws, giving it a more meaningful primordial metanarrative of natural law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> One of the fundamental notions of iusnaturalism is that man is free and no one has power over other men or moral power over another without a mutual act of will.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> Failure to understand or recognize this would lead to conflict. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Iusnaturalism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Iusnaturalism has different variations and these are based on religious and rational perspectives. Two of the most important are classical iusnaturalism and rationalist iusnaturalism. Classical iusnaturalism is associated with theorists such as <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Saint Thomas Aquinas</a>, who maintained in his <i><a href="/wiki/Treatise_on_Law" title="Treatise on Law">Treatise on Law</a></i> that the universal laws of justice have a divine origin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup>   
</p><p>Rationalist or realist iusnaturalism emerged during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries as a consequence of the process of secularization, which affected the relationship between <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> and international law.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Thinkers such as Grotius, Hobbes, and Kant maintain that the shared laws of justice are not revealed by God to humans but can be accessed by reason through an understanding of human nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> This type of iusnaturalism integrates natural law and positive law as species of the same genus since they share one principle of common validity - political power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Moderate iusnaturalism is also another variant, which emphasizes how natural law exists together with positive law within a legal dualism that rejects all <a href="/wiki/Monism" title="Monism">monisms</a> as well as the so-called extreme iusnaturalism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> The extreme type holds that "natural law exists and all that exists is natural law".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-:1-5">[5]</a></sup> It also recognizes that morality is one of the traits for characterizing what law is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
