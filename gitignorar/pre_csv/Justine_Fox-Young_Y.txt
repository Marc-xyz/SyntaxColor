	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Justine Fox-Young</h1>

<p><b>Justine Fox-Young</b> (born 1979) was a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> member of the <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico_House_of_Representatives" title="New Mexico House of Representatives">New Mexico House of Representatives</a>, serving District 30.  She was first elected in 2004, and was re-elected in 2006. Fox-Young was defeated in 2008 by <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> newcomer Karen Giannini.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislative_career">Legislative career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Justine_Fox-Young&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legislative career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004, Fox-Young won a tough primary against incumbent State Representative Bob White to become the youngest sitting member and the youngest woman ever elected to the New Mexico Legislature.  At the time, Fox-Young was the office manager for attorney <a href="/wiki/Mickey_D._Barnett" title="Mickey D. Barnett">Mickey D. Barnett</a>, a former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republican_National_Committeeman" title="Republican National Committeeman">Republican National Committeeman</a> from New Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vic_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-vic-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Fox-Young initially earned a reputation as a <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_Republican" title="Rockefeller Republican">moderate Republican</a>, her vote on a controversial bill approving <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domestic_partnerships" title="Domestic partnerships">domestic partnerships</a> caused some to view her as ideologically <a href="/wiki/Modern_liberalism_in_the_United_States" title="Modern liberalism in the United States">liberal</a>. She was one of only two New Mexico House Republicans to vote for the measure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vic_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-vic-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the 2008 primary election, in which she ran uncontested, Fox-Young claimed to have proof of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voter_fraud" title="Voter fraud">voter fraud</a> in the Democrat <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primary races</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
Soon after, voters whose registrations Fox-Young claimed were fraudulent began to challenge her allegations. The government watchdog group <a href="/wiki/Common_Cause" title="Common Cause">Common Cause</a> said her claims were "simply inaccurate." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vic_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-vic-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> sued Fox-Young for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Invasion_of_privacy" title="Invasion of privacy">invasion of privacy</a>, conspiracy and <a href="/wiki/Negligence" title="Negligence">negligence</a>.  All the charges against her were later dismissed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> 
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Fox-Young was defeated by Giannini for the House seat. The defeat came as a surprise to many, since the seat was viewed as a "safe" Republican seat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vic_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-vic-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
