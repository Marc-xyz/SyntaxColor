<p>The <b>WikiLeaks Party</b> was a micro political party in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theaustralian_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-theaustralian-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WP-5">[5]</a></sup> The party was created in part to support <a href="/wiki/Julian_Assange" title="Julian Assange">Julian Assange</a>'s failed bid for a Senate seat in Australia in the <a href="/wiki/2013_Australian_federal_election" title="2013 Australian federal election">2013 election</a>, where they won 0.66% of the national vote.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-WP-5">[5]</a></sup> The WikiLeaks Party national council consisted of Assange, Matt Watt, Gail Malone, Assange’s biological father John Shipton, Omar Todd and <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Georgatos" title="Gerry Georgatos">Gerry Georgatos</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Assange's decision to run for the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Australian Senate</a> was announced via the <a href="/wiki/WikiLeaks" title="WikiLeaks">WikiLeaks</a> <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account in March 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-7">[7]</a></sup> The intent to form a WikiLeaks Party was announced by Assange in late 2012<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-WP-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TA_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-TA-8">[8]</a></sup> and Assange stated that the party was to be a vehicle for his candidacy for a seat in the Australian Senate in the 2013 election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-WP-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 23 March 2013, the WikiLeaks Party submitted its registrations to the Australian Electoral Commission. The party had over 1,300 fee-paying members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The application was accepted and the party was registered as a political party on 2 July 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Registration_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Registration-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party was involved in <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Druery" title="Glenn Druery">Glenn Druery</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Minor_Party_Alliance" title="Minor Party Alliance">Minor Party Alliance</a> around the <a href="/wiki/2013_Australian_federal_election" title="2013 Australian federal election">2013 federal election</a>, but left after deciding not to preference as per Druery's advice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Julian_Assange">Julian Assange</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Julian Assange">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Julian_Assange" title="Julian Assange">Julian Assange</a></div>
<p>Assange is a native of Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-WP-5">[5]</a></sup> From July 2012 until his eventual eviction and arrest in April 2019, Assange lived in the <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_Ecuador,_London" title="Embassy of Ecuador, London">Embassy of Ecuador, London</a>, having been granted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_asylum" title="Political asylum">political asylum</a> by Ecuador in an attempt to avoid arrest by UK authorities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-15">[15]</a></sup> Assange was unable to leave the Embassy without being arrested by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_Forces_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Police Forces of the United Kingdom">Police Forces of the United Kingdom</a> acting on an <a href="/wiki/Extradition" title="Extradition">extradition</a> order placed on him to travel to <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> to answer <a href="/wiki/Assange_v_Swedish_Prosecution_Authority" title="Assange v Swedish Prosecution Authority">allegations of rape and sexual molestation</a> of two Swedish women. Assange fought the extradition order in the UK Court system from December 2010, but subsequently both the UK <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justice" title="High Court of Justice">High Court of Justice</a> and the UK <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court</a> ruled that the extradition order had been lawfully made and duly dismissed Assange's request for an appeal against the extradition warrant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Assange_Extradition_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Assange_Extradition-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_Supreme_Court_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK_Supreme_Court-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_Statesman_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_Statesman-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_election">2013 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2013 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The party fielded candidates for the Australian Senate in the states of New South Wales, Victoria and Western Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Two polling experts rated the WikiLeaks Party's electoral chances as highly unlikely.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Christine_Milne" title="Christine Milne">Christine Milne</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Australian Greens</a>, was positive about the emergence of the WikiLeaks Party as part of a move away from Australia's two-party system. However, the Greens said they had no intention of stepping aside for Assange in the Victoria Senate election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Equality_Party_(Australia)" title="Socialist Equality Party (Australia)">Socialist Equality Party</a> reaffirmed its intention to defend Assange against persecution, but refused to endorse the WikiLeaks Party, stating that this position represents the "interests of the working class".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>Professor Anne Twomey, an expert on <a href="/wiki/Australian_constitutional_law" title="Australian constitutional law">Australian constitutional law</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sydney" title="University of Sydney">University of Sydney</a>, suggested that if Assange were elected, this could be found invalid in the event of a legal challenge if a court ruled that his relationship with Ecuador breached the prohibition against the election of people "under any [acknowledgement] of allegiance, obedience or adherence to a foreign power".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Twomey_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Twomey-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>The party's campaign was thrown into turmoil just weeks before the election when members objected strongly to the party's voting preferences (see <a href="/wiki/Single_transferable_vote" title="Single transferable vote">single transferable vote</a>)<a class="external autonumber" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160912190956/http://newpolitics.com.au/wikileaks-a-leaky-boat" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, a fascist group was placed above the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Greens" title="Australian Greens">Greens</a> while in Western Australia the <a href="/wiki/National_Party_of_Australia" title="National Party of Australia">National Party</a> was placed above Greens Senator <a href="/wiki/Scott_Ludlam" title="Scott Ludlam">Scott Ludlam</a>, a strong supporter of WikiLeaks and Assange. The WikiLeaks Party blamed an unspecified "admin error" and announced an independent review would be held after the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> When National Council members complained, CEO John Shipton attempted to subvert them and create a new power base. <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Cannold" title="Leslie Cannold">Leslie Cannold</a>, Assange's running mate in Victoria, resigned along with many volunteers and members of the National <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20130908212303/http://cannold.com/articles/article/leslie-resignation-statement-of-fact-on-candidacy-re-the-wikileaks-party/" rel="nofollow">Council</a>.
</p><p>The party published a short, inconclusive review by a party member five months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Former member Gary Lord responded with a comprehensive 20-page report fully examining the party's failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Assange failed in his bid for a Senate seat. It is difficult to separate out his personal vote under the single transferable vote system. The party received 33,683 votes in Victoria from electors who voted the WikiLeaks ticket with Assange at its head and Assange received an additional 8,016 first preference votes from electors who numbered the candidates individually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> The party as a whole received 1.24% (the 7th highest primary vote in Victoria) and reached the 26th round of ballot before being eliminated without the opportunity to receive preference flows. The party received 88,100 votes or 0.66% nationally, but only contested seats in three states. <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Georgatos" title="Gerry Georgatos">Gerry Georgatos</a> came closest to winning a Senate seat for the WikiLeaks Party, reaching the 19th round with only seven rounds to go before being eliminated, also before any opportunity to receive preference flows. He fell about 3,000 primary votes short of being elected, but given that the party received only 9,767 primary votes in Western Australia, this was a large gap.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AEC_2013_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-AEC_2013-28">[28]</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Party_platform">Party platform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Party platform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The WikiLeaks Party subscribes to a libertarian ideology. Specific policies for the 2013 election included introduction of a national shield law to protect a reporter's right not to reveal a source<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and "promoting free information and protection for whistle-blowers".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>CEO John Shipton stated that the party "stands for what Julian espouses — transparency and <a href="/wiki/Accountability" title="Accountability">accountability</a> in government and of course human rights".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NA_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-NA-41">[41]</a></sup> Assange himself has said the WikiLeaks Party would combine "a small, centralised leadership with maximum grassroots involvement" and that the party would advance WikiLeaks' objectives of promoting openness in government and politics and that it would combat intrusions on individual privacy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WP_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-WP-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TA_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-TA-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TC_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-TC-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SC_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-SC-43">[43]</a></sup> The Voice of Russia stated that Shipton in an interview "praised Russian diplomatic skills and Russian President Vladimir Putin and Foreign Minister Sergey Lavrov. Shipton and the WikiLeaks Party believe that the Russian President and Foreign Ministry are forces for peace".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Assange has been reported as saying that he envisions the WikiLeaks Party as bound together by unswerving commitment to the core principles of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Civic_courage" title="Civic courage">civic courage</a> nourished by understanding and truthfulness and the free flow of information and one that will practise in politics what WikiLeaks has done in the field of information.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TC2_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-TC2-45">[45]</a></sup> The Constitution of the WikiLeaks Party lists objectives, including the protection of human rights and freedoms; transparency of governmental and <a href="/wiki/Corporate_action" title="Corporate action">corporate action</a>, policy and information; recognition of the need for <a href="/wiki/Social_inequality" title="Social inequality">equality</a> between generations; and support of <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">Aboriginal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Torres_Strait_Islander" title="Torres Strait Islander">Torres Strait Islander</a> <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TC2_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-TC2-45">[45]</a></sup> The WikiLeaks Party has criticised the Telstra Group's relationship with the FBI and US Department of Justice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPR_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPR-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syria_visit">Syria visit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Syria visit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In December 2013, a delegation from the party, including its chairman John Shipton, visited Syria and met with President <a href="/wiki/Bashar_al-Assad" title="Bashar al-Assad">Bashar al-Assad</a> with the goals of demonstrating "solidarity with the Syrian people and their nation" and improving the party's understanding of the country's civil war. In a statement issued shortly before the visit, the WikiLeaks Party stated that it opposed outside intervention in the war, supported a negotiated peace process and described reports of the <a href="/wiki/Ghouta_chemical_attack" title="Ghouta chemical attack">Ghouta chemical attack</a> by forces loyal to al-Assad in August 2013 as being "unsubstantiated" and comparable to the concerns which were raised over the Iraqi weapons of mass destruction program prior to the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The meeting with President al-Assad was attended by National Council members John Shipton, Gail Malone and by former National Council member Jamal Daoud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theaustralian_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-theaustralian-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The meeting with Assad was criticized by the Australian Prime Minister, Foreign Minister and many WikiLeaks supporters. Shipton stated that the meeting with al-Assad was "just a matter of good manners" and that the delegation had also met with members of the Syrian opposition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> However, these meetings with the opposition have not been verified. Former National Council member and advocate for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shias" title="Shias">Shias</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Jamal Daoud (resigned from the Greens over differences), who accompanied Shipton on the trip, expressed support for Assad on Twitter and on his blog.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2014_election">2014 election</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2014 election">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WikiLeaks Party contested the 5 April re-run of the disqualified 2013 Senate election (Western Australia component). The prior year's lead Senate Candidate Gerry Georgatos recommended to the National Council that he step down for Assange to take the lead Senate position for Western Australia and hopefully get elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> In February, the National Council learned that Assange would be ineligible to contest. Georgatos rejected continuing on as the endorsed candidate and asked that the membership be surveyed as to their preferred candidate. More than 500 WikiLeaks members completed the survey and Georgatos was endorsed as the lead candidate with West TV producer Tibor Meszaros at number 2 and journalist Lucy Nicol at number 3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> One hour before the close of nominations, Georgatos withdrew for "unforeseen personal reasons" and Tibor Meszaros was consequently elevated to lead candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> On 14 April, the AEC draw for the ballot of 33 parties (77 candidates) drew the WikiLeaks Party first.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deregistration">Deregistration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WikiLeaks_Party&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Deregistration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The WikiLeaks Party was deregistered by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Electoral_Commission" title="Australian Electoral Commission">Australian Electoral Commission</a> on 23 July 2015 under s.137(4) of the <i><a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Electoral_Act_1918" title="Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918">Electoral Act</a></i>, for the party's failure to respond to a notice under s.137(1).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dereg_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-dereg-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
