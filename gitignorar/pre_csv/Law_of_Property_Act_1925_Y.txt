<p>The <b>Law of Property Act 1925</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Geo5/15-16/20/contents" rel="nofollow">c 20</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Parliament" title="United Kingdom Parliament">United Kingdom Parliament</a>. It forms part of an interrelated programme of legislation introduced by <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/F._E._Smith,_1st_Earl_of_Birkenhead" title="F. E. Smith, 1st Earl of Birkenhead">Lord Birkenhead</a> between 1922 and 1925. The programme was intended to modernise the English law of <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a>. The Act deals principally with the transfer of <a href="/wiki/Freehold_(law)" title="Freehold (law)">freehold</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leasehold" title="Leasehold">leasehold</a> land by <a href="/wiki/Deed" title="Deed">deed</a>.
</p><p>The LPA 1925, as amended, provides the core of <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">English</a> land law, particularly as regards many aspects of <a href="/wiki/Freehold_(law)" title="Freehold (law)">freehold</a> land which is itself an important consideration in all other types of interest in land.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_Property_Act_1925&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The keynote policy of the act was to reduce the number of <a href="/wiki/Estate_in_land" title="Estate in land">legal estates</a> to two - freehold and leasehold - and generally to make the transfer of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interest_in_land" title="Interest in land">interests in land</a> easier for purchasers. Other policies were to regulate mortgages and as to leases, to regulate mainly their assignment, and to tackle some of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacuna_(law)" title="Lacuna (law)">lacunae</a></i>, ambiguities and shortcomings in the law of property. Innovations included the default creation of <a href="/wiki/Easements_in_English_law" title="Easements in English law">easements</a> under section 62 to reduce unintended denial of access, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leasehold_enfranchisement" title="Leasehold enfranchisement">statutory enlargement</a> under section 153 (applications to convert very long leasehold to freeholds, where no rent has been paid or demanded for a long period of time). 
</p><p>The Act followed a series of land law and policy reforms that had been begun by the <a href="/wiki/1906_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1906 United Kingdom general election">Liberal government</a> starting in 1906. This is how one American legal scholar, <a href="/wiki/Morris_Raphael_Cohen" title="Morris Raphael Cohen">Morris Raphael Cohen</a>, described it:
</p>
<blockquote><p>That which was hidden from <a href="/wiki/Frederic_William_Maitland" title="Frederic William Maitland">Maitland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Williams" title="Joshua Williams">Joshua Williams</a>, and the other great ones, was revealed to a <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">Welsh solicitor</a> who in the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Budget" title="People's Budget">budget of 1910</a> proposed to <a href="/wiki/Land_value_tax" title="Land value tax">tax the land</a> so as to force it on the market. The radically revolutionary character of this proposal was at once recognized in England. It was bitterly fought by all those who treasured what had remained of the old English aristocratic rule. When this budget finally passed, the basis of the old real property law and the effective power of the House of Lords was gone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The legislation of 1925–26 was thus a final completion in the realm of <a href="/wiki/Private_law" title="Private law">private law</a> of the revolution that was fought in 1910 in the forum of <a href="/wiki/Public_law" title="Public law">public law</a>, i.e., in the field of taxation and the power of the House of Lords.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p></blockquote>

<h3><span id="Part_II_.E2.80.93_Contracts.2C_Conveyances_and_other_Instruments"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_II_–_Contracts,_Conveyances_and_other_Instruments">Part II – Contracts, Conveyances and other Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_Property_Act_1925&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Part II – Contracts, Conveyances and other Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Section 70 of the 1925 Act should be considered in conjunction with schedules 1 &amp; 3 when considering interests that override, most notably that to be in receipts of rents and profits is no longer an overriding interest.
</p><p>Section 84 of the Act sets out the powers of an appointed authority to alter or remove <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Restrictive_covenant" title="Restrictive covenant">restrictive covenants</a> on property deeds. This power was later transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Lands_Tribunal_(England,_Wales_and_Northern_Ireland)" title="Lands Tribunal (England, Wales and Northern Ireland)">Lands Tribunal</a> by the Law of Property Act 1969,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and subsequently to the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Tribunal" title="Upper Tribunal">Upper Tribunal</a> by the Transfer of Tribunal Functions (Lands Tribunal and Miscellaneous Amendments) Order 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="Part_IX_.E2.80.93_Voidable_Dispositions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_IX_–_Voidable_Dispositions">Part IX – Voidable Dispositions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_Property_Act_1925&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Part IX – Voidable Dispositions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These sections govern fraud; s.172 was repealed (subject to non-application to then-pending bankruptcies) by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Insolvency_Act_1985&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Insolvency Act 1985 (page does not exist)">Insolvency Act 1985</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>s.173 Every voluntary disposition of land (the main example is a gift) made with intent to defraud a later buyer is voidable at the instance of that buyer. And no such disposition shall be deemed to have been made with intent to defraud by reason only that a subsequent conveyance for valuable consideration was made, if such subsequent conveyance was made after 28 June 1893.</li>
<li>s.174 No acquisition made in good faith, without fraud or unfair dealing, of any reversionary (landlord's or future) interest in real or personal property, for money or money’s worth, shall be liable to be opened or set aside merely on the ground of under value. In this subsection "reversionary interest" includes an expectancy or possibility. This expressly does not affect the jurisdiction of the court to set aside or modify unconscionable bargains.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Part_X_.E2.80.93_Wills_.26_Probate"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_X_–_Wills_&amp;_Probate">Part X – Wills &amp; Probate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_Property_Act_1925&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Part X – Wills &amp; Probate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h3><span id="Part_XI_.E2.80.93_Miscellaneous"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_XI_–_Miscellaneous">Part XI – Miscellaneous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Law_of_Property_Act_1925&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Part XI – Miscellaneous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Section 184 states that in cases of <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_death" title="Simultaneous death">simultaneous death</a> (where there is no evidence as to who lived longer), the deaths will be assumed to have occurred in order of age, oldest first.</li></ul>
