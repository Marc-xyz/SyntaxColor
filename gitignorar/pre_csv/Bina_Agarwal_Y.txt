<p><b>Bina Agarwal</b> is an Indian <a href="/wiki/Development_economics" title="Development economics">development economist</a> and Professor of Development Economics and Environment at the Global Development Institute at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_University_of_Manchester" title="The University of Manchester">The University of Manchester</a>. She has written extensively on land, livelihoods and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Property_rights" title="Property rights">property rights</a>; environment and development; the <a href="/wiki/Political_economy" title="Political economy">political economy</a> of <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>; <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poverty</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economic_inequality" title="Economic inequality">inequality</a>; legal change; and agriculture and technological transformation. Among her best known works is the award-winning book—<i>A Field of One's Own: Gender and Land Rights in South Asia</i>—which has had a significant impact on governments, NGOs, and international agencies in promoting <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_rights" title="Women's rights">women's rights</a> in land and property.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This work has also inspired research in Latin America and globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Contributors_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Contributors-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bina_Agarwal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agarwal's parents were Suraj Mal and Shyama Devi Agarwal, Agarwal named a book prize in their honour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She earned her B.A. and M.A. from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>, and her doctorate in Economics from the <a href="/wiki/Delhi_School_of_Economics" title="Delhi School of Economics">Delhi School of Economics</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Delhi" title="University of Delhi">University of Delhi</a>, her dissertation was <i>Mechanization in Indian Agriculture: An Analytical Study Based on the Punjab</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bina_Agarwal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Her university positions include posts at Princeton, Harvard, Michigan, Minnesota, and New York University. At Harvard she was the first Daniel Ingalls Visiting Professor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bina_Agarwal_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bina_Agarwal-6">[6]</a></sup> Agarwal has also been President of the International Society for Ecological Economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Vice-President of the International Economic Association,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bina_Agarwal_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bina_Agarwal-6">[6]</a></sup> President of the International Association for Feminist Economics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> on the Board of the Global Development Network, and one of the twenty-one members of the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_the_Measurement_of_Economic_Performance_and_Social_Progress" title="Commission on the Measurement of Economic Performance and Social Progress">Commission on the Measurement of Economic Performance and Social Progress</a>, chaired by Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> She has served on the UN Committee for Development Policy (New York) and UNRISD (Geneva). She holds honorary doctorates from the Institute of Social Studies in the Netherlands and the University of Antwerp in Belgium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bina_Agarwal_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bina_Agarwal-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concepts_and_areas_of_focus">Concepts and areas of focus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bina_Agarwal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Concepts and areas of focus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Agarwal's expertise is on subjects related to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rural_economy" title="Rural economy">rural economy</a>. She has creatively used diverse methodologies (from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Econometric_analysis" title="Econometric analysis">econometric analysis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_research" title="Qualitative research">qualitative assessments</a>) and an interdisciplinary approach, to provide insights on land, livelihoods and property rights; environment and development; the political economy of gender; poverty and inequality; law; and agriculture and technological change. She deals especially with the connectedness of <a href="/wiki/Gender_inequality" title="Gender inequality">gender inequality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_exclusion" title="Social exclusion">social exclusion</a>, property, and development. Her pioneering work has had an impact globally both within the academia and among policy makers and practitioners. A large part of her work compares countries, especially within South Asia. In <i>A Field of One's Own</i> (Cambridge University Press, 1994), her most famous work, Agarwal stresses that "the single most important factor affecting women's situation is the gender gap in command over property."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFOOO_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-AFOOO-10">[10]</a></sup> She is also on the editorial board of the <i><a href="/wiki/Journal_of_Women,_Politics_%26_Policy" title="Journal of Women, Politics &amp; Policy">Journal of Women, Politics &amp; Policy</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ed_board_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ed_board-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Spurred on by Agarwal's work, and the successful movement she led in 2004–2005, Indian policy makers passed the Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act in 2005. This Act gives all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> women (married and unmarried) equal rights with men in the ownership and inheritance of property, in particular agricultural land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lincei_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lincei-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Agarwal has consistently challenged standard economic analysis and assumptions. In her writings on the "bargaining approach" to intra-family relations, she challenges unitary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Household_model" title="Household model">household models</a> and extends formal bargaining models to highlight the importance of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_norms" title="Social norms">social norms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_perception" title="Social perception">social perceptions</a> and property ownership in determining women's bargaining power. She also demonstrates the interconnectedness of the family, the community, the market and the state in determining a person's bargaining power in any one sphere. Her paper "Bargaining and Gender Relations" is the single most downloaded paper to date in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Feminist_Economics_(journal)" title="Feminist Economics (journal)">Feminist Economics</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In another article "Bargaining and Legal Change", Agarwal examines how women in India were able to bargain with the State to pass the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_Succession_Act,_1956" title="Hindu Succession Act, 1956">inheritance laws of 1956</a> and bring about its amendment in 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In another important extension of her work on gender, property and power, Agarwal demonstrates in her empirically rigours article "Towards Freedom from Domestic Violence", that women's ability to own and inherit land acts as a significant deterrent against <a href="/wiki/Domestic_violence" title="Domestic violence">marital violence</a>. Her recent books include: <i>Psychology, Rationality and Economic Behaviour</i> (coedited; <a href="/wiki/Palgrave_Macmillan" title="Palgrave Macmillan">Palgrave</a>, 2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <i>Capabilities, Freedom and Equality</i> (co-edited, <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">Oxford University Press</a>, Delhi, 2006).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Her most recently authored book is <i>Gender and Green Governance</i> (Oxford University Press, Oxford and Delhi, 2010) which has been widely cited and favourably reviewed in both <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">academic journals</a> and the popular press (EPW and Indian Express).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Positions_and_awards">Positions and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bina_Agarwal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Positions and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bina Agarwal has held distinguished positions at many international universities, including <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> (she was the first Daniel Ingalls Visiting Professor), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">University of Michigan</a> (Ann Arbor), the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">University of Minnesota</a> (where she held the Winton Chair), and the <a href="/wiki/New_York_University_School_of_Law" title="New York University School of Law">New York University School of Law</a>. In 2006–07, Agarwal was also a Visiting Research Fellow at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kennedy_School_of_Government" title="Kennedy School of Government">Kennedy School of Government</a> of Harvard University.  In addition, she has been Vice-President of the International Economic Association, President of the <a href="/wiki/International_Association_for_Feminist_Economics" title="International Association for Feminist Economics">International Association for Feminist Economics</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAFFE_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-IAFFE-18">[18]</a></sup> and on the board of the <a href="/wiki/Global_Development_Network" title="Global Development Network">Global Development Network</a>. Agarwal is a founding member of the Indian Society for <a href="/wiki/Ecological_economics" title="Ecological economics">Ecological economics</a>. She is one of only two women who served on the Commission for the Measurement of Economic Performance and Social Progress, chaired by Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a> and set up by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sarkozy" title="Nicolas Sarkozy">President Sarkozy</a>. She has also been consultant to the <a href="/wiki/Planning_Commission_(India)" title="Planning Commission (India)">Planning Commission of India</a> and is on the editorial boards of several international academic journals.
</p><p>In 2009 Agarwal was nominated to the board of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Research_Institute_for_Social_Development" title="United Nations Research Institute for Social Development">United Nations Research Institute for Social Development</a> (UNRISD) – such nominations are approved by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">United Nations Economic and Social Council</a> (ECOSOC). On 29 March 2010 the <a href="/wiki/Global_Development_and_Environment_Institute" title="Global Development and Environment Institute">Global Development and Environment Institute</a> (GDAE) awarded her the 2010 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wassily_Leontief_Prize" title="Wassily Leontief Prize">Leontief Prize</a> – an annual award named after Nobel Laureate <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Leontief" title="Wassily Leontief">Wassily Leontief</a>.  GDAE Co-Director Neva Goodwin wrote: "Bina Agarwal embodies the kind of theoretically rigorous, empirically grounded, and policy-oriented economics that the Leontief Prize was created to recognize," and "Her contributions to both scholarship and policy on economic development, the environment, well-being, and gender have been an inspiration to GDAE for many years." She is the currently president-elect of the <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Ecological_Economics" title="International Society for Ecological Economics">International Society for Ecological Economics</a>. She also heads a "Working Group on Disadvantaged Farmers, including Women" for India's 12th Five Year Plan, and is on the Indian Prime Minister's Panel on Land Reform. Additionally, Agarwal is on the advisory board for <a href="/wiki/Academics_Stand_Against_Poverty" title="Academics Stand Against Poverty">Academics Stand Against Poverty</a> (ASAP).
</p><p>In 2017, she received the <a href="/wiki/Balzan_Prize" title="Balzan Prize">Balzan Prize</a> for Gender Studies<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> in recognition of her work in studying women's contribution to agriculture in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
