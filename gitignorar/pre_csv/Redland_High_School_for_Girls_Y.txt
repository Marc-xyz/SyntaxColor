<p><b>Redland High School for Girls</b> was a selective and independent, non-denominational girls' school in the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Redland,_Bristol" title="Redland, Bristol">Redland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, England. The school merged with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Red_Maids%27_School" title="The Red Maids' School">The Red Maids' School</a> in September 2017, with the new merged school named <a href="/wiki/Redmaids%27_High_School" title="Redmaids' High School">Redmaids' High School</a> and based at the Red Maids' site in <a href="/wiki/Westbury-on-Trym" title="Westbury-on-Trym">Westbury-on-Trym</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Admissions">Admissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redland_High_School_for_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Admissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:114px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Redland_High_School_crest.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="113" data-file-width="112" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/e1/Redland_High_School_crest.png" width="112"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Redland_High_School_crest.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Redland High School crest</div></div></div>
<p>Redland High School admitted girls aged 3–18 years and also boys aged 3–6 from 2014–17
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redland_High_School_for_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Redland High School  was founded in 1882. The senior school was housed in an old manor-house known as Redland Court which dates from 1732-35. It was built by John Strachan for <a href="/wiki/John_Cossins" title="John Cossins">John Cossins</a> and was designated by <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> as a grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which underwent many extensions. In October 2006, a building previously belonging to the Junior School was converted into the Music School, expanding the senior school once again. In 2014, they opened an Early Years Foundation Stage Building in conjunction with nearby boys school, QEH.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Site">Site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redland_High_School_for_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The school was spread across five sites: the Senior school, on Redland Court Road; the Music School, also on Redland Court Road; the Junior School, opposite the Senior school; the Sixth form house, on Woodstock Road; and the PE field at <a href="/wiki/Golden_Hill,_Bristol" title="Golden Hill, Bristol">Golden Hill</a>.
</p><p>Following the closure of the school, the Senior School site, along with the Sixth Form House / Bursary was sold, partially demolished and turned into housing<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> with the Junior School site in Grove Park turned into a day nursery<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and the Music School converted into a wellness centre and medical clinic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Houses">Houses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redland_High_School_for_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Houses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The house system was in place from 2003, and there were four houses. These were Maple (red), Chestnut (yellow), Willow (green) and Rowan  (blue). House captains were elected by each house respectively from Year 13. There were also two House Vice-captains from Year 12 and two sports captains from Year 10. In addition, a member of staff served as Head of House on a permanent, unelected basis.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniform">Uniform</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redland_High_School_for_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Uniform">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Redland senior school uniform consisted of a dark green blazer, a tartan skirt, a navy jumper, and a white shirt. For games there was a red shirt and a green skirt. Sixth formers wore their own clothes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_former_students">Notable former students</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Redland_High_School_for_Girls&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Notable former students">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Beryl_Corner" title="Beryl Corner">Beryl Corner</a> (9 December 1910 – 4 March 2007) was the first paediatrician in the south-west of England and one of the British founders of neonatology, the care of newborn babies; she was also the last survivor of a group of women paediatricians whose achievements helped to break down barriers to the advancement of women in medicine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sara_Wheeler" title="Sara Wheeler">Sara Wheeler</a> (20 March 1961–) is a travel writer. After being a pupil at Redland High she went on to study Classics and Modern Languages at Brasenose College, University of Oxford. After writing about her travels on the Greek island of Euboea and in Chile, she was accepted by the US National Science Foundation as their first female writer-in-residence at the South Pole and spent seven months in Antarctica.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nazneen_Rahman" title="Nazneen Rahman">Nazneen Rahman</a> leads research directed at identifying, characterising, and clinically implementing genes that predispose to cancer. She was awarded the No.3 spot in BBC Radio 4's <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woman%E2%80%99s_Hour" title="Woman’s Hour">Woman’s Hour</a></i> 2014 Power List in recognition of her work.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tanya_Beckett" title="Tanya Beckett">Tanya Louise Beckett</a> (20 July 1966–) is an English television and radio journalist.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elisabeth_Hoodless" title="Elisabeth Hoodless">Dame Elisabeth Anne Marian Frost Hoodless</a> (11 February 1941–) was the Executive Director (1975 – 2011) of Community Service Volunteers (CSV), a United Kingdom volunteering and training charity.</li></ul>
