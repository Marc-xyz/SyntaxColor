<p>"<b><span lang="sv" style="font-style:normal;" title="Swedish language text">Du gamla, du fria</span></b>" (<small>Swedish pronunciation:¬†</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Swedish" title="Help:IPA/Swedish">[d âÀê Àá…°amÀêla d âÀê ÀáfriÀêa]</a></span>, English: <span lang="en">"Thou ancient, Thou free"</span>) is the <i>de facto</i> <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>. It was originally named "<b><span lang="sv" style="font-style:normal;" title="Swedish language text">S√•ng till Norden</span></b>" (<small>pronounced¬†</small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Swedish" title="Help:IPA/Swedish">[Ààs…î≈ãÀê t…™l ÀànuÀê…ñ…õn]</a></span>, "Song to the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">North</a>"), but the <a href="/wiki/Incipit" title="Incipit">incipit</a> has since been adopted as the title.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio class="kskin" controls="" data-durationhint="99.704580498866" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons" data-mwtitle="Du_gamla,_du_fria.ogg" data-startoffset="0" id="mwe_player_2" preload="none" style="width:220px"><source data-bandwidth="202760" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-title="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/a/a2/Du_gamla%2C_du_fria.ogg/Du_gamla%2C_du_fria.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg"/><source data-bandwidth="395887" data-height="0" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-title="Original Ogg file (396 kbps)" data-width="0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Du_gamla%2C_du_fria.ogg" type='audio/ogg; codecs="vorbis"'/><track data-dir="ltr" kind="subtitles" label="svenska (sv) subtitles" src="https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/api.php?action=timedtext&amp;title=File%3ADu_gamla%2C_du_fria.ogg&amp;lang=sv&amp;trackformat=srt&amp;origin=%2A" srclang="sv" type="text/x-srt"/></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Du_gamla,_du_fria.ogg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"Du gamla, du fria" (complete choir version).</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="6443" data-file-width="2021" decoding="async" height="542" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg/170px-Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg/255px-Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg/340px-Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Satellite_image_of_Sweden_in_March_2002.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The scenery that inspired the lyrics</div></div></div>
<p>Although the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_constitution" title="Swedish constitution">Swedish constitution</a> makes no mention of a national anthem, "Du gamla, du fria" enjoys universal recognition and is used, for example, at government ceremonies as well as sporting events. It first began to win recognition as a patriotic song in the 1890s, and the issue of its status was debated back and forth up until the 1930s. In 1938, the Swedish public service radio company <a href="/wiki/Sveriges_Radio" title="Sveriges Radio">Sveriges Radio</a> started playing it in the evenings at the end of transmission, which marked the beginning of the <i>de facto</i> status as national anthem the song has had since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the belief that it was adopted as the national anthem in 1866, no such recognition has ever been officially accorded. A kind of official recognition came in 1893, when King <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oscar_II" title="Oscar II">Oscar II</a> rose in honor when the song was played. In 2000 a <a href="/wiki/Riksdag" title="Riksdag">Riksdag</a> committee rejected as "unnecessary" a proposal to give the song official status. The committee concluded that the song has been established as the national anthem by the people, not by the political system, and that it is preferable to keep it that way.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:95px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Richard_Dybeck.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="119" data-file-width="93" decoding="async" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/Richard_Dybeck.jpg" width="93"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Richard_Dybeck.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Richard Dybeck wrote the original lyrics in 1844.</div></div></div>
<p>The original lyrics were written by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dybeck" title="Richard Dybeck">Richard Dybeck</a> in 1844, to the melody of a variant of the ballad <i>K√§restans d√∂d</i>.  The ballad type is classified as D¬†280 in <a href="/wiki/The_Types_of_the_Scandinavian_Medieval_Ballad" title="The Types of the Scandinavian Medieval Ballad">The Types of the Scandinavian Medieval Ballad</a>; the variant from <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4stmanland" title="V√§stmanland">V√§stmanland</a> that Dybeck reproduced is classified as <a href="/wiki/Sveriges_Medeltida_Ballader" title="Sveriges Medeltida Ballader">SMB</a>¬†133¬†G.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  It was recorded by Rosa Wretman in the beginning of the 1840s. Dybeck published the traditional text in <i>Folk-lore I</i>, and the melody in 1845 in his <i>Runa</i>, where he also published his new text "S√•ng till Norden" (English: <span lang="en">"Song to/of the North"</span>).
</p><p>Dybeck himself originally wrote the beginning as "Du gamla, du friska" (English: <span lang="en">"Thou ancient, Thou hale"</span>), but in the late 1850s changed the lyrics to "Du gamla, du fria" (English: <span lang="en">"Thou ancient, Thou free"</span>). The song was already published in several song books and sung with "Du gamla, du friska", but a priest who had known Dybeck took the opportunity to inform the singer most associated with the song, opera singer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Carl_Fredrik_Lundqvist&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Carl Fredrik Lundqvist (page does not exist)">Carl Fredrik Lundqvist</a>, about the change in the year 1900. From that point on, printings of the "friska" version ceased to be seen in song books, but a recording from 1905 where it is sung with "friska" still exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The Swedish composer <a href="/wiki/Edvin_Kallstenius" title="Edvin Kallstenius">Edvin Kallstenius</a> made an orchestral arrangement of the national anthem in 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holm_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Holm-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the early 20th century, many regarded the song unsuitable as a national anthem. From the 1890s it was included in the "patriotic songs" section of song books, but up to the 1920s it was occasionally published just as "folk music". In 1899 a contest to produce a national anthem was held. It led to <a href="/wiki/Verner_von_Heidenstam" title="Verner von Heidenstam">Verner von Heidenstam</a> writing "Sverige", but did not lead to a new national anthem.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Patriotism" title="Patriotism">Patriotic</a> sentiment is notably absent from the text of the original two verses,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (February 2011)">dubious</span></a> <span class="metadata"> ‚Äì <a href="/wiki/Talk:Du_gamla,_du_fria#Dubious" title="Talk:Du gamla, du fria">discuss</a></span></i>]</sup> due to them being written in the spirit of <a href="/wiki/Scandinavism" title="Scandinavism">Scandinavism</a> popular at the time (<i>Norden</i> in general refers to the <a href="/wiki/Nordic_countries" title="Nordic countries">Nordic countries</a> in Swedish, Norwegian and Danish). After the song started to acquire its informal status as the national anthem, various people wrote additional verses to increase the "Swedishness" of the song.  The aforementioned Lundqvist wrote his own third verse beginning with "Jag √§lskar dig Sverige" (I love thee, Sweden), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frans_%C3%96sterblom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frans √ñsterblom (page does not exist)">Frans √ñsterblom</a> wrote four verses beginning with "Jag √§lskar min hembygd" ("I love my native area") and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Louise_Ahl%C3%A9n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Louise Ahl√©n (page does not exist)">Louise Ahl√©n</a> wrote two verses in 1910<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, which are presented as the last two verses in the lyrics section below.
</p>
