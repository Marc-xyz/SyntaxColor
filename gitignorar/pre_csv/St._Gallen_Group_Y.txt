	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">St. Gallen Group</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FuererIvo.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="822" data-file-width="648" decoding="async" height="254" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/FuererIvo.jpg/200px-FuererIvo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/FuererIvo.jpg/300px-FuererIvo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/FuererIvo.jpg/400px-FuererIvo.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FuererIvo.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bishop Ivo Fürer, the St. Gallen group’s host</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>St. Gallen Group</b> was an informal group of high ranking like-minded reformist clerics in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> – described by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Saint_Gallen" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Saint Gallen">Bishop of St. Gallen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivo_F%C3%BCrer" title="Ivo Fürer">Ivo Fürer</a>, who hosted the discussions, as a <i>Freundeskreis</i> ('a circle of friends')<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> – who met annually in or near <a href="/wiki/St._Gallen" title="St. Gallen">St. Gallen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, in January, to freely exchange ideas about issues in their church. The group being informal, it had no official name. <i>Group of St. Gallen</i> is what some of its members called it in their agendas, and the name has become public after a full chapter devoted to it in the biography of Cardinal Danneels, published by Church historians Karim Schelkens and Jurgen Mettepenningen;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>St. Gallen Group</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>, <i>St. Gallen Mafia</i> and <i>St. Gallen Club</i> are alternatives.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_membership">History and membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Gallen_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CardinalCMMartini.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1245" data-file-width="825" decoding="async" height="302" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/CardinalCMMartini.jpg/200px-CardinalCMMartini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/CardinalCMMartini.jpg/300px-CardinalCMMartini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/CardinalCMMartini.jpg/400px-CardinalCMMartini.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CardinalCMMartini.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cardinal Carlo Maria Martini, driving force and inspirator of the St. Gallen Group in its first years<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The impetus for the discussions came from Bishop <a href="/wiki/Ivo_F%C3%BCrer" title="Ivo Fürer">Ivo Fürer</a>, who had been the secretary-general of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_the_Bishops%27_Conferences_of_Europe" title="Council of the Bishops' Conferences of Europe">Council of the Bishops' Conferences of Europe</a> from 1977 till 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> When in 1993 the Vatican imposed a thorough reform of this Council,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Fürer was one of the members who felt that this meant the end of the main raison d'être of the Council, viz. fostering collegiality among European bishops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In consultation with Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Maria_Martini" title="Carlo Maria Martini">Carlo Maria Martini</a>, he decided to invite a group of cardinals, archbishops and bishops for frank, collegial discussions among themselves. 
</p><p>When the group met for the first time, in January 1996, Fürer invited Martini; <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paul_Verschuren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paul Verschuren (page does not exist)">Paul Verschuren</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Diocese_of_Helsinki" title="Catholic Diocese of Helsinki">Helsinki</a>; <a href="/wiki/Jean-F%C3%A9lix-Albert-Marie_Vilnet" title="Jean-Félix-Albert-Marie Vilnet">Jean Vilnet</a>, archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Lille" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Lille">Lille</a>; <a href="/wiki/Johann_Weber" title="Johann Weber">Johann Weber</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Graz-Seckau" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Graz-Seckau">Graz-Seckau</a>; <a href="/wiki/Walter_Kasper" title="Walter Kasper">Walter Kasper</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Rottenburg-Stuttgart" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Rottenburg-Stuttgart">Rottenburg-Stuttgart</a> (later Cardinal), and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Lehmann" title="Karl Lehmann">Karl Lehmann</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Mainz" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Mainz">Mainz</a> (later Cardinal).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 
</p><p>New members, all joining by invitation and all "open-minded" were:
</p>
<ul><li>1999: Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Godfried_Danneels" title="Godfried Danneels">Godfried Danneels</a>, archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Mechelen-Brussels" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Mechelen-Brussels">Mechelen-Brussels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ad_van_Luyn" title="Ad van Luyn">Ad van Luyn</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Rotterdam" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a></li>
<li>2001: <a href="/wiki/Cormac_Murphy-O%27Connor" title="Cormac Murphy-O'Connor">Cormac Murphy-O'Connor</a>, archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Westminster" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Westminster">Westminster</a> (later Cardinal), and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_Dor%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joseph Doré (page does not exist)">Joseph Doré</a>, archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Strasbourg" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a></li>
<li>2002: <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alois_Kothgasser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alois Kothgasser (page does not exist)">Alois Kothgasser</a>, bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Innsbruck" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Innsbruck">Innsbruck</a>, later archbishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Salzburg" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Salzburg">Salzburg</a></li>
<li>2003: <a href="/wiki/Achille_Silvestrini" title="Achille Silvestrini">Achille Silvestrini</a>, a curia Cardinal, and Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Lubomyr_Husar" title="Lubomyr Husar">Lubomyr Husar</a>, Major Archbishop of Lviv and Metropolitan of Galicia in the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Greek_Catholic_Church" title="Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church">Ukrainian Greek Catholic Church</a></li>
<li>2004: <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Policarpo" title="José Policarpo">José Policarpo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Lisbon" title="Patriarch of Lisbon">Patriarch of Lisbon</a> (in 2004).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The group's last meeting, in 2006, was attended by just four members: Fürer, Kothgasser, Danneels and van Luyn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secrecy_and_nickname">Secrecy and nickname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Gallen_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Secrecy and nickname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The founders and members of the group all feeling that the Vatican impeded free discussion among bishops, the meetings were held in secrecy. Members observed "a simple rule: everything could be said, no notes were taken and discretion was observed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The gatherings were revealed after the group had ceased to exist, in 2014 by <a href="/wiki/Austen_Ivereigh" title="Austen Ivereigh">Austen Ivereigh</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and described more extensively in 2015 in Danneels’s authorized biography.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> At the presentation of this biography in September 2015, which was televised by <a href="/wiki/VTM_(TV_channel)" title="VTM (TV channel)">VTM</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Danneels said that the name "Group of St. Gallen" was "deftig" (dignified, respectable), "maar eigenlijk zeiden wij van onszelf en van die groep: de maffia" (but actually we said of ourselves and of that group: the mafia). This provoked laughter. Others later used that name too, not always jocularly; see §5.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues_and_persons_discussed">Issues and persons discussed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Gallen_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Issues and persons discussed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The issues discussed by the group included centralism in the Church, the role of the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_conference" title="Episcopal conference">bishops' conferences</a>, the role and position of priests, sexual morality, the nomination of bishops and collegiality. On all these issues, the Vatican had published documents which the participants found controversial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>All agreed that the prefect of the <a href="/wiki/Congregation_for_the_Doctrine_of_the_Faith" title="Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith">Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Ratzinger_as_Prefect_of_the_Congregation_for_the_Doctrine_of_the_Faith" title="Joseph Ratzinger as Prefect of the Congregation for the Doctrine of the Faith">Joseph Ratzinger</a> was a centralizing and conservative influence in Rome, especially as John Paul's health declined.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> They certainly did not want Ratzinger to succeed him. 
</p><p>Some members deny that they discussed other names, but Fürer contradicts them, and explicitly states that Jorge Mario Bergoglio's was mentioned in the group's discussion on the impending succession of John Paul II. He adds, however, that the members never committed themselves on any candidate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meiningen_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Meiningen-19">[19]</a></sup> Bergoglio's name, however, could only have come up in St. Gallen at the 2002 meeting. Bergoglio was only created a cardinal in February 2001, and Martini, who had met him in 1974, introduced him to some members, who knew him barely or not at all,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Meiningen_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Meiningen-19">[19]</a></sup> at the extraordinary consistory of May 2001. Cardinal Bergoglio did not like the way the curia ran things, and his report on the 2001 bishops' synod earned him high praise all around, including from the St. Gallen Group. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_2005_and_2013_conclaves">Role in the 2005 and 2013 conclaves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St._Gallen_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role in the 2005 and 2013 conclaves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="583" data-file-width="338" decoding="async" height="345" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG/200px-Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG/300px-Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/26/Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kardinaal_III_Danneels_en_Kasper.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cardinals Walter Kasper and Godfried Danneels, two prominent members of the St. Gallen Group</div></div></div> 
<p>Whilst in Rome before the <a href="/wiki/2005_papal_conclave" title="2005 papal conclave">2005 papal conclave</a>, the cardinals who were members of the St. Gallen Group sent their host Ivo Fürer a card saying: "We are here together in the spirit of St. Gallen", <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> and before the conclave they came together for a talk over dinner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> According to an anonymous cardinal's excerpts from whose diary were published by Brunelli, two of them, Lehmann and Danneels, were "the thinking core" of the <i>reformisti</i> during the conclave. These <i>reformisti</i> did not want to vote for Joseph Ratzinger, and tried to prevent his election by giving all their votes to Jorge Mario Bergoglio, who thus might achieve a blocking minority.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> They succeeded, but Bergoglio, "almost in tears", begged not to be elected.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Ratzinger was elected <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a>.
</p><p>The year after Ratzinger’s election, what remained of the group met for the last time. Three of the remaining members, however, participated in the <a href="/wiki/2013_papal_conclave" title="2013 papal conclave">2013 papal conclave</a>: Walter Kasper, Godfried Danneels and Karl Lehmann. Cormac Murphy-O'Connor was too old to participate in the conclave, but he was present in Rome during the pre-conclave period. Unlike in 2005, there is no anonymous source to report from within the conclave on what role they played in the election of Pope Francis. According to Ivereigh, the four worked in concert to advocate the election of Jorge Mario Bergoglio at the conclave, still hoping to elect a more modern leader for the church. Also, in the first edition of his book, Ivereigh writes that "they first secured Bergoglio’s assent". All four cardinals, however, denied this.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cna_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-cna-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPo_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The director of the Holy See Press Office said the cardinals were "surprised and disappointed" at what was written about them and that they "expressly denied this description of events ...  with regard to the conduct of a campaign for [Bergoglio's] election".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In the second edition of his book, Ivereigh replaced the phrase by: "In keeping with conclave rules, they did not ask him if he would be willing to be a candidate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>. But he stood by the rest of his reporting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WaPo_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-WaPo-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some conservative authors have used the term "St. Gallen Mafia" to refer to an alleged conspiracy by members to elect Bergoglio, in violation of the apostolic constitution <i><a href="/wiki/Universi_Dominici_gregis" title="Universi Dominici gregis">Universi Dominici gregis</a></i>, which stipulates:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>81. The Cardinal electors shall [...] abstain from any form of pact, agreement, promise or other commitment of any kind which could oblige them to give or deny their vote to a person or persons. [...] It is not my intention however to forbid, during the period in which the See is vacant, the exchange of views concerning the election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>This term has been used by authors such as <a href="/wiki/Roberto_de_Mattei" title="Roberto de Mattei">Roberto de Mattei</a>, speaking of the "intrigue of Pope Bergoglio's election, piloted by the 'St. Gallen Mafia<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> 
</p>
