	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Cleanup hitter</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Baseball position</div>
<p>In baseball, a <b>cleanup hitter</b> is the fourth hitter in the <a href="/wiki/Batting_order_(baseball)" title="Batting order (baseball)">lineup</a>. They are the ones with the most power in the team and their most important job is to bring runs in, the cleanup hitter “cleans up the bases” meaning that if there are <a href="/wiki/Base_running" title="Base running">runners on the bases</a> the cleanup hitter scores them in ergo the name. There is much theory on how a coach sets up his lineup card before the game in order to maximize the effectiveness of his players during the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Theory">Theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanup_hitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The theory behind the use of the cleanup hitter is that at least one of the batters before him should reach a <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">base</a> in a way possible, usually being a <a href="/wiki/Base_on_balls" title="Base on balls">walk</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Hit_(baseball)" title="Hit (baseball)">base hit</a>. The batters in the beginning of the lineup have a variety of different traits but traditionally the <a href="/wiki/Leadoff_hitter" title="Leadoff hitter">lead off hitter</a> which sits at the number one spot has speed, <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_baseball_(P)" title="Glossary of baseball (P)">plate discipline</a>, and high <a href="/wiki/On-base_percentage" title="On-base percentage">on-base percentage</a>. The second batter is usually a <a href="/wiki/Contact_hitter" title="Contact hitter">contact hitter</a>, meaning they are able to consistently make contact with the ball and put it in play by any means possible to move the runner up and into <a href="/wiki/Scoring_position" title="Scoring position">scoring position</a>. It is a possibility for the first or second batter to bunt their way on base because they both should have good speed. The third batter is usually the best all-around batter that tends to have the highest batting average and has the role of scoring runs himself, but ultimately the job comes down to getting on base for the cleanup hitter to have a turn to bat in the same inning. Now with cleanup hitter coming up to hit if he has runners on base he has a chance to produce runs by getting a hit or by using their power they can hit a <a href="/wiki/Home_run" title="Home run">home run</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Extra-base_hit" title="Extra-base hit">extra base hit</a>. It is often found that the 3rd and 4th batter can switch roles in a game because of the ability of the 3rd batter to also produce runs. The 5th batter in the lineup also has a small responsibility of pushing in runs so he acts like a backup for the cleanup hitter in case he doesn't get the job done. He shares multiple traits with the cleanup hitter therefore can also compete for the spot on the lineup to become a cleanup hitter. After that batters from 6 to 9 descend by their skill level meaning the 9th batter has a tendency of being the worst batter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends">Trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanup_hitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are reoccurring trends each specific batter has, which is what gives them the position in the lineup card. A cleanup hitter has trends in his statistics, which is how cleanup hitters are determined from the rest of the team or even how good of a cleanup hitter they are. A cleanup player tends to hit a lot of home runs and extra base hits, has lower on <a href="/wiki/On-base_percentage" title="On-base percentage">base percentage (OBP)</a>, high number of <a href="/wiki/Run_batted_in" title="Run batted in">runs batted in</a>, have high <a href="/wiki/Slugging_percentage" title="Slugging percentage">slugging percentages</a>, and can also tend to be the player with the most <a href="/wiki/Strikeout" title="Strikeout">strikeouts</a>. Since the cleanup hitter is more of a <a href="/wiki/Power_hitter" title="Power hitter">power hitter</a> than a <a href="/wiki/Contact_hitter" title="Contact hitter">contact hitter</a> so there are a good number of strikeouts, which also explains the low (OBP). Cleanup hitters also commonly attend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_Run_Derby_(Major_League_Baseball)" title="Home Run Derby (Major League Baseball)">home run derbies</a> because they lead in home runs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Even though it is just an event the home run derby is where they get the chance to show case their power without the pressure of being in a game. Although there are athletes that break or don't fit into these trends, either because they are missing a couple of the traits or is an all-round player that can't be categorized to just the cleanup spot. An all-around player is good at most if not all aspects of the game and lead the leader boards in statistics.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_League_vs._National_League">American League vs. National League</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanup_hitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: American League vs. National League">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two leagues in North American Major League Baseball, the <a href="/wiki/American_League" title="American League">American league</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_League" title="National League">National League</a>. A key difference between the two is that the American League has a <a href="/wiki/Designated_hitter" title="Designated hitter">designated hitter (DH)</a>. The DH is a batter that hits for the <a href="/wiki/Pitcher" title="Pitcher">pitcher</a> and never plays defense. Meanwhile, the National League demands that the pitcher hit in the lineup unless another player <a href="/wiki/Pinch_hitter" title="Pinch hitter">pinch hits</a> for the pitcher, in which case the pitcher must leave the game and must be replaced for the team's next defensive half-inning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The DH is important in the American league because it is usually one of the better hitters. It is a trend that the DH is either in the 3rd, 4th, or 5th spot in the lineup. This is taken away when there are <a href="/wiki/Interleague_play" title="Interleague play">interleague games</a> when the National League is the home team, so the American League team does not use a DH, and their pitchers take their turn at bat.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanup_hitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Current">Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleanup_hitter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many examples of batters that have excelled in the cleanup spot of the lineup. These batters have left a mark on contemporary baseball and helped it evolve into the game it is now. One example of a cleanup hitter is <a href="/wiki/Albert_Pujols" title="Albert Pujols">Albert Pujols</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He is considered as a candidate for baseball's hall of fame due to his immense contributions to his team as a cleanup hitter. Albert is also known as “The Machine” which originated due to his power and runs batted in (RBI) statistics. He has bounced around between the three spot and the four spot as a hitter in his career. He currently plays for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels_of_Anaheim" title="Los Angeles Angels of Anaheim">Los Angeles Angels</a> and was recently demoted to DH in the cleanup spot.
</p>
