<p><b>Ammonia transporters</b> (<a class="external text" href="http://www.tcdb.org/search/result.php?tc=1.a.11" rel="nofollow">TC# 1.A.11</a>) are structurally related <a href="/wiki/Membrane_transport_protein" title="Membrane transport protein">membrane transport proteins</a> called Amt proteins (ammonia transporters) in <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plants" title="Plants">plants</a>, methylammonium/ammonium permeases (MEPs) in <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a>, or Rhesus (Rh) proteins in <a href="/wiki/Chordate" title="Chordate">chordates</a>. In humans, the <a href="/wiki/RHAG" title="RHAG">RhAG</a>, <a href="/wiki/RHBG" title="RHBG">RhBG</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RHCG" title="RHCG">RhCG</a> Rhesus proteins constitute <a href="/wiki/Solute_carrier_family" title="Solute carrier family">solute carrier family</a> 42<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> whilst <a href="/wiki/RHD_(gene)" title="RHD (gene)">RhD</a> and <a href="/wiki/RHCE_(gene)" title="RHCE (gene)">RhCE</a> form the <a href="/wiki/Rh_blood_group_system" title="Rh blood group system">Rh blood group system</a>. The three-dimensional structure of the ammonia transport protein AmtB from <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i> has been determined by <a href="/wiki/X-ray_crystallography" title="X-ray crystallography">x-ray crystallography</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15361618_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15361618-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15563598_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15563598-4">[4]</a></sup> revealing a hydrophobic <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The human RhCG ammonia transporter was found to have a similar ammonia-conducting channel structure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhcgstruct_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-rhcgstruct-1">[1]</a></sup> It was proposed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erythrocyte" title="Erythrocyte">erythrocyte</a> Rh complex is a heterotrimer of RhAG, <a href="/wiki/RHD_(gene)" title="RHD (gene)">RhD</a>, and <a href="/wiki/RHCE_(gene)" title="RHCE (gene)">RhCE</a> subunits in which RhD and RhCE might play roles in anchoring the ammonia-conducting RhAG subunit to the cytoskeleton. Based on reconstitution experiments, purified RhCG subunits alone can function to transport ammonia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> RhCG is required for normal <a href="/wiki/Renal_tubular_acidosis" title="Renal tubular acidosis">acid excretion</a> by the mouse <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidney</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Epididymis" title="Epididymis">epididymis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The structure of the ammonia channel from <i>E. coli</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15361618_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15361618-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15563598_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15563598-4">[4]</a></sup> was, at the time of its publication, the highest resolution structure of any integral membrane protein. It shows a trimer of subunits, each made up of 11 <a href="/wiki/Transmembrane_domain" title="Transmembrane domain">transmembrane segments</a> (TMSs) and containing a pseudo two-fold symmetry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Each monomer contains a hydrophobic ammonia conducting channel.
</p><p>While prokaryotic ammonia channel proteins have an N-terminal region which acts as a signal sequence and is cleaved in the mature protein,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> the Rhesus glycoproteins retain this as a 12th transmembrane helix in the mature protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rhcgstruct_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-rhcgstruct-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Substrate_specificity">Substrate specificity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Substrate specificity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most functionally characterized members of the family are ammonium uptake transporters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Some, but not other Amt proteins also transport methylammonium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  Detailed phylogenetic analyses of plant homologues have been published.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In <i>E. coli</i>, <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup></a>, rather than NH<sub>3</sub>, may be the substrate of AmtB, but controversy still exists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> If NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> is transported, K<sup>+</sup> possibly serves as a <a href="/wiki/Counterion" title="Counterion">counter ion</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Antiporter" title="Antiporter">antiport</a> process with K<sup>+</sup>, and that one histidine removes a proton off of NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> to yield NH<sub>3</sub>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_reaction">Transport reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transport reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The generalized transport reaction catalyzed by members of the Amt family are suggested to be:
</p>
<dl><dd>NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> (out) ⇌ NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> (in)</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The X-ray structures have revealed that the pore of the Amt and Rh proteins is characterized by a hydrophobic portion about 12 <a href="/wiki/%C3%85" title="Å">Å</a> long, in which electronic density was observed in the crystallographic study of AmtB from <i>Escherichia coli</i>. This electronic density was initially only observed when crystals were grown in the presence of ammonium, and was thus attributed to ammonia molecules. The Amt/Rh protein mechanism might involve the single-file diffusion of NH<sub>3</sub> molecules. However, the pore could also be filled with water molecules. The possible presence of water molecules in the pore lumen calls for a reassessment of the notion that Amt/Rh proteins work as plain NH<sub>3</sub> channels. Indeed, functional experiments on plant ammonium transporters and Rh proteins suggest a variety of permeation mechanisms including the passive diffusion of NH<sub>3</sub>, the antiport of NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>/H<sup>+</sup>, the transport of NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>, or the <a href="/wiki/Active_transport" title="Active transport">cotransport</a> of NH<sub>3</sub>/H<sup>+</sup>. Lamoureux <i>et al.</i> discuss these mechanisms in light of functional and simulation studies on the AmtB transporter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ammonia_transporter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i>E. coli</i> the AmtB gene is expressed only under limiting nitrogen levels to yield the AmtB protein. It is co-expressed with the GlnK gene which encodes a <a href="/wiki/Pii_nitrogen_regulatory_proteins" title="Pii nitrogen regulatory proteins">PII protein</a>. This protein is also trimeric and remains in the cytoplasm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> It is covalently modified by a U/U deuridylylated group at Y51. The hydrolyzed product, adenosine 5'-diphosphate, orients the surface of GlnK for AmtB blockade. When nitrogen levels outside the cell rise, the ammonia channel must be deactivated to prevent excessive ammonia entering the cell (where ammonia would be combined with glutamate to make glutamine, utilizing <a href="/wiki/Adenosine_triphosphate" title="Adenosine triphosphate">ATP</a> and thereby depleting the cell's ATP reserves). This deactivation is achieved by deuridylylation of the GlnK protein which then binds to the cytoplasmic face of AmtB and inserts a loop into the ammonia conducting pore. At the tip of this loop is an <a href="/wiki/Arginine" title="Arginine">arginine</a> residue which sterically blocks the channel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
