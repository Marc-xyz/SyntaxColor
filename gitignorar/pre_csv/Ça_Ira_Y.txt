<p>"<b><span lang="fr" style="font-style:normal;" title="French language text">Ça ira</span></b>" (French: "it'll be fine") is an emblematic song of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, first heard in May 1790. It underwent several changes in wording, all of which used the title words as part of the <a href="/wiki/Refrain" title="Refrain">refrain</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_version">Original version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87a_Ira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The author of the original words "<span lang="fr" style="font-style:normal;" title="French language text">Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira</span>" was a former soldier by the name of Ladré who made a living as a street singer.
</p><p>The music is a popular <a href="/wiki/Country_dance" title="Country dance">contredanse</a> air called "Le carillon national", and was composed by Bécourt, a violinist (according to other sources: <a href="/wiki/Snare_drum" title="Snare drum">side drum</a> player) of the théâtre Beaujolais. Queen <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">Marie Antoinette</a> herself is said to have often played the music on her <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The title and theme of the refrain were inspired by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>, in France as a representative of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a>, who was very popular among the French people. When asked about the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>, he would reportedly reply, in somewhat broken French, "<span lang="fr" style="font-style:normal;" title="French language text">Ça ira, ça ira</span>" ("It'll be fine, it'll be fine").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>The song first became popular as a worksong during the preparation for the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%AAte_de_la_F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration" title="Fête de la Fédération">Fête de la Fédération</a> of 1790 and eventually became recognized as an unofficial anthem of revolutionaries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sans-culotte_version">Sans-culotte version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87a_Ira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sans-culotte version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At later stages of the revolution, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sans-culotte" title="Sans-culotte">sans-culottes</a> used several much more aggressive stanzas, calling for the lynching of the <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> and the clergy.
</p>
<div class="poem" lang="fr" style="float:left;">
<p>Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>

Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
les aristocrates on les pendra!<br/>
<br/>
Si on n' les pend pas<br/>
On les rompra<br/>
Si on n' les rompt pas<br/>
On les brûlera.<br/>
Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
les aristocrates à la lanterne!<br/>
Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
les aristocrates on les pendra!<br/>
<br/>
Nous n'avions plus ni nobles, ni prêtres,<br/>
Ah ! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira,<br/>
L'égalité partout régnera.<br/>
L'esclave autrichien le suivra,<br/>
Ah ! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira,<br/>
Et leur infernale clique<br/>
Au diable s'envolera.<br/>
Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
les aristocrates à la lanterne!<br/>
Ah! ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
les aristocrates on les pendra!<br/>
Et quand on les aura tous pendus<br/>
On leur fichera la pelle au cul.
</p>
</div>

<a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">The Austrian slave</a> shall follow him<br/>

Ah! It'll be fine, It'll be fine, It'll be fine<br/>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-revolutionary_use">Post-revolutionary use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87a_Ira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Post-revolutionary use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song survived past the <a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Reign of Terror</a>, and, during the <a href="/wiki/French_Directory" title="French Directory">Directory</a>, it became mandatory to sing it before shows. It was forbidden under the <a href="/wiki/French_Consulate" title="French Consulate">Consulate</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ship_of_the_line" title="Ship of the line">ship of the line</a> <i>La Couronne</i> was renamed <i><a href="/wiki/French_ship_%C3%87a_Ira_(1781)" title="French ship Ça Ira (1781)">Ça Ira</a></i> in 1792 in reference to this song.
</p><p>At the 1793 <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Famars" title="Battle of Famars">Battle of Famars</a>, the 14th Regiment of Foot, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_West_Yorkshire_Regiment" title="The West Yorkshire Regiment">The West Yorkshire Regiment</a>, attacked the French to the music of "Ça ira" (the colonel commenting that he would "beat the French to their own damned tune"). The regiment was later awarded the tune as a battle honour and regimental <a href="/wiki/March_(music)" title="March (music)">quick march</a>. It has since been adopted by the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Regiment" title="Yorkshire Regiment">Yorkshire Regiment</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Carl_Schurz" title="Carl Schurz">Carl Schurz</a>, in <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Reminiscences_of_Carl_Schurz/Volume_One/14_Br%C3%BCning_Salon_%E2%80%94_Triumph_of_the_Reaction" title="s:The Reminiscences of Carl Schurz/Volume One/14 Brüning Salon — Triumph of the Reaction">volume 1, chapter 14,</a> of his <i>Reminiscences</i>, reported from exile in England that upon <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>'s <i>coup d'état</i> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_coup_of_1851" title="French coup of 1851">2 December 1851</a>, "Our French friends shouted and shrieked and gesticulated and hurled opprobrious names at Louis Napoleon and cursed his helpers, and danced the <a href="/wiki/Carmagnole" title="Carmagnole">Carmagnole</a> and sang 'Ça ira.'"
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_adaptations">Modern adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%87a_Ira&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An alternative "sans-culotte"-like version was sung by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89dith_Piaf" title="Édith Piaf">Édith Piaf</a> for the soundtrack of the film <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Affairs_in_Versailles" title="Royal Affairs in Versailles">Royal Affairs in Versailles</a></i> (<i>Si Versailles m'était conté</i>) by <a href="/wiki/Sacha_Guitry" title="Sacha Guitry">Sacha Guitry</a>.
</p><p>The song is featured in the 1999 television series <i><a href="/wiki/The_Scarlet_Pimpernel_(TV_series)" title="The Scarlet Pimpernel (TV series)">The Scarlet Pimpernel</a></i>, starring <a href="/wiki/Richard_E._Grant" title="Richard E. Grant">Richard E. Grant</a>. There the lyrics are sung in English as follows:
</p>
<div class="poem">
<p>Ah ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
Over in France there's a revolution<br/>
Ah ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
Watch what you say or you'll lose your head<br/>
Ah ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
Pass some time, see an execution! <br/>
Ah ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
Une deux trois and you fall down dead<br/>
Ah ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
Hear the tale of Marie Antoinette-a!<br/>
Ah ça ira, ça ira, ça ira<br/>
A bloodier sight you have never seen!
</p>
</div>
<p>In an opening scene of the novel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/What_Is_To_Be_Done%3F_(novel)" title="What Is To Be Done? (novel)">What Is To Be Done?</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Nikolay_Chernyshevsky" title="Nikolay Chernyshevsky">Nikolay Chernyshevsky</a>, the protagonist Vera Pavlovna is shown singing a song with <i>ça ira</i> in the refrain, accompanied by a paraphrase outlining the struggle for a socialist utopian future. The 1875 French translator "A.T." produced a four-stanza version on the basis of the paraphrase, which was reproduced in full by <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Tucker" title="Benjamin Tucker">Benjamin Tucker</a> in his translation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  
</p>
