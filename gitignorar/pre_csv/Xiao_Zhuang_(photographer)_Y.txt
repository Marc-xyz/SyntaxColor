<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Chinese photographer (b. 1933)</div>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">This is a <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese name</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">family name</a> is Xiao .</div>
<p><b>Xiao Zhuang 晓庄</b> (born 1933), previously named Zhuang Dongying 庄冬莺, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_photography" title="Chinese photography">Chinese photographer</a> and photo editor. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Xiao_Zhuang_(photographer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Xiao Zhuang began her photographic career working as a photographer for the <i>Lu Shui Newspaper</i> under the No. 22 <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Liberation_Army" title="People's Liberation Army">People's Liberation Army</a> in 1950 and became a photojournalist for the <i>Xinhua News Daily</i> in 1952. She worked as the chief editor of the photography department of the Jiang Su Publishing Company since 1980, as well as for <i>Light and Photography</i> magazine. She retired in 1994 and became a consultant for the Chinese Photographers Association of Jiangsu Province.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> She documented the everyday life of Mao's China between the 1950s and 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Her photographs are regarded as important resources in the field of Chinese studies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2008, Xiao Zhuang was one of very few female photographers featured in the landmark exhibition, <i>Photography from China 1934-2008,</i> hosted in Houston, Texas and commissioned by FotoFest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She was also included in group exhibitions such as <i>Historical Retrospective Part I</i> at the 798 Photo Gallery in 2011 and <i>Grain to Pixel: A Story of Photography in China</i> at Shanghai Center of Photography in 2015<i>.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The latter exhibition was curated by the noted British art critic and historian Karen Smith. It travelled to Australia, where it was shown as <i>China: Grain to Pixel</i> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monash_Gallery_of_Art" title="Monash Gallery of Art">Monash Gallery of Art</a>, Melbourne, in 2016. However, alongside six other works, Xiao Zhuang's <i>The Irrational Times 39, Nanjing</i> was prohibited from being exported by the Chinese government. Instead, the Australian version of the exhibition presented the censored photographs in its exhibition catalogue. Implementing a "patriotic" style, it was uncertain why the government decided to censor Xiao Zhuang's photograph.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Xiao Zhuang was included in edited books such as <i>100 Photographers Focus on China</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and <i>China: Portrait of a Country</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> She also published photo books, such as <i>The Red Albums: Photographic Notes of Xiao Zhuang</i> (English and Chinese).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> <i>The Red Albums</i> contains her photographs from the 1950s to the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
