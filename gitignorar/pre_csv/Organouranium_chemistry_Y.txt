	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Organouranium chemistry</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Uranocene-3D-vdW.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1100" data-file-width="975" decoding="async" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Uranocene-3D-vdW.png/220px-Uranocene-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Uranocene-3D-vdW.png/330px-Uranocene-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Uranocene-3D-vdW.png/440px-Uranocene-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Uranocene-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Uranocene" title="Uranocene">Uranocene</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Organouranium chemistry</b> is the science exploring the properties, structure and reactivity of organouranium compounds, which are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organometallic_compound" title="Organometallic compound">organometallic compounds</a> containing a <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a> <a href="/wiki/Chemical_bond" title="Chemical bond">chemical bond</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The field is of some importance to the nuclear industry and of theoretical interest in <a href="/wiki/Organometallic_chemistry" title="Organometallic chemistry">organometallic chemistry</a>.
</p><p>The development of organouranium compounds started in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> when the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> required volatile uranium compounds for <sup>235</sup>U/<sup>238</sup>U <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> separation. For example, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Gilman" title="Henry Gilman">Henry Gilman</a> attempted to synthesize compounds like tetramethyluranium and others worked on uranium <a href="/wiki/Metal_carbonyl" title="Metal carbonyl">metal carbonyls</a> but none of the efforts met success due to organouranium instability. After the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Ferrocene" title="Ferrocene">ferrocene</a> in 1951, Todd Reynolds and <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Wilkinson" title="Geoffrey Wilkinson">Geoffrey Wilkinson</a> in 1956 synthesized the uranium <a href="/wiki/Metallocene" title="Metallocene">metallocene</a> Cp<sub>3</sub>UCl from <a href="/wiki/Sodium_cyclopentadienide" title="Sodium cyclopentadienide">sodium cyclopentadienide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uranium_tetrachloride" title="Uranium tetrachloride">uranium tetrachloride</a> as a stable but extremely air-sensitive compound. In it the U-Cl bond is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionic_bond" title="Ionic bond">ionic bond</a> and the uranium bonds with the three <a href="/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex" title="Cyclopentadienyl complex">cyclopentadienyl</a> ligands are covalent of the type found in <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_compound" title="Sandwich compound">sandwich compounds</a> with involvement of the uranium 5f <a href="/wiki/Atomic_orbital" title="Atomic orbital">atomic orbitals</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ernst_Otto_Fischer" title="Ernst Otto Fischer">Ernst Otto Fischer</a> in 1962 discovered tetracyclopentadienyluranium Cp<sub>4</sub>U by reaction of NaCp with UCl<sub>4</sub> (6% yield) as a compound stable in air as a solid but not in solution. A zero <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment" title="Molecular dipole moment">molecular dipole moment</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IR_spectroscopy" title="IR spectroscopy">IR spectroscopy</a> revealed that it was also a <a href="/wiki/Sandwich_compound" title="Sandwich compound">sandwich compound</a> with uranium in a <a href="/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry" title="Tetrahedral molecular geometry">tetrahedral molecular geometry</a>. In 1970, Fischer added Cp<sub>3</sub>U to the list of known organouranium compounds by reduction of Cp<sub>4</sub>U with elemental uranium.
</p><p>In 1968, the group of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Streitwieser" title="Andrew Streitwieser">Andrew Streitwieser</a> prepared the stable but <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyrophoric" title="Pyrophoric">pyrophoric</a> compound <a href="/wiki/Uranocene" title="Uranocene">uranocene</a> (COT)<sub>2</sub>U which has an atom of uranium sandwiched between two <a href="/wiki/Cyclooctatetraenide_anion" title="Cyclooctatetraenide anion">cyclooctatetraenide anions</a> (D<sub>8h</sub> <a href="/wiki/Molecular_symmetry" title="Molecular symmetry">molecular symmetry</a>). The uranium f orbitals interact substantially with the <a href="/wiki/Aromaticity" title="Aromaticity">aromatic</a> rings just as the d-orbitals in <a href="/wiki/Ferrocene" title="Ferrocene">ferrocene</a> interact with the Cp ligands. Uranocene differs from ferrocene because its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HOMO" title="HOMO">HOMO</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LUMO" title="LUMO">LUMO</a> are centered on the metal and not on the rings and all reactions thus involve the metal often resulting in ligand - metal cleavage.
</p><p>Uranocenes show ease of reduction of U(IV) compounds to U(III) compounds; otherwise they are fairly unreactive. A close relative that does have sufficient reactivity, obtained by reaction of uranocene with <a href="/wiki/Uranium_borohydride" title="Uranium borohydride">uranium borohydride</a> is the half-sandwich compound (COT)U(BH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> discovered in 1983 by the group of M.J. Ephritikhine. Compounds of this type react in many different ways, for instance alkylation at uranium with <a href="/wiki/Organolithium_reagent" title="Organolithium reagent">organolithium reagents</a> or conversion to hybrid sandwich compounds.
</p><p>Other organouranium compounds are inverted uranocenes with a COT ligand in between two uranium atoms or uranium sandwich compounds with <a href="/wiki/Pentalene" title="Pentalene">pentalenide</a> ligands instead of COT ligands.
</p>
