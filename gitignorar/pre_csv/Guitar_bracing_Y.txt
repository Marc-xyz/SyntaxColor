	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Guitar bracing</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Guitar bracing</b> refers to the system of wooden struts which internally support and reinforce the <a href="/wiki/Sound_board_(music)" title="Sound board (music)">soundboard</a> and back of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitars" title="Acoustic guitars">acoustic guitars</a>.
</p><p>Soundboard or top bracing transmits the forces exerted by the strings from the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_(instrument)" title="Bridge (instrument)">bridge</a> to the rim. The <a href="/wiki/Luthier" title="Luthier">luthier</a> faces the challenge of bracing the instrument to withstand the stress applied by the strings with minimal distortion, while permitting the top to respond as fully as possible to the tones generated by the strings. Brace design contributes significantly to the type of sound a guitar will produce. According to luthiers W. Cumpiano and J. Natelson, "By varying brace design, each builder has sought to produce a sound that conformed to his concept of the ideal."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The back of the instrument is braced to help distribute the force exerted by the neck on the body, and to maintain the tonal responsiveness and structural integrity of the <a href="/wiki/Sound_box" title="Sound box">sound box</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">Materials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Materials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Braces may be made from top woods (spruce or cedar), balsa wood or, in high-end instruments, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_fiber" title="Carbon fiber">carbon fiber</a> composites.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nylon_string_guitar_bracing">Nylon string guitar bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nylon string guitar bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fan_bracing">Fan bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fan bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is the standard bracing pattern on the <a href="/wiki/Classical_guitar" title="Classical guitar">classical guitar</a>, dating to the work of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonio_Torres_Jurado" title="Antonio Torres Jurado">Antonio Torres Jurado</a> in the 19th century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kasha_Bracing">Kasha Bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Kasha Bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1970s, scientist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Kasha" title="Michael Kasha">Michael Kasha</a> radically overhauled every aspect of guitar design to incorporate principles such as <a href="/wiki/Impedance_matching#Mechanics" title="Impedance matching">mechanical impedance matching</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lattice_bracing">Lattice bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lattice bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Australian guitarmaker <a href="/wiki/Greg_Smallman" title="Greg Smallman">Greg Smallman</a> introduced guitars with an extremely thin soundboard, which is supported by bracing in the shape of a lattice. Smallman combines this with heavier, laminated back and sides with a frame. Smallman's guitars are used by <a href="/wiki/John_Williams_(guitarist)" title="John Williams (guitarist)">John Williams</a>.
</p><p>Smallman's design was inspired by research  by Torres who made a guitar with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papier_m%C3%A2ch%C3%A9" title="Papier mâché">papier mâché</a> back and sides to show that the soundboard was the most important factor in guitar sound projection. Smallman also uses two 45 degree pole supports from the bottom to the sound board that transfer sound.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Steel_string_flat-top_guitar_bracing">Steel string flat-top guitar bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Steel string flat-top guitar bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In all steel-string instruments, the ends of the top braces taper at the edge of the soundboard. In most factory built guitars the brace tops are given a round profile, but are otherwise left unshaped. This produces a stronger top and may reduce the number of warranty claims arising from damage, however, over-build tops are less responsive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Braces are usually made from Sitka Spruce (<a href="/wiki/Picea_sitchensis" title="Picea sitchensis">Picea sitchensis</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Some luthiers use Adirondack Spruce, also known as "Red Spruce" (<a href="/wiki/Picea_rubens" title="Picea rubens">Picea rubens</a>), in high end instruments.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="X-Bracing">X-Bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: X-Bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The tops of most <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel_string_acoustic_guitars" title="Steel string acoustic guitars">steel string acoustic guitars</a> are braced using the X-brace system, or a variation of the X-brace system, generally attributed to <a href="/wiki/Christian_Frederick_Martin" title="Christian Frederick Martin">Christian Frederick Martin</a> between 1840 and 1845 for use in gut string guitars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
The system consists of two braces forming an "X" shape across the soundboard below the top of the sound hole. The lower arms of the "X" straddle and support the ends of the bridge. Under the bridge is a hardwood bridge plate which prevents the ball end of the strings from damaging the underside of the soundboard. Below the bridge patch are one or more tone bars which support the bottom of the soundboard. These abut one of the X braces and usually slant down towards the bottom edge of the soundboard. The top tone bar butts against a portion of the bridge patch in most instruments. Above the sound hole a large transverse brace spans the width of the upper bout of the soundboard. Around the lower bout, small finger braces support the area between the X-braces and the edge of the soundboard.
</p><p><br/>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_X-bracing">Double X-bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Double X-bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In this system, two overlapping X shapes form a diamond surrounding the underside of the bridge plate. Some luthiers prefer it where additional strength is required, for instance for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twelve_string_guitar" title="Twelve string guitar">twelve string guitars</a>.
This bracing does not allow the top to move or vibrate as much as it normally would but offers more strength and prevent bellying around the bridge area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A-bracing">A-bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: A-bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This is a variation on X-bracing. The X-shaped structure under the bridge is retained,  but the transverse
strut between the <a href="/wiki/Fingerboard" title="Fingerboard">fingerboard</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soundhole" title="Soundhole">soundhole</a> is replaced by two diagonal braces which splay outward going toward the sound hole.
</p>
<h3><span id="Brace_shape_and_.27voicing.27_or_.27tap_tuning.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brace_shape_and_'voicing'_or_'tap_tuning'">Brace shape and 'voicing' or 'tap tuning'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Brace shape and 'voicing' or 'tap tuning'">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Luthiers building higher quality instruments adjust the stiffness of the top and shape the braces to maximize the response of the top while maintaining structural integrity. Tone bars and bottom halves of the X-braces may be either scalloped or parabolic in shape. Above the X-brace joint, braces usually have a parabolic shape. Experienced luthiers 'voice' or 'tap-tune' the tops and backs of high end guitars to produce optimum tone and responsiveness in the hands of the player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scalloped_vs_parabolic_bracing">Scalloped vs parabolic bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Scalloped vs parabolic bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bracing style and shape will affect the tone of the instrument. According to luthiers Bob Connor and David Mainwaring, "scalloped braces will produce a warmer sounding bass response in the guitar
with smooth mids and crisp highs. Parabolic braces will yield a quick response with a more pronounced mid range and a more focused bottom end."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ladder_bracing">Ladder bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ladder bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This simple system, where braces are arranged parallel to each other and perpendicular
to the direction of the strings, is employed on most guitar backs. It was also once used
on guitar tops, a practice which survives in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maccaferri_guitar" title="Maccaferri guitar">Maccaferri guitar</a>. It is considered more suitable for parlor guitars and lightly strung instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archtop_bracing">Archtop bracing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Guitar_bracing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Archtop bracing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Archtop_guitar" title="Archtop guitar">Archtop guitars</a> originally had two near-horizontal braces or "tone bars" on either side from bridge to neck, a system known as parallel bracing. The braces roughly run under the feet of the archtop guitar's bridge.  X-bracing, similar to that of flat-top guitars was later introduced. Their tops are inherently stronger than flat tops, so less bracing may be required. "Trestle" bracing was a system used on some <a href="/wiki/Gretsch" title="Gretsch">Gretsch</a> archtops<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
