<p><b>Natalia Trayanova</b> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_American_Heart_Association" title="Fellow of the American Heart Association">FAHA</a> <a href="/wiki/Heart_Rhythm_Society" title="Heart Rhythm Society">FHRS</a> is a Professor of <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_engineering" title="Biomedical engineering">Biomedical Engineering</a> in the Department of Medicine at <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a>. She directs the Alliance for Cardiovascular Diagnostic and Treatment Innovation <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natalia_Trayanova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trayanova's father was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physiologist" title="Physiologist">physiologist</a> and director of the Biophysics Institute in Bulgaria.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> Her mother was a Professor of Economics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> She studied physics at <a href="/wiki/Sofia_University" title="Sofia University">Sofia University</a>, graduating in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup> Her father gave her a copy of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plonsey" title="Robert Plonsey">Robert Plonsey</a>'s book, <i>Bioelectric Phenomena</i>, and Trayanova realised she could use her physics expertise in biology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> She earned a PhD from the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Bulgarian Academy of Sciences">Bulgarian Academy of Sciences</a> in 1986, where she studied <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_muscle" title="Skeletal muscle">skeletal muscle</a> fibre biopotentials<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_and_career">Research and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Natalia_Trayanova&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1986 Trayanova joined <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a> working with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plonsey" title="Robert Plonsey">Robert Plonsey</a> on rhythmic dysfunction in the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1995 she was appointed Associate Professor at <a href="/wiki/Tulane_University" title="Tulane University">Tulane University</a>, where she was awarded the several awards for teaching excellence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup> She began to develop computer models for the heart but found that the cardiologists were not enthusiastic about computer modelling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, several research institutions asked Trayanova to relocate and join them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup> She was awarded a <a href="/wiki/Fulbright_Program" title="Fulbright Program">Fulbright Program</a> Visiting Professorship and spent several months at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a>.
</p><p>In 2006 Trayanova was recruited to <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">Johns Hopkins University</a> as a Professor in the <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_Biomedical_Engineering" title="Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering">Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering</a> and Institute for Computational Science.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Her work considers computational simulations of the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> She was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_Engineering_Society" title="Biomedical Engineering Society">Biomedical Engineering Society</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Heart_Association" title="American Heart Association">American Heart Association</a> in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2011 she developed a computational framework that allowed virtual drug screening, simulating the drug-channel interactions and predicting the impact of drugs on electrical activity of the heart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012 she was named the Murray B Sachs Endowed Chair in <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_Biomedical_Engineering" title="Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering">Johns Hopkins Biomedical Engineering</a> Department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2013 she was awarded the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> Directorâs Pioneer Award, which allowed her to develop a virtual electrophysiology lab.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">[10]</a></sup> The award gave her $2.5 million over five years to develop patient-specific computational models of the heart, allowing for doctors to provide personalised treatment and diagnoses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">[10]</a></sup> She has received extensive support from the Maryland Innovation Initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2019, she was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Technology_International" title="Women in Technology International">Women in Technology International</a> Hall of Fame,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and she also received the 2019 <a href="/wiki/Heart_Rhythm_Society" title="Heart Rhythm Society">Heart Rhythm Society</a> Distinguished Scientist Award <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>She is the Chief Scientific Officer of <i>Cardiosolv Ablation Technologies</i>, a start-up that develops computational tools to help the treatment of v<a href="/wiki/Ventricular_tachycardia" title="Ventricular tachycardia">entricular tachycardia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> She gave a <a href="/wiki/TED_(conference)" title="TED (conference)">TED</a> talk in 2017 entitled <i>Your Personal Virtual Heart.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> She was selected by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> to take part in a briefing at <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Hill" title="Capitol Hill">Capitol Hill</a> looking to defend the federal funding of scientific research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> She was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/International_Academy_of_Medical_and_Biological_Engineering" title="International Academy of Medical and Biological Engineering">International Academy of Medical and Biological Engineering</a> in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  She has been featured on Reddit AMA r/science,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> has been interviewed by the BBC, NPR, the Economist, and has been on the Amazing Things Podcast.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
