<p><b>James William Turner</b>, known as <b>Jim Turner</b> (born February 6, 1946), is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> who was the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Representative" title="U.S. Representative">U.S. Representative</a> for <a href="/wiki/Texas%27s_2nd_congressional_district" title="Texas's 2nd congressional district">Texas's 2nd congressional district</a> from 1997 until 2005.
</p>

<h2><span id="Early_life.2C_education.2C_and_early_career"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life,_education,_and_early_career">Early life, education, and early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Turner_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education, and early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Turner was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Lewis,_Washington" title="Fort Lewis, Washington">Fort Lewis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_(U.S._state)" title="Washington (U.S. state)">Washington</a>, but reared in <a href="/wiki/Crockett,_Texas" title="Crockett, Texas">Crockett</a> in <a href="/wiki/Houston_County,_Texas" title="Houston County, Texas">Houston County</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">East Texas</a>. He received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">bachelor's degree</a> in business, and simultaneously earned an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">MBA</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">J.D.</a>, all from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>. Following graduation, he was commissioned in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=United_states_Army&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="United states Army (page does not exist)">United states Army</a> and served eight years (active and reserve), attaining the rank of <a href="/wiki/Captain" title="Captain">captain</a>.  His legal career in Texas included his own law practice in his hometown of Crockett, his partnership in the Austin office of Hughes &amp; Luce, LLP and serving Of Counsel with Hance Scarborough, LLP.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="State_politics">State politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Turner_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: State politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to being elected to Congress, Turner held several state and local offices. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Legislature" title="Texas Legislature">Texas House of Representatives</a> from 1981 to 1984, <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> of Crockett from 1989 to 1991, and a member of the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Senate" title="Texas Senate">Texas Senate</a> from 1991 to 1996. He succeeded <a href="/wiki/Kent_Caperton" title="Kent Caperton">Kent Caperton</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bryan,_Texas" title="Bryan, Texas">Bryan</a> in the Senate. Caperton did not seek reelection in 1990, and Turner defeated the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Lou_Zaeske" title="Lou Zaeske">Lou Zaeske</a>, also of Bryan, head of the Texas <a href="/wiki/English-only_movement" title="English-only movement">English-only movement</a>. For two years, Turner was an executive assistant to Texas <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Texas" title="Governor of Texas">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Mark_White" title="Mark White">Mark White</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._House_of_Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Turner_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: U.S. House of Representatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, 2nd district U.S. Representative <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Wilson_(Texas_politician)" title="Charlie Wilson (Texas politician)">Charlie Wilson</a>, known for his role in funding the resistance to <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">communist</a> government, decided not to run for a thirteenth term. Turner won the Democratic nomination to succeed him and was handily elected in November 1996, when <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> was reelected as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">U.S. President</a>. He was reelected three times with no substantive opposition. Congressman Turner sat on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Armed_Services" title="United States House Committee on Armed Services">House Armed Services Committee</a>, and was the Ranking Member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Homeland_Security" title="United States House Committee on Homeland Security">House Committee on Homeland Security</a>. Considered a somewhat fiscally <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Democrat" title="Conservative Democrat">Conservative Democrat</a>, Turner co-chaired the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Dog_Coalition" title="Blue Dog Coalition">Blue Dog Coalition</a> and was a member of the <a href="/wiki/New_Democrat_Coalition" title="New Democrat Coalition">New Democrat Coalition</a>.
</p><p>In 2003, Turner was one of the targets of a highly <a href="/wiki/2003_Texas_redistricting" title="2003 Texas redistricting">controversial redistricting</a> engineered by <a href="/wiki/Tom_DeLay" title="Tom DeLay">Tom DeLay</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Texas_Legislature" title="Texas Legislature">Texas Legislature</a> dismantled his district, which covered a large portion of <a href="/wiki/East_Texas" title="East Texas">East Texas</a> stretching from <a href="/wiki/Lufkin,_Texas" title="Lufkin, Texas">Lufkin</a> to the suburbs of <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, and split its territory among three districts. The largest portion was shifted to the 8th District, represented by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Republican_Party" title="United States Republican Party">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Brady" title="Kevin Brady">Kevin Brady</a>, who had been elected the same year as Turner. While Turner had represented more of the new 8th than Brady, most of the 8th's vote was cast in heavily Republican <a href="/wiki/Montgomery_County,_Texas" title="Montgomery County, Texas">Montgomery County</a>, which has as much population as the rest of the district combined. His home in Crockett was thrown into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Worth" title="Fort Worth">Fort Worth</a>/<a href="/wiki/Arlington,_Texas" title="Arlington, Texas">Arlington</a>-based 6th district, an even more Republican area represented by the then ten-term incumbent <a href="/wiki/Joe_Barton" title="Joe Barton">Joe Barton</a> (who vacates the seat in 2019). Barton represented 96 percent of the population of the new district. Believing that he stood no realistic chance of staying in Congress, Turner decided not to seek a fifth term in 2004.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-political_career">Post-political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Turner_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was briefly mentioned in 2006 as a potential candidate for governor of Texas against <a href="/wiki/Rick_Perry" title="Rick Perry">Rick Perry</a> or the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> seat then held by Republican <a href="/wiki/Kay_Bailey_Hutchison" title="Kay Bailey Hutchison">Kay Bailey Hutchison</a> but now the domain of Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Cruz" title="Ted Cruz">Ted Cruz</a>.
</p><p>In 2005, Turner joined the Washington office of <a href="/wiki/Arnold_%26_Porter" title="Arnold &amp; Porter">Arnold &amp; Porter</a>, LLP as the head of the Public Policy and Legislative Practice Group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  In 2017, he became associated with the Austin law firm of Hance Scarborough, LLP, where he is Of Counsel and works in their government relations practice group.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jim_Turner_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He and his wife, Ginny, were married in 1970. They have two children, <a href="/wiki/John_Turner_(Texas_politician)" title="John Turner (Texas politician)">John Turner</a> and Susan Turner Nold, who are both <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">attorneys</a> in Texas. John Turner, who practices in the <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a> firm of <a href="/wiki/Haynes_and_Boone" title="Haynes and Boone">Haynes and Boone</a>, is a state representative for House District 114.
</p>
