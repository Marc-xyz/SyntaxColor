	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Selarang Barracks incident</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">British prisoner-of-war revolt</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="437" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg/220px-The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg/330px-The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg/440px-The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:The_No_Escape_Incident_Art.IWMART1541729.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A view from above of a square crowded with prisoners-of-war surrounded by the buildings of a military barracks. There is one building to the left and one to the right, with another building in the background with trees and vegetation either side of it. Within the square are thousands of prisoners, some visible at work in the foreground, and a large number of tents, some with a red cross symbol painted on them.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Selarang Barracks incident</b>, also known as the <b>Barrack Square incident</b> or the <b>Selarang Square Squeeze</b>, was a revolt of British and Australian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prisoners-of-war" title="Prisoners-of-war">prisoners-of-war</a> (POWs) interned in a Japanese camp in <a href="/wiki/Changi" title="Changi">Changi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>. 
</p><p>The events started on 30 August 1942 after the Japanese recaptured four POWs escaped from the Selarang Barracks camps, and required that the other prisoners sign a pledge not to escape. After they refused, they were forced to crowd in the areas around the barracks square for nearly five days with little water and no sanitation. The executions of the recaptured POWs failed to break the men. The commanders, however, finally capitulated on 5 September when their men started to fall ill and die from <a href="/wiki/Dysentery" title="Dysentery">dysentery</a>. Upon signing the pledge, the men were allowed to return to the barracks buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Changi">Changi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Changi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Changi_Museum_Display.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Changi_Museum_Display.jpg/220px-Changi_Museum_Display.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Changi_Museum_Display.jpg/330px-Changi_Museum_Display.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Changi_Museum_Display.jpg/440px-Changi_Museum_Display.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Changi_Museum_Display.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A display of POW artefacts at the Changi Chapel and Museum. The picture in the background shows Changi Prison during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a></div></div></div>
<p>Built in 1938, the Selarang Barracks was part of the Changi Garrison, a heavily fortified coastal defence where most of the British forces were based during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Singapore" title="Battle of Singapore">Battle of Singapore</a>. The Selarang Barracks housed the 2nd Battalion <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Highlanders" title="Gordon Highlanders">Gordon Highlanders</a>, a British Army infantry regiment which recruited its soldiers mainly from North East Scotland. The Royal Engineers and the 9th Coastal Artillery Regiment of the Royal Artillery were based in nearby Kitchener Barracks and Roberts Barracks respectively. After the British <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Singapore#Fall_of_Singapore" title="Battle of Singapore">surrender of Singapore</a> on 15 February 1942, Allied POWs were ordered by the Japanese to march to Changi for internment. As the British-built <a href="/wiki/Changi_Prison" title="Changi Prison">Changi Prison</a> was already crowded with Allied POWs and civilians, the surrounding barracks including Selarang Barracks were used by the Japanese as a holding area for Australian and British POWs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 30 August 1942, as a pre-emptive measure, the newly arrived Japanese Commander General Shimpei Fukuye wanted the wholly British and Australian POWs interned at Selarang Barracks in Changi to sign a "No Escape Pledge" after the recapture of four escaped prisoners from Changi Prison earlier. The four escapees were Australian <a href="/wiki/Corporal" title="Corporal">Corporal</a> Rodney Breavington and <a href="/wiki/Private_(rank)" title="Private (rank)">Private</a> Victor Gale, and English soldiers, Private Harold Waters and Private Eric Fletcher. The pledge reads: "I the undersigned, hereby solemnly swear on my honour that I will not, under any circumstances, attempt to escape."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> With three exceptions, everyone refused to sign, because the prisoners saw it as their duty to escape if they could. Under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geneva_Convention" title="Geneva Convention">Geneva Convention</a>, POWs had the right to attempt to escape and they were not supposed to be punished if they were recaptured. However, at that time, Japan was not a signatory to the Geneva Convention. General Fukuye was furious at the mass display of insubordination and the following day he ordered all prisoners, except the three who had agreed to sign, to congregate at the parade square in Selarang Barracks. What ensued was to become known as the "Selarang Barracks Incident".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Selarang Barracks, originally built to accommodate 800 men, consisted of a parade ground surrounded on three sides by three-storey buildings. A number of smaller houses for officers and married couples were spread out in the spacious grounds. Nearly 17,000 men<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> crammed into a parade ground of about 128 by 210 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metres" title="Metres">metres</a> and in the surrounding areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-1">[1]</a></sup> An Australian POW, George Aspinall documented the situation:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The first and most urgent problem we had to face up to was the lack of toilet facilities. Each barracks building had about four to six toilets, which were flushed from small cisterns on the roofs. But the Japanese cut the water off, and these toilets couldn't be used. The Japanese only allowed one water tap to be used, and people used to line up in the early hours of the morning and that queue would go on all day. You were allowed one water bottle of water per man per day, just one quart for your drinking, washing, and everything else. Not that there was much washing done under the circumstances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Executions">Executions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Executions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When there were no signs of the POWs backing down on the third day, General Fukuye ordered the Commander of the British and Australian troops in Changi, Lt-Col E. B. Holmes and his deputy, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Colonel" title="Lieutenant Colonel">Lieutenant Colonel</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Galleghan" title="Frederick Galleghan">Frederick Galleghan</a>, to attend the execution of the four recent escapees: Breavington, Gale, Waters and Fletcher. One of the Australians, Breavington, pleaded to no avail that he was solely responsible for the escape attempt and should be the only one executed. Their executions were carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Indian_National_Army" title="Indian National Army">Indian National Army</a> guards with rifles on 2 September. The initial volley was non-fatal, and the wounded men had to plead to be finished off.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the executions, the prisoners remained firm as the days ensued. Without food and little water available and coupled with latrine pits, kitchens and hospital beds crowded into an area of about a square kilometre, dysentery broke out quickly and the sick began to die. Realising that more would die needlessly, the prisoners’ commanders decided that they and their men would sign the pledge "under duress". On 4 September, Lt-Col Holmes issued a written order to his men:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The requirement by the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a>, issued under their Order No.17 dated 31 August '42 that all ranks of the POW Camp Changi, should be given the opportunity to sign a certificate of promise not to escape, has now been amended in a revised Imperial Japanese Army Order No.17 dated 2 September '42 to a definite order that all officers, <a href="/wiki/Non-commissioned_officer" title="Non-commissioned officer">NCOs</a>, and Men of the POW Camp shall sign this undertaking.
</p><p>I therefore now order that these certificates will be signed by all ranks, and handed by Area Commanders to Command Headquarters by 1100 hrs on 5 September '42. The circumstances in which I have been compelled to issue this order will be made the subject of Selarang Special Order No. 3 which will be issued later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>As the Japanese weren't familiar with British names, the POWs signed using false or meaningless names. One of the most common signatures among the Australians was <a href="/wiki/Ned_Kelly" title="Ned Kelly">Ned Kelly</a>, a famous Australian folk hero.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ABC_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-5">[5]</a></sup> After the signing was completed, the Japanese allowed the prisoners back to their former areas on 5 September, thus ending the incident.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore_War_Crimes_Trial">Singapore War Crimes Trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Singapore War Crimes Trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the Singapore War Crimes Trial in 1946, General Fukuye was sentenced to death on 28 February and executed by firing squad on 27 April at the spot where the four POWs had been shot three years earlier. Fukuye died instantaneously after shouting "Banzai" (Long live the Emperor.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Epitaphs">Epitaphs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Epitaphs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The four executed POWs were later honoured and buried at the <a href="/wiki/Kranji_War_Memorial" title="Kranji War Memorial">Kranji War Memorial</a> after the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selarang_Barracks_today">Selarang Barracks today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Selarang Barracks today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the war, the Selarang Barracks became the home for most of the Australian Army units of <a href="/wiki/ANZUK" title="ANZUK">ANZUK</a>, a tripartite force formed by Australia, New Zealand and the United Kingdom to defend the <a href="/wiki/Asia-Pacific" title="Asia-Pacific">Asia-Pacific</a> region, until its disbandment in 1974. Today, Selarang Barracks is the headquarters for the 9th Division of the <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Armed_Forces" title="Singapore Armed Forces">Singapore Armed Forces</a> (SAF), and access to the camp is restricted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remembrance">Remembrance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Selarang_Barracks_incident&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Remembrance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Changi_Museum.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="525" data-file-width="700" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Changi_Museum.jpg/220px-Changi_Museum.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Changi_Museum.jpg/330px-Changi_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Changi_Museum.jpg/440px-Changi_Museum.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Changi_Museum.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The main entrance leading to the Changi Chapel and Museum at Upper Changi Road North, Singapore. In the background lies the replica of the <a href="/wiki/Changi_Prison#Changi_Chapel_and_Museum" title="Changi Prison">Changi Chapel</a></div></div></div>
<p>In order to honour the deeds and inspirational stories that unfolded in Changi during the war, the Changi Chapel and Museum was built in 1988 as a dedication to all those who lived and died in Singapore during the years of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. It also serves as an important educational institution and resource centre with documentation of significant events of the Japanese Occupation in Singapore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <i>Selarang Barracks Incident</i>, <i>Double Tenth Incident</i> and other similar stories that were mentioned by the survivors of Changi Prison are retold on the <a href="/wiki/Storyboard" title="Storyboard">storyboard</a> displays for posterity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> There are also showcases containing tools, materials and personal belongings of POWs and other artefacts related or used during World War II. The items displayed in the showcases were donated from organizations, POWs and their families, as well as other visitors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 19 April 1996, <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Foreign_Affairs_(Australia)" title="Minister for Foreign Affairs (Australia)">Australian Foreign Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Downer" title="Alexander Downer">Alexander Downer</a> and his wife, Nicky, made a personal pilgrimage to Changi Prison, to see the cell his father was kept in as a prisoner-of-war during World War II. His father, Sir Alexander Downer, was interned in Selarang Barracks from 1942 to 1943 and then in Changi Prison from 1943 to 1944. Downer also visited the Changi Memorial Chapel, where Sir Alexander had worshipped during his internment, the Selarang Barracks, and the camp parade square, where 17,000 Allied POWs were ordered to assemble in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
