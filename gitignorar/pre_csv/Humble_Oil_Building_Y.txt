<p>The <b>Humble Oil Building</b>, is a historic office building, designed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance_architecture" title="Italian Renaissance architecture">Italian Renaissance architecture</a> style,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houston_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-houston-2">[2]</a></sup>  located at 1212 Main Street in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a> and listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. It was constructed by <a href="/wiki/Humble_Oil" title="Humble Oil">Humble Oil and Refining Company</a> in 1921. The tower section was added on to the building in 1936. The building complex served as the company headquarters for <a href="/wiki/Humble_Oil" title="Humble Oil">Humble Oil and Refining Company</a> from 1921 until 1963, when the company moved into what is now the <a href="/wiki/ExxonMobil_Building" title="ExxonMobil Building">ExxonMobil Building</a> at 800 Bell Street. In 2003, the building complex was renovated for use as a combination hotel and apartments, the apartment section was converted to additional hotel rooms in 2015.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Humble_Oil_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original 9-story structure occupied half of a city block between Main Street and Travis Street, with the front facing Polk Street. This original structure was designed by prominent New York architects <a href="/wiki/Clinton_and_Russell" title="Clinton and Russell">Clinton and Russell</a> and cost approximately $1.2 million. It was the largest office building in Houston at the time, containing approximately 196,000 sq ft (18,200 m<sup>2</sup>) of space.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houston_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-houston-2">[2]</a></sup> A 1932 renovation added a central <a href="/wiki/Air_conditioning" title="Air conditioning">air conditioning</a> system to the building, the first in any Houston office building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HH_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HH-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Humble Oil and Refining Company expanded the building in 1936 with an adjacent 17-story tower. This expansion was designed by <a href="/wiki/John_F._Staub" title="John F. Staub">John F. Staub</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Franzheim" title="Kenneth Franzheim">Kenneth Franzheim</a>. Additional expansions were made in 1940 and 1947; and the complex now contains 529,809 sq ft (49,220.9 m<sup>2</sup>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houston_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-houston-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-chron-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997, the building complex was purchased by Historic Restoration Inc., with plans to adapt the buildings into a mixed-use hotel, apartments, and retail space. The plans moved forward in 1999 with a loan from the City of Houston in the amount of $4.6 million in order to encourage the development. The plans were completed in 2003 and the building opened as a Courtyard by Marriott hotel, a Residence Inn hotel, and the Humble Tower apartments. It contained 360 hotel rooms, 82 'luxury' apartments, 6,721 sq ft (624.4 m<sup>2</sup>) of street-level retail, and a parking garage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-houston_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-houston-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chron_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-chron-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-biz_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-biz-5">[5]</a></sup> It was listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a> in 1999, under the category "Late 19th and 20th Century Revivals".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nris_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nris-1">[1]</a></sup> The apartment section was converted to a third Marriott-branded Hotel in 2015, a Spring Hill Suites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
