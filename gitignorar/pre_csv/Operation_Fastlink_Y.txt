	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Operation Fastlink</h1>

<p><b>Operation FastLink</b> is a coordination of four separate, simultaneous <a href="/wiki/Sting_operation" title="Sting operation">undercover investigations</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">FBI</a>, the FBI Cyber Division, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a>, the Computer Crimes and Intellectual Property Section (CCIPS) of the Criminal Division and <a href="/wiki/Interpol" title="Interpol">Interpol</a>. The four different investigations have not been publicly enumerated, but the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">U.S. Department of Justice</a> has said in at least one <a class="external text" href="https://www.usdoj.gov/criminal/cybercrime/laiPlea.htm" rel="nofollow">press release</a> that "Operation Higher Education" is the largest component, with participation from twelve nations. <a class="external text" href="https://www.usdoj.gov/opa/pr/2006/February/06_crm_103.html" rel="nofollow">Mention</a> has also been made of an investigation into pre-release music groups led by FBI agents from the Washington Field Office. As of March 6, 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Investigation" title="Federal Bureau of Investigation">Federal Bureau of Investigation</a> <a class="external text" href="https://www.fbi.gov/newhaven/press-releases/2009/nh030609b.htm" rel="nofollow">states</a> that <b>Operation FastLink</b> has yielded 60 convictions. The raids occurred in similar fashion to those from <a href="/wiki/Operation_Buccaneer" title="Operation Buccaneer">Operation Buccaneer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_Site_Down" title="Operation Site Down">Operation Site Down</a>. Other somewhat-related law enforcement actions include Operation Gridlock and <a href="/wiki/Operation_D-Elite" title="Operation D-Elite">Operation D-Elite</a>.
</p><p>The operation led to the successful busts of nearly 100 individuals involved in illegal copying of copyrighted software (i.e. "<a href="/wiki/Copyright_infringement" title="Copyright infringement">software piracy</a>"), and alterations thereof, worldwide. There were around 120 total searches executed in 27 <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">state</a> and in 10 foreign countries. Foreign searches were conducted in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.
</p><p>Among the prolific <a href="/wiki/Warez" title="Warez">warez</a> release groups targeted by Fastlink were <a href="/wiki/Fairlight_(group)" title="Fairlight (group)">Fairlight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kalisto_(warez_group)" title="Kalisto (warez group)">Kalisto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echelon_(warez)" title="Echelon (warez)">Echelon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Class_(warez)" title="Class (warez)">Class</a>, and DEViANCE—all of which specialized in spreading unlicensed copies of computer and console video games. Recent convictions have included members of music release groups <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Production_Crew" title="Apocalypse Production Crew">Apocalypse Production Crew</a> and Chromance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cases">Cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Fastlink&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Jathan Desir, 26 (aka jd333), a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a> student of <a href="/wiki/Iowa_City,_Iowa" title="Iowa City, Iowa">Iowa City, Iowa</a>, pleaded guilty on December 22, 2004 to charges related to his role and was sentenced to one year's probation, $5,000 fine, and $300 CVF assessment, <a class="external text" href="https://www.plainsite.org/dockets/ing0e2q6/iowa-southern-district-court/usa-v-desir/" rel="nofollow">which was completed in 2010</a>. The case was 4:04-cr-00336 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.iasd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Iowa</a>.</li>
<li>Abell (a.k.a. "joebob") pleaded guilty to conspiracy to commit copyright infringement on February 28, 2005. He was sentenced to 15 months in prison, 400 hours of community service and two years' probation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The case is 5:04-cr-00681 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.txwd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Western District of Texas</a>.</li>
<li>Lai (a.k.a. "doplgnger") pleaded guilty to one count of conspiracy to commit criminal copyright infringement on January 6, 2006 related to his administering several warez FTP sites. He was sentenced on June 18, 2007 to three years of probation, the first six months of which he must serve confined to his home. He was also ordered to pay a fine in the amount of $7,200 and to perform 120 hours of community service. The case is 3:06-cr-00004 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ctd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut</a>.</li>
<li>Kleinberg (a.k.a. "basilisk") pleaded guilty on March 8, 2005 and faces a maximum sentence of 10 years in prison. Sentencing was originally scheduled for July 1, 2005, but has been delayed to January 6, 2006. He admitted to being a supplier for <a href="/wiki/Fairlight_(group)" title="Fairlight (group)">Fairlight</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalisto_(warez_group)" title="Kalisto (warez group)">Kalisto</a> while being Executive Editor of <a class="external text" href="http://www.game-over.net/" rel="nofollow">Game Over Online Magazine</a>. The case is 3:05-cr-00049 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ctd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut</a>. Kleinberg eventually received a reduced sentence of 3 years' probation, 200 hours of community service and was ordered to pay a $15,000 fine. <a class="external autonumber" href="http://areacontrol.org/news.php?extend.518.9" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>.</li>
<li>Jeffrey Lerman (a.k.a. "ht"), 20, a <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park" title="University of Maryland, College Park">University of Maryland, College Park</a> student from <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>, New York, pleaded guilty on March 8, 2005 and was sentenced to six months of <a href="/wiki/House_arrest" title="House arrest">house arrest</a>, three years' probation, and a $7,500 fine.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.technologynewsdaily.com/node/7737" rel="nofollow">[2]</a> Lerman repackaged and restructured PlayStation 2 games, originally distributed on DVDs, for redistribution on CDs for <a href="/wiki/Kalisto_(warez_group)" title="Kalisto (warez group)">Kalisto</a>. The case is 3:05-cr-00050 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ctd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut</a>.</li>
<li>Albert Bryndza, 32, of <a href="/wiki/Flushing,_Queens" title="Flushing, Queens">Flushing, New York</a>, pleaded guilty on March 8, 2005 and faces a maximum sentence of 5 years in prison. Sentencing was originally scheduled for July 1, 2005, but has been delayed to January 13, 2006. The case is 3:05-cr-00051 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.ctd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the District of Connecticut</a>.</li>
<li>Weiss pleaded guilty on September 12, 2005 to criminal copyright infringement. Sentenced on January 12, 2006 to three years' probation, pay $20,914 in restitution, and complete 500 hours of community service. Unknown case from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.nyed.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of New York</a>.</li>
<li>Ringe was indicted for criminal copyright infringement on September 13, 2005. Unknown case from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.azd.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the District of Arizona</a>.</li>
<li>Christopher M. Streit was originally indicted on a criminal copyright infringement charge in the Southern District of Iowa. On March 10, 2005, his case was transferred to the Eastern District of North Carolina. He pleaded guilty on April 11, 2005 and was sentenced to five years' probation, 180 days of home confinement, and imposed a $500 fine on September 6, 2005. The case is 5:05-cr-00066 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.nced.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of North Carolina</a>.</li>
<li>Joseph J. Huss, Jr., pleaded guilty to two counts of conspiracy and criminal copyright infringement on July 28, 2005. He was sentenced to five years' probation, a $200 special assessment, and a $5,000 fine for each of the counts on November 10, 2005. The case is 2:05-cr-00002 from <a class="external text" href="http://www.paed.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania</a>.</li>
<li>Timothy Mentzer's three criminal copyright infringement charges were transferred from the Southern District of Iowa. He pleaded guilty on August 3, 2005. He was scheduled to be sentenced June 19, 2006. The case is 2:05-cr-00002 from <a class="external text" href="http://www.paed.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Pennsylvania</a>.</li>
<li>George Scott Hayes, 31, of Danville, Virginia, pleaded guilty on February 13, 2006 to one count of criminal copyright infringement related to his involvement in music release group "Chromance." Sentencing was scheduled for May 19, 2006. The case is 1:06-cr-00032 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.vaed.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia</a>.</li>
<li>Derek A. Borchardt (a.k.a. 'Mickey'), 21, of Charlotte, North Carolina; Ramiesh Yogarada, 18, of Winston-Salem, North Carolina; Matthew B. Howard, 24, of Longmont, Colorado; and Aaron O. Jones, 31, of Hillsboro, Oregon each pleaded guilty on February 28, 2006 to one count of conspiracy to commit copyright infringement for their involvement in the music release group <a href="/wiki/Apocalypse_Production_Crew" title="Apocalypse Production Crew">Apocalypse Production Crew</a>. Sentencing was scheduled for May 19, 2006. The case is 1:06-cr-00041 from the <a class="external text" href="http://www.vaed.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia</a>.</li>
<li>Sergey Ribiakost, 21, of Bardonia, New York, pleaded guilty on April 17, 2007 to one count of conspiracy to commit copyright infringement for his involvement with the music release group Apocalypse Production Crew. Sentencing was scheduled for July 10, 2007. The case is in the <a class="external text" href="http://www.vaed.uscourts.gov/" rel="nofollow">U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia</a></li>
<li>Bryan Thomas Black, 30, of Waterloo, Illinois pleaded guilty on March 6, 2009 to one count of conspiracy to commit criminal infringement of a copyright for his involvement in a multinational software infringement organization. Sentencing was scheduled for May 26, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USDOJ_BryanThomasBlack_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-USDOJ_BryanThomasBlack-2">[2]</a></sup></li></ul>
