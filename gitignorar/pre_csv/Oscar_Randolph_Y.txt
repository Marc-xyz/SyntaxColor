<p><b>Oscar De Wolf Randolph</b> (September 28, 1884 – December 31, 1964) was an <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> player and coach and <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal</a> minister. Randolph Hall at <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Episcopal_School" title="Virginia Episcopal School">Virginia Episcopal School</a> was named in his honor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> One description of his personality was that of a "cold fish".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was the son of <a href="/wiki/Isham_Randolph" title="Isham Randolph">Isham Randolph</a>, and a descendant of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_Randolph" title="Sir John Randolph">Sir John Randolph</a>.
</p><p>Randolph played <a href="/wiki/Quarterback" title="Quarterback">quarterback</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Cavaliers_football" title="Virginia Cavaliers football">Virginia Cavaliers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">University of Virginia</a>. He was a protege of then Virginia quarterback <a href="/wiki/John_Beverly_Pollard" title="John Beverly Pollard">John Pollard</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and "one of the fastest quarters Virginia has ever had."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 1906, Randolph was selected for the <a href="/wiki/College_Football_All-Southern_Team" title="College Football All-Southern Team">All-Southern team</a> published in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Post" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He was a teammate of <a href="/wiki/William_C._Gloth" title="William C. Gloth">Billy Gloth</a>.
</p><p>After UVA, Randolph entered the <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Theological_Seminary" title="Virginia Theological Seminary">Virginia Theological Seminary</a> and helped coach the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_High_School_(Alexandria,_Virginia)" title="Episcopal High School (Alexandria, Virginia)">Episcopal High School</a> football team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He helped coach the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backfield" title="Backfield">backfields</a> of Virginia and <a href="/wiki/VMI_Keydets_football" title="VMI Keydets football">VMI</a> in later years.
</p><p>He was the rector of <a href="/wiki/Robert_E._Lee" title="Robert E. Lee">Robert E. Lee</a> Memorial Episcopal Church  in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Virginia" title="Lexington, Virginia">Lexington</a>, and conducted the funeral of Lee's son.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
