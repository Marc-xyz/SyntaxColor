<p><b>Israeli Sign Language</b>, or ISL, is the most commonly used <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">sign language</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf" title="Deaf">deaf</a> <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a> of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. Some other sign languages are also used in Israel, among them <a href="/wiki/Al-Sayyid_Bedouin_Sign_Language" title="Al-Sayyid Bedouin Sign Language">Al-Sayyid Bedouin Sign Language</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Israeli_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The history of ISL goes back to 1873 in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, where <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marcus_Reich&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marcus Reich (page does not exist)">Marcus Reich</a>, a <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Germany" title="History of the Jews in Germany">German Jew</a>, opened a special school for Jewish deaf children. At the time, it was considered one of the best of its kind, which made it popular with Jewish deaf children from all over the world as well as non-Jews. In 1932, several teachers from this school opened the first school for Jewish deaf children in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>. The sign language used in the Jerusalemite school was influenced by the <a href="/wiki/German_Sign_Language" title="German Sign Language">German Sign Language</a> (DGS), but other sign languages or signing systems brought by immigrants also contributed to the emerging language, which started out as a <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a>. A local <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole</a> gradually emerged, which became ISL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MSPA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-MSPA-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>ISL still shares many features and vocabulary items with DGS, although it is too far apart today to be considered a dialect of the latter.
</p><p>During the 1940s, ISL became the language of a well-established community of Jewish deaf people in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv" title="Tel Aviv">Tel Aviv</a>. Today ISL is the most used and taught sign language in Israel, and serves as the main mode of communication for most deaf people in Israel, including Jewish, <a href="/wiki/Arab_citizens_of_Israel" title="Arab citizens of Israel">Muslim and Christian Arabs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Druze" title="Druze">Druze</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouins</a>. Some Arab, Druze, and Bedouin towns and villages have sign languages of their own.
</p><p>In addition to ISL, there is also <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> <a href="/wiki/Manually_coded_language" title="Manually coded language">manually coded language</a> used as a tool to teaching deaf children the Hebrew language, and for communication between deaf and hearing people.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deaf_community">Deaf community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Israeli_Sign_Language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Deaf community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The beginnings of an established Deaf community in Israel started with the 1936 Tel-Aviv <a href="/wiki/Purim" title="Purim">Purim</a> parade, when groups from Jerusalem, Tel-Aviv and Haifa met for the first time. This led to the creation of the Association of the Deaf in Israel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Currents-Keller_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Currents-Keller-3">[3]</a></sup> The first official board was elected in 1944, with Moshe Bamberger as its first president. The association organized lectures, trips, and holiday celebrations. The community grew as refugees from World War Two fled to Israel, and the association helped new arrivals integrate into the Israeli community by helping them learn Israeli Sign Language and helping them find work. The association completed their Tel-Aviv headquarters, Helen Keller House, in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Currents-Keller_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Currents-Keller-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The first school for the deaf was established in 1932, a strict boarding school in Jerusalem that taught <a href="/wiki/Oralism" title="Oralism">oralism</a>. Two other oralist schools were established in Tel-Aviv in 1941 and Haifa in 1949. The emphasis on oralism began to change in the 1970s, when Izchak Schlesinger began to research ISL, and with Israel hosting the Fourth International Conference on Deafness in 1973. 
</p>
