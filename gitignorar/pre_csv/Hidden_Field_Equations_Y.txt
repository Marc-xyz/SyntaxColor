	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hidden Field Equations</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Hidden Fields Equations (HFE)</b>, also known as <b>HFE trapdoor function</b>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_key" title="Public key">public key</a> <a href="/wiki/Cryptosystem" title="Cryptosystem">cryptosystem</a> which was introduced at <a href="/wiki/Eurocrypt" title="Eurocrypt">Eurocrypt</a> in 1996 and proposed by <span class="languageicon">(in French)</span> <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Patarin" title="fr:Jacques Patarin">Jacques Patarin</a> following the idea of the <a href="/wiki/Matsumoto_zeta_function" title="Matsumoto zeta function">Matsumoto</a> and Imai system. It is based on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynomials" title="Polynomials">polynomials</a> over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finite_fields" title="Finite fields">finite fields</a> <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <msub>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi mathvariant="double-struck">F</mi>
          </mrow>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi>q</mi>
          </mrow>
        </msub>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q}}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img alt="{\mathbb  {F}}_{q}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbb96e056c071d13fc7702013f9273e7f5cd88a7" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:2.409ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span> of different size to disguise the relationship between the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Private_key" title="Private key">private key</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_key" title="Public key">public key</a>. HFE is in fact a family which consists of basic HFE and combinatorial versions of HFE. The HFE family of cryptosystems is based on the hardness of the problem of finding solutions to a system of multivariate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadratic_equations" title="Quadratic equations">quadratic equations</a> (the so-called MQ problem) since it uses private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affine_transformations" title="Affine transformations">affine transformations</a> to hide the extension field and the private <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polynomials" title="Polynomials">polynomials</a>. Hidden Field Equations also have been used to construct digital signature schemes, e.g. Quartz and Sflash.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="HFE_variations">HFE variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hidden_Field_Equations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: HFE variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hidden Field Equations has four basic variations namely <b>+,-,v and f</b> and it is possible to combine them in various way. The basic principle is the following:
</p>
<dl><dd>01. The <b>+</b> sign consists of linearity mixing of the public equations with some random equations.</dd>
<dd>02. The <b>-</b> sign is due to <a href="/wiki/Adi_Shamir" title="Adi Shamir">Adi Shamir</a> and intends to remove the redundancy 'r' of the public equations.</dd>
<dd>03. The <b>f</b> sign consists of fixing some <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle f}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <mi>f</mi>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle f}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img alt="f" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:1.279ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span> input variables of the public key.</dd>
<dd>04. The <b>v</b> sign is defined as a construction and sometimes quite complex such that the inverse of the function can be found only if some v of the variables called vinegar variables are fixed. This idea is due to Jacques Patarin.</dd></dl>
<p>The operations above preserve to some extent the trapdoor solvability of the function.
</p><p>HFE- and HFEv are very useful in signature schemes as they prevent from slowing down the signature generation and also enhance the overall security of HFE whereas for <a href="/wiki/Encryption" title="Encryption">encryption</a> both HFE- and HFEv will lead to a rather slow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decryption" title="Decryption">decryption</a> process so neither too many equations can be removed (HFE-) nor too many variables should be added (HFEv). Both HFE- and HFEv were used to obtain Quartz.
</p><p>For encryption, the situation is better with HFE+ since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decryption" title="Decryption">decryption</a> process takes the same amount of time, however the public key has more equations than variables.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="HFE_attacks">HFE attacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hidden_Field_Equations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: HFE attacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two famous attacks on HFE:
</p><p>Recover the Private Key (<a href="/wiki/Adi_Shamir" title="Adi Shamir">Shamir</a>-Kipnis): The key point of this attack is to recover the private key as sparse univariate polynomials over the extension field <span class="mwe-math-element"><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q^{n}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
  <semantics>
    <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
      <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
        <msub>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mi mathvariant="double-struck">F</mi>
          </mrow>
          <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <msup>
              <mi>q</mi>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                <mi>n</mi>
              </mrow>
            </msup>
          </mrow>
        </msub>
      </mstyle>
    </mrow>
    <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle \mathbb {F} _{q^{n}}}</annotation>
  </semantics>
</math></span><img alt="{\mathbb  {F}}_{{q^{n}}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/885cafbbd811da8ecf8097b4dda7c6587f50100a" style="vertical-align: -1.005ex; width:3.381ex; height:2.843ex;"/></span>. The attack only works for basic HFE and fails for all its variations.
</p><p>Fast Gröbner Bases (Faugère): The idea of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Charles_Faug%C3%A8re" title="Jean-Charles Faugère">Faugère</a>'s attacks is to use fast algorithm to compute a <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%B6bner_basis" title="Gröbner basis">Gröbner basis</a> of  the system of polynomial equations. Faugère broke the HFE challenge 1 in 96 hours in 2002, and in 2003 Faugère and <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Joux" title="Antoine Joux">Joux</a> worked together on the security of HFE.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
