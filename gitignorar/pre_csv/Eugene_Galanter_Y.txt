<p><b>Eugene Galanter</b> was one of the modern founders of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a>. He was an academic in the field of <a href="/wiki/Experimental_psychology" title="Experimental psychology">experimental psychology</a> and an author. Dr. Galanter was Professor Emeritus of Psychology end Quondam Director of the Psychophysics Laboratory at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  He was also the co-founder,  Chairman of the Board of Directors and Chief Scientific Officer of Children’s Progress,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> an award-winning New York City-based company that specializes in the use of computer technology in early education.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The company's assessments and reports have been used in 40 states and 9 countries.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Galanter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After serving in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Galanter attended <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmore College</a>, receiving an Honors <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">B.A.</a> in 1950. He went on to graduate school in psychology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> and after receiving his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph.D." title="Ph.D.">Ph.D.</a> in 1953, he was appointed Assistant Professor of <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_psychology" title="Mathematical psychology">Mathematical Psychology</a> in the University of Pennsylvania's Department of Psychology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Additionally, during several leaves in the 1950s, Galanter collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Smith_Stevens" title="Stanley Smith Stevens">S. S. Stevens</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s psychoacoustics laboratory, co-authoring several publications.
</p><p>While a fellow at the <a href="/wiki/Center_for_Advanced_Study_in_the_Behavioral_Sciences" title="Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences">Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a> Galanter began a collaboration with <a href="/wiki/George_Armitage_Miller" title="George Armitage Miller">George A. Miller</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Karl_H._Pribram" title="Karl H. Pribram">Karl H. Pribram</a>, that resulted in the book <i>Plans and the Structure of Behavior</i>, a seminal work in the development of <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive psychology</a> published in 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mind_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-mind-7">[7]</a></sup> By 1956, Galanter had started working towards a theoretical model that would integrate <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">cognitive processes</a> into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Behavioral_psychology" title="Behavioral psychology">behaviorist's</a> stimulus-response framework.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In <i>Plans and the Structure of Behavior</i>, Miller, Galanter, and Pribram proposed that "some mediating organization of experience is necessary" between the stimulus and its behavioral response, i.e., that a cognitive feedback loop, which includes monitoring devices, must control the acquisition of the stimulus-response relationship. This feedback loop, the proposed fundamental unit of behavior, was referred to in <i>Plans</i> as the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=T.O.T.E.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="T.O.T.E. (page does not exist)">T.O.T.E.</a></i>, an abbreviation of its steps - test, operate, test, exit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the publication of <i>Plans and the Structure of Behavior</i>, Galanter, along with colleagues Robert Bush and <a href="/wiki/R._Duncan_Luce" title="R. Duncan Luce">Duncan Luce</a>, worked to move the field of psychology closer to the other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_sciences" title="Natural sciences">natural sciences</a> by advancing the position of mathematical psychology within the discipline as a whole. Their argument was that all psychological phenomena, if properly measured and reduced to quantifiable variables, would reveal law-like rules that govern human behavior and thought. Together, Galanter, Bush, and Luce edited the three volume <i>Handbook of Mathematical Psychology</i>, which was published in 1963.
</p><p>After leaving the University of Pennsylvania, Galanter held positions at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Washington" title="University of Washington">University of Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> before becoming the Gelhorn Professor of Psychology at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, where he was also Director of the Psychophysics Laboratory and, for a time, Chairman of the Department of Psychology. In addition to his work in <a href="/wiki/Psychophysics" title="Psychophysics">psychophysics</a> and mathematical psychology, Galanter continues to publish in various subfields of <a href="/wiki/Psychometrics" title="Psychometrics">psychometrics</a> including psychoeducational assessment and motivational measurement. Galanter has been honored by <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, which awarded him its Distinguished Scientist Research Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>After co-founding Children's Progress, Galanter went on to serve as Chief Scientific Officer. Galanter and his daughter Michelle Galanter co-invented and hold the United States patent for the Galanter Educational Evaluation Lattice,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which is licensed exclusively to Children’s Progress and is the basis for the Children’s Progress Academic Assessment (CPAA).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_assessment" title="Dynamic assessment">dynamic assessment</a> is based on a developmental model of learning and is grounded in the work of psychologist <a href="/wiki/Lev_Vygotsky" title="Lev Vygotsky">Lev Vygotsky</a>.  Unlike traditional assessments that determine whether a student’s answers are right or wrong, the CPAA adjusts to every response and provides hints and <a href="/wiki/Instructional_scaffolding" title="Instructional scaffolding">scaffolding</a> whenever a student struggles with a concept. This technology then gives teachers better information about each student, and enables them to deliver targeted instruction, tailored to each student’s <a href="/wiki/Zone_of_proximal_development" title="Zone of proximal development">zone of proximal development</a> (ZPD).
</p><p>As part of the NWEA (Northwest Evaluation Association) acquisition of Children's Progress in mid-2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Dr. Galanter worked with NWEA to further its mission : Helping all Kids Learn.
</p>
