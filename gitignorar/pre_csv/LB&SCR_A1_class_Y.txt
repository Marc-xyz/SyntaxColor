<p>The <a href="/wiki/London,_Brighton_and_South_Coast_Railway" title="London, Brighton and South Coast Railway">London, Brighton and South Coast Railway</a> (LB&amp;SCR) <b>A1 Class</b> is an <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> class of <a href="/wiki/0-6-0" title="0-6-0">0-6-0</a>T <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a>. Designed by <a href="/wiki/William_Stroudley" title="William Stroudley">William Stroudley</a>, 50 members of the class were built in 1872 and between 1874 and 1880, all at <a href="/wiki/Brighton_railway_works" title="Brighton railway works">Brighton Works</a>. The class has received several nicknames, initially being known as "<b>Rooters</b>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kesr.org.uk_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-kesr.org.uk-1">[1]</a></sup> by their south London crews. However, the engines were more famously known as "<b>Terriers</b>" on account of the distinctive 'bark' of the exhaust beat. Later in their careers, some engines were known as "<b>Hayling Billy</b>" on account of their work on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayling_Island_Branch_Line" title="Hayling Island Branch Line">Hayling Island branch line</a>. A pub of this name on the island was briefly home to the engine which is now No.W8 <i>Freshwater</i>.
</p><p>After displacement from their original workings out of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Bridge_railway_station" title="London Bridge railway station">London Bridge</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Victoria_railway_station" title="London Victoria railway station">London Victoria</a> by more powerful locomotives from the <a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_D1_class" title="LB&amp;SCR D1 class">D class</a> and the early stages of the <a href="/wiki/London,_Brighton_and_South_Coast_Railway#Railway_electrification" title="London, Brighton and South Coast Railway">LB&amp;SCR overhead electrification scheme</a>, some representatives of the class were sold to other operators, while the majority of the remainder were put to work on branch lines in <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> and on non-revenue earning work such as shunting.
</p><p>With these new uses being found, the class remained in use on the system, surviving to be taken into ownership by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" title="Southern Railway (Great Britain)">Southern Railway</a> from 1923 and by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Railways" title="British Railways">British Railways</a> from 1948. Although the number of engines dwindled following the Second World War as the work they were used for was either dieselised or lost to rail through the closure of branch lines and yards, a number continued in operation through into the 1960s, most famously on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hayling_Island_Branch_Line" title="Hayling Island Branch Line">Hayling Island Branch Line</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>. The withdrawal of the final members of the class finally came in 1963,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200736_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200736-2">[2]</a></sup> the line to Hayling having closed in November 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200649_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200649-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Eight members of the class were purchased privately for preservation, with two other examples being donated by British Railways to the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Railway_Museum" title="Canadian Railway Museum">Canadian Railway Museum</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Railway_Museum" title="National Railway Museum">National Railway Museum</a>. One of these engines, No.55 <i>Stepney</i>, is best known as being the first locomotive to arrive at the <a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a>, which was itself the first preserved standard gauge steam-operated passenger railway in the world when it opened in August 1960, and also for appearing in <a class="mw-redirect" href='/wiki/List_of_Railway_Series_Books#Stepney_the_"Bluebell"_Engine' title="List of Railway Series Books"><i>Stepney the "Bluebell" Engine</i></a>, one of the books in the original Railway Series written by the <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Awdry" title="Wilbert Awdry">Reverend W. Awdry</a> which also gave birth to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_the_Tank_Engine" title="Thomas the Tank Engine">Thomas the Tank Engine</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="London.2C_Brighton_.26_South_Coast_Railway_.281872-1923.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="London,_Brighton_&amp;_South_Coast_Railway_(1872-1923)">London, Brighton &amp; South Coast Railway (1872-1923)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: London, Brighton &amp; South Coast Railway (1872-1923)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1447" data-file-width="2403" decoding="async" height="132" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg/220px-Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg/330px-Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg/440px-Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Horsted_Keynes_Bluebell_Railway_train_in_1964_geograph-2687674-by-Ben-Brooksbank.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>No.55 <i>Stepney</i> carrying the early LB&amp;SCR 'Improved Engine Green' livery.</div></div></div>
<p>Originally known as 'A' class, these diminutive tank locomotives were designed in 1870 to haul commuter trains on the heavily congested lines in South and South-East London. These included routes from <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge_station" title="London Bridge station">London Bridge</a> to both <a href="/wiki/East_Croydon_station" title="East Croydon station">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Croydon_station" title="West Croydon station">West Croydon</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Victoria_Station" title="London Victoria Station">London Victoria</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sutton_railway_station_(London)" title="Sutton railway station (London)">Sutton</a> and the line from Victoria to London Bridge via <a href="/wiki/Peckham_Rye_railway_station" title="Peckham Rye railway station">Peckham Rye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark_Hill_railway_station" title="Denmark Hill railway station">Denmark Hill</a>, as well as operating on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/East_London_Railway" title="East London Railway">East London Railway</a> under the Thames through the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Tunnel" title="Thames Tunnel">Thames Tunnel</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Marc_Isambard_Brunel" title="Marc Isambard Brunel">Marc Isambard Brunel</a>. Six locomotives were built for these services during 1872, and were successful thanks to their high acceleration between the closely spaced station stops and the use of lightweight trains. A further 44 were built between June 1874 and September 1880 to complement the original six.
</p><p>The locomotives were finished in the livery known as "Stroudley's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Improved_Engine_Green" title="Improved Engine Green">Improved Engine Green</a>", which was actually an <a href="/wiki/Ochre" title="Ochre">ochre</a> colour, not green. This is historically attributed to colour-blindness, an affliction from which Stroudley reportedly suffered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The actual shade of the livery has been much disputed, with opinions ranging from a more yellow type of livery to a livery closer to brown, as worn by the model named <i>Como</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Brighton_Toy_and_Model_Museum" title="Brighton Toy and Model Museum">Brighton Toy and Model Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Shortly after construction, No.40 <i>Brighton</i> was chosen by <a href="/wiki/William_Stroudley" title="William Stroudley">William Stroudley</a> to represent the <a href="/wiki/London,_Brighton_and_South_Coast_Railway" title="London, Brighton and South Coast Railway">LB&amp;SCR</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Exhibition_of_1878" title="Paris Exhibition of 1878">Paris Exhibition of 1878</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwsteamrailway.com_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-iwsteamrailway.com-6">[6]</a></sup> and won a gold medal for workmanship. On a run from <a href="/wiki/Gare_Maritime_de_Dieppe" title="Gare Maritime de Dieppe">Dieppe</a> to Paris, arranged to persuade the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemins_de_Fer_de_l%27Ouest" title="Chemins de Fer de l'Ouest">Chemins de Fer de l'Ouest</a> that the company's boat trains that met the LB&amp;SCR ferries from Newhaven could make better time to the capital, <i>Brighton</i> maintained a speed of nearly 50 mph, previously unheard of on that line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the last two decades of the 19th century, London was expanding outwards as suburbs became more built up and were absorbed into the built-up area of London itself. Towns such as Croydon, Sutton and Norwood, which had previously been commuter towns separate from London, grew to form one large area of housing. Much of this was because of the success of the 'Terriers' themselves, as they had speeded up suburban passenger services, encouraging people to move out of the centre of London. Trains became progressively heavier while the need for shorter journey times remained the same. As a result, the engines gradually became largely unsuited for their original purpose, and they were replaced by the larger <a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_D1_class" title="LB&amp;SCR D1 class">D class</a> tank engines. However the 'Terriers' were so reliable that they were put on other work, often finding use on branch line passenger and freight workings and as shunting engines.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1519" data-file-width="1810" decoding="async" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG/220px-FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG/330px-FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG/440px-FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FENCHURCH_and_MET_Set_KC_23I02I08.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>No.672 'Fenchurch' carrying the later LB&amp;SCR umber livery. It is seen at Kingscote with a train of Metropolitan Railway carriages.</div></div></div>
<p>Twenty-three members of the class were withdrawn between 1898 and 1905, but the majority of these were sold in working order rather than scrapped. Purchasers of these locomotives included the <a href="/wiki/Newhaven,_East_Sussex" title="Newhaven, East Sussex">Newhaven Harbour Company</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Central_Railway" title="Isle of Wight Central Railway">Isle of Wight Central Railway</a> (four locomotives), <a href="/wiki/Pauling_%26_Co." title="Pauling &amp; Co.">Pauling &amp; Co.</a> (five locomotives), the <a href="/wiki/Kent_and_East_Sussex_Railway" title="Kent and East Sussex Railway">Kent and East Sussex Railway</a> (two locomotives), the <a href="/wiki/London_and_South_Western_Railway" title="London and South Western Railway">London and South Western Railway</a> (two locomotives), and the <a href="/wiki/South_Eastern_and_Chatham_Railway" title="South Eastern and Chatham Railway">South Eastern and Chatham Railway</a>. Others were transferred from capital to <a href="/wiki/SR_departmental_locomotives" title="SR departmental locomotives">departmental</a> (non-revenue earning) stock as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Station_pilot" title="Station pilot">pilots</a> at <a href="/wiki/Brighton_railway_works" title="Brighton railway works">Brighton locomotive works</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancing_Carriage_Works" title="Lancing Carriage Works">Lancing carriage works</a>. It is likely that the remainder of the class would have been withdrawn over the subsequent few years if the railway had not adopted <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Push-pull_train" title="Push-pull train">push-pull</a> or 'motor-train' working on many lightly used branch line services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley1969159_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley1969159-8">[8]</a></sup>  The 'A1' class (as the locomotives were designated after 1905) were found ideally suited for conversion to this form of working.
</p><p>Between 1911 and 1913 twelve survivors were re-boilered under the instruction of <a href="/wiki/D._E._Marsh" title="D. E. Marsh">Douglas Earle Marsh</a>, Stroudley's successor as CME of the LB&amp;SCR, with another four so treated after the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">Great War</a>; these engines formed the <b>A1X Class</b> with an increased weight of 28.2 long tons (28.7 t).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluebell-railway.co.uk_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-bluebell-railway.co.uk-9">[9]</a></sup> Other changes made during the rebuilding were the extension of the smokebox (removing the smokebox 'wings', and moving the sandboxes from this same area (where they formed a forward extension of the leading driving wheel splasher) to below the running plate (although some locos rebuilt later retained the original sandboxes).  The tube which had been used as part of the original condensing arrangement between the smokebox and side-tank was also removed.  The engines were repainted during this time in 'Marsh Umber' livery.
</p>
<h3><span id="Southern_Railway_.281923-1948.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_Railway_(1923-1948)">Southern Railway (1923-1948)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Southern Railway (1923-1948)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg/220px-Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg/330px-Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg/440px-Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W8_Wootton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Southern Railway 0-6-0T Class A1X W8 'Freshwater' runs round the train at Wootton on the Isle of Wight Steam Railway</div></div></div>
<p>Fifteen of the class remained in LB&amp;SCR stock at the formation of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" title="Southern Railway (Great Britain)">Southern Railway</a> on 1 January 1923, but this figure was increased to 24 by the inclusion of those which had previously been sold to other constituent railways. Several examples were withdrawn over the next few years, and two were sold to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weston,_Clevedon_and_Portishead_Railway" title="Weston, Clevedon and Portishead Railway">Weston, Clevedon and Portishead Railway</a> in 1925 and 1937. As with the withdrawals under the LB&amp;SCR, the engines were not withdrawn en masse, but instead over a long period of time. The first A1X to be withdrawn by the Southern Railway was No.42 <i>Tulse Hill</i> in 1925, and among the engines withdrawn was the now-famous No.55 <i>Stepney</i>, which only escaped being scrapped by virtue of the need for lighter engines on Hayling Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluebell-railway.co.uk_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-bluebell-railway.co.uk-9">[9]</a></sup> The two locomotives, No.43 <i>Gipsy Hill</i> and No.53 <i>Ashtead</i>, sold to the WCPR in 1925 and 1937 (see above) were renumbered No.2 <i>Portishead</i> and No.4 respectively. On the closure of that line in 1940, both locomotives entered service with the GWR, as No.5 <i>Portishead</i> and No.6 respectively, and survived into BR ownership. No.5 <i>Portishead</i> was scrapped in 1954, No.6 in 1948.
</p><p>Many of the locomotives remained in traffic, both departmental and in regular stock. They lasted longer than most classes of pre-grouping tank engine under the Southern Railway, primarily due to the presence of several <a href="/wiki/Light_railway" title="Light railway">light railways</a> which came under the Southern Railway's jurisdiction at the grouping, such as the lines from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenterden_railway_station" title="Tenterden railway station">Tenterden</a> and Hayling Island, as well as on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>. Most of these lines needed small engines such as the 'Terrier' class due to severe weight restrictions. Among the engines transferred into departmental use was the former No.82 <i>Boxhill</i>, which was restored to original condition, painted in the yellow ochre livery of Stroudley and used as a shunter at Brighton Works,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-terriertrust.org.uk_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-terriertrust.org.uk-10">[10]</a></sup> being numbered 380S and named after the works itself. This engine was replaced by 377S, formerly No.35 <i>Morden</i>, in 1948. Although much photographed toward the end of steam, this locomotive was not preserved but was scrapped at Eastleigh in 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass199578_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass199578-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the Southern Railway concentrating on the design of larger locomotives for the increasingly heavy express passenger traffic on the South Eastern and South Western sections, with designs such as the <a href="/wiki/LSWR_N15_class" title="LSWR N15 class">King Arthur</a>, <a href="/wiki/SR_V_Schools_class" title="SR V Schools class">Schools</a> and <a href="/wiki/SR_Lord_Nelson_class" title="SR Lord Nelson class">Lord Nelson</a> classes, and later the <a href="/wiki/SR_Merchant_Navy_class" title="SR Merchant Navy class">Bulleid Pacifics</a>, as well as the expansion of the <a href='/wiki/Railway_electrification_in_Great_Britain#650_V-850_V_DC,_Third_Rail_"Southern_Electric"' title="Railway electrification in Great Britain">electrified third rail system</a> from suburban roles (a system which had replaced the <a href="/wiki/London,_Brighton_and_South_Coast_Railway#Railway_electrification" title="London, Brighton and South Coast Railway">LB&amp;SCR overhead electrification</a>, which in turn had replaced the role of the D class tanks on suburban traffic from around 1910), the company was not concerned about replacing the veteran tank engines on branch line workings, especially as many of the smaller branch lines were not economically viable for electrification, or were isolated from the rest of the electrified network. As a result, the Southern Railway felt that perseverance with the older locomotives such as the A1X on rural routes was the most economically viable option.
</p>
<h3><span id="British_Railways_.281948-1963.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Railways_(1948-1963)">British Railways (1948-1963)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: British Railways (1948-1963)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1944" data-file-width="2592" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg/220px-British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg/330px-British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg/440px-British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terrier_32678_Tenterden_Town.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>British Railways 0-6-0T Class A1X No.32678 and a Birdcage Brake Coach at Tenterden Town recreating past scenes from the early 1950s on rural branch lines</div></div></div>
<p>At nationalisation in 1948, one "A1" and 14 "A1X" locomotives entered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Railways" title="British Railways">British Railways</a> stock. All but one of these locomotives were based on the former Southern Railway, which became the Southern Region under British Railways, with the other based on the Western Region, having been inherited by the <a href="/wiki/Great_Western_Railway" title="Great Western Railway">Great Western Railway</a> from the Weston, Clevedon and Portishead Railway. BR gave the locomotives the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_classification" title="Power classification">power classification</a> <b>0P</b>, indicating that the locomotives were primarily passenger locomotives, but were among the least powerful on the system.
</p><p>The locomotives remained employed much as they had been under Southern Railway ownership, with roles centred on working over the lightly laid and weight restricted branches. However, the class were becoming increasingly expensive to maintain through physical wear and increased age of components. The future of the class was thrown into doubt with the publication of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1955_Modernisation_Plan" title="1955 Modernisation Plan">Modernisation Plan</a>, which made provision to close many of the routes still operated by the class. The main two routes still retaining 'Terrier' operations were the Kent &amp; East Sussex Railway and the Hayling Island branch. The former closed due to being unprofitable in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The line to Hayling Island remained profitable, however, primarily due to the heavy traffic in summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200648_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200648-13">[13]</a></sup> The condition of the bridge over <a href="/wiki/Langstone_Harbour" title="Langstone Harbour">Langstone Harbour</a> was, however, deteriorating, and the Southern Region designated the bridge as being beyond economic repair. The final scheduled services over the line were run on 3 November 1963, and a special 'topped and tailed' by 32636 and 32670 the following day. The final scheduled train was notable for being one of the last steam hauled mixed trains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200649_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200649-3">[3]</a></sup> With the closure of this line, BR decided to withdraw the last remaining members of the class from traffic. At the time of her withdrawal following the closure of the line, No.32636 (formerly 72 <i>Fenchurch</i>) was the oldest working steam engine in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Railways" title="British Railways">British Railways</a> ownership.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-14">[14]</a></sup> The final operational A1X with BR was No.32678, which remained in service from Brighton shed until 10 August 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWelch200736_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelch200736-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbering">Numbering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Numbering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The locomotives were originally numbered between 35 and 84,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley1969169–170_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley1969169–170-15">[15]</a></sup> and most were given names of London boroughs or other areas of local importance which were served by the LB&amp;SCR suburban trains they were built to operate (such as <i>Whitechapel</i>, <i>Surrey</i> and <i>Thames</i>), and indeed also after areas around <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a> itself, such as <a href="/wiki/Kemp_Town" title="Kemp Town">Kemp Town</a>. However, there were exceptions, such as No.82 which was, in fact, named after <a href="/wiki/Box_Hill,_Surrey" title="Box Hill, Surrey">Box Hill</a>, a peak of the <a href="/wiki/North_Downs" title="North Downs">North Downs</a> in Surrey, just outside <a href="/wiki/Dorking" title="Dorking">Dorking</a>.
</p><p>From 1900, the two-digit numbers were prefixed with a 6 and their names were replaced with the inscription LBSC on their side tanks (for example, No.55 <i>Stepney</i> became No.655). This was fairly standard LB&amp;SCR practice; as engines got old, they were given higher numbers so that newer engines could have lower numbers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley1969157_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley1969157-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Further renumbering took place under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Railway_(Great_Britain)" title="Southern Railway (Great Britain)">Southern Railway</a> ownership. Initially, most locomotives retained their pre-Grouping numbers with a letter prefix denoting the main works that was responsible for maintaining the class. Ex-LBSC locomotives, being maintained at Brighton, received B prefixes, thus engine No.654 became B654). The exceptions to this rule were the engines based on the Isle of Wight, which were given a "W" prefix. In 1930 this system was replaced by a new numbering scheme, whereby former LB&amp;SCR engines had their numbers increased by 2000. Under this scheme, No. B654 became No.2654.
</p><p>A final renumbering in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Railways" title="British Railways">British Railways</a> ownership saw the remaining members of the class renumbered by the addition of 30,000 to their Southern Railway numbers (2678 becoming 32678, for instance), in line with the national renumbering scheme drawn up by British Railways in 1948.
</p><p>The famous Brighton Works shunting engine 377S (i.e. no. 377 in Service stock), by this stage the only member of the class not in revenue earning service, became DS377 (the "DS" standing for Departmental, Southern region).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation">Preservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Preservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For a complete list of the class, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_LB%26SCR_A1_class_locomotives" title="List of LB&amp;SCR A1 class locomotives">List of LB&amp;SCR A1 class locomotives</a>.</div>

<p>Ten members of the class have been preserved, chiefly in the South of England. The engines are listed in order of their LB&amp;SCR identity; the identity in bold is their current running identity as of January 2010.
</p>
<ul><li>40 <i>Brighton</i> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Steam_Railway" title="Isle of Wight Steam Railway">Isle of Wight Steam Railway</a> as <b><a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_A1X_Class_W11_Newport" title="LB&amp;SCR A1X Class W11 Newport">W11 <i>Newport</i></a></b>)</li>
<li>46 <i>Newington</i> (preserved at the Isle of Wight Steam Railway as <b><a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_A1X_Class_W8_Freshwater" title="LB&amp;SCR A1X Class W8 Freshwater">W8 <i>Freshwater</i></a></b>)</li>
<li>50 <i>Whitechapel</i> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Spa_Valley_Railway" title="Spa Valley Railway">Spa Valley Railway</a> as no. <b>32650 <i>Sutton</i></b>)</li>
<li><b>54 <i>Waddon</i></b> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Railway_Museum" title="Canadian Railway Museum">Canadian Railway Museum</a>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_A1X_class_55_Stepney" title="LB&amp;SCR A1X class 55 Stepney">55 <i>Stepney</i></a></b> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a>)</li>
<li>62 <i>Martello</i> (preserved at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bressingham_Steam_Museum" title="Bressingham Steam Museum">Bressingham Steam Museum</a> as <b>662 <i>Martello</i> </b>; as <b>32662</b> from May 2011)</li>
<li>70 <i>Poplar</i> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Kent_and_East_Sussex_Railway" title="Kent and East Sussex Railway">Kent and East Sussex Railway</a> as <b>3 <i>Bodiam</i></b>; as <b>32670</b> from April 2011)</li>
<li>72 <i>Fenchurch</i> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a> as <b>672 <i>Fenchurch</i></b>)</li>
<li>78 <i>Knowle</i> (preserved at the Kent &amp; East Sussex Railway as <b>32678</b>)</li>
<li><b>82 <i>Boxhill</i></b> (preserved at the <a href="/wiki/National_Railway_Museum" title="National Railway Museum">National Railway Museum</a>).</li></ul>
<p>As of November 2019, two Terriers are in operation. In the UK, steam locomotives may only be run for 10 years at a time between major overhauls (boiler certificates), and as such only engines which have been returned to traffic within the last ten years are classed as "operational". These engines are <i>Newport</i> (able to run until 2024).,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and <i>Martello</i> (able to run until 2026). 72 <i>Fenchurch's</i> boiler certificate expired in February 2011, and 55 <i>Stepney</i> was withdrawn ahead of its certificate expiration in March 2014. No. 50 <i>Whitechapel</i>/<i><b>Sutton</b></i>, is currently being restored at the Spa Valley Railway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <i>Knowle</i>, <i>Bodiam</i> and <i>Freshwater</i> are all dismantled for ten-yearly overhauls.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preservation_history">Preservation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Preservation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1563" data-file-width="2437" decoding="async" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg/220px-British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg/330px-British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg/440px-British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:British_Railways_Class_A1X_Terriers_No_32678_and_No_32670_Tenterden_Town.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Both British Railways Class A1X No. 32678 and No. 32670 at Tenterden Town on the Kent and East Sussex Railway</div></div></div>
<p>No.55 <i>Stepney</i> (as no. 32655) was the first engine to arrive at the first standard gauge preserved line in the UK, the <a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a> on 17 May 1960,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bluebell-railway.co.uk_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-bluebell-railway.co.uk-9">[9]</a></sup> effectively making it the <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/doyen" title="wikt:doyen">doyen</a> of the current heritage railway movement. Following purchase by the fledgling society the locomotive was run under its own steam from Brighton shed to <a href="/wiki/Horsted_Keynes_railway_station" title="Horsted Keynes railway station">Horsted Keynes</a> hauling the two carriages the society also now owned. The locomotive then went on to haul the reopening train in August of that year.
</p><p>No.72 <i>Fenchurch</i> was purchased by the <a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a> in 1964. After much initial use, the locomotive was retired for overhaul in 1970, and returned to traffic in 1972 for its centenary.  However, it was withdrawn in 1975, and with a new firebox re-entered service in 1980, running until 1988. At its next overhaul, the need to replace one pair of the original wrought iron wheels due to the detection of cracks in a wheel-hub meant the locomotive was not returned to traffic until 2001.  Although carrying an A1X boiler, the smokebox was rebuilt during that overhaul such that it now looks very similar to its original A1 form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>An equally famous member of the class to No.55, arguably, is No.82 <i>Boxhill</i>. The engine was restored by the Southern Railway to original condition (it had remained as an A1, not having been rebuilt as an A1X) and used at exhibitions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass199568_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass199568-19">[19]</a></sup> before being moved into the original British Transport Museum in <a href="/wiki/Clapham" title="Clapham">Clapham</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-terriertrust.org.uk_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-terriertrust.org.uk-10">[10]</a></sup> It was subsequently moved to the museum at York where it still resides.
</p><p>Also preserved in a national museum, No.54 <i>Waddon</i> was donated by British Rail to the Canadian railway museum in 1963 and was shipped on 23 August that year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbscr.demon.co.uk_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-lbscr.demon.co.uk-20">[20]</a></sup> After a number of years stored in the open, British ex-pats living in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> formed a group to work on the locomotive and restore it to pristine condition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lbscr.demon.co.uk_20-1"><a href="#cite_note-lbscr.demon.co.uk-20">[20]</a></sup>  Waddon was not rebuilt as an A1X, having been fitted with an SECR boiler after being sold to that railway, but was later fitted with, and now carries an A1X boiler, whilst retaining the shorter A1-type smokebox.
</p><p>Three members of the class were sold by British Rail to <a href="/wiki/Butlins" title="Butlins">Butlins</a> for display at their holiday camps. No.40 <i>Brighton</i> (now <i>Newport</i>) was sent to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butlins_Pwllheli" title="Butlins Pwllheli">Butlins Pwllheli</a> in 1964<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwsteamrailway.com_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-iwsteamrailway.com-6">[6]</a></sup> and initially loaned to the Isle of Wight steam railway in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass1995100_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass1995100-21">[21]</a></sup> It was sold to them four years later for a price of £35,000. A lengthy restoration was completed in 1989, and Newport continued to run until 2002 when withdrawn for overhaul. The locomotive's boiler was found to be life-expired, and it was found cheaper to order a new boiler made (for £70,000) in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iwsteamrailway.com_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-iwsteamrailway.com-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IOW_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-IOW-22">[22]</a></sup> In October 2010, the new boiler was delivered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>No.62 <i>Martello</i> went to Butlins Heads-of-Ayr camp in 1963, and was displayed alongside <a href="/wiki/LMS_Princess_Coronation_Class_6233_Duchess_of_Sutherland" title="LMS Princess Coronation Class 6233 Duchess of Sutherland">46233 <i>Duchess of Sutherland</i></a> before being loaned, and then sold to the museum at Bressingham.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass199595_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass199595-24">[24]</a></sup> The locomotive remained there for several years in a shed before removal and full restoration, which commenced in the late 1990s. In her Marsh Umber livery numbered 662 with LBSC on her tanks, she has visited several heritage railways in the UK since being returned to traffic. In May 2011 she was repainted in BR black as 32662 with the late BR logo, at Loughborough on the Great Central in readiness to join 32670 and 32678 on the K&amp;ESR for the <i>Last Train Commemoration</i> that took place on 11 June 2011.
</p><p>No.78 <i>Knowle</i> went to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Butlins_Minehead" title="Butlins Minehead">Butlins Minehead</a> camp<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-25">[25]</a></sup> and was displayed alongside <a href="/wiki/LMS_Princess_Coronation_Class_6229_Duchess_of_Hamilton" title="LMS Princess Coronation Class 6229 Duchess of Hamilton">46229 <i>Duchess of Hamilton</i></a> before being purchased by the K&amp;ESR in 1975. This locomotive is now owned by The Terrier Trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceC_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceC-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Also owned by this society is No.70 <i>Poplar</i> (usually running as K&amp;ESR No.3 <i>Bodiam</i>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodiam_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-bodiam-26">[26]</a></sup> which was initially purchased by the Wheels brothers of Brighton in 1964 from BR,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodiam_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-bodiam-26">[26]</a></sup> and was used in the early years of operation on the original <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kent_and_East_Sussex_Light_Railway" title="Kent and East Sussex Light Railway">Kent and East Sussex Light Railway</a> as well as on the same restored heritage railway. Following withdrawal in 1985, the engine remained out of use for a decade before the K&amp;ESR and The Terrier Trust bought the locomotive and agreed a restoration plan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bodiam_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-bodiam-26">[26]</a></sup> She returned to traffic in May 2006 at the <i>All Terriers Great and Small</i> Gala in her original Rother Valley Railway livery of Oxford Blue, then in April 2011 was repainted BR black as 32670 with the late BR logo, to feature at the <i>Last Train Commemoration</i> that took place on 11 June 2011. Both of the Terriers continue to be regular performers on the K&amp;ESR; the short trains and light loading gauge making them ideally suited to operating on the line.
</p><p>No. 50 <i>Whitechapel</i> was purchased by <a href="/wiki/London_Borough_of_Sutton" title="London Borough of Sutton">Municipal Borough of Sutton and Cheam</a> in 1963 (after the originally intended purchase, No.61 <i>Sutton</i>, had been cut up). The intention was to display the engine outside the new Civic Centre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spavalleyrailway.co.uk_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-spavalleyrailway.co.uk-27">[27]</a></sup> and the engine was offered a home on the K&amp;ESR until the work was completed. The engine was restored and used to haul the opening train on the preserved K&amp;ESR in 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass1995113_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass1995113-28">[28]</a></sup> After slipping down the overhaul queue at Rolvenden, the engine was moved to the Spa Valley Railway by the owners (the engine still being owned by Sutton Borough Council) and restoration work commenced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spavalleyrailway.co.uk_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-spavalleyrailway.co.uk-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p><p>No.46 <i>Newington</i> has perhaps the most unusual history. Following withdrawal in 1963, the locomotive was sold to the Sadler Rail Coach company based at Droxford, Hampshire on the disused <a href="/wiki/Meon_Valley_Railway" title="Meon Valley Railway">Meon Valley Railway</a>. It was used on occasion between the Droxford and Wickham stations on that line. Subsequently, it was sold to the Portsmouth-based Brickwoods brewery in the Spring of 1966. The brewery wanted to purchase it for use outside a new public house to be opened on Hayling Island named <i>The Hayling Billy</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-29">[29]</a></sup> The engine was displayed there for a number of years, becoming locally famous, before being donated to the Isle of Wight Steam Railway in 1979.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass1995119_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMiddlemass1995119-30">[30]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_line_operation">Main line operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Main line operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>None of the preserved members of the class have been restored to full main line running condition. However, some consideration was given to doing so during the restoration of No.62 <i>Martello</i> in the early years of the 21st century, in order to facilitate the appearance of the engine at open days and exhibitions in an operational condition. However, the costs involved led to the idea being abandoned.
</p><p>Prior to the end of steam and the banning of steam engines from the national network in 1968, No.55 <i>Stepney</i> worked two special trains over the metals of the Southern Region. The first trip ran on 21 October 1962 and saw No.55 doublehead with LSWR <a href="/wiki/LSWR_415_class" title="LSWR 415 class">Adams radial tank</a> No.488 on the <a href="/wiki/Haywards_Heath_railway_station" title="Haywards Heath railway station">Haywards Heath</a> to <a href="/wiki/Horsted_Keynes_railway_station" title="Horsted Keynes railway station">Horsted Keynes</a> and return section of a rail tour from London Victoria to the <a href="/wiki/Bluebell_Railway" title="Bluebell Railway">Bluebell Railway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGough199845_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGough199845-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> The second train ran from <a href="/wiki/Brighton_railway_station" title="Brighton railway station">Brighton</a> to Horsted Keynes on 27 October 1963<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> for members of the Bluebell Railway Preservation Society, which saw the locomotive double-head with another former LB&amp;SCR locomotive, <a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_E4_class" title="LB&amp;SCR E4 class">E4</a> No.473 <i>Birch Grove</i>, and was run to mark the closure of the line from Haywards Heath to Horsted Keynes.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1696" data-file-width="2415" decoding="async" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg/220px-Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg/330px-Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg/440px-Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Southern_Railway_Class_A1X_W11_Wootton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Southern Railway No. W11 runs round the train at Wootton on the Isle of Wight Steam Railway. This is one of the locomotives which could in future work through to Ryde.</div></div></div>
<p>No.55 returned to Brighton station in 1991 to mark the 150th anniversary of the opening of the <a href="/wiki/London_and_Brighton_Railway" title="London and Brighton Railway">London and Brighton Railway</a> to Brighton. The locomotive was left 'cold' (i.e. not in steam) and was coupled to another ex-LB&amp;SCR locomotive, <i><a href="/wiki/LB%26SCR_B1_class" title="LB&amp;SCR B1 class">Gladstone</a></i>, and stabled during the day on the eastern-most platform (number 9). The engines were the centre-piece of an exhibition which also included, among others, former Southern Railway 'King Arthur' No.777 <i>Sir Lamiel</i>, <a href="/wiki/BR_Standard_Class_7" title="BR Standard Class 7">Britannia class</a> No.70000 <i>Britannia</i> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BR_standard_class_4_tank" title="BR standard class 4 tank">BR Standard 4MT</a> No.80072, a class of locomotive built at Brighton.
</p><p>In August 1975, No.72 <i>Fenchurch</i> represented the LB&amp;SCR and the Bluebell Railway at the Rail 150 Steam Cavalcade held at <a href="/wiki/Shildon" title="Shildon">Shildon</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a>, an event held to mark the 150th anniversary of the opening of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stockton_%26_Darlington_Railway" title="Stockton &amp; Darlington Railway">Stockton &amp; Darlington Railway</a>, which featured the engine running over a section of the BR network, although the line was subject to special restrictions at the time. Additionally, the engine also ran a number of shuttles into the wagon works complex from the railway station, top-and-tailing the workings with <a href="/wiki/NER_Class_P3" title="NER Class P3">NER Class P3</a> No.65894 from the <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire_Moors_Railway" title="North Yorkshire Moors Railway">North Yorkshire Moors Railway</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>If future plans to extend the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_Steam_Railway" title="Isle of Wight Steam Railway">Isle of Wight Steam Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Smallbrook_Junction_railway_station" title="Smallbrook Junction railway station">Smallbrook Junction</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ryde_St_John%27s_Road_railway_station" title="Ryde St John's Road railway station">Ryde St. John's</a> are successful, this may see members of that railways fleet, including their two A1X engines, running over <a href="/wiki/Network_Rail" title="Network Rail">Network Rail</a> metals between the two locations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_A1_class_in_popular_culture">The A1 class in popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The A1 class in popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An A1X class locomotive, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minor_Characters_(The_Railway_Series)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minor Characters (The Railway Series) (page does not exist)">Stepney</a></i> appears in <a class="mw-redirect" href='/wiki/List_of_Railway_Series_Books#Stepney_the_"Bluebell"_Engine' title="List of Railway Series Books"><i>Stepney the "Bluebell" Engine</i></a>, one of the books in <a href="/wiki/The_Railway_Series" title="The Railway Series">The Railway Series</a> of <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_literature" title="Children's literature">children's books</a> written by the <a href="/wiki/Wilbert_Awdry" title="Wilbert Awdry">Rev. W. Awdry</a>. <i>Boxhill</i> was referred to in a later book in the series: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Railway_Series_Books#Thomas_and_the_Great_Railway_Show" title="List of Railway Series Books"><i>Thomas &amp; the Great Railway Show</i></a>. Stepney also appears in the television spin-off <a href="/wiki/Thomas_%26_Friends" title="Thomas &amp; Friends">Thomas &amp; Friends</a>.
</p><p>The 1975 <a href="/wiki/Ken_Russell" title="Ken Russell">Ken Russell</a> film <a href="/wiki/Lisztomania_(film)" title="Lisztomania (film)">Lisztomania</a> includes a sequence shot on the Bluebell Railway which features No.72 <i>Fenchurch</i> smashing a grand piano left on the line while running at speed (actually filmed with the engine running at 25 mph, the speed limit of the line, with the film sped up for impact).
</p><p>A 1961 film version of Anna Karenina, parts of which were filmed on 'The Bluebell Railway', included No.55 Stepney disguised as a Russian locomotive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_A1_class_overseas">The A1 class overseas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The A1 class overseas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two of the three locomotives sold to Pauling &amp; Co. were exported to South America and one worked on the La Plata tramway in the 1920s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley1969157_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley1969157-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Eight locomotives were built by local Australian builders for the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Government_Railways" title="New South Wales Government Railways">New South Wales Government Railways</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> – to the LB&amp;SCR's general arrangement and drawings – and entered traffic as the N67 class at about the same time as the A1X class in England. They were essentially identical engines, except for a simpler cab, larger bunker, larger sandboxes and other various detail differences. They became redundant from about 1890 after the introduction of larger and more powerful tank locomotives and many were fitted with small cranes in place of the bunker. In this form they were put to work as shunters and as "coal grabs" - small mobile coal loaders for refuelling larger locomotives at depots.  Some saw service with other groups into the 1930s. They were less successful than their English cousins due to rather different operating conditions and all were scrapped before the start of the preservation era.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Models">Models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=LB%26SCR_A1_class&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hornby_Railways" title="Hornby Railways">Hornby Railways</a> manufacture models of the A1 &amp; A1x in <a href="/wiki/OO_gauge" title="OO gauge">OO gauge</a> while <a href="/wiki/Dapol" title="Dapol">Dapol</a> produce them in <a href="/wiki/N_scale" title="N scale">N scale</a>, <a href="/wiki/OO_gauge" title="OO gauge">OO gauge</a> (in conjunction with Rails of Sheffield) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O_gauge" title="O gauge">O gauge</a>.
</p>
