<p>The <b>Sud-Ouest S.O.1221 Djinn</b> is a <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> two-seat light <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> designed and manufactured by aircraft manufacturer <a href="/wiki/SNCASO" title="SNCASO">Sud-Ouest</a>, which later became <a href="/wiki/Sud_Aviation" title="Sud Aviation">Sud Aviation</a>. It was the first indigenously-developed French helicopter, as well as being one of the first practical European helicopters to be produced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The Djinn was also the first rotorcraft to harness <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tip-jet" title="Tip-jet">tip-jet</a> propulsion to enter production.
</p><p>The Djinn was developed to function as a practical implementation of the earlier experimental <a href="/wiki/Sud-Ouest_Ariel" title="Sud-Ouest Ariel">Sud-Ouest Ariel</a> rotorcraft. Atypically, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotor_blade" title="Rotor blade">rotors</a> were driven by <a href="/wiki/Tip_jet" title="Tip jet">compressed-air jets</a> at the end of each blade, which had the benefit of eliminating the need for an anti-torque tail rotor. On 2 January 1953, the proof-of-concept <b>S.O.1220</b> performed its <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a>; it was followed by the first of the <b>S.O.1221 Djinn</b> prototypes on 16 December 1953. During the subsequence test program, one of the prototypes was recorded as having achieved a world altitude record.
</p><p>Having been suitably impressed by the performance of the Djinn during testing, both the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a> and the <a href="/wiki/German_Army" title="German Army">German Army</a> chose to procure the type, as well as a number of other customers. Operationally, the type was used for various purposes, including <a href="/wiki/Liaison_aircraft" title="Liaison aircraft">liaison</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aerial_observation" title="Aerial observation">aerial observation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trainer_(aircraft)" title="Trainer (aircraft)">training</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Casualty_evacuation" title="Casualty evacuation">casualty evacuation</a>. The Djinn was phased out of production during the 1960s as a consequence of the greater success of the comparably more conventional <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale_Alouette_II" title="AÃ©rospatiale Alouette II">AÃ©rospatiale Alouette II</a> and <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale_Alouette_III" title="AÃ©rospatiale Alouette III">AÃ©rospatiale Alouette III</a> helicopters.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sud-Ouest_Djinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Positive experiences that had gained from the earlier <a href="/wiki/Sud-Ouest_Ariel" title="Sud-Ouest Ariel">Ariel</a> experimental <a href="/wiki/Tip_jet" title="Tip jet">tip jet</a> helicopter had given French aviation company <a href="/wiki/SNCASO" title="SNCASO">Sud-Ouest</a> a strong desire to pursue development of a practical light helicopter that would harness this technology. Out of their efforts towards this aim emerged the design of a light twin-seat rotorcraft, which was promptly designated as the <i>S.O.1221 Djinn</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgow_74_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcgow_74-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boyne_101_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-boyne_101-1">[1]</a></sup> While this newer design did not share an identical tip jet system to the Ariel, the type did rely upon the same basic concept of feeding compressed air, which was generated by an onboard pump, to the tips of the vehicle's rotor blades to drive the movement of the blades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popu_air_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-popu_air-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>A single seat prototype, designated <i>S.O.1220</i>, was constructed to function as an aerial test bed for the rotorcraft's propulsion concept.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-popu_air_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-popu_air-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> On 2 January 1953, the S.O.1220, which was a simple uncovered structure with an exposed seat for the pilot, conducted its <a href="/wiki/Maiden_flight" title="Maiden flight">maiden flight</a>; early test flights of the rotorcraft proved the viability of the propulsion system. Accordingly, it was decided to proceed with the production of five two-seat prototypes, designated as the <i>S.O.1221</i>; the first of these flew on 16 December 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgow_74_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-mcgow_74-3">[3]</a></sup> Within a few days, one of the prototypes had climbed to a recorded altitude of 4,789Â m (15,712Â ft) to establish a record in its class.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the late 1950s, an improved version of the Djinn, tentatively designated as the <i>Djinn III</i> or <i>Super Djinn</i>, was being studied by <a href="/wiki/Sud_Aviation" title="Sud Aviation">Sud Aviation</a>, who held the intention of pursuing further development of the type at one point. As envisioned, the modifications present upon the projected Super Djinn variant would have included the adoption of the newer <a href="/wiki/Turbomeca_Palouste" title="Turbomeca Palouste">Turbomeca Palouste IV</a> engine; alongside other changes, it would have possessed both greater power and endurance than the original production model.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_512_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-flight_512-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design">Design</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sud-Ouest_Djinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="800" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg/220px-Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg/330px-Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg/440px-Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aerospatiale_SO_1221_Djinn_Rotor_Detail.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Close up view of the Djinn's upper section and rotor</div></div></div>
<p>The Sud-Ouest Djinn was the first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tip-jet" title="Tip-jet">tip-jet</a> propelled rotorcraft to enter production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_512_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-flight_512-8">[8]</a></sup> As such, the propulsion system involved a <a href="/wiki/Turbomeca_Palouste" title="Turbomeca Palouste">Turbomeca Palouste</a> <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">gas turbine</a> engine that drove a pump to produce compressed air, which was in turn fed into the aircraft's hollow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotor_blade" title="Rotor blade">rotor blades</a> to exit at their tips, inducing the blades to turn as a consequence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_114_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-flight_114-10">[10]</a></sup> The rotor hub assembly, which freely oscillated around a spherical thrust bearing, was mounted upon a steel pylon via means of a <a href="/wiki/Ball_joint" title="Ball joint">ball joint</a> and rubber <a href="/wiki/Shock_absorber" title="Shock absorber">shock absorbers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> Unlike some of the experimental tip-jet designs of the era, a 'cold' tip-jet approach was used, meaning that there was no <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a> activity present at the exhaust nozzles installed at the ends of the rotor blades. Consequently, the nozzles produced little noise in their operation, and were claimed by Sud-Ouest to be "noiseless".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_114_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-flight_114-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The all-metal rotor blades, through which the compressed air travelled to be ejected at the blade tips, consist of a spar of <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/taper" title="wikt:taper">tapering</a> thickness, a <a href="/wiki/Honeycomb_structure" title="Honeycomb structure">honeycomb</a>-filled <a href="/wiki/Trailing_edge" title="Trailing edge">trailing edge</a>, and feature faired roots in order to the increase their lift coefficient.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> It was claimed that the blades possessed a highly homogenous structure, a highly finished surface and a constant profile, and could be easily dismantled to facilitate inspection of the main spar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> Additionally, the blade had no risk of <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">icing</a> as a consequence of the internal current of warm air passing through. The Djinn used relatively standard flight controls, such as the <a href="/wiki/Helicopter_flight_controls#Collective" title="Helicopter flight controls">collective pitch</a> control (upon which the throttle was positioned) and adjustable friction trims.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Apart from its method of propulsion, the Djinn was a relatively conventional helicopter, featuring a two-seat side-by-side configuration in an enclosed cabin. The cockpit is provided with an array of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plexiglas" title="Plexiglas">plexiglas</a> panels, which provide the occupants with a high level of all-round visibility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> Directly to the rear of the cockpit was the rotorcraft's single Palouste engine. The engine lacked any electronics and had to be hand-started via a <a href="/wiki/Crank_(mechanism)" title="Crank (mechanism)">crank</a> handle located on the starboard side of the fuselage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgow_74_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-mcgow_74-3">[3]</a></sup> The Djinn possessed the ability to fly at high overload weights for around 5 sec by correctly harnessing the accumulated <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">kinetic energy</a> of the rotor; this 'jump' technique was successfully used to achieve <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">certification</a> for it to be operated to carry heavy payloads, some of which could represent up to 54 per cent of the rotorcraft's overall gross weight.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_512_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-flight_512-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Djinn featured a two-seat cockpit as standard, upon which a single pilot and passenger could be accommodated. A range of specialised equipment could be outfitted to suit mission roles, such as <a href="/wiki/Stretcher" title="Stretcher">stretcher</a> carriers, spray bars, <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> sets, electrical systems, additional fuel tanks, and a load-bearing <a href="/wiki/Lifting_hook" title="Lifting hook">hook</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_733_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-flight_733-12">[12]</a></sup> Akin to the later <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale_Alouette_II" title="AÃ©rospatiale Alouette II">AÃ©rospatiale Alouette II</a> helicopter, the Djinn made considerable use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pneumatic" title="Pneumatic">pneumatic</a> systems. According to aerospace publication <a href="/wiki/Flight_International" title="Flight International">Flight International</a>, considerable weight savings had been on the rotorcraft via the use of <a href="/wiki/Bleed_air" title="Bleed air">bleed air</a> to operate various cockpit indicators and functions, including the <a href="/wiki/Flight_instruments" title="Flight instruments">blind-flying instruments</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_gauge" title="Fuel gauge">fuel gauges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heater_core" title="Heater core">cabin heaters</a>, windshield <a href="/wiki/Demister_(vapor)" title="Demister (vapor)">demisters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trim_tab" title="Trim tab">trimmers</a>, air/sea rescue <a href="/wiki/Hoist_(device)" title="Hoist (device)">hoists</a> and crop-spraying equipment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_512_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-flight_512-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rotorcraft was equipped with an uncovered tail boom, outfitted with twin <a href="/wiki/Fin" title="Fin">fins</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Rudder" title="Rudder">rudder</a>; both directional control and stability of the vehicle were provided via the controlled deflection of exhaust gases from the engine.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mut_20_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-mut_20-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> As a consequence of the torque-free method of rotor propulsion, the Djinn did not require the presence of an anti-torque <a href="/wiki/Tail_rotor" title="Tail rotor">tail rotor</a>. A simplistic wheel-equipped twin-skid arrangement was also present; this was retractable via the same manually-operated crank handle used for engine ignition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_733_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-flight_733-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> The Djinn could also be deployed with multiple armaments. In the ground-attack role, it could be equipped with a single <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a>, which was manned from the second seat of the cockpit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup> For performing <a href="/wiki/Anti-tank_warfare" title="Anti-tank warfare">anti-tank warfare</a>, the Djinn was able to equip a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guided_missile" title="Guided missile">guided missile</a>; when deployed in an effective manner against an adversary, the manufacturer claimed the combination to be extremely difficult for an opposing <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> to sight its adversary and to defend itself during the anticipated rapid speed of engagements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flight_376_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-flight_376-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sud-Ouest_Djinn&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Having observed the development of the Djinn with considerable interest, the <a href="/wiki/French_Army" title="French Army">French Army</a> encouraged the construction of a pre-production batch of 22 helicopters, which were used for evaluation purposes. The first of these pre-production aircraft flew on 23 September 1954. Three of the pre-production helicopters were subsequently acquired by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a>, designating it as the <i>YHO-1</i>, for the purpose of participating in their own series of trials; according to aviation author Stanley S. McGowen, the US Army held little interest in the type.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgow_74_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-mcgow_74-3">[3]</a></sup> According to author Wayne Mutza, the US Army had found the YHO-1 to be an excellent weapons platform, but had been compelled to abandon interest in the programme by political opposition to the procurement of a rotorcraft that had non-American origins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mut_19_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-mut_19-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The French Army ordered a total of 100 helicopters. The Djinn was operated by the French Army in variety of mission roles, such as liaison, observation, training purposes; when flown with a single pilot, it could be outfitted with two external litters for the <a href="/wiki/Casualty_evacuation" title="Casualty evacuation">casualty evacuation</a> mission. In addition to the French military, a further ten countries placed orders for the type; such as a batch of six rotorcraft which were procured by the <a href="/wiki/German_Army" title="German Army">German Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgow_74_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-mcgow_74-3">[3]</a></sup> Production of the Djinn came to an end during the mid-1960s, by which point a total of 178 Djinns had been constructed; the type had effectively been replaced by the more conventional and highly successful <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9rospatiale_Alouette_II" title="AÃ©rospatiale Alouette II">AÃ©rospatiale Alouette II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-boyne_101_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-boyne_101-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Some Djinns had been sold onto civil operators; in this capacity, it was often equipped for <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> purposes, being fitted with chemical tanks and spray bars in this role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgow_74_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-mcgow_74-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
