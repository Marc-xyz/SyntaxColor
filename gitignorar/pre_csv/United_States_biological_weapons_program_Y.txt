<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Early_history_.281918-41.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history_(1918-41)">Early history (1918-41)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history (1918-41)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_weapons" title="Biological weapons">biological weapons</a> program</b> officially began in spring 1943 on orders from U.S. President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Roosevelt">Franklin Roosevelt</a>. Research continued following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> as the U.S. built up a large stockpile of <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological agents</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_weapon" title="Biological weapon">weapons</a>. Over the course of its 27-year history, the program weaponized and stockpiled the following seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bio-agent" title="Bio-agent">bio-agents</a> (and pursued basic research on many more):
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_anthracis" title="Bacillus anthracis">Bacillus anthracis</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Francisella_tularensis" title="Francisella tularensis">Francisella tularensis</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Tularemia" title="Tularemia">tularemia</a>)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Brucella" title="Brucella">Brucella</a></i> spp (<a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Coxiella_burnetii" title="Coxiella burnetii">Coxiella burnetii</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q-fever" title="Q-fever">Q-fever</a>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_equine_encephalitis_virus" title="Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus">Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus (VEE)</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Botulinum_toxin" title="Botulinum toxin">Botulinum toxin</a> (<a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a>)</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staphylococcal_enterotoxin_B" title="Staphylococcal enterotoxin B">Staphylococcal enterotoxin B</a></li></ul>
<p>Throughout its history, the U.S. bioweapons program was secret. It was later revealed that laboratory and field testing (some of the latter using simulants on non-consenting individuals) had been common. The official policy of the United States was first to deter the use of bio-weapons against U.S. forces and secondarily to retaliate if deterrence failed.
</p><p>In 1969, President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> ended all offensive (i.e., non-defensive) aspects of the U.S. bio-weapons program. In 1975 the U.S. ratified both the 1925 <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Protocol" title="Geneva Protocol">Geneva Protocol</a> and the 1972 <a href="/wiki/Biological_Weapons_Convention" title="Biological Weapons Convention">Biological Weapons Convention</a> (BWC)â€”international treaties outlawing biological warfare. Recent U.S. <a href="/wiki/Biodefense" title="Biodefense">biodefense</a> programs, however, have raised concerns that the U.S. may be pursuing research that is outlawed by The United States. Initial interest in any form of biological warfare came at the close of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The only agent the U.S. tested was the toxin <a href="/wiki/Ricin" title="Ricin">ricin</a>, a product of the castor plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> The U.S. conducted tests concerning two methods of ricin dissemination: the first, which involved adhering the toxin to <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">shrapnel</a> for delivery by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Artillery_shell" title="Artillery shell">artillery shell</a>, was successful;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> the second, delivering an aerosol cloud of ricin, was proven less successful in these tests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> Neither delivery method was perfected before the war in Europe ended.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 1920s suggestions that the U.S. began a biological weapons program were coming from within the Chemical Warfare Service (CWS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> Chief of the CWS, <a href="/wiki/Amos_Fries" title="Amos Fries">Amos Fries</a>, decided that such a program would not be "profitable" for the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> Japan's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shiro_Ishii" title="Shiro Ishii">Shiro Ishii</a> began promoting biological weapons during the 1920s and toured biological research facilities worldwide, including in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> Though Ishii concluded that the U.S. was developing a bio-weapons programs, he was incorrect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> In fact, Ishii concluded that each major power he visited was developing a bio-weapons program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">interwar period</a> continued, the United States did not emphasize biological weapons development or research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> While the U.S. was spending very little time on biological weapons research, its future allies and enemies in the upcoming second World War were researching the potential of biological weapons as early as 1933.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="World_War_II_.281941-45.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II_(1941-45)">World War II (1941-45)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II (1941-45)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the World War I-era interest in ricin, as World War II erupted, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> still maintained the position that biological weapons were, for the most part, impractical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garrett_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-garrett-3">[3]</a></sup> Other nations, notably France, Japan and the United Kingdom, thought otherwise and had begun their own biological weapons programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garrett_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-garrett-3">[3]</a></sup> Thus, as late as 1942 the U.S. had no biological weapons capabilities. Initial interest in biological weapons by the Chemical Warfare Service began in 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddy2_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-croddy2-4">[4]</a></sup> That fall, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_War" title="U.S. Secretary of War">U.S. Secretary of War</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_L._Stimson" title="Henry L. Stimson">Henry L. Stimson</a> requested that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">National Academy of Sciences</a> (NAS) undertake consideration of U.S. biological warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zilinskas_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-zilinskas-5">[5]</a></sup> He wrote to Dr. <a href="/wiki/Frank_B._Jewett" title="Frank B. Jewett">Frank B. Jewett</a>, then president of the NAS:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Because of the dangers that might confront this country from potential enemies employing what may be broadly described as biological warfare, it seems advisable that investigations be initiated to survey the present situation and the future possibilities. I am therefore, asking if you will undertake the appointment of an appropriate committee to survey all phases of this matter. Your organization already has before it a request from The Surgeon General for the appointment of a committee by the Division of Medical Sciences of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">National Research Council</a> to examine one phase of the matter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In response the NAS formed a committee, the <a href="/wiki/War_Bureau_of_Consultants" title="War Bureau of Consultants">War Bureau of Consultants</a> (WBC), which issued a report on the subject in February 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zilinskas_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-zilinskas-5">[5]</a></sup> The report, among other items, recommended the research and development of an offensive biological weapons program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zilinskas_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-zilinskas-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The British, and the research undertaken by the WBC, pressured the U.S. to begin biological weapons research and development and in November 1942 U.S. President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franklin_Roosevelt" title="Franklin Roosevelt">Franklin Roosevelt</a> officially approved an American biological weapons program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-7">[7]</a></sup> In response to the information provided by the WBC, Roosevelt ordered Stimson to form the <a href="/wiki/War_Research_Service" title="War Research Service">War Research Service</a> (WRS).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zilinskas_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-zilinskas-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moreno_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-moreno-8">[8]</a></sup> Established within the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Security_Agency" title="Federal Security Agency">Federal Security Agency</a>, the WRS' stated purpose was to promote "public security and health",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moreno_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-moreno-8">[8]</a></sup> but, in reality, the WRS was tasked with coordinating and supervising the U.S. biological warfare program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zilinskas_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-zilinskas-5">[5]</a></sup> In the spring of 1943 the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Biological_Warfare_Laboratories" title="U.S. Army Biological Warfare Laboratories">U.S. Army Biological Warfare Laboratories</a> were established at Fort (then Camp) Detrick in Maryland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ryan-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin3-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Though initially, under <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Merck" title="George Merck">George Merck</a>, the WRS contracted several universities to participate in the U.S. biological weapons program, the program became large quickly and before long it was under the full control of the CWS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moreno_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-moreno-8">[8]</a></sup> By November 1943 the biological weapons facility at Detrick was completed, in addition, the United States constructed three other facilities - a biological agent production plant at <a href="/wiki/Vigo_County,_Indiana" title="Vigo County, Indiana">Vigo County</a> near <a href="/wiki/Terre_Haute,_Indiana" title="Terre Haute, Indiana">Terre Haute, Indiana</a>, a field-testing site on <a href="/wiki/Horn_Island_(Mississippi)" title="Horn Island (Mississippi)">Horn Island</a> in Mississippi, and another field site near <a href="/wiki/Granite_Peak" title="Granite Peak">Granite Peak</a> in Utah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moreno_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-moreno-8">[8]</a></sup> According to an official history of the period, "the elaborate security precautions taken [at Camp Detrick] were so effective that it was not until January 1946, 4 months after <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VJ_Day" title="VJ Day">VJ Day</a>, that the public learned of the wartime research in biological weapons".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Cold_War_.281947-69.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War_(1947-69)">Cold War (1947-69)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cold War (1947-69)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the United States biological warfare program progressed into an effective, military-driven research and production program, covered in controversy and secrecy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> Production of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological warfare</a> agents went from "factory-level to laboratory-level".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddyhart_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-croddyhart-13">[13]</a></sup> By 1950 the principal U.S. bio-weapons facility was located at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Detrick" title="Camp Detrick">Camp Detrick</a> in Maryland under the auspices of the Research and Engineering Division of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Army_Chemical_Corps" title="U.S. Army Chemical Corps">U.S. Army Chemical Corps</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitby_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitby-14">[14]</a></sup> Most of the research and development was done there, while production and testing occurred at Pine Bluff, Arkansas, and <a href="/wiki/Dugway_Proving_Ground" title="Dugway Proving Ground">Dugway Proving Ground</a>, Utah. <a href="/wiki/Pine_Bluff_Arsenal" title="Pine Bluff Arsenal">Pine Bluff Arsenal</a> began production of weapons-grade agents by 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zubay_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-zubay-15">[15]</a></sup> From 1952-1954 the Chemical Corps maintained a biological weapons research and development facility at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Terry" title="Fort Terry">Fort Terry</a> on <a href="/wiki/Plum_Island_(New_York)" title="Plum Island (New York)">Plum Island</a>, New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin4-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheelis_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wheelis-17">[17]</a></sup> Fort Terry's focus was on anti-animal biological weapon research and development; the facility researched more than a dozen potential BW agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheelis_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-wheelis-17">[17]</a></sup> From the end of World War II through the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, the U.S. Army, the Chemical Corps and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> all expanded their biological warfare programs significantly, especially concerning delivery systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddyhart_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-croddyhart-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zubay_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-zubay-15">[15]</a></sup> Throughout the cold war, the United States and the Soviet Union would combine to produce enough biological weapons to kill everyone on Earth.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another substantive expansion phase was during the Kennedy-Johnson years, after <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">McNamara</a> initiated <a href="/wiki/Project_112" title="Project 112">Project 112</a> as a comprehensive initiative, starting in 1961. Despite an increase in testing, the readiness for biological warfare remained limited after this program. A 10 November 1969 report by the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Interdepartmental_Political-Military_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Interdepartmental Political-Military Group (page does not exist)">Interdepartmental Political-Military Group</a> submitted its findings to the Nixon administration that the American BW capability was limited:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USBW-32_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-USBW-32-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>No large inventory of dry (powdered) anti-personnel lethal or incapacitating biological agents is maintained and only eight aircraft spray disseminators are in the inventory. No missile delivery capabilities are currently maintained for delivery of biological agents, although a bomblet containing warhead for the sergeant missile has been standardized, but not produced in quantity. Small quantities of both lethal and incapacitating biological agents are maintained in special warfare devices.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Field testing of the biological weapons was completed covertly and successfully with simulants and agents dispersed over wide, open areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> The first American large-scale aerosol vulnerability test occurred in the San Francisco Bay Area in September 1950, using two types of bacteria (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacillus_globigii" title="Bacillus globigii">Bacillus globigii</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Serratia_marcescens" title="Serratia marcescens">Serratia marcescens</a></i>) and fluorescent particles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> Bacillus species were chosen in these tests because of their spore-forming abilities, and their similarities to <i><a href="/wiki/Bacillus_anthracis" title="Bacillus anthracis">Bacillus anthracis</a></i>, a causing agent of <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>S. marcescens</i> was used because it is easily identifiable from its red pigment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-4"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1966, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">New York City Subway</a> was contaminated with Bacillus globigii in an attempt to simulate the spreading of anthrax in a large urban population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frischknecht_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frischknecht-21">[21]</a></sup>  More field tests involving pathogenic species were conducted at Dugway Proving Ground, Utah and anti-animal studies were conducted at <a href="/wiki/Eglin_Air_Force_Base" title="Eglin Air Force Base">Eglin Air Force Base</a>, Florida.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-5"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>At the time, many scientists disagreed with the creation of biological weapons. <a href="/wiki/Theodor_Rosebury" title="Theodor Rosebury">Theodor Rosebury</a>, who previously worked as a supervisor at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Detrick" title="Camp Detrick">Camp Detrick</a>, issued a warning against the development of biological weapons during the cold war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1945, Rosebury left Camp Detrick during a period of time when scientists could publish the results of their research.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-1"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> Rosebury published <i>Peace or Pestilence?</i> in 1949, which explained his views on why biological weapons should be banned by world powers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-2"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> By the time his book was available, publications were becoming more restricted and the extent of the Soviet threat of biological weapons was being overstated by Congress and the media.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-3"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1969, Harvard biologist <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Meselson" title="Matthew Meselson">Matthew Meselson</a> argued that the biological warfare programs would eventually hurt US security because potential enemy nations could easily emulate these weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-4"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>The general population remained uninformed of any breakthroughs concerning biological warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-5"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> This included new production plants for anthrax, <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>, and anti-crop agents, as well as the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_bomb" title="Cluster bomb">cluster bomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-6"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> The U.S. public was also unaware of ongoing studies, particularly the environmental and open-air experiments that were taking place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-6"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> One of the more controversial experiments was conducted in 1951, when a disproportionate number of African Americans were exposed to the fungus <i>Aspergillus fumigatus</i>, to see if they were more susceptible to infection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-7"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> Some scientists reasoned that such knowledge would help them prepare a defense against a more deadly form of the fungus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-8"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> The same year, workers at the Norfolk Supply Center in Norfolk, Virginia, were unknowingly exposed to <i>Aspergillus fumigatus</i> spores.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-9"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> Another case of human research was the biodefense medical research program, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Whitecoat" title="Operation Whitecoat">Operation Whitecoat</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-7"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> This decade-long experiment on volunteer Seventh Day Adventist servicemen exposed them to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tularaemia" title="Tularaemia">tularaemia</a> via aerosols.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-8"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> They were then treated with antibiotics. The goal of the experiment, unknown to the volunteers, was to standardize tularaemia bomb-fill for attacks on civilian populations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-9"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s, the U.S. changed its main approach from biological agents aimed to kill to those that would incapacitate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-10"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> In 1964, research programs studied <a href="/wiki/Enterotoxin_type_B" title="Enterotoxin type B">Enterotoxin type B</a>, which can cause food poisoning. New research initiatives also included <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophylaxis" title="Prophylaxis">prophylaxis</a>, the preventative treatment of diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-11"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup> Pathogens studied included the biological agents causing a myriad of diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glanders" title="Glanders">glanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melioidosis" title="Melioidosis">melioidosis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venezuelan_equine_encephalitis" title="Venezuelan equine encephalitis">Venezuelan equine encephalitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Q_fever" title="Q fever">Q fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coccidioidomycosis" title="Coccidioidomycosis">coccidioidomycosis</a>, and other plant and animal pathogens.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Franz_12-12"><a href="#cite_note-Franz-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> brought public awareness to the U.S. biological weapons program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-10"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> The use of chemicals, riot-control agents, and herbicides like <a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a> drew international criticism, and negatively affected the U.S. public opinion on the development of biological weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guillemin_22-11"><a href="#cite_note-Guillemin-22">[22]</a></sup> Highly controversial human research programs and open air experiments were discovered. <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_Guillemin" title="Jeanne Guillemin">Jeanne Guillemin</a>, wife of biologist <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Meselson" title="Matthew Meselson">Matthew Meselson</a>, summarized the controversy:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wampler_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wampler-23">[23]</a></sup> 
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The entire experimental legacy is dismaying, from the hundreds of dead monkeys at Fort Detrick to the spectacle of Seventh Day Adventist soldiers, the vaccinated volunteers in Project Whitecoat, strapped to chairs amid cages of animals in the Utah sunlight as Q fever aerosols are blown over them. Most chilling are the mock scenarios played out in urban areas: light bulbs filled with simulated BW agents being dropped in New York subways, men in Washington National Airport spraying pseudo-BW from briefcases, and similar tests in California and Texas and over the Florida Keys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wampler_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Wampler-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_Richard_Nixon" title="Presidency of Richard Nixon">Nixon administration</a> felt an urgent need to respond to the growing negative perception of biological weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wampler_23-2"><a href="#cite_note-Wampler-23">[23]</a></sup> The realization that biological weapons may become the poor man's atom bomb also contributed to the end of the U.S. biological weapons program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frischknecht_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-Frischknecht-21">[21]</a></sup>  Subsequently, President Nixon announced that the U.S. was unilaterally renouncing its biological warfare program, ultimately signing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biological_and_Toxin_Weapons_Convention" title="Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention">Biological and Toxin Weapons Convention</a> in 1972.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Richardt_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Richardt-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="End_of_the_program_.281969-73.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="End_of_the_program_(1969-73)">End of the program (1969-73)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: End of the program (1969-73)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Statement_on_Chemical_and_Biological_Defense_Policies_and_Programs" title="Statement on Chemical and Biological Defense Policies and Programs">Statement on Chemical and Biological Defense Policies and Programs</a></div>
<p>President <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a> issued his "Statement on Chemical and Biological Defense Policies and Programs" on November 25, 1969 in a speech from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Detrick" title="Fort Detrick">Fort Detrick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-miller-25">[25]</a></sup> The statement ended, unconditionally, all U.S. offensive biological weapons programs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graham_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-graham-26">[26]</a></sup> Nixon noted that biological weapons were unreliable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graham_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-graham-26">[26]</a></sup> and stated:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-miller-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The United States shall renounce the use of lethal biological agents and weapons, and all other methods of biological warfare. The United States will confine its biological research to defensive measures such as immunization and safety measures.</p></blockquote>
<p>In his speech Nixon called his move "unprecedented"; and it was in fact the first review of the U.S. biological warfare program since 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin-27">[27]</a></sup> Despite the lack of review, the biological warfare program had increased in cost and size since 1961; when Nixon ended the program the budget was $300 million annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cirincione_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-cirincione-28">[28]</a></sup> Nixon's statement confined all biological weapons research to defensive-only and ordered the destruction of the existing <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._biological_weapons_topics" title="List of U.S. biological weapons topics">U.S. biological arsenal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>U.S. biological weapons stocks were destroyed over the next few years. A $12 million disposal plan was undertaken at <a href="/wiki/Pine_Bluff_Arsenal" title="Pine Bluff Arsenal">Pine Bluff Arsenal</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mangold_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-mangold-30">[30]</a></sup> where all U.S. anti-personnel <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological agents</a> were stored.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> That plan was completed in May 1972 and included decontamination of facilities at Pine Bluff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mangold_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-mangold-30">[30]</a></sup> Other agents, including anti-crop agents such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wheat_stem_rust" title="Wheat stem rust">wheat stem rust</a>, were stored at <a href="/wiki/Beale_Air_Force_Base" title="Beale Air Force Base">Beale Air Force Base</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountain_Arsenal" title="Rocky Mountain Arsenal">Rocky Mountain Arsenal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> These anti-crop agents, along with agents at Fort Detrick used for research purposes were destroyed in March 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Budget_history">Budget history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Budget history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the onset of the U.S. biological weapons program in 1943 through the end of World War II the United States spent $400 million on biological weapons, mostly on research and development.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin5_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin5-31">[31]</a></sup> The budget for fiscal year 1966 was $38 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart2_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-smart2-32">[32]</a></sup> When Nixon ended the U.S. bio-weapons program it represented the first review of the U.S. biological warfare program since 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin-27">[27]</a></sup> Despite the lack of review, the biological warfare program had increased in cost and size since 1961; when Nixon ended the program the budget was $300 million annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin_27-3"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cirincione_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-cirincione-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geneva_Protocol_and_BWC">Geneva Protocol and BWC</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geneva Protocol and BWC">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1925 <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Protocol" title="Geneva Protocol">Geneva Protocol</a>, ratified by most major powers in the 1920s and 30s, had still not been ratified by the United States at the dawn of World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graham_26-2"><a href="#cite_note-graham-26">[26]</a></sup> Among the Protocol's provisions was a ban on bacteriological warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obrien_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-obrien-33">[33]</a></sup> The Geneva Protocol had encountered opposition in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a>, in part due to strong lobbying against it by the Chemical Warfare Service, and it was never brought to the floor for a vote when originally introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graham_26-3"><a href="#cite_note-graham-26">[26]</a></sup> Regardless, on June 8, 1943 President Roosevelt affirmed a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No-first-use" title="No-first-use">no-first-use</a> policy for the United States concerning biological weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graham_26-4"><a href="#cite_note-graham-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obrien_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-obrien-33">[33]</a></sup> Even with Roosevelt's declaration opposition to the Protocol remained strong; in 1949 the Protocol was among several old treaties returned to President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> unratified.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-graham_26-5"><a href="#cite_note-graham-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>When Nixon ended the U.S. bio-weapons program in 1969 he also announced that he would resubmit the Geneva Protocol to the U.S. Senate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-5"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> This was a move Nixon was considering as early as July 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-6"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> The announcement included language that indicated the Nixon administration was moving toward an international agreement on an outright ban on bio-weapons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-7"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> Thus, the Nixon administration became the world's leading anti-biological weapons voice calling for an international treaty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-miller-25">[25]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eighteen_Nation_Disarmament_Committee" title="Eighteen Nation Disarmament Committee">Eighteen Nation Disarmament Committee</a> was discussing a British draft of a biological weapons treaty which the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> approved in 1968 and that <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> supported.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin_27-4"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin-27">[27]</a></sup> These arms control talks would eventually lead to the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Weapons_Convention" title="Biological Weapons Convention">Biological Weapons Convention</a>, the international treaty outlawing biological warfare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carter_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-carter-34">[34]</a></sup> Prior to the Nixon announcement only Canada supported the British draft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-8"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> Beginning in 1972, the Soviet Union, United States and more than 100 other countries signed the BWC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_25-3"><a href="#cite_note-miller-25">[25]</a></sup> The United States ratified the Geneva Protocol in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-fas-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agents_studied_and_weaponized">Agents studied and weaponized</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Agents studied and weaponized">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the U.S. biological warfare program ended in 1969 it had developed six mass-produced, battle-ready biological weapons in the form of agents that cause <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tularemia" title="Tularemia">tularemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brucellosis" title="Brucellosis">brucellosis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q-fever" title="Q-fever">Q-fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_equine_encephalitis_virus" title="Venezuelan equine encephalitis virus">VEE</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Botulism" title="Botulism">botulism</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddyhart_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-croddyhart-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staphylococcal_enterotoxin_B" title="Staphylococcal enterotoxin B">staphylococcal enterotoxin B</a> was produced as an incapacitating agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddyhart_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-croddyhart-13">[13]</a></sup> In addition to the agents that were ready to be used, the U.S. program conducted research into the weaponization of more than 20 other agents. They included: <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_equine_encephalitis" title="Eastern equine encephalitis">EEE</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_equine_encephalitis" title="Western equine encephalitis">WEE</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argentinian_hemorrhagic_fever" title="Argentinian hemorrhagic fever">AHF</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hantavirus" title="Hantavirus">Hantavirus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bolivian_hemorrhagic_fever" title="Bolivian hemorrhagic fever">BHF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lassa_fever" title="Lassa fever">Lassa fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glanders" title="Glanders">glanders</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-note1_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-note1-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Melioidosis" title="Melioidosis">melioidosis</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-note1_36-1"><a href="#cite_note-note1-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">plague</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Psittacosis" title="Psittacosis">psittacosis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rift_Valley_fever" title="Rift Valley fever">Rift Valley fever</a> (RVF), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chikungunya_virus" title="Chikungunya virus">CHIKV</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_blight_of_potato" title="Late blight of potato">late blight of potato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rinderpest" title="Rinderpest">rinderpest</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newcastle_disease" title="Newcastle disease">Newcastle disease</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avian_influenza" title="Avian influenza">bird flu</a>, and the toxin <a href="/wiki/Ricin" title="Ricin">ricin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weaponslist_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-weaponslist-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Besides the numerous pathogens that afflict human beings, the U.S. had developed an arsenal of anti-agriculture biological agents. These included rye stem rust spores (stored at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edgewood_Arsenal" title="Edgewood Arsenal">Edgewood Arsenal</a>, 1951â€“1957), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wheat_stem_rust" title="Wheat stem rust">wheat stem rust</a> spores (stored at the same facility 1962â€“1969),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitby_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitby-14">[14]</a></sup> and the causative agent of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rice_blast" title="Rice blast">rice blast</a> (stored at Fort Detrick 1965â€“1966).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitby_14-2"><a href="#cite_note-whitby-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>A U.S. facility at Fort Terry focused primarily on anti-animal biological agents. The first agent that was a candidate for development was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foot_and_mouth_disease" title="Foot and mouth disease">foot and mouth disease</a> (FMD).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheelis_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-wheelis-17">[17]</a></sup> Besides FMD, five other top-secret biological weapons projects were commissioned on Plum Island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carroll_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-38">[38]</a></sup> The other four programs researched included RVF, rinderpest, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_swine_fever" title="African swine fever">African swine fever</a>, plus eleven miscellaneous exotic animal diseases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheelis_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-wheelis-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-carroll_38-1"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-38">[38]</a></sup> The eleven miscellaneous pathogens were: <a href="/wiki/Bluetongue_disease" title="Bluetongue disease">Blue tongue virus</a>, bovine influenza, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bovine_virus_diarrhea" title="Bovine virus diarrhea">bovine virus diarrhea</a> (BVD), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fowl_plague" title="Fowl plague">fowl plague</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visna_virus" title="Visna virus">goat pneumonitis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mycobacteria" title="Mycobacteria">mycobacteria</a>, "N" virus, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newcastle_disease" title="Newcastle disease">Newcastle disease</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sheep_pox" title="Sheep pox">sheep pox</a>, Teschers disease, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vesicular_stomatitis" title="Vesicular stomatitis">vesicular stomatitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wheelis_17-4"><a href="#cite_note-wheelis-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Work on delivery systems for the U.S. bioweapons arsenal led to the first mass-produced biological weapon in 1952, the <a href="/wiki/M33_cluster_bomb" title="M33 cluster bomb">M33 cluster bomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddy1_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-croddy1-39">[39]</a></sup> The M33's sub-munition, the pipe-bomb-like cylindrical <a href="/wiki/M114_bomb" title="M114 bomb">M114 bomb</a>, was also completed and battle-ready by 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddy1_39-1"><a href="#cite_note-croddy1-39">[39]</a></sup> Other delivery systems researched and at least partially developed during the 1950s included the <a href="/wiki/E77_balloon_bomb" title="E77 balloon bomb">E77 balloon bomb</a> and the <a href="/wiki/E86_cluster_bomb" title="E86 cluster bomb">E86 cluster bomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitby_14-3"><a href="#cite_note-whitby-14">[14]</a></sup> The peak of U.S. biological weapons delivery system development came during the 1960s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> Production of cluster bomb submunitions began to shift from cylindrical to spherical bomblets, which had a larger coverage area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kirby_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-kirby-40">[40]</a></sup> Development of the spherical <a href="/wiki/E120_bomblet" title="E120 bomblet">E120 bomblet</a> took place in the early 1960s<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-countermeasures_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-countermeasures-41">[41]</a></sup> as did development of the <a href="/wiki/M143_bomblet" title="M143 bomblet">M143 bomblet</a>, similar to the chemical <a href="/wiki/M139_bomblet" title="M139 bomblet">M139 bomblet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-smart_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-smart-1">[1]</a></sup> The experimental <a href="/wiki/Flettner_rotor_bomblet" title="Flettner rotor bomblet">Flettner rotor bomblet</a> was also developed during this time period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eitzen_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-eitzen-42">[42]</a></sup> The Flettner rotor was called, "probably one of the better devices for disseminating microorganisms", by <a href="/wiki/William_C._Patrick_III" title="William C. Patrick III">William C. Patrick III</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usphs_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-usphs-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alleged_uses">Alleged uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alleged uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Leitenberg and Zilinskas in <i>The Soviet Biological Weapons Program: A History</i> (2012):
</p>
<blockquote><p>[There was] ... a history of dozens of allegations made by various branches of the Soviet government for the entire post-World War II period charging that the United States had used biological weapons. As best as is known, all of these allegations are deliberate, fraudulent concoctions. The most notorious of these were the allegations made by the Soviet Union, the People's Republic of China, and North Korea, charging that the United States used biological weapons during the 1950-1953 Korean War. There was a brief respite from these charges between 1987 and 1992, but after that former Soviet military officials who were part of the Soviet Union's own BW program once again began to repeat the same discredited charges.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_War">Korean War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Korean War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Allegations_of_biological_warfare_in_the_Korean_War" title="Allegations of biological warfare in the Korean War">Allegations of biological warfare in the Korean War</a></div>
<p>In 1952, during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, the Chinese and North Koreans insinuated that mysterious outbreaks of disease in North Korea and China<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stueck_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-stueck-45">[45]</a></sup> were due to U.S. biological attacks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-regis_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-regis-46">[46]</a></sup> Despite contrary assertions from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross" title="International Red Cross">International Red Cross</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">World Health Organization</a>, whom the Chinese denounced as Western-biased, the Chinese government pursued an investigation by the <a href="/wiki/World_Peace_Council" title="World Peace Council">World Peace Council</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin2_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin2-47">[47]</a></sup> A committee led by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Needham" title="Joseph Needham">Joseph Needham</a> gathered evidence for a report that included testimony from eyewitnesses, doctors, and four American Korean War prisoners who confirmed use of biological weapons by the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin2_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin2-47">[47]</a></sup> The U.S. government denied the accusations and their denial was generally supported by top scientists in the West.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin2_47-2"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin2-47">[47]</a></sup> In eastern Europe, China, and North Korea it was widely believed that the accusations were true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stueck_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-stueck-45">[45]</a></sup> A 1988 book on the Korean War, by Western historians <a href="/wiki/Jon_Halliday" title="Jon Halliday">Jon Halliday</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Cumings" title="Bruce Cumings">Bruce Cumings</a> also suggested the claims might be true.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usnews_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-usnews-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1998, Stephen Endicott and Edward Hagermann claimed that the accusations were true in their book, <i>The United States and Biological Warfare: Secrets from the Early Cold War and Korea</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-endicott_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-endicott-50">[50]</a></sup> The book received mixed reviews, some calling it "bad history"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reviews_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-reviews-51">[51]</a></sup> and "appalling",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-regis_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-regis-46">[46]</a></sup> while others praised the authors' case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reviews_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-reviews-51">[51]</a></sup> In the same year Endicott's book was published, Kathryn Weathersby and Milton Leitenberg of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War_International_History_Project" title="Cold War International History Project">Cold War International History Project</a> at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson_Center" title="Woodrow Wilson Center">Woodrow Wilson Center</a> in Washington released a cache of Soviet and Chinese documents that revealed the North Korean claim to have been an elaborate <a href="/wiki/Disinformation" title="Disinformation">disinformation</a> campaign.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usnews_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-usnews-48">[48]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwihp_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-cwihp-52">[52]</a></sup> In addition, a Japanese journalist claims to have seen similar evidence of a Soviet disinformation campaign and that the evidence supporting its occurrence was faked.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin2_47-3"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin2-47">[47]</a></sup> In 2001, <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> historian Herbert Romerstein supported Weathersby and Leitenberg, observing that Endicott's researches were purely based on accounts provided by the Chinese government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Romerstein_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-Romerstein-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2010, the allegations were investigated by the <a href="/wiki/Al_Jazeera_English" title="Al Jazeera English">Al Jazeera English</a> news program <i><a href="/wiki/People_%26_Power" title="People &amp; Power">People &amp; Power</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffreys1_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-jeffreys1-54">[54]</a></sup> In this program, Professor Mori Masataka investigated historical artifacts in the form of bomb casings from US biological weapons, contemporary documentary evidence and eyewitness testimonies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffreys1_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-jeffreys1-54">[54]</a></sup> He concluded that the United States did, in fact, test biological weapons on North Korea during the Korean War.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jeffreys1_54-2"><a href="#cite_note-jeffreys1-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuba">Cuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been rumored that the U.S. employed biological weapons against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">Communist</a> island nation of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a> claimed that evidence exists implicating the U.S. in biological warfare in Cuba,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chomsky_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-chomsky-55">[55]</a></sup> but these claims are disputed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nieto_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-nieto-57">[57]</a></sup> Allegations in 1962 held that CIA operatives had contaminated a shipment of sugar while it was in storage in Cuba.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blum_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-blum-58">[58]</a></sup> Also in 1962, a  Canadian agricultural technician assisting the Cuban government claimed he was paid $5,000 to infect Cuban turkeys with the deadly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newcastle_disease" title="Newcastle disease">Newcastle disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blum_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-blum-58">[58]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-note3_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-note3-59">[59]</a></sup> Though the technician later claimed he had just pocketed the money, many Cubans and some USA citizens believed a clandestinely administered <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological weapons agent</a> was responsible for a subsequent outbreak of the disease in Cuban turkeys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blum_58-2"><a href="#cite_note-blum-58">[58]</a></sup>  In 1971 the first serious outbreak of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swine_flu" title="Swine flu">swine flu</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a> occurred in Cuba, and Cubans alleged that U.S. covert biological warfare was responsible for this outbreak, which led to the preemptive slaughter of 500,000 pigs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup> Evidence linking these incidents to biological warfare has not been confirmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Accusations have continued to come out of Havana charging U.S. use of bio-weapons on the island. The Cuban government blamed the U.S. for a 1981 outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a> that sickened more than 300,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-3"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup> Dengue, a <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vector-borne</a> disease usually carried by mosquitoes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blum_58-3"><a href="#cite_note-blum-58">[58]</a></sup> killed 158 people that year in Cuba, including 101 children under 15.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-4"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup> Tensions between the two countries, coupled with confirmed U.S. research into <a href="/wiki/Entomological_warfare" title="Entomological warfare">entomological warfare</a> during the 1950s, made these charges seem not implausible to some people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-5"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-blum_58-4"><a href="#cite_note-blum-58">[58]</a></sup> Dengue fever also occurs naturally in the Caribbean basin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_56-6"><a href="#cite_note-levy-56">[56]</a></sup> and the dengue virus is not considered a weaponizable "<a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological threat agent</a>" or <a href="/wiki/Select_agent" title="Select agent">select agent</a> by international authorities attempting to control bio-weapons.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimentation_and_testing">Experimentation and testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Experimentation and testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_military_anti-plant_research" title="United States military anti-plant research">United States military anti-plant research</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entomological_testing">Entomological testing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Entomological testing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Entomological_warfare#United_States" title="Entomological warfare">U.S. Cold War entomological warfare program</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> seriously researched the potential of <a href="/wiki/Entomological_warfare" title="Entomological warfare">entomological warfare</a> (EW) during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. EW is a specific type of biological warfare which aims to use insects as weapon, either directly or through their potential to act as <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a>. During the 1950s the United States conducted a series of field tests using entomological weapons. <a href="/wiki/Operation_Big_Itch" title="Operation Big Itch">Operation Big Itch</a>, in 1954, was designed to test munitions loaded with uninfected fleas (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xenopsylla_cheopis" title="Xenopsylla cheopis">Xenopsylla cheopis</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-croddy3_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-croddy3-60">[60]</a></sup> In May 1955 over 300,000 <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a> mosquitoes (<i><a href="/wiki/Aedes_aegypti" title="Aedes aegypti">Aedes aegypti</a></i>) were dropped over parts of the U.S. state of Georgia to determine if the air-dropped mosquitoes could survive to take meals from humans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-rose-61">[61]</a></sup> The mosquito tests were known as <a href="/wiki/Operation_Big_Buzz" title="Operation Big Buzz">Operation Big Buzz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-novick_62-0"><a href="#cite_note-novick-62">[62]</a></sup> The U.S. engaged in at least two other EW testing programs, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Drop_Kick" title="Operation Drop Kick">Operation Drop Kick</a> and <a href="/wiki/Operation_May_Day" title="Operation May Day">Operation May Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_61-1"><a href="#cite_note-rose-61">[61]</a></sup> A 1981 Army report outlined these tests as well as multiple cost-associated issues that occurred with EW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rose_61-2"><a href="#cite_note-rose-61">[61]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinical_trials">Clinical trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Clinical trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Operation_Whitecoat" title="Operation Whitecoat">Operation Whitecoat</a> involved the controlled testing of many serious agents on military personnel consented to experimentation, and understood the risks involved.  No deaths are known to have resulted from this program.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vulnerability_field_tests">Vulnerability field tests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Vulnerability field tests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_military_venues">In military venues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In military venues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In August 1949 a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Biological_Warfare_Laboratories" title="United States Army Biological Warfare Laboratories">U.S. Army Special Operations Division</a>, operating out of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Detrick" title="Fort Detrick">Fort Detrick</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, set up its first test at <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">The Pentagon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> Operatives sprayed harmless bacteria into the building's air conditioning system and observed as the microbes spread throughout the Pentagon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeline_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-timeline-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>The U.S. military acknowledges that it tested several chemical and biological weapons on US military personnel in the desert facility, including the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=East_Demilitarization_Area&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="East Demilitarization Area (page does not exist)">East Demilitarization Area</a> near <a href="/wiki/Deseret_Chemical_Depot" title="Deseret Chemical Depot">Deseret Chemical Depot</a>/<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deseret_Chemical_Test_Center" title="Deseret Chemical Test Center">Deseret Chemical Test Center</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Douglas,_Utah" title="Fort Douglas, Utah">Fort Douglas, Utah</a>, but takes the position that the tests have contributed to long-term illnesses in only a handful of exposed personnel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sltrib.com_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-sltrib.com-64">[64]</a></sup>
Veterans who took part believe they were also exposed to <a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange" title="Agent Orange">Agent Orange</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Department of Veterans Affairs">Department of Veterans Affairs</a> denies almost all claims for care and compensation made by veterans who believe they got sick as a result of the tests. The U.S. military for decades remained silent about "<a href="/wiki/Project_112" title="Project 112">Project 112</a>" and its victims, a slew of tests overseen by the Army's Deseret Test Center in Salt Lake City. Project 112 starting in the 1960s tested chemical and biological agents, including VX, sarin and E. coli, on military personnel who did not know they were being tested. After the Defense Department finally acknowledged conducting the tests on unwitting human subjects, it agreed to help the Veterans' Affairs Department track down those who were exposed, but a Government Accountability Office report in 2008 scolded the military for ceasing the effort.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sltrib.com_64-1"><a href="#cite_note-sltrib.com-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_civilian_venues">In civilian venues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In civilian venues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Medical experiments were conducted on a large scale on civilians who had not consented to participate.  Often, these experiments took place in urban areas in order to test dispersion methods.  Questions were raised about detrimental health effects after experiments in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco,_California" title="San Francisco, California">San Francisco</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, were followed by a spike in hospital visits; however, in 1977 the <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> determined that there was no association between the testing and the occurrence of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmd_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-wmd-65">[65]</a></sup> The San Francisco test involved a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> ship that sprayed <i><a href="/wiki/Serratia_marcescens" title="Serratia marcescens">Serratia marcescens</a></i> from the bay; it traveled more than 30 miles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmd_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-wmd-65">[65]</a></sup> One dispersion test involved laboratory personnel disguised as passengers spraying harmless bacteria in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_National_Airport" title="Washington National Airport">Washington National Airport</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmd_65-2"><a href="#cite_note-wmd-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Scientists tested biological pathogens, including <i>Bacillus globigii</i>, which were thought to be harmless, at public places such as subways. Light bulbs containing <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacillus_globigii" title="Bacillus globigii">Bacillus globigii</a></i> were dropped in  <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>'s <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_Subway" title="New York City Subway">subway system</a>; the result was strong enough to affect people prone to illness (also known as Subway Experiment).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-66">[66]</a></sup> Based on the circulation measurements, thousands of people would have been killed if a dangerous microbe was released in the same manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmd_65-3"><a href="#cite_note-wmd-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Jet_aircraft" title="Jet aircraft">jet aircraft</a> released material over <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Texas" title="Victoria, Texas">Victoria, Texas</a>, that was monitored in the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Keys" title="Florida Keys">Florida Keys</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmd_65-4"><a href="#cite_note-wmd-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="GAO_Report">GAO Report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: GAO Report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a> (GAO) released report GAO-08-366 titled, "Chemical and Biological Defense, DOD and VA Need to Improve Efforts to Identify and Notify Individuals Potentially Exposed during Chemical and Biological Tests." The report stated that tens of thousands of military personnel and civilians may have been exposed to biological and chemical substances through <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">DOD</a> tests. In 2003, the DOD reported it had identified 5,842 military personnel and estimated 350 civilians as being potentially exposed during the testing, known as <a href="/wiki/Project_112" title="Project 112">Project 112</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gao366_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-gao366-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>The GAO scolded the U.S. Department of Defense's (DOD) 2003 decision to stop searching for people affected by the tests was premature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sltrib.com_64-2"><a href="#cite_note-sltrib.com-64">[64]</a></sup> The GAO report also found that the DoD made no effort to inform civilians of exposure, and that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Department of Veterans Affairs">United States Department of Veterans Affairs</a> (VA) is failing to use available resources to inform veterans of possible exposure or to determine if they were deceased. After the DoD halted efforts to find those who may have been affected by the tests, veteran health activists and others identified approximately 600 additional individuals who were potentially exposed during Project 112.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gao366_67-1"><a href="#cite_note-gao366-67">[67]</a></sup> Some of the individuals were identified after the GAO reviewed records stored at the <a href="/wiki/Dugway_Proving_Ground" title="Dugway Proving Ground">Dugway Proving Ground</a>, others were identified by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Institute_of_Medicine" title="Institute of Medicine">Institute of Medicine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sltrib1_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-sltrib1-68">[68]</a></sup> Many of the newly identified suffer from long term illnesses that may have been caused by the biological or chemical testing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sltrib2_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-sltrib2-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Current_.28post-1969.29_bio-defense_program"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_(post-1969)_bio-defense_program">Current (post-1969) bio-defense program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_States_biological_weapons_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Current (post-1969) bio-defense program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/United_States_biological_defense_program" title="United States biological defense program">United States biological defense program</a></div>
<p>Both the U.S. bio-weapons ban and the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Weapons_Convention" title="Biological Weapons Convention">Biological Weapons Convention</a> restricted any work in the area of biological warfare to <a href="/wiki/Biodefense" title="Biodefense">defensive in nature</a>. In reality, this gives BWC member-states wide latitude to conduct biological weapons research because the BWC contains no provisions for monitoring or enforcement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-littlewood_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-littlewood-70">[70]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cirincione2_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-cirincione2-71">[71]</a></sup> The treaty, essentially, is a gentlemen's agreement amongst members backed by the long-prevailing thought that biological warfare should not be used in battle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-littlewood_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-littlewood-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>After Nixon declared an end to the U.S. bio-weapons program, debate in the Army centered around whether or not toxin weapons were included in the president's declaration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-9"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> Following Nixon's November 1969 order, scientists at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Detrick" title="Fort Detrick">Fort Detrick</a> worked on one toxin, <a href="/wiki/Staphylococcus" title="Staphylococcus"><i>Staphylococcus</i> enterotoxin type B</a> (SEB), for several more months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mauroni_29-10"><a href="#cite_note-mauroni-29">[29]</a></sup> Nixon ended the debate when he added toxins to the bio-weapons ban in February 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guillemin_27-5"><a href="#cite_note-guillemin-27">[27]</a></sup> The U.S. also ran a series of experiments with <a href="/wiki/Anthrax" title="Anthrax">anthrax</a>, code named <a href="/wiki/Project_Bacchus" title="Project Bacchus">Project Bacchus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Project_Clear_Vision" title="Project Clear Vision">Project Clear Vision</a> and <a href="/wiki/Project_Jefferson" title="Project Jefferson">Project Jefferson</a> in the late 1990s and early 2000s.
</p><p>In recent years certain critics have claimed the U.S. stance on biological warfare and the use of biological agents has differed from historical interpretations of the BWC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas1_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-fas1-72">[72]</a></sup> For example, it is said that the U.S. now maintains that the Article I of the BWC (which explicitly bans bio-weapons), does not apply to "non-lethal" biological agents.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas1_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-fas1-72">[72]</a></sup> Previous interpretation was stated to be in line with a definition laid out in Public Law 101-298, the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Weapons_Anti-Terrorism_Act_of_1989" title="Biological Weapons Anti-Terrorism Act of 1989">Biological Weapons Anti-Terrorism Act of 1989</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas2_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-fas2-73">[73]</a></sup> That law defined a biological agent as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas2_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-fas2-73">[73]</a></sup> 
</p>
<blockquote><p>any micro-organism, virus, infectious substance, or biological product that may be engineered as a result of biotechnology, or any naturally occurring or bioengineered component of any such microorganism, virus, infectious substance, or biological product, capable of causing death, disease, or other biological malfunction in a human, an animal, a plant, or another living organism; deterioration of food, water, equipment, supplies, or material of any kind ...</p></blockquote>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Federation_of_American_Scientists" title="Federation of American Scientists">Federation of American Scientists</a>, U.S. work on non-lethal agents exceeds limitations in the BWC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fas1_72-2"><a href="#cite_note-fas1-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
