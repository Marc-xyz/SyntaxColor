<p>"<b>Pack Up Your Troubles in Your Old Kit-Bag, and Smile, Smile, Smile</b>" is the full name of a <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> marching song, published in 1915 in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>. It was written by Welsh songwriter <a href="/wiki/George_Henry_Powell" title="George Henry Powell">George Henry Powell</a> under the pseudonym of "George Asaf", and set to music by his brother <a href="/wiki/Felix_Powell" title="Felix Powell">Felix Powell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was featured in the American show <i>Her Soldier Boy</i>, which opened in December 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Performers associated with this song include <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Edward_Hamilton_(baritone)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Edward Hamilton (baritone) (page does not exist)">Edward Hamilton</a>, the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Victor_Military_Band&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Victor Military Band (page does not exist)">Victor Military Band</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_F._Harrison&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James F. Harrison (page does not exist)">James F. Harrison</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_Johnson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Murray Johnson (page does not exist)">Murray Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reinald_Werrenrath" title="Reinald Werrenrath">Reinald Werrenrath</a>, and the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Knickerbocker_Quartet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Knickerbocker Quartet (page does not exist)">Knickerbocker Quartet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>A later play presented by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">National Theatre</a> recounts how these <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> stars rescued the song from their rejects pile and re-scored it to win a wartime competition for a <a href="/wiki/March_(music)" title="March (music)">marching song</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It became very popular, boosting British morale despite the horrors of that war. It was one of a large number of <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> songs aimed at maintaining morale, recruiting for the forces, or defending Britain's war aims. Another of these songs, "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_a_Long_Way_to_Tipperary" title="It's a Long Way to Tipperary">It's a Long Way to Tipperary</a>", was so similar in musical structure that the two were sometimes sung side by side.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>The cartoon character <a href="/wiki/Snoopy" title="Snoopy">Snoopy</a> sometimes listened to the song as he fantasized about being a WWI flying ace. In the Halloween special, <a href="/wiki/Schroeder_(Peanuts)" title="Schroeder (Peanuts)">Schroeder</a> plays a series of WWI songs on his piano, one of which was "Pack Up Your Troubles in Your Old Kit-Bag". In multiple comics Snoopy can also be seen on his doghouse, singing "It's a Long, Long Way to Tipperary", "Pack Up Your Troubles in Your Old Kit-Bag" and "Over There". He then concludes saying "We World War I flying aces are very sentimental."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In another strip, he also questions how can you pack up your troubles in a kit-bag.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_in_Your_Old_Kit-Bag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song is best remembered for its chorus:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd>Pack up your troubles in your old <a href="/wiki/Duffel_bag" title="Duffel bag">kit-bag</a></dd>
<dd>And smile, smile, smile,</dd>
<dd>While you've a <a href="/wiki/Match#Friction_matches" title="Match">lucifer</a> to light your <a href="/wiki/Cigarette" title="Cigarette">fag</a>,</dd>
<dd>Smile, boys, that's the style.</dd>
<dd>What's the use of worrying?</dd>
<dd>It never was worth while</dd>
<dd>So pack up your troubles in your old kit-bag</dd>
<dd>And smile, smile, smile.</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_languages">In other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_in_Your_Old_Kit-Bag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Dutch version goes:
</p>
<dl><dd>Pak al je zorgen in je plunjezak en fluit, fluit, fluit!</dd>
<dd>Aan alle moeilijkheden heb je lak, fluit man en 't is uit!</dd>
<dd>Waarom zou je treuren, het helpt je niet vooruit,</dd>
<dd>Dus: pak al je zorgen in je plunjezak en fluit, fluit, fluit.</dd></dl>
<p>The Spanish version
</p>
<dl><dd>Guarda tus penas en el fondo del morral y rie ya!</dd>
<dd>Ponte contento y así venceras la dificultad!</dd>
<dd>Siempre estarás alegre, nunca triste estaras, jamás!</dd>
<dd>Guarda tus penas en el fondo del morral y rie ya.!</dd></dl>
<p>The German version:
</p>
<dl><dd>Weit ist der Weg zurück ins Heimatland, so weit, so weit</dd>
<dd>Dort bei den Sternen über'm Waldesrand liegt die alte Zeit</dd>
<dd>Jeder brave Musketier sehnt heimlich sich nach dir</dd>
<dd>Weit ist der Weg zurück ins Heimatland, ja weit, so weit!</dd></dl>
<p>The Norwegian translation "Legg dine sørger i en gammel sekk" (possibly 1916) and the Swedish "Lägg dina sorger i en gammal säck" (1917) were by written by Karl-Ewert Christenson (1888–1965) and recorded by singer <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Rolf" title="Ernst Rolf">Ernst Rolf</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_performances">Other performances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_in_Your_Old_Kit-Bag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other performances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Florrie_Forde" title="Florrie Forde">Florrie Forde</a> performed it throughout the United Kingdom in 1916.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyler_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Other performers associated with this song include Helen Clark, Reinald Werrenrath, and <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Seagle" title="Oscar Seagle">Oscar Seagle</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyler2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler2-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cilla_Black" title="Cilla Black">Cilla Black</a> performed the song as a comedy/singing sketch on her <a href="/wiki/Variety_show" title="Variety show">variety</a> <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Surprise_Surprise_(TV_series)" title="Surprise Surprise (TV series)">Surprise Surprise</a></i>.
</p><p>The original version was sampled in and inspired the song "<a href="/wiki/Pack_Up" title="Pack Up">Pack Up</a>" by English musician <a href="/wiki/Eliza_Doolittle_(singer)" title="Eliza Doolittle (singer)">Eliza Doolittle</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_movies">In movies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_in_Your_Old_Kit-Bag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In movies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The song appears in several movies, including <i><a href="/wiki/Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_(1932_film)" title="Pack Up Your Troubles (1932 film)">Pack Up Your Troubles</a></i> (1932) with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurel_%26_Hardy" title="Laurel &amp; Hardy">Laurel &amp; Hardy</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/High_Pressure_(film)" title="High Pressure (film)">High Pressure</a></i> (1932), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shopworn_Angel" title="The Shopworn Angel">The Shopworn Angel</a></i> (1938).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tyler2_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tyler2-11">[11]</a></sup> It is also featured in <i><a href="/wiki/For_Me_and_My_Gal_(film)" title="For Me and My Gal (film)">For Me and My Gal</a></i> (1942) starring <a href="/wiki/Judy_Garland" title="Judy Garland">Judy Garland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gene_Kelly" title="Gene Kelly">Gene Kelly</a>, and “On Moonlight Bay” with Gordon MacRae and Doris Day (1951).  
</p><p>The song also featured briefly in the 1979 film <i><a href="/wiki/All_That_Jazz_(film)" title="All That Jazz (film)">All That Jazz</a></i>, sung between Joe Gideon (<a href="/wiki/Roy_Scheider" title="Roy Scheider">Roy Scheider</a>) and a hospital orderly. It was sang during a march in the 2010 film <a href="/wiki/Private_Peaceful_(film)" title="Private Peaceful (film)">Private Peaceful</a>, based on the book by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Morpurgo" title="Michael Morpurgo">Michael Morpurgo</a>.
</p><p>Also played by Linus in "it's the Great Pumpkin Charlie Brown," and in an episode of " The Waltons"
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_references">Literary references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_in_Your_Old_Kit-Bag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Literary references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The title of <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Owen" title="Wilfred Owen">Wilfred Owen</a>'s bitter anti-war poem <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Poems_by_Wilfred_Owen/Smile,_Smile,_Smile" title="s:Poems by Wilfred Owen/Smile, Smile, Smile">"Smile, Smile, Smile"</a> (September 1918) was derived from the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>Richard Thompson, famous for often dark and gloomy themes in his music, released an album in 2003 titled '<a href="/wiki/The_Old_Kit_Bag" title="The Old Kit Bag">The Old Kit Bag</a>'.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li>In John Dickson Carr's novel novel "The Devil in Velvet", the protagonist—a WWI veteran—hears the song in a nightmare of his war experiences: "He heard a great noise of voices singing to music. It was a cheerful song, roared out with mighty cheerfulness, yet underneath every word ran a strain of heartbreak." (Ch.XIV).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_game_usage">Video game usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pack_Up_Your_Troubles_in_Your_Old_Kit-Bag&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Video game usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>A female splicer can be heard singing the song in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioshock_Infinite:_Burial_at_Sea" title="Bioshock Infinite: Burial at Sea">Bioshock Infinite: Burial at Sea</a> Part 2.</li>
<li>The character Probst Wyatt III, from 2014's <a href="/wiki/Wolfenstein:_The_New_Order" title="Wolfenstein: The New Order">Wolfenstein: The New Order</a> sings this song as part of a diversionary tactic during the game's prologue.</li>
<li>Edwards, McManus and Townsend can be heard singing this song in the chapter "Through Mud and Blood" in the <a href="/wiki/Battlefield_1" title="Battlefield 1">Battlefield 1</a> campaign. It can be heard at random after the final tank battle.</li></ul>
