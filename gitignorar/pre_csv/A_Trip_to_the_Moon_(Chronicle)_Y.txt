<p><i><b>A Trip to the Moon</b></i> is a 1964 <a href="/wiki/Television_film" title="Television film">television</a> <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Comedy_film" title="Comedy film">comedy film</a>, produced as an episode of the <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a> series <i>Chronicle</i>. The script was written by <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Miller" title="Jonathan Miller">Jonathan Miller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bo_Goldman" title="Bo Goldman">Robert Goldman</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i>. All characters are portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bennett" title="Alan Bennett">Alan Bennett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Cook" title="Peter Cook">Peter Cook</a>, Miller, and <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Moore" title="Dudley Moore">Dudley Moore</a>, who had first worked together in the revue <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Fringe" title="Beyond the Fringe">Beyond the Fringe</a></i>.
</p><p>The hour-long show adapts the plot of Verne's novel to comment satirically on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_race" title="Moon race">Moon race</a> then underway between the United States and the Soviet Union, juxtaposing Victorian ideas and aesthetics with contemporary themes of space travel. It received mixed reviews, and Miller later dismissed it as a failure.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Trip_to_the_Moon_(Chronicle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1865, six months after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Appomattox" title="Battle of Appomattox">Battle of Appomattox</a>, the "artillery intellectuals" at the Baltimore Gun Club are bored and eager for a new project. They decide to send a rocket to the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zany_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zany-1">[1]</a></sup> After building the largest gun in the world to <a href="/wiki/Verneshot" title="Verneshot">fire off the rocket</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Adams-2">[2]</a></sup> members of the Club set off inside the capsule, furnished in plush Victorian taste and stocked with fine wines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardner_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Trip_to_the_Moon_(Chronicle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Miller, following his success in the revue <i><a href="/wiki/Beyond_the_Fringe" title="Beyond the Fringe">Beyond the Fringe</a></i>, was hired as a film and television journalist for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>. Criticizing the programming he saw as "shuddering fluorescent jelly," and inspired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newton_Minow" title="Newton Minow">Newton Minow</a>'s famous "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wasteland_Speech" title="Wasteland Speech">Wasteland Speech</a>" in 1961, Miller decided to move from reviewing into television production, hoping to provide innovative and intellectual content for the medium.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bassett_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bassett-4">[4]</a></sup> Because the United States and the Soviet Union were vying to achieve a <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">Moon landing</a>, Miller conceived the idea of a satire based on Jules Verne's <i>From the Earth to the Moon</i>, as he explained to the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It is a stylized comedy. We've gone off the track and into an insane area. I read the Verne novel about the time of the moon shot and was impressed by the strong contrast of moon travel and Victorian travel. … Our space capsule looks like a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pullman_car" title="Pullman car">Pullman car</a>. And there we are—toasting each other in velvet smoking jackets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardner_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The film was produced for <i>Chronicle</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gardner_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner-3">[3]</a></sup> a CBS documentary series that aired alternately with the <i><a href="/wiki/CBS_Reports" title="CBS Reports">CBS Reports</a></i>. <i>Chronicle</i>, broadcast on Wednesday nights from October 1963 through April 1964 and hosted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Collingwood_(journalist)" title="Charles Collingwood (journalist)">Charles Collingwood</a>, focused on modern culture and its historical origins, as a counterpoint to the "hard news" slant the <i>CBS Reports</i> emphasized. Other episodes in the series were devoted to topics such as <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_United_States" title="Constitution of the United States">Constitution of the United States</a>, and the British <a href="/wiki/Music_hall" title="Music hall">music hall</a> tradition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The series was nominated for "Outstanding Program Achievement in the Field of News Commentary or Public Affairs" at the <a href="/wiki/16th_Primetime_Emmy_Awards" title="16th Primetime Emmy Awards">16th Primetime Emmy Awards</a> in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1120" data-file-width="900" decoding="async" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG/220px-Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG/330px-Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG/440px-Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beyond_the_Fringe_original_cast.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moore, Bennett, Cook, and Miller had previously appeared together in <i>Beyond the Fringe</i> (pictured)</div></div></div>
<p>Miller co-wrote the script with Goldman, who also produced the show.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adams_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Adams-2">[2]</a></sup> Miller reunited the original cast of <i>Beyond the Fringe</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bassett_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bassett-4">[4]</a></sup>—Bennett, Cook, Moore, and himself—to play all the characters in the show, each actor taking on several roles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPT_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPT-7">[7]</a></sup> Between the taped scenes with the actors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTreview_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYTreview-8">[8]</a></sup> the show features <a href="/wiki/Montage_(filmmaking)" title="Montage (filmmaking)">montage</a> sequences by the animator <a href="/wiki/Fred_Mogubgub" title="Fred Mogubgub">Fred Mogubgub</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPT_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-SPT-7">[7]</a></sup> edited from <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent film</a> clips,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPT_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-SPT-7">[7]</a></sup> animation, and still images.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zany_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Zany-1">[1]</a></sup> One such montage covers the entire <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a> in a single minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPT_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-SPT-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Prince_(composer)" title="Robert Prince (composer)">Robert Prince</a> contributed the music score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPT_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-SPT-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A_Trip_to_the_Moon_(Chronicle)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg_Times" title="St. Petersburg Times">St. Petersburg Times</a></i> described the show as "delicious satire," singling out Mogubgub's contributions, Prince's score, and Verne's prophetic themes for special praise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPT_7-5"><a href="#cite_note-SPT-7">[7]</a></sup> The syndicated TV Key column called the show "the kind of frolic you'd expect from one of the authors of <i>Beyond the Fringe</i> … Human beings are alternately lampooned and applauded for the faulty but imaginative creatures they are."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zany_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Zany-1">[1]</a></sup> However, the <i>New York Times</i> panned it, calling it "a weird and stilted mélange" and commenting: "The difficulty for the players … was that Mr. Verne's imagination was infinitely more interesting than their labored attempts to make light of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYTreview_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYTreview-8">[8]</a></sup> Miller later described the show as "just boring and no bloody good."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bassett_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bassett-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
