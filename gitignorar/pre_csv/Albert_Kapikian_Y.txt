	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Albert Kapikian</h1>

<p><b>Albert Zaven Kapikian</b> (1930 – 2014) was an Armenian-American virologist who developed the first licensed <a href="/wiki/Vaccine" title="Vaccine">vaccine</a> against <a href="/wiki/Rotavirus" title="Rotavirus">rotavirus</a>, the most common cause of severe <a href="/wiki/Diarrhea" title="Diarrhea">diarrhea</a> in infants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabin_Gold_Medal" title="Sabin Gold Medal">Sabin Gold Medal</a> for his pioneering work on the vaccine.  He is the 13th recipient of this recognition, awarded annually by the <a href="/wiki/Sabin_Vaccine_Institute" title="Sabin Vaccine Institute">Sabin Vaccine Institute</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  Called the father of human gastroenteritis virus research, Kapikian identified the first norovirus, initially called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwalk_virus" title="Norwalk virus">Norwalk virus</a>, in 1972; and he and his colleagues at the National Institutes of Health identified the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hepatitis_A_virus" title="Hepatitis A virus">hepatitis A virus</a> in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kapikian graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cornell_Medical_College" title="Cornell Medical College">Cornell Medical College</a> in 1956 and began a career with the <a href="/wiki/National_Institutes_of_Health" title="National Institutes of Health">National Institutes of Health</a> in 1957.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  He was chief of the epidemiology section of the Laboratory of Infectious Diseases at NIH’s National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases (NIAID), a position he held for 45 years. In 1998 he was appointed Deputy Director of the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Allergy_and_Infectious_Diseases" title="National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases">National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Kapikian died on February 24, 2014, at the age of 83.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
