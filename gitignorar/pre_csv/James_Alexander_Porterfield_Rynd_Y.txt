	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">James Alexander Porterfield Rynd</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

<b>James Alexander Porterfield Rynd</b> (6 April 1846 – 17 March 1917) was an Irish <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> player and lawyer.
</p><p>He was born on 6 April 1846 the son of Dublin solicitor James William Goodlatte Rynd<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingsInnsBKF_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-KingsInnsBKF-1">[1]</a></sup> and Isabella Susannah Stephens Rynd. Porterfield Rynd's uncle (his fathers half brother) was Dr. <a href="/wiki/Francis_Rynd" title="Francis Rynd">Francis Rynd</a> the inventor of the hollow needle syringe. His cousin Maria Rynd was <a href="/wiki/First_Lady" title="First Lady">first lady</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a> in 1879.
</p><p>He won the Dublin Chess Congress No. 3 Tournament in 1865. This tournament, run alongside an international chess tournament in Dublin, was reserved for residents of Ireland and is widely considered to be the first <a href="/wiki/Irish_Chess_Championship" title="Irish Chess Championship">Irish Chess Championship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Porterfield Rynd's birth date is often listed incorrectly as 1855 this has led some books to cast doubt on his claim to the Irish championship in 1865, such as <i>The Guinness Book of Chess Records</i> by Ken Whyld.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> But further articles and his obituaries discount this and state he was born in 1846 and died aged 71 in 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was accepted as the Irish Chess Champion until 1886 when a tournament was hosted by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Chess_Union" title="Irish Chess Union">Irish Chess Association</a> to find an Irish Champion. Rynd won the title again in 1892.
Porterfield Rynd held an LLB, in 1869 he entered the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Inns" title="King's Inns">King's Inns</a>, Dublin, and was called to the Bar in 1874.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KingsInnsBKF_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-KingsInnsBKF-1">[1]</a></sup> On 7 September 1869 he married Anna Cranwill and on 9 October 1873 his first child was born: Kenneth Arly Rynd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Easily the most colourful personage in the place was Porterfield Rynd, one of the ablest members of the Dublin bar—a man who, if he had been half as devoted to the drudgery of work as he was to the allurement of play, could easily have attained the highest honours in the judiciary.</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was a member of <a href="/wiki/Clontarf,_Dublin" title="Clontarf, Dublin">Clontarf</a> Tennis and Chess Club, and played many sports in his youth. Rynd was a member of Dublin Chess Club and played in the first ever <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_Cup" title="Armstrong Cup">Armstrong Cup</a>, the oldest Irish league competition. He briefly wrote a column for the <i>Irish Sportsman and Farmer</i>.
</p><p>In the 1890s Porterfield Rynd edited a chess column which regularly appeared on the back page of the Saturday issue of Dublin's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evening_Herald" title="Evening Herald">Evening Herald</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> He was not burdened with modesty and the title <i>Irish Champion</i> appears beside his column.
</p><p>He died in Dublin on 17 March 1917, his obituary was in the Irish Times of Monday 19 March 1917, <i>RYND – 17 March 1917 JAMES ALEXANDER PORTERFIELD RYND, Barrister-at-Law, in his 71st year</i>, and there was also an obituary in the Belfast Newsletter 22 March 1917.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Alexander_Porterfield_Rynd&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Porterfield Rynd was a <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionist</a> and produced pamphlet of his thoughts on the subject in 1906 for the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Unionist_Alliance" title="Irish Unionist Alliance">Irish Unionist Association</a>.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>A letter of Rynd's outlining support for the unionist cause are contained in letters to <a href="/wiki/Bonar_Law" title="Bonar Law">Bonar Law</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> to whom the <i>Colourful</i> Rynd was described as being a confidante.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Rynd was also associated with the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Liberal_Unionist_Association" title="Dublin Liberal Unionist Association">Dublin Liberal Unionist Association</a>, serving as its chairman.
</p>
