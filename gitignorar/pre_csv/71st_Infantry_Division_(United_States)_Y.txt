</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=71st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The division was first organized as the 71st Light Division (Pack, Jungle), intended for use in the mountainous jungle areas of the <a href="/wiki/Asiatic-Pacific_Theater" title="Asiatic-Pacific Theater">Pacific theater</a>. Smaller than the standard infantry division, at about 9,000 personnel, its primary means of transport was hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mules</a> controlled by several <a href="/wiki/Quartermaster" title="Quartermaster">quartermaster</a> pack companies of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_American" title="African American">African-American</a> <a href="/wiki/Arriero" title="Arriero">muleteers</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a> several <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">battalions</a> of <a href="/wiki/M116_howitzer" title="M116 howitzer">75mm</a> <a href="/wiki/Mountain_gun" title="Mountain gun">pack howitzers</a>, which could be broken down and carried by mule train. The <a href="/wiki/14th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="14th Infantry Regiment (United States)">14th Infantry Regiment</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Regular_Army_(United_States)" title="Regular Army (United States)">Regular Army</a> unit which had been stationed in the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal_Zone" title="Panama Canal Zone">Panama Canal Zone</a> for years prior to the war and had received extensive training in jungle operations during that time was assigned to the division to provide the nucleus of jungle expertise. After training at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camp_Carson" title="Camp Carson">Camp Carson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, the division was sent to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Hunter_Liggett" title="Fort Hunter Liggett">Hunter Ligget Military Reservation</a> in the mountains inland from <a href="/wiki/Big_Sur" title="Big Sur">Big Sur</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, where it maneuvered against the <a href="/wiki/89th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="89th Infantry Division (United States)">89th Light Division</a> as a test of the light division concept. As a result of the test it was decided that the light divisions had insufficient manpower and firepower to be effective and the concept was abandoned. The 71st Division was sent to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Benning" title="Fort Benning">Fort Benning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, where it was reorganized and retrained as a standard infantry division, although it remained unusual in having Regular Army infantry regiments assigned to a division raised in the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_United_States" title="Army of the United States">Army of the United States</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_chronicle">Combat chronicle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=71st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Combat chronicle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:These_women_and_children_were_liberated_when_the_concentration_camp_for_Jewish_prisoners_at_Lambach,_Austria,_was_overrun_by_the_71st_Infantry_Division_Death_rate_at_the_camp,_mostly_from_starvation,_was_reputed_to(...)_-_NARA_-_531345.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="502" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="184" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/These_women_and_children_were_liberated_when_the_concentration_camp_for_Jewish_prisoners_at_Lambach%2C_Austria%2C_was_overrun_by_the_71st_Infantry_Division_Death_rate_at_the_camp%2C_mostly_from_starvation%2C_was_reputed_to%28...%29_-_NARA_-_531345.gif/220px-thumbnail.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/These_women_and_children_were_liberated_when_the_concentration_camp_for_Jewish_prisoners_at_Lambach%2C_Austria%2C_was_overrun_by_the_71st_Infantry_Division_Death_rate_at_the_camp%2C_mostly_from_starvation%2C_was_reputed_to%28...%29_-_NARA_-_531345.gif/330px-thumbnail.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/These_women_and_children_were_liberated_when_the_concentration_camp_for_Jewish_prisoners_at_Lambach%2C_Austria%2C_was_overrun_by_the_71st_Infantry_Division_Death_rate_at_the_camp%2C_mostly_from_starvation%2C_was_reputed_to%28...%29_-_NARA_-_531345.gif/440px-thumbnail.gif 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:These_women_and_children_were_liberated_when_the_concentration_camp_for_Jewish_prisoners_at_Lambach,_Austria,_was_overrun_by_the_71st_Infantry_Division_Death_rate_at_the_camp,_mostly_from_starvation,_was_reputed_to(...)_-_NARA_-_531345.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jewish women and children who were liberated from a concentration camp by the 71st Infantry Division during the final days of World War II in Europe</div></div></div> 
<p>The 71st Infantry Division arrived at <a href="/wiki/Le_Havre" title="Le Havre">Le Havre</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, 6 February 1945, and trained at Camp Old Gold with headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Lim%C3%A9sy" title="Limésy">Limesy</a>. The division moved east, relieved the <a href="/wiki/100th_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="100th Infantry Division (United States)">100th Infantry Division</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ratzwiller" title="Ratzwiller">Ratswiller</a> and saw its first action on 11 March 1945. Their ouster of the Germans from France began 15 March. The division moved through outer belts of the <a href="/wiki/Siegfried_Line" title="Siegfried Line">Siegfried Line</a>, captured <a href="/wiki/Pirmasens" title="Pirmasens">Pirmasens</a>, 21 March, and crossed the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> at <a href="/wiki/Oppenheim" title="Oppenheim">Oppenheim</a>, 30 March. The 71st continued the advance, taking <a href="/wiki/Coburg" title="Coburg">Coburg</a> without resistance, cutting the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_9" title="Bundesautobahn 9">Munich-Berlin autobahn</a>, 13 April, and capturing <a href="/wiki/Bayreuth" title="Bayreuth">Bayreuth</a> after fierce opposition on 16 April. Moving south, the Division destroyed Schönfeld, 18 April, took <a href="/wiki/Sulzbach-Rosenberg" title="Sulzbach-Rosenberg">Rosenberg</a>, crossed the <a href="/wiki/Naab" title="Naab">Naab</a> River at <a href="/wiki/Kallm%C3%BCnz" title="Kallmünz">Kallmünz</a> on 24 April and crossed the <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> on 26 April. <a href="/wiki/Regensburg" title="Regensburg">Regensburg</a> fell on the next day and <a href="/wiki/Straubing" title="Straubing">Straubing</a> on 28 April. As resistance crumbled, the division crossed the <a href="/wiki/Isar" title="Isar">Isar</a> on 29 April and entered <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, 2 May.
</p><p>Participated in the liberation of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_concentration_camps" title="Nazi concentration camps">concentration camps</a> including one in Austria called <a href="/wiki/Gunskirchen" title="Gunskirchen">Gunskirchen</a> Lager, a subcamp of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mauthausen-Gusen_concentration_camp" title="Mauthausen-Gusen concentration camp">Mathausen</a>, on 4 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> A pamphlet was produced by the US Army after they liberated the camp, called "The Seventy-First came to Gunskirchen Lager." The book recounts in detail, and with graphic photos, the tragedy they found in the camp. The complete booklet is available for free on-line.
</p><p>The 71st organized and occupied defensive positions along the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enns_River" title="Enns River">Enns River</a> and contacted <a href="/wiki/Red_Army#The_Great_Patriotic_War" title="Red Army">Russian forces</a> east of <a href="/wiki/Linz" title="Linz">Linz</a>, 8 May, the day before hostilities ceased, having gone further east than any other U.S. Army unit. The division was assigned occupational duties until it left for home and inactivation 1 March 1946.
</p><p>During the last several weeks of the war, the <a href="/wiki/761st_Tank_Battalion_(United_States)" title="761st Tank Battalion (United States)">761st Tank Battalion</a>, an African-American unit, was attached to the 71st Division and fought with it. The 71st Division is also the formation in which Lt. <a href="/wiki/John_Eisenhower" title="John Eisenhower">John D. Eisenhower</a>, General <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a>'s son, served.
</p><p>In January 1946, Colonel William Westmoreland was appointed commander, and was responsible for leading the units that had not yet been deactivated back to the United States so they could be demobilized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alaska">Alaska</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=71st_Infantry_Division_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Alaska">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1954 the 71st Infantry Division was reactivated in the northwest United States and <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> as the division headquarters for several geographically separated units, to include the <a href="/wiki/53rd_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="53rd Infantry Regiment (United States)">53d Infantry Regiment</a> headquartered at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Base_Elmendorf-Richardson#Fort_Richardson" title="Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson">Fort Richardson</a>, Alaska, with additional units stationed at <a href="/wiki/Fort_Greely" title="Fort Greely">Fort Greely</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Regiment (United States)">4th</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/5th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="5th Infantry Regiment (United States)">5th</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Infantry regiments at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joint_Base_Lewis-McChord#Fort_Lewis" title="Joint Base Lewis-McChord">Fort Lewis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. The <a href="/wiki/747th_Tank_Battalion_(United_States)" title="747th Tank Battalion (United States)">723rd Tank Battalion</a> was also withdrawn from the Army Reserve and activated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Irwin" title="Fort Irwin">Camp Irwin</a> and assigned to the 71st.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In this status the division was known as a "static division" not capable of or intended for deployment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> (A second "static" unit, the <a href="/wiki/23rd_Infantry_Division_(United_States)" title="23rd Infantry Division (United States)">23d Infantry Division</a>, was activated in the Caribbean region.) 
</p><p>Units of the reactivated 71st Infantry Division included the following:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Headquarters</li>
<li>Headquarters Company</li>
<li>Medical Detachment, Division HQ</li>
<li>71st Infantry Division Band</li>
<li>71st Military Police Company</li>
<li>771st Ordnance Battalion</li>
<li>71st Quartermaster Company</li>
<li>71st Replacement Company</li>
<li>71st Signal Company</li>
<li>71st Reconnaissance Company</li>
<li>723d Tank Battalion (120mm Gun)</li>
<li>371st Medical Battalion</li>
<li>271st Engineer Battalion (Combat)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/4th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="4th Infantry Regiment (United States)">4th Infantry Regiment</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/5th_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="5th Infantry Regiment (United States)">5th Infantry Regiment</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/53rd_Infantry_Regiment_(United_States)" title="53rd Infantry Regiment (United States)">53d Infantry Regiment</a></li>
<li>Headquarters and Headquarters Battery, 71st Division Artillery</li>
<li>Medical Detachment, 71st Division Artillery</li>
<li>555th Field Artillery Battalion (105mm Howitzer, Towed)</li>
<li>564th Field Artillery Battalion (155mm Howitzer, Towed)</li>
<li>167th Antiaircraft Artillery Battalion (AW)(SP)</li>
<li>274th Armored Field Artillery Battalion (105mm Howitzer, SP)</li>
<li>607th Armored Field Artillery Battalion (105mm Howitzer, SP)</li></ul>
<p>The division lasted in this status for less than two years, being inactivated at Fort Lewis on 15 September 1956.
</p>
