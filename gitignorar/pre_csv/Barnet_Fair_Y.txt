<p><b>Barnet Fair</b> is an annual horse and pleasure fair held near Mays Lane, <a href="/wiki/Chipping_Barnet" title="Chipping Barnet">Barnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, on the first Monday in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarnetOnline_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BarnetOnline-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Fair takes place over three days starting on 4 September. It still operates under its royal Charter which is issued by Barnet council. The focus in the present-day Fair is no longer on horses and other livestock; but it is rather a pleasure fair instead.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnet_Fair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The fair began in 1588 when <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a> granted a charter to the Lord of the Manor of Barnet to hold a fair twice yearly, in addition to the weekly <a href="/wiki/Barnet_Market" title="Barnet Market">Barnet Market</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnet4U_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barnet4U-2">[2]</a></sup> Originally held in June and October, Lord John Tomlinson changed the dates to April and September in 1758 for the reason of improving business.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnet4U_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barnet4U-2">[2]</a></sup> From 1881, the April fair ceased.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnet4U_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Barnet4U-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The focus of the fair was its livestock, with animals being brought from across the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarnetOnline_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-BarnetOnline-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnet4U_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Barnet4U-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1834, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> reported that Barnet Fair was England's largest cattle market, with up to 40,000 animals on display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnet4U_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Barnet4U-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>From the eighteenth century, the festivities also used to include horse racing, although these ceased in 1870 when the railway station was built on the ground in which they had been held.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BarnetOnline_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-BarnetOnline-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barnet4U_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Barnet4U-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barnet_Fair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1896, a film was made about Barnet Fair, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Barnet_Horse_Fair_(film)" title="Barnet Horse Fair (film)">Barnet Horse Fair</a></i>.
</p><p>The term 'Barnet Fair', normally shortened to 'Barnet', has become <a href="/wiki/Rhyming_slang" title="Rhyming slang">rhyming slang</a> for 'haircut'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Barnet Fair" is the name of a song by <a href="/wiki/Steeleye_Span" title="Steeleye Span">Steeleye Span</a>.
</p>
