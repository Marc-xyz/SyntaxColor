	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Lexicographic product of graphs</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Graph-lexicographic-product.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="317" data-file-width="668" decoding="async" height="142" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Graph-lexicographic-product.svg/300px-Graph-lexicographic-product.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Graph-lexicographic-product.svg/450px-Graph-lexicographic-product.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Graph-lexicographic-product.svg/600px-Graph-lexicographic-product.svg.png 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Graph-lexicographic-product.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The lexicographic product of graphs.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Graph_theory" title="Graph theory">graph theory</a>, the <b>lexicographic product</b> or <b>(graph) composition</b> <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i></span>  of graphs <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">G</span> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">H</span> is a graph such that
</p>
<ul><li>the vertex set of <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i></span> is the <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_product" title="Cartesian product">cartesian product</a> <span class="texhtml"><i>V(G)</i> × <i>V(H)</i></span>; and</li>
<li>any two vertices <span class="texhtml">(<i>u</i>,<i>v</i>)</span> and <span class="texhtml">(<i>x</i>,<i>y</i>)</span> are adjacent in <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i></span> if and only if either <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">u</span> is adjacent with <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">x</span> in <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">G</span> or <span class="texhtml"><i>u</i> = <i>x</i></span> and <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">v</span> is adjacent with <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">y</span> in <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">H</span>.</li></ul>
<p>If the edge relations of the two graphs are <a href="/wiki/Order_theory" title="Order theory">order relations</a>, then the edge relation of their lexicographic product is the corresponding <a href="/wiki/Lexicographical_order" title="Lexicographical order">lexicographic order</a>.
</p><p>The lexicographic product was first studied by <a href="/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff" title="Felix Hausdorff">Felix Hausdorff</a> (<a href="#CITEREFHausdorff1914">1914</a>). As <a href="#CITEREFFeigenbaumSchäffer1986">Feigenbaum &amp; Schäffer (1986)</a> showed, the problem of recognizing whether a graph is a lexicographic product is equivalent in complexity to the <a href="/wiki/Graph_isomorphism_problem" title="Graph isomorphism problem">graph isomorphism problem</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lexicographic_product_of_graphs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lexicographic product is in general <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commutativity" title="Commutativity">noncommutative</a>: <span class="texhtml"><i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i> ≠ <i>H</i> ∙ <i>G</i></span>. However it satisfies a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distributivity" title="Distributivity">distributive law</a> with respect to disjoint union: <span class="texhtml">(<i>A</i> + <i>B</i>) ∙ <i>C</i> = <i>A</i> ∙ <i>C</i> + <i>B</i> ∙ <i>C</i></span>.
In addition it satisfies an identity with respect to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complement_(graph_theory)" title="Complement (graph theory)">complementation</a>: <span class="texhtml">C(<i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i>) = C(<i>G</i>) ∙ C(<i>H</i>)</span>. In particular, the lexicographic product of two <a href="/wiki/Self-complementary_graph" title="Self-complementary graph">self-complementary graphs</a> is self-complementary.
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independence_number" title="Independence number">independence number</a> of a lexicographic product may be easily calculated from that of its factors (<a href="#CITEREFGellerStahl1975">Geller &amp; Stahl 1975</a>):
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">α(<i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i>) = α(<i>G</i>)α(<i>H</i>)</span>.</dd></dl>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clique_number" title="Clique number">clique number</a> of a lexicographic product is as well multiplicative:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">ω(<i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i>) = ω(<i>G</i>)ω(<i>H</i>)</span>.</dd></dl>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chromatic_number" title="Chromatic number">chromatic number</a> of a lexicographic product is equal to the <a href="/wiki/Fractional_coloring#Definitions" title="Fractional coloring"><i>b</i>-fold chromatic number</a> of <i>G</i>, for <i>b</i> equal to the chromatic number of <i>H</i>:
</p>
<dl><dd><span class="texhtml">χ(<i>G</i> ∙ <i>H</i>) = χ<sub>b</sub>(<i>G</i>)</span>, where <i>b</i> = χ(<i>H</i>).</dd></dl>
<p>The lexicographic product of two graphs is a <a href="/wiki/Perfect_graph" title="Perfect graph">perfect graph</a> if and only if both factors are perfect (<a href="#CITEREFRavindraParthasarathy1977">Ravindra &amp; Parthasarathy 1977</a>).
</p>
