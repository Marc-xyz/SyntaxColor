	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Social fund</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>social fund</b> (sometimes also called <i>Social Investment Fund</i>, <i>Social Fund for Development</i>, <i>Social Action Fund</i>, <i>National Solidarity Fund</i> or <i>Social Development Agency</i>) is an institution, typically in a <a href="/wiki/Developing_country" title="Developing country">developing country</a>, that provides financing (usually grants) for small-scale public investments targeted at meeting the needs of poor and vulnerable communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Social Funds also aim at contributing to <a href="/wiki/Social_capital" title="Social capital">social capital</a> and development at the local level. In many cases they serve as innovators and demonstrators of new methods of decentralized participatory decision-making, management, and accountability that may be adopted for broader application by public sector organizations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence_of_social_funds">Prevalence of social funds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_fund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prevalence of social funds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007 Social Funds existed in more than 45 countries, predominantly in poorer and smaller developing countries that receive significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_Development_Assistance" title="Official Development Assistance">official development assistance</a>. However, a Social Fund also exists in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, a country that has recently joined the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a>, as well as in many other Eastern European countries. Probably the largest Social Fund is the Pakistan Poverty Alleviation Fund (PPAF) with a resource base of US$ 500 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Social Funds have channeled close to US$ 5 billion of <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> funding in Africa alone between 1999 and 2005 <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and have channeled more than ten billion dollars from all donors and governments' own resources over the past 20 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_evolution">History and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_fund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first Social Fund was created in 1987 in <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>. During the 1990s Social Funds spread quickly throughout Latin America and Africa with the intellectual and financial backing of the World Bank and other donors.
</p><p>The first generation social funds were created to serve as short-term safety nets to soften the impact of <a href="/wiki/Structural_adjustment" title="Structural adjustment">structural adjustment</a> policies on the poor, which was mainly achieved by providing temporary employment. Second generation social funds have adopted more explicit institutional strategies aimed at empowerment and capacity building of communities as well as local governments in the context of <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralization</a>.
</p><p>Social Funds were created as temporary agencies that would be phased out once capacity of line agencies had been strengthened. Some Social Funds, such as in <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, are now in the process of being phased out, and others, such as in <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, are supposed to be closed down by law a few years from now. However, many Social Funds may well remain permanent institutions fulfilling important functions that line agencies may not be well set up to perform.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_fund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the benefits of Social Funds have been their ability to better reach poor constituencies, to reduce <a href="/wiki/Political_corruption" title="Political corruption">corruption</a> and to introduce innovations. Social Funds have pioneered community-driven development (CDD), whereby <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Community-based_organization" title="Community-based organization">community-based organizations</a> (typically representing a few hundred people or less, often in rural areas) administrate funds themselves and choose where to invest them, thus increasing transparency and accountability for the use of funds. This approach also builds the self-confidence and capacity of local communities. It also helps projects to better meet local needs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_fund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Social Funds have been criticized for  displacing or weakening existing institutions such as sectoral ministries and departments, particularly since they often - but not always - offer salaries that are significantly higher than in the public sector. Another criticism is that there has been no exit strategy to phase out Social Funds, although they were intended to be temporary institutions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evaluation">Evaluation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Social_fund&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evaluation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002 the World Bank carried out the first systematic, cross-country evaluation of Social Funds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The evaluation covered social funds in Armenia, Bolivia, Honduras, Nicaragua, Peru, and Zambia. Investments had been concentrated in education, health, water and sanitation sub-projects. Each evaluation reviewed the social fund’s poverty targeting, sustainability, welfare impacts, and costs. One of the evaluation's main conclusions was that the Social Funds evaluated were indeed effective at reaching the poor and extremely poor communities and households, something that had been disputed given allegedly limited capabilities of poor communities to manage funds and execute sub-projects.
</p>
