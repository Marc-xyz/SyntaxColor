<p><b>Samuel Hammond</b> <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Divinity" title="Doctor of Divinity">D.D.</a> (died December 10, 1665, in <a href="/wiki/Hackney_(parish)" title="Hackney (parish)">Hackney</a>) was a Church of England minister, and later a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonconformism" title="Nonconformism">nonconformist</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Samuel_Hammond_(minister)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hammond is said to have been a ‘butcher's son of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>’,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Venn-2">[2]</a></sup> although the <i>Oxford Dictionary of National Biography</i> notes no butchers of his surname on the lists of freemen of that city.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-odnb_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1638 Hammond entered <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="King's College, Cambridge">King's College, Cambridge</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Sizar" title="Sizar">sizar</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Venn-2">[2]</a></sup> There he was <a href="/wiki/Servitor" title="Servitor">servitor</a> to Dr. <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Collins_(theologian)" title="Samuel Collins (theologian)">Samuel Collins</a> (1576–1651), professor of divinity at Cambridge, and by the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Montagu,_1st_Earl_of_Manchester" title="Henry Montagu, 1st Earl of Manchester">Earl of Manchester's</a> interest obtained a fellowship in <a href="/wiki/Magdalene_College,_Cambridge" title="Magdalene College, Cambridge">Magdalene College</a>. He created a great impression in the university by his preaching in St. Giles's Church, and obtained many pupils and followers. Sir <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arthur_Hesilrigge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arthur Hesilrigge (page does not exist)">Arthur Hesilrigge</a> took him into the north of England as his chaplain, and he settled for some time as minister in <a href="/wiki/Bishopwearmouth" title="Bishopwearmouth">Bishopwearmouth</a>, but moved from there to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle</a>. An order of the common council, dated 5 November 1652, appointed him as preacher at <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_Cathedral" title="Newcastle Cathedral">St. Nicholas Church, Newcastle</a>, on Sunday and lecturer on Thursday, at a salary of £100.
</p><p>While at Newcastle Hammond was concerned in the examination and exposure of an impostor named Thomas Ramsay. This man's frauds were exposed in a tract entitled <i>A False Jew: or a Wonderful Discovery of a Scot, baptized at London for a Christian, circumcised at Rome to act a Jew, rebaptized at Hexham for a Believer, but found out at Newcastle to be a Cheat</i>. The dedicatory epistles are signed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Weld_(minister)" title="Thomas Weld (minister)">Tho. Weld</a>, Sam. Hammond, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cuthbert_Sydenham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cuthbert Sydenham (page does not exist)">Cuth. Sidenham</a>, and Wil. Durant. The tract contains a second title-page and pagination, which is the <i>Declaration and Confession</i> published by the impostor under the name of Joseph ben Israel. The Baptist minister of <a href="/wiki/Hexham" title="Hexham">Hexham</a>, Thomas Tillam, supposed himself unfairly treated in this pamphlet, and replied to it by<i>‘Banners of Love displayed …; or an Answer to a Narrative stuffed with Untruths, by four Newcastle Gentlemen</i>. Hammond also helped to write a tract attacking the <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>, entitled <i>The Perfect Pharise, under Monkish Holines, opposing the Fundamental Principles of the Doctrine of the Gospel, … manifesting himself in the Generation of men called Quakers</i>. Hammond's name comes third among five Newcastle ministers who sign this tract. An introductory epistle ‘to the Reader’ by Hammond appears in a book called <i>God's Judgements upon Drunkards, Swearers, and Sabbath-Breakers</i>.
</p><p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Restoration" title="English Restoration">Restoration</a> he was ejected from his charge at Newcastle, and retired to <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> as minister to the society of merchants there. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Hyde,_1st_Earl_of_Clarendon" title="Edward Hyde, 1st Earl of Clarendon">Lord-Chancellor Hyde</a> objected to renew the charter of the society of merchants, which was nearly expired, if they retained Hammond, and he was compelled to leave. He went first to <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>, where a merchant named Cutler befriended him. The historian <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Calamy_(historian)" title="Edmund Calamy (historian)">Edmund Calamy</a> mentions with praise a letter from Stockholm as having ‘something of the spirit and style of the martyrs,’ though it was apparently never printed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">[1]</a></sup> Hammond then proceeded to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danzig" title="Danzig">Danzig</a>, and finally to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, taking up his abode in <a href="/wiki/Hackney_(parish)" title="Hackney (parish)">Hackney</a>. He died on 10 December 1665.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
