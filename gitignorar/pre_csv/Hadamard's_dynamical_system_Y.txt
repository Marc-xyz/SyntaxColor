	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Hadamard's dynamical system</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Chaotic dynamical system, a type of "billiards"</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, the <b>Hadamard dynamical system</b> (also called <b>Hadamard's billiard</b> or the <b>Hadamard–Gutzwiller model</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical system</a>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_billiards" title="Dynamical billiards">dynamical billiards</a>. Introduced by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Hadamard" title="Jacques Hadamard">Jacques Hadamard</a> in 1898,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and studied by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Gutzwiller" title="Martin Gutzwiller">Martin Gutzwiller</a> in the 1980s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> it is the first dynamical system to be proven <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a>.
</p><p>The system considers the motion of a free (<a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">frictionless</a>) <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">particle</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Bolza_surface" title="Bolza surface">Bolza surface</a>, i.e, a two-dimensional surface of genus two (a donut with two holes) and constant negative <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">curvature</a>; this is the simplest compact <a href="/wiki/Riemann_surface" title="Riemann surface">Riemann surface</a>. Hadamard was able to show that every particle trajectory moves away from every other: that all trajectories have a positive <a href="/wiki/Lyapunov_exponent" title="Lyapunov exponent">Lyapunov exponent</a>.
</p><p>Frank Steiner argues that Hadamard's study should be considered to be the first-ever examination of a chaotic dynamical system, and that Hadamard should be considered the first discoverer of chaos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He points out that the study was widely disseminated, and considers the impact of the ideas on the thinking of <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernst_Mach" title="Ernst Mach">Ernst Mach</a>.
</p><p>The system is  particularly important in that in 1963, <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Sinai" title="Yakov Sinai">Yakov Sinai</a>, in studying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinai%27s_billiards" title="Sinai's billiards">Sinai's billiards</a> as a model of the classical ensemble of a Boltzmann–Gibbs gas, was able to show that the motion of the atoms in the gas follow the trajectories in the Hadamard dynamical system.
</p>
