<p><b>Fruit soju</b> (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko">과일소주</span>, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Korean" title="Romanization of Korean">romanized</a>: </small><i lang="ko-Latn" title="Korean-language romanization">gwail-soju</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Flavored_liquor" title="Flavored liquor">flavored</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a>, an alcoholic drink originally from Korea. In 2015, <i>Sunhari</i>, the first fruit-flavored <a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a> brand was released by beverage manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Lotte_Chilsung" title="Lotte Chilsung">Lotte Chilsung</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fruit_soju&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the first fruit soju was released, standard <a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a> was popular in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> as an alcoholic drink for adults. However, some people found soju difficult to drink due to its high alcohol content and strong flavor. Its popularity declined because of Korean societal changes regarding work, which resulted in people not having enough time to drink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, the first fruit soju, <i>Sunhari</i>, was released. It was flavored with <a href="/wiki/Citron" title="Citron">citron</a> and had a lower alcohol content compared to traditional soju, making it popular with younger consumers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> After <i>Sunhari</i> had become popular in the soju market, other companies released their own versions. <i>Joenday</i> released three types; <i>Chamisle</i> released <i>Jamongisle</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">grapefruit</a> flavored soju that became as popular as <i>Sunhari</i>. <i>Sunhari</i> retained the strong taste of traditional soju, whereas <i>Jamongisle</i> offered a sweeter, milder taste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The popularity of fruit soju continued for a year after the release of <i>Sunhari</i>, however in March 2016, sales decreased as the hype that first surrounded fruit soju disappeared. Today, fruit soju is not consumed often, and consequently, it is no longer widely available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Currently, sparkling <a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a> is preferred over all other types of this beverage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of Korea's fruit soju brands were released in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, including <i>Dew on Jamong (자몽에 이슬)</i> and <i>Sunhari</i>. <a href="/wiki/Lotte_Chilsung" title="Lotte Chilsung">Lotte Chilsung</a> released <i>Sunhari (순하리)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">LA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">Colorado</a>, USA, exporting 9,600 boxes (20 bottles per box) in February 2016 and 150,000 boxes in March 2016. A Korean company located in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> produced three flavours of fruit soju.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Fruit soju has a distinct fruit flavor and lower alcohol content compared to standard <a href="/wiki/Soju" title="Soju">soju</a>. The beverage comes in a variety of flavors, such as <a href="/wiki/Blueberry" title="Blueberry">blueberry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">grapefruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a>. Special limited editions of fruit soju have been produced, such as a 9,000 bottle limited release batch of <i>C1 Lime</i> soju from the <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a> company <i>Daesun Jujo (대선주조)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fruit soju contains large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">sugar</a>, with each bottle containing 32.4 grams (1.14 oz). The <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calorie</a> content of each bottle of fruit soju totals 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KCal" title="KCal">kcal</a>; more calories than one bowl of rice and 5 g (0.18 oz) more sugar than a serving of <a href="/wiki/Cola" title="Cola">Coke</a>, contributors to <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> and an increase of visceral fat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2016 article identified the most popular fruit soju for university students as <i>Dew on <a href="/wiki/Grapefruit" title="Grapefruit">Grapefruit</a> (자몽에 이슬)</i>, with students indicating they preferred this less-sweet version of the beverage. The second-most popular fruit soju in this demographic was <i>Bokbadeun Brother (복받은 부라더)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
