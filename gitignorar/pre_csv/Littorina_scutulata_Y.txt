<p><i><b>Littorina scutulata</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sea_snail" title="Sea snail">sea snail</a>, a marine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gastropod" title="Gastropod">gastropod</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollusk" title="Mollusk">mollusk</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Littorinidae" title="Littorinidae">Littorinidae</a>, the winkles or periwinkles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WoRMS_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-WoRMS-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>This species lives lower on rocks than does <i>L. planaxis</i>, and migrates up and down rocks with the tide. It crawls out of tidepools at night. Often, it  hides at low tide in cracks or <a href="/wiki/Barnacle" title="Barnacle">barnacle shells</a>.  The waves on both sides of the foot are out of phase with one another (ditaxic). It feeds mainly on <a href="/wiki/Diatom" title="Diatom">diatom films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">microscopic algae</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lichens" title="Lichens">lichens</a>, etc., and will also feed on <i><a href="/wiki/Pelvetia" title="Pelvetia">Pelvetia</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulva_(genus)" title="Ulva (genus)">Ulva</a></i>, and other larger algae. <i>L. scutulata</i> breeds in all seasons except summer. Eggs are laid underwater, individually packaged in flattened capsules within a sausage-shaped gelatinous mass coiled in a spiral and holding over 2000 eggs.  Its eye anatomy is similar to that of the land snail <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helix_aspera" title="Helix aspera">Helix aspera</a></i>. In Oregon, over 10% of individuals harbor parasitic flukes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOL_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-EOL-2">[2]</a></sup><i>Leptasterias hexactis</i> feeds on this snail.  It is distributed from <a href="/wiki/Kodiak_Island" title="Kodiak Island">Kodiak Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, to Bahia de Tortuga, <a href="/wiki/Baja_California" title="Baja California">Baja California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EOL_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-EOL-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specimen_living_in_human_knee">Specimen living in human knee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Littorina_scutulata&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Specimen living in human knee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a minor beach accident seven year old Paul Franklin from Orange County, California fell onto a rock where eggs of the species were situated, injuring his knee. After the wound showed signs of infection, having become inflamed and swollen to the size of an orange, Paul's mother Rachel deflated the swelling, from which a single snail was found in the exudate.
</p><p>According to biologist Dan Riskin, Littorina scutulata can survive for weeks in a wide variety of water temperatures and salt levels and the subcutaneous tissue of the human body is a mirror of these environmental conditions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
