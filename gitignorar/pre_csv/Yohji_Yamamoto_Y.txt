<p><b>Yohji Yamamoto</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">å±±æ¬ èå¸</span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>Yamamoto YÅji</i>, born 1943<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Glass_Magazine_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Glass_Magazine-1">[1]</a></sup>)</span> is a Japanese fashion designer based in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. Considered a master tailor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-style_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-style-2">[2]</a></sup> alongside those such as <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Vionnet" title="Madeleine Vionnet">Madeleine Vionnet</a>, he is known for his avant-garde tailoring featuring Japanese design aesthetics.
</p><p>Yamamoto has won notable awards for his contributions to fashion, including the Chevalier of <a href="/wiki/Ordre_des_Arts_et_des_Lettres" title="Ordre des Arts et des Lettres">Ordre des Arts et des Lettres</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vogue_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-vogue-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Medals_of_Honor_(Japan)" title="Medals of Honor (Japan)">Medal of Honor with Purple Ribbon</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ordre_national_du_M%C3%A9rite" title="Ordre national du MÃ©rite">Ordre national du MÃ©rite</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Designer_for_Industry" title="Royal Designer for Industry">Royal Designer for Industry</a> and the Master of Design award by <a href="/wiki/Fashion_Group_International" title="Fashion Group International">Fashion Group International</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wa_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-wa-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yohji_Yamamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, Yamamoto graduated from <a href="/wiki/Keio_University" title="Keio University">Keio University</a> with a degree in law in 1966. He gave up a prospective legal career to assist his mother in her dressmaking business, from where he learned his tailoring skills.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> He further studied <a href="/wiki/Fashion_design" title="Fashion design">fashion design</a> at <a href="/wiki/Bunka_Fashion_College" title="Bunka Fashion College">Bunka Fashion College</a>, getting a degree in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wwd1_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-wwd1-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Designer">Designer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yohji_Yamamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Designer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yamamoto debuted in Paris in 1981. In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> in 1983, Yamamoto said of his designs, "I think that my men's clothes look as good on women as my women's clothing [â¦] When I started designing, I wanted to make men's clothes for women."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> More recently he has expounded: "When I started making clothes for my line Yâs in 1977, all I wanted was for women to wear menâs clothes. I jumped on the idea of designing coats for women. It meant something to me â the idea of a coat guarding and hiding a womanâs body. I wanted to protect the womanâs body from something â maybe from menâs eyes or a cold wind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>His commercially successful main line, Yohji Yamamoto (women/men) and Y's, are especially popular in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. These two lines are also available at his flagship stores in Paris and Antwerp, and at high-end department stores worldwide. Other principal lines include Pour Homme, Costume d'Homme, and the <a href="/wiki/Diffusion_line" title="Diffusion line">diffusion line</a> Coming Soon. Yohji Yamamoto Inc. reported in 2007 that the sales of Yamamoto's two main lines average above $100Â million annually.
</p><p>Yamamoto is known for an avant-garde spirit in his clothing, frequently creating designs far removed from current trends. His signature oversized silhouettes often feature drapery in varying textures. Yohji' collections are predominately made in black, a colour which Yamamoto has described as âmodest and arrogant at the same time. Black is lazy and easy - but mysterious. But above all black says this: "I donât bother you - donât bother me".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Yamamoto's work has also become familiar to consumers through his collaborations with other fashion brands, including <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> (Y-3), <a href="/wiki/Herm%C3%A8s" title="HermÃ¨s">HermÃ¨s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mikimoto" title="Mikimoto">Mikimoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandarina_Duck" title="Mandarina Duck">Mandarina Duck</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Era_Cap_Company" title="New Era Cap Company">New Era Cap Company</a>; and with artists of different genres, such as <a href="/wiki/Tina_Turner" title="Tina Turner">Tina Turner</a>, Sir <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Placebo_(band)" title="Placebo (band)">Placebo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takeshi_Kitano" title="Takeshi Kitano">Takeshi Kitano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Barenboim" title="Daniel Barenboim">Daniel Barenboim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pina_Bausch" title="Pina Bausch">Pina Bausch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heiner_M%C3%BCller" title="Heiner MÃ¼ller">Heiner MÃ¼ller</a>.
</p><p>Yohji Yamamoto was invited to curate the second issue of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_MAGAZINE_curated_by&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A MAGAZINE curated by (page does not exist)">A MAGAZINE curated by</a> in 2005, following <a href="/wiki/Martin_Margiela" title="Martin Margiela">Martin Margiela</a>.
</p><p>Poor decisions by finance managers pushed the brand into debts of more than 65Â million US dollars in 2009, which angered Yamamoto and led to a company restructuring from 2009 to 2010. The <a href="/wiki/Private_equity" title="Private equity">private equity</a> firm Integral Corp was identified as the Japanese company who will restructure the Yohji Yamamoto Inc and by November 2010 the company was out of debt and avoiding the risk of bankruptcy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Yohji has expressed a deep love for designing clothing, going so far as to say he 'cannot imagine [himself] retired'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fashion_advocacy">Fashion advocacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yohji_Yamamoto&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fashion advocacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2008, the Yohji Yamamoto Fund for Peace was established to foster development of China's fashion industry and to help heal the long-standing enmity <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sino-Japanese_relations" title="Sino-Japanese relations">between China and Japan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cpaffc1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-cpaffc1-10">[10]</a></sup> Each year, an emerging Chinese designer will be awarded with a two-year scholarship to a fashion college in Japan or Europe, and a male or female Chinese fashion model will be selected to make a runway debut during the Paris <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pr%C3%AAt-%C3%A0-porter" title="PrÃªt-Ã -porter">prÃªt-Ã -porter</a> season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Yamamoto has been quoted as saying: "they must have so many angry young people. Being a fashion designer or an artist, you have to be angry."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  Of the fashion show he staged in Beijing in spring 2008 to launch this initiative, Yamamoto said, "It's not political. I am going to open a store here, then Chinese people will come and shop there, and then they are happy. The real art is making people happy, but also asking questions about society."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
