	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Ronald Pellar</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><dl><dd><i>Not to be confused with singer <a href="/wiki/Ron_Dante" title="Ron Dante">Ron Dante</a>.</i></dd></dl>
<p><b>Ronald Pellar</b> is an <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypnotist" title="Hypnotist">hypnotist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraudster" title="Fraudster">fraudster</a> who performed under the <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage names</a> <b>Ronald Dante</b> and <b>Dr. Dante</b> and who was briefly married to actress <a href="/wiki/Lana_Turner" title="Lana Turner">Lana Turner</a>. He has been <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conviction_(law)" title="Conviction (law)">convicted</a> of or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guilty_plea" title="Guilty plea">pleaded guilty</a> to several criminal offenses, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_fraud" title="Mail fraud">mail fraud</a> in connection with his operation of the <a href="/wiki/Diploma_mill" title="Diploma mill">diploma mill</a> <a href="/wiki/Columbia_State_University" title="Columbia State University">Columbia State University</a> and <a href="/wiki/Attempted_murder" title="Attempted murder">attempted murder</a> for trying to contract for the murder of another hypnotist and entertainer. As of 2006, he had been listed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a> for 20 years for having been paid the highest-ever lecture fee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup> He has been known by as many as 40 <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">aliases</a>, including Phil Harris, Earl Clevenger, and Bonnie Ritchie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Married_Lana_Turner_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Married_Lana_Turner-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Pellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pellar has given different accounts of his age and personal history. In a 2006 interview, Pellar said he was born in 1920;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup> this birthdate is consistent with the age of 49 that was reported in 1969, when he married Lana Turner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EugeneMarriage_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-EugeneMarriage-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1985 he was reported to be 57 years old, indicating a birth date in 1927 or 1928.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In 2003 he was reported to be 73 years old<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBI_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FBI-5">[5]</a></sup> and in 2004 he was reported to be 74 years old,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Married_Lana_Turner_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Married_Lana_Turner-2">[2]</a></sup> indicating a birth date in 1929 or 1930.
</p><p>In a 2006 interview he said that he spent his early childhood in <a href="/wiki/Kuala_Lumpur" title="Kuala Lumpur">Kuala Lumpur</a>, where his parents and a sister were killed in an attack by <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysian</a> insurgents, leaving the 5-year-old Ronald Pellar and his 10-year-old brother, who were sent to live in an <a href="/wiki/Orphanage" title="Orphanage">orphanage</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup> Pellar says that he and his brother "walked away" from the orphanage when he was 11, becoming <a href="/wiki/Street_children" title="Street children">street children</a>. He claims that he ran a successful street business as a teenager, repackaging cheap watches to appear to be expensive brands and selling them to people who thought they were getting bargain prices because the products were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stolen_goods" title="Stolen goods">stolen goods</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup> According to Pellar, by the time he was 18 he had amassed enough money to move from the streets to a suite in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palmer_House_Hotel" title="Palmer House Hotel">Palmer House Hotel</a>, together with other street kids who worked for him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup> In her autobiography, Lana Turner said that he had told her that he had grown up in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and held a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">psychology</a> from a university there, but reporters who investigated this story had found it to be untrue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Pellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of the 1960s, Pellar was established as a hypnotist in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>, where he was photographed in the company of a number of celebrities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He was using the name Ronald Dante and working as a hypnotist in Los Angeles <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclubs</a> as of May 1969, when he became the seventh and last husband of actress Lana Turner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EugeneMarriage_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-EugeneMarriage-3">[3]</a></sup> The marriage ended later that year. In her autobiography, Turner said that Pellar stole about $100,000 worth of jewelry from her and defrauded her of about $35,000; she <a href="/wiki/Lawsuit" title="Lawsuit">sued</a> him and was awarded $25,000 in damages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Pellar had six other marriages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>A weekend course in <a href="/wiki/Hypnotherapy" title="Hypnotherapy">hypnotherapy</a> that Pellar conducted in Chicago on June 1–2, 1986, yielded him more than $3 million in lecture fees, giving him a place in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinness_Book_of_World_Records" title="Guinness Book of World Records">Guinness Book of World Records</a> for having been paid the highest-ever lecture fee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997 Pellar was convicted of ten <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">counts</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criminal_contempt" title="Criminal contempt">criminal contempt</a> for violating an earlier <a href="/wiki/Injunction" title="Injunction">injunction</a> against making <a href="/wiki/Misrepresentation" title="Misrepresentation">false representations</a>, issued in connection with a "<a href="/wiki/Permanent_makeup" title="Permanent makeup">permanent makeup</a>" business and a <a href="/wiki/Paralegal" title="Paralegal">paralegal</a> training academy, and in 1998 he was <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(law)" title="Sentence (law)">sentenced</a> to 67 months in <a href="/wiki/Prison" title="Prison">prison</a> for the contempt conviction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTC_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTC-9">[9]</a></sup> He fled before the last day of his trial,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTC_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-FTC-9">[9]</a></sup> but was later captured in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and sent to prison. In 1998, the Illinois Attorney General successfully shut down various diploma mills including Columbia State University which were attributed to Pellar. In 2004, while still serving his 1998 contempt sentence, he <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guilty_plea" title="Guilty plea">pled guilty</a> to nine <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">counts</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mail_fraud" title="Mail fraud">mail fraud</a> associated with the Columbia State University diploma mill operation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>  For that guilty plea he received a sentence of eight months in prison and was ordered to pay <a href="/wiki/Restitution" title="Restitution">restitution</a> of $45,835 and <a href="/wiki/Asset_forfeiture" title="Asset forfeiture">forfeit</a> a $1.5 million <a href="/wiki/Yacht" title="Yacht">yacht</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCN_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCN-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerald_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gerald-12">[12]</a></sup> For the charges he faced, he could have been sentenced to up to 45 years in federal prison and fined as much as $2.25 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBI_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-FBI-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ronald_Pellar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006, Pellar was living in a <a href="/wiki/Mobile_home" title="Mobile home">mobile home</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pauma_Valley,_California" title="Pauma Valley, California">Pauma Valley, California</a>, and said he had <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Pellar is the subject of a 2010 short <a href="/wiki/Documentary_film" title="Documentary film">documentary film</a> <i>Mr. Hypnotism</i> by <a href="/wiki/Bradley_Beesley" title="Bradley Beesley">Bradley Beesley</a> and <a href="/wiki/NPR" title="NPR">NPR</a> reporter Jennifer Sharpe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
