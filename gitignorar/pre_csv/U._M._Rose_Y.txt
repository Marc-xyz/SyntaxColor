	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">U. M. Rose</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Uriah Milton Rose</b> (March 5, 1834 – August 12, 1913) was an American lawyer. He "disliked his first name intensely and never used his first name when he could avoid it".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Approachable, affable, and kind,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tribute_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tribute-2">[2]</a></sup> graceful and courteous,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>18</span></sup> he was called "the most scholarly lawyer in America"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>676</span></sup> and "one of the leading legal lights of the nation",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> "a towering figure in the...life of Little Rock".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tribute_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tribute-2">[2]</a></sup> He was a founder of the <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a>, of which he was twice president, 1891–92 and 1901-02.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Another Arkansas judge, J. T. Coston, described him thus:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Arkansas is the home of the late U. M. Rose, a scholar and statesman. Judge Rose was one of the great lawyers not only of Arkansas but of the United States. Cultured, refined and modest as a woman, with a titanic intellect, he was a general favorite wherever he was known. Judge Dillon, after being thrown with him on numerous occasions at long intervals, pronounced Judge Rose the most cultured man he had ever known. He loved his profession, and I heard him state only a year or two before he died, while attending the Arkansas Bar Association, that during his more than half a century experience in the practice of law he had never had a serious misunderstanding with a brother lawyer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> called him "the brainiest man I have ever met".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conclusion_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Conclusion-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U._M._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rose was born in <a href="/wiki/Bradfordsville,_Kentucky" title="Bradfordsville, Kentucky">Bradfordsville</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, on a farm of 300 or 400 acres,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoir_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Memoir-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>8</span></sup> on March 5, 1834, son of Joseph and Nancy Rose. His father was a physician and a <a href="/wiki/Campbellite" title="Campbellite">Campbellite</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>12</span></sup> He was his parents' third son and had two half-siblings from his father's first marriage to a Miss Armstrong from <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup> As there was no school in Bradfordsville, he was taught by a tutor; he was studying <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> at age five and later remarked that he could not remember when he could not read.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> He studied French and German with Europeans who happened to be in Bradfordsville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>18–19</span></sup> His mother died in 1848 and his father in 1849. Since his father's estate was worth less than his debts, "the children were thrown out". Rose then worked in the village store, where he also resided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup> When this did not leave him time to study, he resigned and worked on a farm, as a field hand, for board and $5 a month.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>173</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conclusion_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Conclusion-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoir_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Memoir-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>10</span></sup>
</p><p>When Rose was 17, lawyer Rutherford Harrison Roundtree, who met him when visiting the farm, hired him as a deputy county clerk, and gave him "a home in his house" in <a href="/wiki/Lebanon,_Kentucky" title="Lebanon, Kentucky">Lebanon, Kentucky</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoir_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Memoir-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>10</span></sup> There, he "learned a great deal about legal forms" and attended court, hearing the locally-famous attorneys <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ben_Hardin" title="Ben Hardin">Ben Hardin</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joshua_F._Bell" title="Joshua F. Bell">Joshua F. Bell</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoir_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-Memoir-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>10</span></sup> To advance his legal education he enrolled at <a href="/wiki/Transylvania_University" title="Transylvania University">Transylvania University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lexington,_Kentucky" title="Lexington, Kentucky">Lexington, Kentucky</a>. "When Judge Rose, no more than a youth, presented himself for admission to the school, he impressed <a href="/wiki/George_Robertson_(congressman)" title="George Robertson (congressman)">Justice Robertson</a> so much with his earnestness and apparent willingness to learn, that Justice Robertson took him to live at his own splendid mansion. It was while at Justice Robertson's home that Judge Rose met <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Webster" title="Daniel Webster">Webster</a>, and many other of the prominent men of the time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conclusion_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-Conclusion-8">[8]</a></sup> He graduated in six months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>13</span></sup>
</p><p>On October 25, 1853, Rose married Margaret T. Gibbs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golden_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Golden-12">[12]</a></sup> daughter of William Gibbs, who was a grandson of George Washington's aide and bodyguard <a href="/wiki/Caleb_Gibbs" title="Caleb Gibbs">Caleb Gibbs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conclusion_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-Conclusion-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U._M._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Batesville">In Batesville</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U._M._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Batesville">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After graduating in 1853, in search of warmer weather, Rose, his new wife, and his brother-in-law William T. Gibbs moved to <a href="/wiki/Batesville,_Arkansas" title="Batesville, Arkansas">Batesville, Arkansas</a>, in 1853. He set up a law practice there in partnership with Gibbs. The Roses had three children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup> "The couple bought a house on the north side of Main Street, between Third and Fourth Streets, and U. M. Rose rented a small building next door to use as an office."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>175</span></sup> ("He lived on the lot now [1935] occupied by a filling station just across the street from where the Crouch furniture store is now l[oca]ted. His law office was located in his yard."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>) Rose subsequently moved his practice to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Arkansas" title="Washington, Arkansas">Washington, Arkansas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batesville_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Batesville-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1860, he was appointed chancellor (<a href="/wiki/Court_of_equity" title="Court of equity">chancery</a> judge and chief county officer) of <a href="/wiki/Pulaski_County,_Arkansas" title="Pulaski County, Arkansas">Pulaski County</a>; "county judges in Arkansas have served for decades as one of the strongest political forces in the state."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> "The [Pulaski County] chancellor's office was the only such office in the state and thus had statewide jurisdiction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup> He held this position until <a href="/wiki/Union_Army" title="Union Army">Union forces</a> captured the state capital on September 1, 1863. Although initially opposed to secession, on the practical ground that the South could not win a war against the North,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>175</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22</span></sup> he backed the Confederacy throughout the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>. (Both he and his brother-in-law each owned a slave, though his ran away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>175</span></sup>) He took an oath to support the Confederacy, without which he could not have continued as a state judge;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>176</span></sup> when briefly captured by Union forces that occupied Batesville, he refused to swear allegiance to the Federal government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>176</span></sup> The Confederacy named him state historian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>177</span></sup> He moved with the pro-Confederate Arkansas government to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_Arkansas" title="Washington, Arkansas">Washington, Arkansas</a> in 1863, following the fall of Little Rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>22</span></sup> Rose, "along with all men who had the welfare of the state at heart",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>23</span></sup> "worked hard" to convince Arkansas voters not to ratify the <a href="/wiki/Reconstruction_era#State_constitutional_conventions:_1867–69" title="Reconstruction era">Reconstruction Constitution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>181</span></sup> (As a Confederate loyalist, he had lost the right to vote.) He was chairman of the Resolutions Committee of the state Democratic party, determined "to preserve 'WHITE MAN'S government in a WHITE MAN'S COUNTRY'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>181</span></sup> (His wife was later active in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daughters_of_the_Confederacy" title="Daughters of the Confederacy">Daughters of the Confederacy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Little_Rock">In Little Rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U._M._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In Little Rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The end of the Confederacy meant the end of his position as judge. Moving to <a href="/wiki/Little_Rock,_Arkansas" title="Little Rock, Arkansas">Little Rock</a> in 1865, where he and his wife had four additional children,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-4"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup> he set up a law partnership, much later to become the <a href="/wiki/Rose_Law_Firm" title="Rose Law Firm">Rose Law Firm</a>, with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_C._Watkins" title="George C. Watkins">George C. Watkins</a> (1815–1872), former chief justice of Arkansas. This lasted until Watkins' retirement, five years later. He then brought in John Green, who died suddenly in May 1875.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>188</span></sup> His son George B. Rose then became associated with him, under the firm name of U. M. &amp; G. B. Rose.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> In 1893 Wilson Hemingway, associate justice of the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Supreme_Court" title="Arkansas Supreme Court">Arkansas Supreme Court</a> since 1889, resigned from the court to join them. In 1905, the firm merged with Cantrell and Loughborough, and the partners adopted the named Rose, Hemingway, Cantrell, and Loughborough, which remained the name of the firm long after Rose’s death. The firm did not become the Rose Law Firm until 1980;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>188</span></sup> no Rose has been associated with the firm since George's death in 1942.
</p><p>He was offered a position as U.S. Senator but declined, saying "I have no love of political life. I have seen much of it at a distance. I regard it for the most part as a sham and a delusion, and often it is a shame and a disgrace."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>187</span></sup>
</p><p>A man of learning in the law, science, and literature, Rose knew both <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>; poems translated by him from the French of <a href="/wiki/Sully_Prudhomme" title="Sully Prudhomme">Sully Prudhomme</a> and the German of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe" title="Goethe">Goethe</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schiller" title="Schiller">Schiller</a> were published posthumously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> When faced with a colonial document, he "exhibited a degree of almost perfect familiarity with Spanish".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> He was "deeply versed in the <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classics</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Conclusion_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-Conclusion-8">[8]</a></sup> and was also a noted and "enthralling"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> public speaker. In 1872 was the first of several trips to Europe, in which he visited Italy, Austria, Scandinavia, Russia, and Turkey; he also visited Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memoirg_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Memoirg-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>26</span></sup>
</p><p>His library, which his widow donated to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Arkansas_Library_System" title="Central Arkansas Library System">Little Rock Public Library</a>, contained over 8,000 volumes, including almost 2,000 books in French and German; there are two published catalogues of it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> He was the organizer of the <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_Bar_Association" title="Arkansas Bar Association">Arkansas Bar Association</a>, whose first meeting was in his office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>194</span></sup> and its president from 1899 to 1900; he was a charter member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a> and its president from 1891 to 1892 and again 1901 to 1902. <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a> appointed him U.S. ambassador to the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_of_1899_and_1907#Hague_Convention_of_1907" title="Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907">Second Peace Conference at The Hague</a> in 1907.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-5"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup> In preparation for that responsibility he spent time in Washington, D.C., where he was "a daily caller at [the] <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rose died at his home at 620 West 3rd St., Little Rock, Arkansas, on August 12, 1913, as a consequence of a fall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> "The funeral was attended perhaps by the largest number ever gathered on a similar occasion."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> People attended from "every section of the state".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Batesville_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Batesville-14">[14]</a></sup> State offices were closed by the governor so employees could attend the funeral.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p><p>He and his wife had 9 children who survived to adulthood, in order of birth: John M. Rose (1855-1915), an attorney who practiced next door to his father but not in partnership with him,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird2_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Bird2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>188 n. 60</span></sup> William G. Rose of <a href="/wiki/Butler,_Missouri" title="Butler, Missouri">Butler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independence,_Missouri" title="Independence, Missouri">Independence, Missouri</a> (1857–1941), his partner George B. Rose (1860–1942), Janata Rose "Fanny" Dickinson of Little Rock (1863–1932), Lawrence (1866, died in infancy), Ellen Rose Gibbon or Gibbons of Los Angeles (1867–1915), Emma Rose Coleman (1870–1907), Charles C. Rose of Little Rock (1872–1954), Prof. Lewis Henry Rose of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a> and of Chicago (1874–1921), and Mrs. (Jessie Rose) Hay Watson Smith of Brooklyn, N.Y., and Little Rock (1877-1953).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Golden_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-Golden-12">[12]</a></sup>
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> His son George was "known and honored not only as a most successful lawyer, but as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Litterateur" title="Litterateur">litterateur</a>, art critic and scholar."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> His grandson U.M. Rose was president of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Mexico_Bar_Association" title="New Mexico Bar Association">New Mexico Bar Association</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U._M._Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rose was the only delegate from Arkansas among the 75 lawyers who formed the <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saratoga_Springs,_New_York" title="Saratoga Springs, New York">Saratoga Springs, New York</a> in 1872. He was president from 1891 to 1892 and again from 1901 to 1902.
</p><p>In 1882, at his suggestion, 68 lawyers from across the state formed the Arkansas State Bar Association. "Rose was elected chairman of the association's first executive committee and, between 1898 and 1899, served as president."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bird_10-6"><a href="#cite_note-Bird-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1916, a new <a href="/wiki/U.M._Rose_School" title="U.M. Rose School">U.M. Rose School</a> was opened at 13th and State Streets in Little Rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> It became a central building of Little Rock Junior College when that institution was founded in 1927; when the Junior College became the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas_at_Little_Rock" title="University of Arkansas at Little Rock">University of Arkansas at Little Rock</a> and moved to a larger campus, the former campus was used by <a href="/wiki/Philander_Smith_College" title="Philander Smith College">Philander Smith College</a>, where the building is currently (2019) the James M. Cox Administration Building.
</p><p>In 1917, the state of <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> donated a <a href="/wiki/Uriah_M._Rose_(Ruckstull)" title="Uriah M. Rose (Ruckstull)">marble statue of Rose</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">U.S. Capitol</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Statuary_Hall_Collection" title="National Statuary Hall Collection">National Statuary Hall Collection</a>. In 2019 the decision was made to replace his statue, and that of <a href="/wiki/James_Paul_Clarke" title="James Paul Clarke">James Paul Clarke</a>, with statues of <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daisy_Lee_Gatson_Bates" title="Daisy Lee Gatson Bates">Daisy Lee Gatson Bates</a>. In the case of Rose, the reason stated is that "he sided with the Confederacy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cash_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cash-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1944, a United States <a href="/wiki/Liberty_ship" title="Liberty ship">Liberty ship</a> named the <i><a href="/wiki/SS_Uriah_M._Rose" title="SS Uriah M. Rose">SS Uriah M. Rose</a></i> was launched. She was scrapped in 1972.
</p>
