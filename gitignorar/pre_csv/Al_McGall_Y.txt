<p><b>Albert McGall</b> was a professional <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Track_and_field" title="Track and field">track and field</a> coach.
</p><p>McGall coached the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newark_Tornadoes" title="Newark Tornadoes">Newark Tornadoes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> in two games during the <a href="/wiki/1930_NFL_season" title="1930 NFL season">1930 NFL season</a>. The Tornadoes, under McGall, lost both games; in official statistics, McGall is credited with a third loss, but by the third game he had already been fired and replaced with <a href="/wiki/Jack_Fish_(American_football)" title="Jack Fish (American football)">Jack Fish</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxymuk_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-maxymuk-1">[1]</a></sup> 
</p><p>McGall had more success outside the NFL, coaching the football team of his <a href="/wiki/Alma_mater" title="Alma mater">alma mater</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stevens_Tech" title="Stevens Tech">Stevens Tech</a>, from 1915 to 1924 and posting two undefeated seasons. Between 1925 and 1928 he doubled as coach of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orange_Tornadoes" title="Orange Tornadoes">Orange Tornadoes</a> football team and as assistant track and field coach at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>; at Yale, he coached Olympic pole vault champion and <a href="/wiki/Men%27s_pole_vault_world_record_progression" title="Men's pole vault world record progression">world record</a> holder <a href="/wiki/Sabin_Carr" title="Sabin Carr">Sabin Carr</a>, as well as six-time United States champion <a href="/wiki/Fred_Sturdy" title="Fred Sturdy">Fred Sturdy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxymuk_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-maxymuk-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> When the Tornadoes joined the NFL in 1929, McGall was replaced as coach by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Depler" title="Jack Depler">Jack Depler</a> until his brief return in 1930. After that, he mostly coached track and field; he was track coach at <a href="/wiki/Muhlenberg_College" title="Muhlenberg College">Muhlenberg College</a> when he died of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">heart attack</a> on September 9, 1941.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-maxymuk_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-maxymuk-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
