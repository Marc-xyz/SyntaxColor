<p><b>Libanius</b> (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el">Λιβάνιος</span>, <i>Libanios</i>; c. 314 – 392 or 393) was a <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a> teacher of rhetoric of the <a href="/wiki/Sophist" title="Sophist">Sophist</a> school. During the rise of <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">hegemony</a> in the later <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, he remained unconverted and in religious matters was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Pagan" title="Hellenistic Pagan">pagan Hellene</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libanius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Libanius was born into a once-influential, deeply cultured family of <a href="/wiki/Antioch" title="Antioch">Antioch</a> that had recently come into diminished circumstances. At fourteen years old he began his study of <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>, for which he withdrew from public life and devoted himself to philosophy. Unfamiliar with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_(language)" title="Latin (language)">Latin literature</a>, he deplored its influence.
</p><p>He studied in <a href="/wiki/History_of_Athens#Roman_Athens" title="History of Athens">Athens</a> and began his career in <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> as a private tutor. He was temporarily exiled to <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">Nicomedia</a> but returned to Constantinople and taught there until 354.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GS370_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-GS370-1">[1]</a></sup> Before his exile, Libanius was a friend of the emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a>, with whom some correspondence survives, and in whose memory he wrote a series of orations; they were composed between 362 and 365. In 354 he accepted the chair of rhetoric in Antioch, his birthplace, where he stayed until his death. His pupils included both pagans and Christians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GS370_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-GS370-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Libanius used his arts of rhetoric to advance various private and political causes.  He attacked the increasing imperial pressures on the traditional city-oriented culture that had been supported and dominated by the local upper classes.  He is known to have protested against the <a href="/wiki/Persecution_of_pagans_in_the_late_Roman_Empire" title="Persecution of pagans in the late Roman Empire">persecution of pagans in the late Roman Empire</a>.  In 386, he appealed without success to emperor Theodosius to prevent the destruction of a temple in <a href="/wiki/Edessa" title="Edessa">Edessa</a>, and pleaded for toleration and the preservation of the temples against the predation of Christian monks, who he claimed: 
</p>
<dl><dd>"hasten to attack the temples with sticks and stones and bars of iron, and in some cases, disdaining these, with hands and feet. Then utter desolation follows, with the stripping of roofs, demolition of walls, the tearing down of statues and the overthrow of altars, and the priests must either keep quiet or die. After demolishing one, they scurry to another, and to a third, and trophy is piled on trophy, in contravention of the law. Such outrages occur even in the cities, but they are most common in the countryside. Many are the foes who perpetrate the separate attacks, but after their countless crimes this scattered rabble congregates and they are in disgrace unless they have committed the foulest outrage...Temples, Sire, are the soul of the countryside: they mark the beginning of its settlement, and have been passed down through many generations to the men of today. In them the farming communities rest their hopes for husbands, wives, children, for their oxen and the soil they sow and plant. An estate that has suffered so has lost the inspiration of the peasantry together with their hopes, for they believe that their labour will be in vain once they are robbed of the gods who direct their labours to their due end. And if the land no longer enjoys the same care, neither can the yield match what it was before, and, if this be the case, the peasant is the poorer, and the revenue jeopardized."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The surviving works of Libanius, which include over 1,600 letters, 64 speeches and 96 progymnasmata (rhetorical exercises), are valuable as a historical source for the changing world of the later 4th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GS370_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-GS370-1">[1]</a></sup> His oration "A Reply To <a href="/wiki/Aelius_Aristides" title="Aelius Aristides">Aristides</a> On Behalf Of The Dancers" is one of the most important records of Roman <a href="/wiki/Concert_dance" title="Concert dance">concert dance</a>, particularly that immensely popular form known as <a href="/wiki/Pantomime" title="Pantomime">pantomime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His first <i>Oration I</i> is an autobiographical narrative, first written in 374 and revised throughout his life, a scholar's account that ends as an old exile's private journal. Progymnasma 8 (see below for explanation of a "progymnasma") is an imaginary summation of the prosecution's case again a physician charged with poisoning some of his patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Libanius was not a Christian his students included such notable <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christians</a> as <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodore_of_Mopsuestia" title="Theodore of Mopsuestia">Theodore of Mopsuestia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Despite his friendship with the restorationist Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julian_the_Apostate" title="Julian the Apostate">Julian</a> he was made an honorary <i><a href="/wiki/Praetorian_prefect" title="Praetorian prefect">praetorian prefect</a></i> by the Christian Emperor <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">Theodosius I</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Libanius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>64 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oration" title="Oration">orations</a> in the three fields of oratory: judicial, deliberative and epideictic, both orations as if delivered in public and orations meant to be privately read (aloud) in the study. The two volumes of selections in the <a href="/wiki/Loeb_Classical_Library" title="Loeb Classical Library">Loeb Classical Library</a> devote one volume to Libanius' orations that bear on the emperor Julian, the other on Theodosius; the most famous is his "Lamentation" about the desecration of the temples (Περὶ τῶν Ἱερῶν);</li>
<li>51 <i>declamationes</i>, a traditional public-speaking format of Rhetoric in Antiquity, taking set topics with historical and <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">mythological</a> themes (translations into English by e.g. D.A. Russell, "Libanius: Imaginary Speeches"; M. Johansson, "Libanius' Declamations 9 and 10";</li>
<li>96 <i>progymnasmata</i> or compositional exercises for students of rhetoric, used in his courses of instruction and widely admired as models of good style;</li>
<li>57 <i>hypotheses</i> or introductions to <a href="/wiki/Demosthenes" title="Demosthenes">Demosthenes</a>' orations (written <i>ca</i> 352), in which he sets them in historical context for the novice reader, without polemics;</li>
<li>1545 letters have been preserved, more letters than those of Cicero. Some 400 additional letters in Latin were later accepted, purporting to be translations, but a dispassionate examination of the texts themselves shows them to be misattributed or forgeries, by the Italian <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">humanist</a> Francesco Zambeccari in the 15th century. Among his correspondents there was <a href="/wiki/Censorius_Datianus" title="Censorius Datianus">Censorius Datianus</a>.</li></ul>
