<p><i><b>Rafter Romance</b></i> is an American 1933 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Code" title="Pre-Code">pre-Code</a> <a href="/wiki/Romantic_comedy" title="Romantic comedy">romantic comedy</a> film directed by <a href="/wiki/William_A._Seiter" title="William A. Seiter">William A. Seiter</a> and released by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RKO_Radio_Pictures" title="RKO Radio Pictures">RKO Radio Pictures</a>. The film, which was based on the 1932 novel of the same name by John Wells, stars <a href="/wiki/Ginger_Rogers" title="Ginger Rogers">Ginger Rogers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Foster_(director)" title="Norman Foster (director)">Norman Foster</a> and George Sidney, and features <a href="/wiki/Robert_Benchley" title="Robert Benchley">Robert Benchley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laura_Hope_Crews" title="Laura Hope Crews">Laura Hope Crews</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinn_Williams_(actor)" title="Guinn Williams (actor)">Guinn Williams</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafter_Romance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mary Carroll (<a href="/wiki/Ginger_Rogers" title="Ginger Rogers">Ginger Rogers</a>) is a young woman from upstate who came to New York City to find a job and a career, but whose money has almost run out. Both she and Jack Bacon (<a href="/wiki/Norman_Foster_(director)" title="Norman Foster (director)">Norman Foster</a>), an aspiring artist who lives in the same <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Village" title="Greenwich Village">Greenwich Village</a> building, are behind on their rent and their landlord, Max Eckbaum (George Sidney), a good-natured soul who nevertheless has expenses to meet, comes up with a solution: Move Mary into Jack's loft and have them share the apartment on a shift basis. They would never see each other or know who the other is, since Jack is out all night and sleeps during the day, and Mary is taking a job selling refrigerators by telephone, which keeps her out all day.
</p><p>However, both manage to get a very bad impression of each other after realizing the other is of the opposite sex from articles of clothing lying about. A series of misunderstandings leads to a series of pranks aimed at each other: Jack places a bucket in the shower, and when Mary goes in it falls on her head. Then she places Jack's suit in the shower, so that it gets wet. In retaliation, he saws Mary's bed in half so that it would come apart when she sits on it.
</p><p>The situation gets complicated when the couple accidentally meet outside their apartment and, not knowing who the other is, begin to fall in love. Matters get worse when Mary's boss, lecherous H. Harrington Hubbell (<a href="/wiki/Robert_Benchley" title="Robert Benchley">Robert Benchley</a>), tries to invite her out for dinner, while Jack's would-be "patron", a lonely, libidinous, rich older woman, Elise Peabody Willington Smythe (<a href="/wiki/Laura_Hope_Crews" title="Laura Hope Crews">Laura Hope Crews</a>), tries to maintain her monopoly over Jack.
</p><p>When Jack accompanies Mary to a company picnic, they slip away from the group together and miss the bus back to town, forcing them to take a taxi. When they arrive at Jack's home, Mary realizes that Jack is her roommate. Trying to allay what he assumes are her suspicions about the arrangement, and unaware Mary is the person with whom he has been sharing the attic loft, Jack strongly denounces his co-tenant to her until the landlord comes and explains all.
</p><p>Elise and Hubbell also arrive at the apartment, where Elise tries to bribe Mary, while a protective cabdriver, Fritzie (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinn_Williams_(actor)" title="Guinn Williams (actor)">Guinn Williams</a>), punches Hubbell, mistaking him for Jack. Realizing his mistake, Fritzie then goes to his cab where Jack is pleading with Mary. Fritzie is about to punch Jack when Mary intervenes, and the cab drives off with Jack and Mary kissing in the backseat. Asked if they will get married, the landlord says, "I arranged it."
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rafter_Romance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Production on <i>Rafter Romance</i> began in mid-June 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> RKO initially announced that <a href="/wiki/Joel_McCrea" title="Joel McCrea">Joel McCrea</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorothy_Jordan_(film_actress)" title="Dorothy Jordan (film actress)">Dorothy Jordan</a> would star in the film, and then paired <a href="/wiki/Lew_Ayres" title="Lew Ayres">Lew Ayres</a> with Ginger Rogers, before casting Norman Foster as the male lead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmnotes_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcmnotes-2">[2]</a></sup> Foster and Rogers had been teamed before on <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Man_of_Manhattan" title="Young Man of Manhattan">Young Man of Manhattan</a></i> in 1930, and also made <i>Professional Sweetheart</i> together later in 1933.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmart_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-tcmart-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Four years after the release of <i>Rafter Romance</i>, producer <a href="/wiki/Merian_C._Cooper" title="Merian C. Cooper">Merian C. Cooper</a> re-made it as <i><a href="/wiki/Living_on_Love" title="Living on Love">Living on Love</a></i>, with <a href="/wiki/James_Dunn_(actor)" title="James Dunn (actor)">James Dunn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Bourne" title="Whitney Bourne">Whitney Bourne</a>, also for RKO.  The plots of other films, including the German film <i><a href="/wiki/I_by_Day,_You_by_Night" title="I by Day, You by Night">Ich bei Tag und du bei Nacht</a></i> (1932), the French film <i>A Moi le jour, à toi nuit</i> (1932) and the British <i><a href="/wiki/Early_to_Bed_(1933_film)" title="Early to Bed (1933 film)">Early to Bed</a></i> (1933) have similarities to <i>Rafter Romance</i>, but are actually based on a completely separate screenplay by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Liebmann" title="Robert Liebmann">Robert Liebmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Sz%C3%A9kely_(writer)" title="János Székely (writer)">János Székely</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmnotes_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcmnotes-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Merian C. Cooper accused RKO of not paying him all the money contractually due for the films he produced in the 1930s. A settlement was reached in 1946, giving Cooper complete ownership of six RKO titles: 
</p>
<ul><li><i>Rafter Romance</i></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Double_Harness" title="Double Harness">Double Harness</a></i> (1933) with <a href="/wiki/Ann_Harding" title="Ann Harding">Ann Harding</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Powell" title="William Powell">William Powell</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Right_to_Romance" title="The Right to Romance">The Right to Romance</a></i> (1933) with Ann Harding and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Young_(actor)" title="Robert Young (actor)">Robert Young</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/One_Man%27s_Journey" title="One Man's Journey">One Man's Journey</a></i> (1933) with <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Barrymore" title="Lionel Barrymore">Lionel Barrymore</a></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Living_on_Love" title="Living on Love">Living on Love</a></i> (1937)</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/A_Man_to_Remember" title="A Man to Remember">A Man to Remember</a></i> (1938)</li></ul>
<p>According to an interview with a retired RKO executive, Cooper withdrew the films, only allowing them to be shown on television in 1955-1956 in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.  In 2007 the films were shown at the <i><a href="/wiki/Film_Forum" title="Film Forum">Film Forum</a></i> in New York, and <a href="/wiki/Turner_Classic_Movies" title="Turner Classic Movies">Turner Classic Movies</a>, which had acquired the rights to the six films after extensive legal negotiations, broadcast them, their first full public exhibition in over 70 years. TCM, in association with the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">Brigham Young University</a> Motion Picture Archive, had searched many film archives throughout the world to find copies of the films in order to create new 35mm prints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmnotes_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-tcmnotes-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tcmart_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-tcmart-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
