<p><b>Sherman Square</b> is a diminutive public space bounded by <a href="/wiki/Broadway_(Manhattan)" title="Broadway (Manhattan)">Broadway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenth_Avenue_(Manhattan)" title="Tenth Avenue (Manhattan)">Amsterdam Avenue</a>, and West 70th Street on the <a href="/wiki/Upper_West_Side" title="Upper West Side">Upper West Side</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, just south of <a href="/wiki/Verdi_Square" title="Verdi Square">Verdi Square</a>. Acquired by the city in 1849, the small parcel of land was named in 1891 for <a href="/wiki/William_Tecumseh_Sherman" title="William Tecumseh Sherman">William Tecumseh Sherman</a>, who had once lived nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s and 1970s, Sherman Square and the nearby Verdi Square were known by local drug users and dealers as "Needle Park".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> This provided the title and general setting for the gritty 1971 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Panic_in_Needle_Park" title="The Panic in Needle Park">The Panic in Needle Park</a></i> (set in Sherman Square, according to its credits), directed by <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Schatzberg" title="Jerry Schatzberg">Jerry Schatzberg</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Al_Pacino" title="Al Pacino">Al Pacino</a> in his second role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
