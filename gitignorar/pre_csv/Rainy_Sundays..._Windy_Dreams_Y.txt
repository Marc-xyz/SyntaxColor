<p><i><b>Rainy Sundays... Windy Dreams</b></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWD_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWD-1">[1]</a></sup> is <a href="/wiki/Andy_Irvine_(musician)" title="Andy Irvine (musician)">Andy Irvine</a>'s first solo album, produced by <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B3nal_Lunny" title="Dónal Lunny">Dónal Lunny</a> and recorded at Dublin's <a href="/wiki/Windmill_Lane_Studios" title="Windmill Lane Studios">Windmill Lane Studios</a> in late 1979. It was released in January 1980 by <a href="/wiki/Tara_Music" title="Tara Music">Tara Records</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-O'Toole_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-O'Toole-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>274</span></sup>
</p><p>Andy Irvine's first solo album showcased songs and tunes from two of his main influences: side one (on the vinyl <a href="/wiki/LP_record" title="LP record">LP</a>) featured pieces inspired by <a href="/wiki/Irish_traditional_music" title="Irish traditional music">Irish traditional music</a>, and side two concentrated on <a href="/wiki/Balkan_music" title="Balkan music">Balkan music</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recording">Recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainy_Sundays..._Windy_Dreams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The album opens with a trilogy of songs ("The Emigrants"), comprising: "The Green Fields Of Amerikay" (which Irvine learnt from <a href="/wiki/Len_Graham_(singer)" title="Len Graham (singer)">Len Graham</a>), "Farewell To Old Ireland"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>56–58</span></sup> (Irvine's adaptation of "The Emigrant's Farewell", H743<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>200</span></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Sam_Henry_(musicologist)" title="Sam Henry (musicologist)">Sam Henry</a>'s collection) and "Edward Connors"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>60–62</span></sup> (which Irvine learnt from Eddie Butcher of <a href="/wiki/Magilligan" title="Magilligan">Magilligan</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWDSN_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-RSWDSN-5">[5]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Then comes "The Longford Weaver"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>62–64</span></sup> (H745<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>47</span></sup> in Sam Henry's, where it is also known as "Long Cookstown" or "Nancy Whiskey"); it segues into "Christmas Eve" (reel).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWDSN_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-RSWDSN-5">[5]</a></sup> 
</p><p>The Irish set concludes with "Farewell To Balleymoney"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>66–67</span></sup> (H615<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>343</span></sup> in Sam Henry's collection).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWDSN_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-RSWDSN-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Balkan set begins with "Romanian Song (Blood and Gold)",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>68–69</span></sup> based on a Romanian song collected by <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>, re-written by Irvine and Jane Cassidy and set to the music of a <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_dances" title="Bulgarian dances">Bulgarian dance</a> tune in the 'paidushka' rhythm of <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: 'Georgia Pro', Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', Times, 'Times New Roman', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>5</b></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>16</b></sub></span></span></span>; the song then segues into "<a href="/wiki/Paidushko_horo" title="Paidushko horo">Paidushko Horo</a>", an extensive collection of musical phrases borrowed from Bulgarian dance tunes in the same rhythm and performed at breakneck speed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWDSN_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-RSWDSN-5">[5]</a></sup> 
</p><p>"King Bore And The Sandman",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWDSN_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-RSWDSN-5">[5]</a></sup> in mixed rhythms of <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: 'Georgia Pro', Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', Times, 'Times New Roman', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>6</b></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>8</b></sub></span></span></span>, <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: 'Georgia Pro', Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', Times, 'Times New Roman', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>9</b></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>8</b></sub></span></span></span> and <span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode;"><span style="font-family: 'Georgia Pro', Georgia, 'DejaVu Serif', Times, 'Times New Roman', serif;"><span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:0.85em;font-size:80%;text-align:center"><sup style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>4</b></sup><br/><sub style="font-size:inherit;line-height:inherit;vertical-align:baseline"><b>4</b></sub></span></span></span>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>70–72</span></sup> is Irvine's energetic lament about his times in <a href="/wiki/Bucharest" title="Bucharest">Bucharest</a> and "dedicated to the man, in the public house, we are always trying to avoid".
</p><p>The original album (LP) closes with the self-penned "Rainy Sundays",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>72–76</span></sup> reminiscing about a young lady called Vida with whom Irvine pursued "a one-sided romance in Ljubljana years ago."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>72</span></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RSWDSN_5-5"><a href="#cite_note-RSWDSN-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The CD version of <i>Rainy Sundays... Windy Dreams</i>, released in 1989, includes a bonus track of "Bonny Woodhall"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Irvine_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-Irvine-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>24–25</span></sup> featuring Irvine with Mick Hanly during their live performance as a duo at 'The 4th Irish Folk Festival' in Germany on April 30, 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4thIrishFFSN_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-4thIrishFFSN-6">[6]</a></sup> "Bonny Woodhall" is Irvine's interpretation of "Bonny Woodha' " (H476 in Sam Henry's <i>Songs of the People</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Henry_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Henry-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>84</span></sup>), which he also set to new music. On this track, Irvine accompanied himself on <a href="/wiki/Fylde_Guitars" title="Fylde Guitars">Fylde</a> 'Octavius' bouzouki (with the bottom two courses strung in <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">octave</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
