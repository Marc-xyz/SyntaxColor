	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Enzyme Function Initiative</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>The <b>Enzyme Function Initiative</b> (<b>EFI</b>) is a large-scale collaborative project aiming to develop and disseminate a robust strategy to determine <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzyme</a> function through an integrated sequence–structure-based approach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>  The project was funded in May 2010 by the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_General_Medical_Sciences" title="National Institute of General Medical Sciences">National Institute of General Medical Sciences</a> as a Glue Grant which supports the research of complex biological problems that cannot be solved by a single research group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PAR-07-412_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-PAR-07-412-3">[3]</a></sup>  The EFI was largely spurred by the need to develop methods to identify the functions of the enormous number proteins discovered through <a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">genomic</a> sequencing projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The dramatic increase in genome sequencing technology has caused the number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peptide_sequence" title="Peptide sequence">protein sequences</a> deposited into public databases to grow apparently exponentially.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>  To cope with the influx of sequences, databases use computational predictions to auto-annotate individual protein's functions.  While these computational methods offer the advantages of being extremely high-throughput and generally provide accurate broad classifications, exclusive use has led to a significant level of misannotation of enzyme function in protein databases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>  Thus although the information now available represents an unprecedented opportunity to understand cellular metabolism across a wide variety of organisms, which includes the ability to identify molecules and/or reactions that may benefit human quality of life, the potential has not been fully actualized.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The biological community's ability to characterize newly discovered proteins has been outstripped by the rate of genome sequencing, and the task of assigning function is now considered the rate-limiting step in understanding biological systems in detail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Integrated_strategy_for_functional_assignment">Integrated strategy for functional assignment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Integrated strategy for functional assignment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EFI is developing an integrated sequence-structure based strategy for functional assignment by predicting the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_specificity" title="Substrate specificity">substrate specificities</a> of unknown members of mechanistically diverse <a href="/wiki/Protein_family" title="Protein family">enzyme superfamilies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIBIOCHEM_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-EFIBIOCHEM-9">[9]</a></sup>  The approach leverages conserved features within a given superfamily such as known chemistry, identity of <a href="/wiki/Active_site" title="Active site">active site</a> functional groups, and composition of specificity-determining residues, motifs, or structures to predict function but relies on multidisciplinary expertise to streamline, refine, and test the predictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  The integrated sequence-strategy under development will be generally applicable to deciphering the ligand specificities of any functionally unknown protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIBIOCHEM_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-EFIBIOCHEM-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By NIGMS program mandate, Glue Grant consortia must contain core resources and bridging projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PAR-07-412_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PAR-07-412-3">[3]</a></sup> The EFI consists of six scientific cores which provide bioinformatic, structural, computational, and data management expertise to facilitate functional predictions for enzymes of unknown function targeted by the EFI. At the beginning of the grant, these predictions were tested by five Bridging Projects representing the amidohydrolase, enolase, GST, HAD, and isoprenoid synthase enzyme superfamilies. Three Bridging Projects now remain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIBIOCHEM_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-EFIBIOCHEM-9">[9]</a></sup> In addition, the Anaerobic Enzymology Pilot Project was added in 2014 to explore the Radical SAM superfamily and Glycyl Radical Enzyme superfamily.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_cores">Scientific cores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scientific cores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bioinformatics core contributes <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bioinformatic" title="Bioinformatic">bioinformatic</a> analysis by collecting and curating complete sequence data sets, generating sequence similarity networks, and classification of superfamily members into subgroups and families for subsequent annotation transfer and evaluation as targets for functional characterization.
</p><p>The protein core develops cloning, expression, and <a href="/wiki/Protein_purification" title="Protein purification">protein purification</a> strategies for the enzymes targeted for study.
</p><p>The structure core fulfills the <a href="/wiki/Structural_biology" title="Structural biology">structural biology</a> component for EFI by providing high resolution structures of targeted enzymes.
</p><p>The computation core performs <i>in silico</i> <a href="/wiki/Docking_(molecular)" title="Docking (molecular)">docking</a> to generate rank-ordered lists of predicted substrates for targeted enzymes using both experimentally determined and/or homology modeled protein structures.
</p><p>The microbiology core examines <i>in vivo</i> functions using genetic techniques and <a href="/wiki/Metabolomics" title="Metabolomics">metabolomics</a> to complement <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i> functions determined by the Bridging Projects.
</p><p>The data and dissemination core maintains a public database for experimental data (EFI-DB).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFI-DB_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-EFI-DB-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bridging_projects">Bridging projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bridging projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Enolase_superfamily" title="Enolase superfamily">enolase superfamily</a> contains evolutionarily related enzymes with a (β/α)7β‑barrel (TIM‑barrel) fold which primarily catalyze metal-assisted epimerization/racemization or β-elimination of carboxylate substrates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Haloacid_dehydrogenase_superfamily" title="Haloacid dehydrogenase superfamily">Haloacid dehydrogenase superfamily</a> contains evolutionarily related enzymes with a Rossmanoid α/β fold with an inserted "cap" region which primarily catalyze metal-assisted nucleophilic catalysis, most frequently resulting in phosphoryl group transfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The isoprenoid synthase (I) superfamily contains evolutionarily related enzymes with a mostly all α-helical fold and primarily catalyze trans-prenyl transfer reactions to form elongated or cyclized <a href="/wiki/Isoprene" title="Isoprene">isoprene</a> products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Anaerobic Enzymology bridging project will explore radical-dependent enzymology, which allows the execution of unusual chemical transformations via an iron-sulfur cluster cleaving S-Adenosyl methionine (SAM) and producing a radical intermediate, or alternatively, abstraction of a hydrogen from glycine producing a glycyl radical. The superfamilies containing these enzymes are largely unexplored and thus, ripe with the potential for functional discoveries. The acquisition of an anaerobic protein production pipeline coupled with the installation of a Biosafety Level 2 anaerobic chamber for culturing human gut microbes has readied the EFI to pursue anaerobic enzymology.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Deliverables">Deliverables</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Deliverables">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EFI's primary deliverable is development and dissemination of an integrated sequence/structure strategy for functional assignment.  The EFI now offers access to two high-throughput docking tools, a web tool for comparing protein sequences within entire protein families, and a web tool for composing a genome context inventory based on a protein sequence similarity network. Additionally, as the strategy is developed, data and clones generated by the EFI are made freely available via several online resources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EFIBIOCHEM_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-EFIBIOCHEM-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Enzyme_Function_Initiative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EFI was established in May 2010 with $33.9 million in funding over a five-year period (grant number GM093342).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
