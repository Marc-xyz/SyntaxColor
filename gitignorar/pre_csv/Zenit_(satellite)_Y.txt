<p><b>Zenit</b> (Russian: <span lang="ru">Зени́т</span>, <small>IPA: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Russian" title="Help:IPA/Russian">[zʲɪˈnʲit]</a></span>, <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">Zenith</a>) was a series of military <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spy_satellite" title="Spy satellite">photoreconnaissance satellites</a> launched by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> between 1961 and 1994. To conceal their nature, all flights were given the public <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmos_(satellite)" title="Cosmos (satellite)">Kosmos</a> designation.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The basic design of the Zenit satellites was similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vostok_spacecraft" title="Vostok spacecraft">Vostok</a> manned spacecraft, sharing the return and service modules.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup> It consisted of a spherical re-entry capsule 2.3 metres (7.5 ft) in diameter with a mass of around 2,400 kilograms (5,300 lb). This capsule contained the camera system, its film, recovery beacons, parachutes and a destruct charge. In orbit, this was attached to a service module that contained batteries, electronic equipment, an orientation system and a liquid fuelled rocket engine that would slow the Zenit for re-entry, before the service module detached. The total length in orbit was around 5 metres (16 ft) and the total mass was between 4,600 and 4,740 kilograms (10,140 and 10,450 lb).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the American <a href="/wiki/Corona_(satellite)" title="Corona (satellite)">Corona</a> spacecraft, the return capsule carried both the film and the cameras and kept them in a temperature controlled pressurised environment. This simplified the design and engineering of the camera system but added considerably to the mass of the satellite. An advantage was that cameras could be reused.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Early Zenits were launched using the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vostok_rocket" title="Vostok rocket">Vostok rocket</a>; later versions used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voskhod_rocket" title="Voskhod rocket">Voskhod</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_(rocket_family)" title="Soyuz (rocket family)">Soyuz</a> rockets. The first flights were launched from <a href="/wiki/Baikonur_Cosmodrome" title="Baikonur Cosmodrome">Baikonur Cosmodrome</a> but subsequent launches also took place at <a href="/wiki/Plesetsk_Cosmodrome" title="Plesetsk Cosmodrome">Plesetsk Cosmodrome</a>.
</p><p>Most Zenits flew in a slightly elliptical <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">perigee</a> of around 200 kilometres (120 miles) and an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">apogee</a> between 250 and 350 kilometres (160 and 220 miles); the missions usually lasted between 8 and 15 days.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1956, the Soviet government issued a secret decree that authorised the development of  'Object D' which led to the program to launch <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_3" title="Sputnik 3">Sputnik 3</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a> was a simplified spin-off of the Object D program.) The text of the decree remains secret, but it apparently authorised another satellite program – ‘Object OD-1’ – which was to be used for photo-reconnaissance from space.
</p><p>By 1958, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S.P._Korolev_Rocket_and_Space_Corporation_Energia" title="S.P. Korolev Rocket and Space Corporation Energia">OKB-1</a> design bureau was simultaneously working on Object OD-1 and Object OD-2 – an early design for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vostok_spacecraft" title="Vostok spacecraft">Vostok</a> manned spacecraft. The development of Object OD-1 was experiencing serious difficulties so the head of OKB-1, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Korolev" title="Sergei Korolev">Sergei Korolev</a>, initiated work to see if a design based on Object OD-2 could be used for an unmanned photo-reconnaissance satellite. This may have been a political manoeuvre that would enable him to continue the manned space program and avoid diverting more of OKB-1's resources into Object OD-1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harford_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-harford-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-gorin-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-day_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-day-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite bitter opposition from the military, the Soviet government endorsed Korolev's approach and issued decrees on 22 and 25 May 1959 that ordered the development of three different spacecraft, all based on the same basic, Object OD-2, design. Spacecraft 1K would be a simplified prototype, 2K was to be a reconnaissance satellite and 3K was to be for manned flights. The name <i>Vostok</i> was also initially used for all three of these craft. But in 1961 the name became publicly known as the name of <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a>'s spacecraft so the 'Vostok 2' reconnaissance satellite was renamed 'Zenit 2'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astro_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-astro-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first Zenit launch attempt took place on 11 December 1961, but there was a fault in the rocket's third stage and the spacecraft was destroyed using its destruct charge. The second attempt — publicly referred to as <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_4" title="Kosmos 4">Kosmos 4</a> — was successfully launched on 26 April 1962 and re-entered three days later. However a failure in the orientation system meant few useful pictures were obtained; usable pictures had a resolution of about 5 to 7 meters. Launch #3 on 1 June failed dramatically when the one of the 8A92 booster's strap-ons shut down at liftoff and fell onto the pad, exploding on impact. The rest of the launch vehicle crashed 300 meters away. This accident caused considerable damage to LC-1, delaying the flights of <a href="/wiki/Vostok_3" title="Vostok 3">Vostok 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vostok_4" title="Vostok 4">Vostok 4</a> by two months.
</p><p>On 28 July, LC-1 was back in service to host the next Zenit flight, which performed successfully. Testing continued over the next year largely without incident, but on 10 July 1963 another launch accident occurred in practically identical fashion to the June 1962 failure. Once again, one of the strap-ons shut down at liftoff, separated from the booster, and exploded on impact with the pad while the rest of the launch vehicle crashed a few hundred feet away.
</p><p>Examination of telemetry from the booster indicated that some sort of electrical failure occurred, but subsequent investigation took three months and finally concluded that a switch designed to cut power to the strap-ons at staging had malfunctioned, possibly due to liftoff-induced vibration. The electrical circuit in the 8A92 was redesigned and it never again experienced problems with the strap-ons shutting down at launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cosmopark_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cosmopark-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many versions of the satellite were developed for different reconnaissance missions and flights continued until 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sven_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-sven-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_variants">Zenit variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Zenit variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_2">Zenit 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Zenit 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zenit 2 was the first version to be launched in 1961 (there was no Zenit 1.)
</p><p>The arrangement of cameras varied, but most flights carried four cameras of 1000 mm focal length, and one of 200 mm focal length. The single lower resolution camera was intended to provide low-resolution pictures that would help give a context to the high-resolution pictures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Each camera had 1500 frames of film and from 200 kilometres (120 miles), each frame held an image of a 60-by-60-kilometre (37-by-37-mile) square. The ground resolution was stated to be 10 to 15 metres (33 to 49 ft) although some unofficial sources claim it was much better—one source claims the number of cars in a car park could be counted. The cameras were developed at the <a href="/wiki/Krasnogorsky_Zavod" title="Krasnogorsky Zavod">Krasnogorsk Optical-Mechanical Factory</a> near Moscow. The Krasnogorsk factory, which had been producing a variety of optical equipment for the military since 1942, was also the manufacturer of the popular <a href="/wiki/Zenit_(camera)" title="Zenit (camera)">Zenit SLR cameras</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zenit 2s also carried <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ELINT" title="ELINT">ELINT</a> equipment called Kust-12M (bush) to receive <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> radar signals. The satellites carried a <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antenna</a>, around 1 metre (3.3 ft) in diameter, that is associated with this equipment. However, it is unclear if the antenna transmitted recorded signals to the ground or was for intercepting radar signals. In the latter case they would have been recorded on magnetic tape, to be retrieved after the return capsule landed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hendrickx_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-hendrickx-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>There were 81 Zenit 2 launches, 58 were successful and 11 were partially successful. There were 12 failed missions, 5 because of a satellite malfunction and 7 because of a failure in the launch vehicle.
</p><p>First flight – <a href="/wiki/Kosmos_4" title="Kosmos 4">Kosmos 4</a>, 1962. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_344&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 344 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 344</a>, 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-z2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-z2-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-2_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-2-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_2M">Zenit 2M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Zenit 2M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Improvements included a new camera system and the addition of solar panels. As the spacecraft mass was increased to 6300 kg, the Vostok rocket was replaced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voskhod_rocket" title="Voskhod rocket">Voskhod rocket</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz_rocket" title="Soyuz rocket">Soyuz rockets</a>. In common with Zenit 2 satellites this also had an ELINT payload.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hendrickx_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-hendrickx-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_208&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 208 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 208</a>, 1968. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1044&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1044 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1044</a>, 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-z2m_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-z2m-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-z2mn_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-z2mn-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-2m_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-2m-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_4">Zenit 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Zenit 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike Zenit 2, little information on Zenit 4 has been released. The Zenit 4 was intended for high resolution photography and carried one camera of 3000 mm focal length as well as a 200 mm camera. The focal length of the main camera was greater than the diameter of the capsule so the camera made use of a mirror to fold the light path. The ground resolution is not publicly known but it is believed to have been 1–2 m.
</p><p>The Zenit 4 had a mass of 6300 kg—around 1500 kg more than the Zenit 2. So, instead of the Vostok rocket, it was launched by the heavier Voskhod rocket. A probable total of 76 Zenit 4's were flown.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gorin2_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-gorin2-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_22&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 22 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 22</a>, 1963. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_355&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 355 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 355</a>, 1970.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-z4_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-z4-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-4_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-4-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_4_M">Zenit 4 M</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Zenit 4 M">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An improved version of the Zenit 4, the Zenit 4M carried a new camera, solar panels, and a restartable engine so the satellite's orbit could be altered during the course of its mission. Mission duration was 13 days.
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_251&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 251 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 251</a>, 1968. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_667&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 667 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 667</a>, 1974.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-4m_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-4m-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-4m_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-4m-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Zenit_4_MK_.2F_Zenit_4_MKM"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_4_MK_/_Zenit_4_MKM">Zenit 4 MK / Zenit 4 MKM</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Zenit 4 MK / Zenit 4 MKM">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These may have been versions of the Zenit 4 designed specifically to fly in lower orbits to improve image resolution. Some sources claim they were fitted with devices to compensate for aerodynamic drag and to withstand the effects of <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic_heating" title="Aerodynamic heating">aerodynamic heating</a>.
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_371&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 371 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 371</a>, 1970. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1214&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1214 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1214</a>, 1980.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-4mkt_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-4mkt-19">[19]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-4mk_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-4mk-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-4mkt_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-4mkt-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-4mkm_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-4mkm-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_4_MT">Zenit 4 MT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Zenit 4 MT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A special version of the Zenit 4M intended for <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographical</a> photography. It carried a SA-106 topographic camera, a laser altimeter and Doppler apparatus.
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_470&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 470 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 470</a>, 1971. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1398&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1398 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1398</a>, 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-4mt_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-4mt-23">[23]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-4mt_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-4mt-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_6U">Zenit 6U</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Zenit 6U">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A ‘universal’ version of the Zenit, intended for both low-altitude, high-resolution missions and higher-altitude, general observation missions. All flights used the Soyuz launch vehicle. There were 96 launches.
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_867&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 867 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 867</a>, 1976. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1685&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1685 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1685</a>, 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-6_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-6-25">[25]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-6_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-6-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zenit_8">Zenit 8</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Zenit_(satellite)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Zenit 8">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This was intended for military <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartographic</a> photography. It used a Soyuz launch vehicle and launches took place from both Baikonur and Plesetsk. It had a 15-day orbital life. Similar satellites were referred to using the ‘<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resurs" title="Resurs">Resurs</a>’ designation.
</p><p><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_2281&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 2281 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 2281</a>, was the last Zenit flight.
</p><p>First flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_1571&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 1571 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 1571</a>, 1984. Last flight – <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kosmos_2281&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kosmos 2281 (page does not exist)">Kosmos 2281</a>, 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gunter-8_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-gunter-8-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zarya-8_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-zarya-8-28">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the end of the programme, one Zenit-8 satellite was launched on the maiden flight of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soyuz-2_(rocket)" title="Soyuz-2 (rocket)">Soyuz-2</a> rocket, as a <a href="/wiki/DemoSat" title="DemoSat">DemoSat</a>. The satellite was placed on a sub-orbital trajectory, and intentionally impacted the Pacific Ocean shortly after launch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astro2_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-astro2-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
