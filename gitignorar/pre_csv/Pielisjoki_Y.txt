<p><b>Pielisjoki</b> (River Pielinen)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is a 67-kilometre (42 mi) long river in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Finland" title="Eastern Finland">Eastern Finland</a>. It is the sixth most fast-flowing river in Finland and the largest river draining into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Saimaa" title="Lake Saimaa">Lake Saimaa</a>. The river connects the two largest lakes in <a href="/wiki/North_Karelia" title="North Karelia">North Karelia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pielinen" title="Pielinen">Pielinen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pyh%C3%A4selk%C3%A4" title="Lake Pyhäselkä">Pyhäselkä</a> (part of Greater Saimaa). The city of <a href="/wiki/Joensuu" title="Joensuu">Joensuu</a> is situated at the mouth of the river.
</p><p>The river was born shortly after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">last glaciation</a> when the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Uimaharju&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Uimaharju (page does not exist)">Uimaharju</a> <a href="/wiki/Esker" title="Esker">esker</a> was broken under the pressure of Pielinen waters, which previously drained north but were redirected by tilting of the ground as a result of post-glacial <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">land uplift</a>.
</p><p>Ten <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canal_locks" title="Canal locks">canal locks</a> were built for navigation on the river in the late 19th century. In the 1950s and 1960s two power plants were built that block the river,  Kaltimo and Kuurna. At the same time associated new canals were constructed that replaced the previous navigation system.
</p>
