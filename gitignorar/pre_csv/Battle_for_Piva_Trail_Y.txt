<p>The <b>Battle for Piva Trail</b> was a battle between <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a> forces on <a href="/wiki/Bougainville_Island" title="Bougainville Island">Bougainville Island</a>. The battle took place on 8–9 November 1943 during the <a href="/wiki/Bougainville_campaign" title="Bougainville campaign">Bougainville campaign</a> in the days following the US <a href="/wiki/Landings_at_Cape_Torokina" title="Landings at Cape Torokina">landing at Cape Torokina</a> earlier in the month.
</p><p>The fighting took place inland from the US beachhead, as the Japanese began moving troops from the <a href="/wiki/23rd_Infantry_Regiment_(Imperial_Japanese_Army)" title="23rd Infantry Regiment (Imperial Japanese Army)">23rd Infantry Regiment</a> north from southern Bougainville. These troops subsequently clashed with a blocking force of US Marines that had been positioned along the <a href="/wiki/Piva_Trail" title="Piva Trail">Piva Trail</a> to protect one of the key avenues of approach towards Cape Torokina. It had been intended that the 23rd Infantry Regiment would co-ordinate their  assault with a counter landing at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Koromokina_Lagoon" title="Battle of Koromokina Lagoon">Koromokina Lagoon</a>, but ultimately this did not occur as the main assault was delayed until after the counter landing was defeated. The fighting for the Piva Trail resulted in heavy casualties for the Japanese and was followed by a series of actions throughout November and December 1943 as US forces sought to expand their perimeter around Cape Torokina.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_for_Piva_Trail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early November, US forces had <a href="/wiki/Landings_at_Cape_Torokina" title="Landings at Cape Torokina">landed around Cape Torokina</a> and established a beachhead, as part of Allied efforts to advance towards the main Japanese base around <a href="/wiki/Rabaul" title="Rabaul">Rabaul</a>, the isolation and reduction of which was a key objective of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Cartwheel" title="Operation Cartwheel">Operation Cartwheel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Facing the American beachhead at Cape Torokina were troops of the <a href="/wiki/Seventeenth_Army_(Japan)" title="Seventeenth Army (Japan)">Japanese 17th Army</a>, commanded by General <a href="/wiki/Harukichi_Hyakutake" title="Harukichi Hyakutake">Harukichi Hyakutake</a>, the main elements of which were drawn from Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Masatane_Kanda" title="Masatane Kanda">Masatane Kanda</a>'s <a href="/wiki/6th_Division_(Imperial_Japanese_Army)" title="6th Division (Imperial Japanese Army)">6th Division</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In response, the Japanese attempted a counter landing at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Koromokina_Lagoon" title="Battle of Koromokina Lagoon">Koromokina Lagoon</a>, and began moving reinforcements north from southern Bougainville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>To protect the narrow beachhead while further reinforcements and supplies arrived, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Marine_Raiders" title="Marine Raiders">2nd Marine Raider Battalion</a> advanced inland and set up a roadblock on the <a href="/wiki/Piva_Trail" title="Piva Trail">Piva Trail</a>, around the junction with the <a href="/wiki/Numa_Numa_Trail" title="Numa Numa Trail">Numa–Numa Trail</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shaw236_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Shaw236-5">[5]</a></sup> one of the key avenues of approach towards Cape Torokina. In an effort to reinforce the troops landing around Koromokina Lagoon, the <a href="/wiki/23rd_Infantry_Regiment_(Imperial_Japanese_Army)" title="23rd Infantry Regiment (Imperial Japanese Army)">Japanese 23rd Infantry Regiment</a>, advancing from southern Bougainville, was tasked with dislodging the US troops holding the Piva Trail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The Japanese intent had been to co-ordinate actions on both sides of the US perimeter, but ultimately the landing around Koromokina Lagoon was defeated before the 23rd Infantry Regiment could mount a full-scale attack against the Marine defenses along the Piva Trail.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle">Battle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_for_Piva_Trail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Battle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="798" data-file-width="562" decoding="async" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg/220px-Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg/330px-Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg/440px-Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bougainville_Operation_map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Japanese troop movements on Bougainville during the fighting on the island between November 1943 and March 1944</div></div></div>
<p>On the night of 5–6 November, the Marines drove off two preliminary attacks from the Japanese 23rd Infantry Regiment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Colonel <a href="/wiki/Edward_A._Craig" title="Edward A. Craig">Edward A. Craig</a>, commander of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/9th_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="9th Marine Regiment (United States)">9th Marines</a> to which 2nd Raider Battalion was attached,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> surmised that this was just a preparatory action and brought up more Raiders to reinforce the roadblock. When the Japanese attacked the following afternoon (7 November), the Marines were ready and drove the Japanese back to Piva village. Early on the morning of 8 November, Major General <a href="/wiki/Shun_Iwasa" title="Shun Iwasa">Shun Iwasa</a>, commander of the Japanese 6th Division's infantry group (the Iwasa Detachment),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> renewed the attack with two full battalions. The Americans brought up men of the 3rd Raider Battalion to protect the flanks of the Marines already engaged, as well as some light tanks. Frontal attacks by the Japanese were repulsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Major General <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allen_Turnage" title="Allen Turnage">Allen H. Turnage</a>, in command of the <a href="/wiki/3rd_Marine_Division" title="3rd Marine Division">3rd Marine Division</a>, determined that the Japanese lodged on the trail represented a threat to the airstrips and had to be removed, with the assault beginning on the morning of 9 November. The Japanese had several well-placed machine guns and were attempting an attack of their own; a bloody stalemate developed. Private First Class <a href="/wiki/Henry_Gurke" title="Henry Gurke">Henry Gurke</a> of the 3rd Marine Raider Battalion was awarded the second <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> of the Bougainville Campaign during this fight by throwing himself on a grenade and saving his foxhole-mate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Marine firepower eventually proved too much for the Japanese, who retreated to and through Piva Village in the early and mid-afternoon. The Marines took possession of the vital intersection of the Piva and Numa Numa Trails.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>On 10 November, an air strike consisting of 12 <a href="/wiki/Grumman_TBF_Avenger" title="Grumman TBF Avenger">Avenger torpedo bombers</a> attacked Japanese positions in front of the Marines along the Numa-Numa Trail. Around 10:00 hours, the Marines began to advance towards the village, supported by artillery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The movement was unopposed and large amounts of equipment and dead bodies were found around the abandoned Japanese positions. Two battalions of the 9th Marine Regiment subsequently followed them up and occupied Piva village just after 13:00 hours. As the beachhead around Cape Torokina was expanded, the 9th Marines managed to link up with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3rd_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="3rd Marine Regiment (United States)">3rd Marines</a> on their left. Meanwhile, the 2nd Raider Battalion was relieved and moved back into a reserve position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Battle_for_Piva_Trail&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Casualties during the fighting for Piva Trail were heavy. It is estimated that the Japanese may have lost up to 550 killed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> with 125 being killed on the first day and over 140 killed on the second day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gailey101_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Gailey101-1">[1]</a></sup> The Marines lost 20 killed and 57 wounded over the course of both days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gailey101_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Gailey101-1">[1]</a></sup> In the aftermath, US forces began expanding the perimeter around the beachhead, systematically advancing to several inland defense lines. A reconnaissance patrol subsequently advanced up the Numa–Numa Trail and identified several sites for airfields beyond the beachhead. The <a href="/wiki/21st_Marine_Regiment_(United_States)" title="21st Marine Regiment (United States)">21st Marines</a> were dispatched to advance up the Numa–Numa Trail to clear the area for construction to begin. They were subsequently ambushed, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Coconut_Grove" title="Battle of the Coconut Grove">Battle of the Coconut Grove</a> on 13–14 November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>  A further action would be fought around <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Piva_Forks" title="Battle of Piva Forks">Piva Forks</a> in late November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Meanwhile, further reinforcements and supplies were landed at Cape Torokina, and US Army forces began arriving to reinforce the Marines. In late December work on the airfields was complete and aerial bombing raids began against the Japanese base around Rabaul, while minelaying aircraft interdicted Japanese sea lanes of communication through the <a href="/wiki/Buka_Passage" title="Buka Passage">Buka Passage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Having initially held back forces to reinforce northern Bougainville, as the Japanese high command realized that the Cape Torokina lodgment was not a ruse, and would not be followed by a further assault on <a href="/wiki/Buka,_Papua_New_Guinea" title="Buka, Papua New Guinea">Buka</a>, they slowly began moving troops from southern Bougainville, landing in small numbers around Empress Augusta Bay throughout late December. Meanwhile, several air attacks were launched on the US perimeter, while artillery was moved up and began shelling the perimeter from <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hellzapoppin_Ridge_and_Hill_600A" title="Battle of Hellzapoppin Ridge and Hill 600A">Hellzapoppin Ridge and Hill 600A</a>. In late December, these features became the scene of further fighting as the 21st Marine Regiment was ordered to capture the Japanese positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> After this, there was a lull in the fighting on Bougainville as the Japanese decided to delay plans to launch a concerted counterattack, postponing the <a href="/wiki/Bougainville_counterattack" title="Bougainville counterattack">assault on Torokina</a> until March 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
