<p><i><b>Nannophrys naeyakai</b></i> is a species of <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frogs</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Dicroglossidae" title="Dicroglossidae">Dicroglossidae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frost_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Frost-3">[3]</a></sup> It is <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemic</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, where it is only known from two localities in <a href="/wiki/Ampara_District" title="Ampara District">Ampara</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monaragala_District" title="Monaragala District">Monaragala Districts</a> between 200 and 620 m above sea level. It was <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> as a new species in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>N. naeyakai</i> can be distinguished from the other <i><a href="/wiki/Nannophrys" title="Nannophrys">Nannophrys</a></i> species by the details of the <a href="/wiki/Tubercle" title="Tubercle">tubercles</a> on the fourth toe, sharp and narrow <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symphysial" title="Symphysial">symphysial</a> knob on the anterior edge of <a href="/wiki/Mandible" title="Mandible">mandible</a> and small <a href="/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Hands_and_arms" title="Anatomical terms of location">palmar</a> tubercles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>N. naeyakai</i> is only active for a short period December–February, as the streams it lives in are seasonal. Outside the rainy season it hides underground. The species is more active during the night than at day time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IUCN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-IUCN-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
