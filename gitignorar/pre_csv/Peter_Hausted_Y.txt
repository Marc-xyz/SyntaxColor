	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Peter Hausted</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

<b>Peter Hausted</b> (c. 1605 – 20 July 1644), <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Divinity" title="Doctor of Divinity">Doctor of Divinity</a>, was a "playwright, poet, preacher" in early 17th-century England. In his own time, he was notorious as a flamboyant preacher against <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puritan" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> and sectarian dissent in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, and was remembered for the riot that accompanied the <a href="/wiki/1632_in_literature" title="1632 in literature">1632</a> debut of his play <i>The Rival Friends</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings">Beginnings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Hausted&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Beginnings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hausted was born at <a href="/wiki/Oundle" title="Oundle">Oundle</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>. He earned an M.A. at <a href="/wiki/Queens%27_College,_Cambridge" title="Queens' College, Cambridge">Queens' College, Cambridge</a>, and pursued a career in the Church of England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> For a time he was the curate at <a href="/wiki/Uppingham" title="Uppingham">Uppingham</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Hausted participated in college theatricals as an actor; he was in the cast of the <a href="/wiki/1631_in_literature" title="1631 in literature">1631</a> Cambridge production of <i>Fucus Histriomastix</i>, probably written by Queens' College's Robert Ward.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Hausted&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The scandal over <i>The Rival Friends</i> involved a visit by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Charles_I_of_England" title="King Charles I of England">Charles I</a> and Queen <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Maria_of_France" title="Henrietta Maria of France">Henrietta Maria</a> to Cambridge and the University in March 1632. As part of the entertainment, the University scheduled a performance of Hausted's <i>The Rival Friends</i>, a seven-hour-long play filled with anti-Puritan and anti-sectarian satire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainey_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-chainey-4">[4]</a></sup>  In preparation for the event, the University authorities issued an edict, warning the student body not to indulge in "...any rude or immodest exclamations...nor any humming, hawking, whistling, hissing, or laughing...or any stamping or knocking, nor any such uncivil or unscholarlike or boyish behavior...." And above all, "no tobacco."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The performance that ensued (and curiously, <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">John Milton</a> might have been in the audience)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> was a theatrical disaster, a near riot in the faces of the King and Queen. Reacting to the disgrace, the University's vice-chancellor <a href="/wiki/Henry_Butts" title="Henry Butts">Henry Butts</a> committed suicide by hanging himself<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chainey_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-chainey-4">[4]</a></sup> on Easter Sunday, which was also April Fool's Day.
</p><p>When <i>The Rival Friends</i> was published later in the year, the title page stated that the play was "Cried down by boys, faction, envy, and confident ignorance, approv'd by the judicious, and now exposed to the public censure, by the author," which gives a taste of Hausted's style.
</p><p>Hausted's troubles were not confined to the single incident of the 1632 riot. In 1634 he was attacked by a mob at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_St_Mary%27s" title="Great St Mary's">University church</a> for preaching too wildly against the Puritans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_works">Other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Hausted&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to <i>The Rival Friends</i>, Hausted was the author of the Latin play <i>Senile Odium</i>, performed at the University in 1631 and printed in <a href="/wiki/1633_in_literature" title="1633 in literature">1633</a>. As a playwright, Hausted has been classed among the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Ben_(literary_group)" title="Sons of Ben (literary group)">Sons of Ben</a>, the followers of the comedic style of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Jonson" title="Ben Jonson">Ben Jonson</a>. Like Jonson, Hausted has been noted for his indulgence in "self-praise and invective."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was also a minor poet and translator; his translation of <a href="/wiki/Raphael_Thorius" title="Raphael Thorius">Raphael Thorius</a>'s <i>Hymnus Tabaci</i>, or "Hymn to Tobacco," was published posthumously in <a href="/wiki/1651_in_literature" title="1651 in literature">1651</a> ("Tobacco, King of Plants I well may call, / Others have single virtues, this hath all").
</p><p>Hausted was a colleague of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Randolph_(poet)" title="Thomas Randolph (poet)">Thomas Randolph</a>, another "Son of Ben" from <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a> — though the two were "rival friends" for a time, their conflict rooted in the controversy around Hausted's play. Randolph's <i>The Jealous Lovers</i> had been performed on the same day as <i>The Rival Friends</i>, and had been as great a success as Hausted's had been a failure. Eventually the two patched up their quarrel; Hausted wrote his colleague's epitaph after Randolph's death in 1635.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_mortem">Post mortem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Hausted&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post mortem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hausted died on 20 July 1644, during the siege of <a href="/wiki/Banbury_Castle" title="Banbury Castle">Banbury Castle</a> in the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. Early critics like <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Gosse" title="Edmund Gosse">Edmund Gosse</a> reported that he died in combat, "shot on the ramparts;" more recent research indicates that he probably died of plague.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>A Satire Against Separatists</i>, printed in <a href="/wiki/1675_in_literature" title="1675 in literature">1675</a>, has been variously attributed to Hausted and to <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Cowley" title="Abraham Cowley">Abraham Cowley</a>.
</p>
