<p>The <b>Democratic People's Republic of Korea calendar</b>, <b>DPRK calendar</b>, or <b><i>Juche</i> calendar</b> (<small>Korean pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Korean" title="Help:IPA/Korean">[tɕutɕʰe]</a></span>), named after the <i><a href="/wiki/Juche" title="Juche">Juche</a></i> ideology, is the system of year-numbering used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_People%27s_Republic_of_Korea" title="Democratic People's Republic of Korea">Democratic People's Republic of Korea</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korean_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The calendar borrows elements from two historical calendars used in <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, the traditional system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Korean_era_names" title="Korean era names">Korean era names</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian calendar</a> in which years are tied to the traditional birth of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>. In contrast to these two, the <i>Juche</i> calendar begins with the birth of the founder of the Democratic People's Republic, <a href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung" title="Kim Il-sung">Kim Il-sung</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Logie2012_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Logie2012-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The decree on the <i>Juche</i> calendar was adopted on 8 July 1997, on the third anniversary of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_of_Kim_Il-sung" title="Death of Kim Il-sung">death of Kim Il-sung</a>. The same decree also designated the birth anniversary of Kim Il-sung as the <a href="/wiki/Day_of_the_Sun" title="Day of the Sun">Day of the Sun</a>. The birth year of Kim Il-sung, 1912 in the Gregorian calendar, became "Juche 1" in the North Korean calendar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee2001_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lee2001-2">[2]</a></sup> Thus, the current year 2019 is "Juche 108", the next year  2020 will be "Juche 109", and so on.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dimitrov2013_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dimitrov2013-3">[3]</a></sup> This was done as a means of advancing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kim_Il-sung%27s_cult_of_personality" title="Kim Il-sung's cult of personality">Kim Il-sung's cult of personality</a>.
</p><p>The calendar began to be implemented on 9 September 1997, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Day_of_the_Foundation_of_the_Republic_(North_Korea)" title="Day of the Foundation of the Republic (North Korea)">Day of the Foundation of the Republic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lee2001_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lee2001-2">[2]</a></sup> On that date, newspapers, news agencies, radio stations, public transport, and birth certificates began to use <i>Juche</i> years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Korean_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The year 1912 is "Juche 1" in the North Korean calendar. There are no "before Juche 1" years, and years before 1912 are given numbers based on the Christian calendar only. Ranges of years that begin before 1912 and end after it are also given in Christian calendar numbers only.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KCNA1997_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-KCNA1997-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Any other years after 1912 will be given in either <i>Juche</i> years only, or in <i>Juche</i> years and the corresponding year in the Christian calendar in parentheses. In material pertaining to relations with foreign countries, "the Juche Era and the Christian era may be used on the principles of independence, equality and reciprocity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KCNA1997_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-KCNA1997-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
