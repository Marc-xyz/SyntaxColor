<p><b>Ribosome recycling factor</b> or <b>ribosome release factor</b> (<b>RRF</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> found in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterial" title="Bacterial">bacterial</a> <a href="/wiki/Cell_(biology)" title="Cell (biology)">cells</a> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eukaryotic" title="Eukaryotic">eukaryotic</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Organelles" title="Organelles">organelles</a>, specifically <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondria" title="Mitochondria">mitochondria</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chloroplasts" title="Chloroplasts">chloroplasts</a>. It functions to recycle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribosomes" title="Ribosomes">ribosomes</a> after completion of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a>. In humans, the mitochrondrial version is coded by the <b>MRRF</b> gene.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">Discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome_recycling_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ribosome recycling factor was discovered in the early 1970s by the work of Akira Kaji and Akikazu Hiroshima at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  Their work described the requirement for two protein factors to release ribosomes from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a>. These two factors were identified as RRF, an unknown <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a> until then, and Elongation Factor G (EF-G), a protein already identified and known to function in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protein_synthesis" title="Protein synthesis">protein synthesis</a>. RRF was originally called Ribosome <i>Releasing</i> Factor but is now called Ribosome <i>Recycling</i> Factor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome_recycling_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recent evidence suggests RRF may accomplish the recycling of ribosomes by splitting ribosomes into subunits, thereby releasing the bound <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MRNA" title="MRNA">mRNA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_RRF_function">Loss of RRF function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome_recycling_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Loss of RRF function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">Bacteria</a> (specifically <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>), loss of the <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> encoding RRF is deleterious.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This makes RRF a possible target for new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibacterial" title="Antibacterial">antibacterial</a> drugs.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">Yeast</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mitochondrial" title="Mitochondrial">mitochondrial</a> RRF (mtRRF) is encoded by a gene in the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>. Loss of function of this <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">gene</a> leads to mitochondrial <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> instability and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Respiratory" title="Respiratory">respiratory</a> incompetence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_binding_to_ribosomes">Structure and binding to ribosomes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ribosome_recycling_factor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Structure and binding to ribosomes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The crystal structure of RRF was first determined by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/X-ray_diffraction" title="X-ray diffraction">X-ray diffraction</a> in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The most striking revelation was that RRF is a near-perfect structural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mimic" title="Mimic">mimic</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNA</a>, in both size and dimensions. One view of RRF can be seen <a class="external text" href="http://www.pharm.kyoto-u.ac.jp/structbl/gallery/structure/rrf.jpg" rel="nofollow">here</a>.
</p><p>Despite the tRNA-mimicry, RRF binds to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribosomes" title="Ribosomes">ribosomes</a> quite differently from the way <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TRNA" title="TRNA">tRNA</a> does.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It has been suggested that ribosomes bind proteins (or <a href="/wiki/Protein_domain" title="Protein domain">protein domain</a>) of similar shape and size to tRNA, and this, rather than function, explains the observed structural mimicry.
</p>
