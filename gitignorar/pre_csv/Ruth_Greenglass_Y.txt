<p><b>Ruth Leah Greenglass</b> (née Printz; April 30, 1924 – April 7, 2008) was a spy for the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> along with her husband, <a href="/wiki/David_Greenglass" title="David Greenglass">David Greenglass</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Greenglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ruth Leah Printz was born to a Jewish family on April 30, 1924, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> to Max Printz and Tillie Leiter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-times-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-2">[2]</a></sup> She grew up in the same neighborhood, the <a href="/wiki/Lower_East_Side" title="Lower East Side">Lower East Side</a>, as her future husband, David Greenglass. She graduated with honors from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seward_Park_High_School" title="Seward Park High School">Seward Park High School</a> at 16. Although quite young, she and Greenglass wanted to marry before he was drafted to serve in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. They married in late November 1942 when he was 20 and she was 18. They shared an interest in politics and joined the <a href="/wiki/Young_Communist_League" title="Young Communist League">Young Communist League</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Espionage">Espionage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Greenglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Espionage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julius_Rosenberg" title="Julius Rosenberg">Julius Rosenberg</a> became a Soviet agent, working under Alexander Feklissov.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In September 1944, Rosenberg suggested to Feklissov that he should consider recruiting his brother-in-law, David, and his wife. Feklissov met the couple and on 21 September, he reported to Moscow: "They are young, intelligent, capable, and politically developed people, strongly believing in the cause of communism and wishing to do their best to help our country as much as possible. They are undoubtedly devoted to us (the Soviet Union)." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>David wrote to his wife, "My darling, I most certainly will be glad to be part of the community project (espionage) that Julius and his friends (the Russians) have in mind." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>After her husband was drafted and inducted into the Army in 1943, Ruth continued to visit him. In November 1944, she visited him in <a href="/wiki/Albuquerque,_New_Mexico" title="Albuquerque, New Mexico">Albuquerque, New Mexico</a>, where he was working as a machinist on the <a href="/wiki/Manhattan_Project" title="Manhattan Project">Manhattan Project</a> at <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory#The_Manhattan_Project" title="Los Alamos National Laboratory">Los Alamos</a>. During that visit, she asked him to forward any information on the project to his brother-in-law, Julius.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rosenberg_trial">Rosenberg trial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Greenglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rosenberg trial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">FBI</a> questioned him about suspected espionage activities, David agreed to confess to his own activities and to testify against <a href="/wiki/Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg" title="Julius and Ethel Rosenberg">Julius and Ethel Rosenberg</a> in exchange for immunity for his wife so that she could remain at home with their two children.
</p><p>At the trial, Ruth implicated Ethel in the espionage ring by testifying that Ethel had typed up the notes that David had provided. Ruth testified that both Rosenbergs had urged her to persuade her husband to become involved in espionage. Ruth's testimony was crucial in securing Ethel's conviction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Greenglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She rejoined her husband after his release from prison in 1960, and they lived in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> under assumed names with their children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-obit-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>She died on April 7, 2008, at the age of 83, a fact that became widely known only when the government, numbering her among the deceased witnesses, released her grand jury testimony a few weeks later. David Greenglass died in 2014, aged 92.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-times-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-obit-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ruth_Greenglass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reliability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The truth of Ruth Printz Greenglass's testimony at the Rosenberg trial has been questioned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> 
</p><p>In September 2008, her grand jury transcripts were released and showed that when testifying before the grand jury in August 1950, she was asked, "Didn't you write [the information] down on a piece of paper?" She replied, "Yes, I wrote [the information] down [on] a piece of paper and [Julius Rosenberg] took it with him."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>At the trial she testified that Ethel had typed up the notes about the atomic bomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
