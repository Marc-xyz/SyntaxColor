	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Botlikh people</h1>

<p>The <b>Botlikh</b> people (also known as <b>Bótligh</b>, <b>Botlig</b>, <b>Botlog</b> or <b>Buikhatli</b>) are an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avar%E2%80%93Andi%E2%80%93Dido_peoples" title="Avar–Andi–Dido peoples">Andi–Dido people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Dagestan" title="Dagestan">Dagestan</a>. Until the 1930s they were considered a distinct people. Since that time they have been classified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caucasian_Avars" title="Caucasian Avars">Caucasian Avars</a> and have faced a campaign to have them assimilate into that population.  
</p><p>The Botlikh are primarily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunni_Muslims" title="Sunni Muslims">Sunni Muslims</a>. They numbered 3,354 people in 1926. They speak the <a href="/wiki/Botlikh_language" title="Botlikh language">Botlikh language</a>, which belongs to the <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">Northeast Caucasian language family</a>. According to the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Census_(2002)" title="Russian Census (2002)">Russian Census (2002)</a> only 16 people in Russia declared themselves as Botlikhs (none of them in Dagestan), and 90 people declared speaking the Botlikh language. The number of speakers is higher, about 5,500, according to a survey by Koryakov in 2006.
</p><p>The village of Botlikh is just north of the <a href="/wiki/Andi_Koysu" title="Andi Koysu">Andi Koysu</a> River. During the <a href="/wiki/Murid_War" title="Murid War">Murid War</a> Russian forces gathered here for their final push against Shamil. During the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dagestan_Uprising_(1920)" title="Dagestan Uprising (1920)">Dagestan Uprising (1920)</a> the Reds were defeated here several times.
</p>
