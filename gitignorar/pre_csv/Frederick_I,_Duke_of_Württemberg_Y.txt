<p><b>Friedrich I of Württemberg</b> (19 August 1557, in <a href="/wiki/Montb%C3%A9liard" title="Montbéliard">Montbéliard</a> – 29 January 1608, in <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>) was the son of <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg-M%C3%B6mpelgard" title="George I of Württemberg-Mömpelgard">George</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montb%C3%A9liard" title="Montbéliard">Mömpelgard</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Barbara_of_Hesse" title="Barbara of Hesse">Barbara of Hesse</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Philip_I,_Landgrave_of_Hesse" title="Philip I, Landgrave of Hesse">Philip I, Landgrave of Hesse</a>.
</p><p>Several references are made to him in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Merry_Wives_of_Windsor" title="The Merry Wives of Windsor">The Merry Wives of Windsor</a></i>, in which a series of anti-German jokes start with a horse theft, several references are made to German travellers in England and to a German duke who is <i>not</i> expected to come to Windsor.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I,_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg,_KG.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1158" data-file-width="1158" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I%2C_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg%2C_KG.png/220px-Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I%2C_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg%2C_KG.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I%2C_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg%2C_KG.png/330px-Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I%2C_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg%2C_KG.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I%2C_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg%2C_KG.png/440px-Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I%2C_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg%2C_KG.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Frederick_I,_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg,_KG.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arms of Frederick I, Duke of Württemberg, KG</div></div></div>
<p>Frederick of Mömpelgard was <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> to the dukedom of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Duchy of Württemberg">Württemberg</a> when he visited <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Berkshire" title="Windsor, Berkshire">Windsor</a> and other English cities in 1592. He developed a desire to be made a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knight_of_the_Garter" title="Knight of the Garter">Knight of the Garter</a> and solicited <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth</a> for the honor repeatedly. After he had inherited the dukedom and become more prominent in affairs, she admitted him to the order. In a calculated slight, he was not informed of his admission in time to attend the investiture in spring 1597, the ceremony for which <i>The Merry Wives of Windsor</i> was written. Thus references to Mömpelgard's earlier visit and his <i>not</i> being in Windsor were jokes intended for the play's first audience, and appear in the <a href="/wiki/First_Folio" title="First Folio">First Folio</a> edition of the play, taken from the first private performance, but not in the 1602 <a href="/wiki/Quarto" title="Quarto">Quarto</a> derived from public theatrical production.
</p><p>In May 1604 <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">King James I</a> sent <a href="/wiki/William_Dethick" title="William Dethick">William Dethick</a> to invest Frederick in the order, and he presented jewels including two garters with diamonds and rubies, and two St Georges with diamonds and rubies, and a gold chain or collar, made by the London goldsmiths, <a href="/wiki/John_Spilman" title="John Spilman">John Spilman</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Herrick_(MP)" title="William Herrick (MP)">William Herrick</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1599, Frederick I issued an order that a new town should be established at the northern extremity of the <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> by the name of <a href="/wiki/Freudenstadt" title="Freudenstadt">Freudenstadt</a>. The aim was for the town to become the new residence of the Duchy of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">Württemberg</a> as it was closer to Mömpelgard than the Württemberg capital <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>. However, Frederick I died in 1608 and his plans never came to fruition.
</p><p>The sons of Frederick I established the ducal house of <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg-Neuenstadt" title="Württemberg-Neuenstadt">Württemberg-Neuenstadt</a>, a branch line of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="House of Württemberg">House of Württemberg</a> after a <i>Fürstbrüderlicher Vergleich</i> — a mutual agreement made between ducal brothers on 7 June 1617. The eldest son, <a href="/wiki/John_Frederick,_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="John Frederick, Duke of Württemberg">Johann Friedrich</a>, assumed barony over the Duchy of Württemberg while the second youngest son, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Achilles,_Duke_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg-Neuenstadt" title="Frederick Achilles, Duke of Württemberg-Neuenstadt">Frederick Achilles</a>, was bequeathed Neuenstadt Castle and an annual endowment of 10,000 <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">guilder</a>.
</p>
