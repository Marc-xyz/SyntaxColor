<p><b>Eugene Talmadge</b> (September 23, 1884 – December 21, 1946) was an American <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> who served two terms as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Georgia" title="List of Governors of Georgia">67th Governor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a> from 1933 to 1937, and a third term from 1941 to 1943. Elected to a fourth term in November 1946, he died before his inauguration, scheduled for January 1947. Only Talmadge and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_E._Brown" title="Joseph E. Brown">Joe Brown</a>, in the mid-19th century, have been elected four times as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Georgia" title="Governor of Georgia">Governor of Georgia</a>. He is well known for actively promoting segregation and <a href="/wiki/White_supremacy" title="White supremacy">white supremacy</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and for advocating for racism in the Georgia university system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Early_life.2C_education_and_career"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life,_education_and_career">Early life, education and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Talmadge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, education and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eugene Talmadge was born in 1883 in <a href="/wiki/Forsyth,_Georgia" title="Forsyth, Georgia">Forsyth, Georgia</a>, to Thomas and Carrie (Roberts) Talmadge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> He attended the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia" title="University of Georgia">University of Georgia</a> and graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Georgia_School_of_Law" title="University of Georgia School of Law">university's law school</a>. While at UGA, he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Phi_Kappa_Literary_Society" title="Phi Kappa Literary Society">Phi Kappa Literary Society</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigma_Nu" title="Sigma Nu">Sigma Nu</a> fraternity.
</p><p>Talmadge set up a law practice in <a href="/wiki/Telfair_County,_Georgia" title="Telfair County, Georgia">Telfair County, Georgia</a>. He twice ran for the <a href="/wiki/Georgia_General_Assembly" title="Georgia General Assembly">Georgia state legislature</a> and lost both times. He was elected as state agriculture commissioner in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Talmadge was re-elected commissioner in 1928<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and 1930.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>As commissioner, Talmadge used the newspaper of his department to give advice to farmers and talk about his political views, extolling the virtues of a laissez-faire  economic policy  and individual action to improve the well-being of farmers. During his time as agriculture commissioner, Talmadge also developed a reputation for being a corrupt, freewheeling individual who disregarded standard ethics and played by his own set of rules.  He maintained widespread support among Georgia’s rural community. He was also an “admitted <a href="/wiki/Flagellation" title="Flagellation">flogger</a> and racial <a href="/wiki/Demagogue" title="Demagogue">demagogue</a> who presided over a <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Klan</a>-ridden regime”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_Senate" title="Georgia Senate">State Senate</a> concluded that Talmadge violated a state law requiring that fertilizer fees collected by the department be deposited in the state treasury.  He was criticized for paying himself and family members more than $40,000 in salaries and expenses, and using department funds to make trips to the <a href="/wiki/Kentucky_Derby" title="Kentucky Derby">Kentucky Derby</a>.  Accused of “stealing” $20,000 in order to raise the price of hogs, Talmadge told one group of farmers, “Sure, I stole it!  But I stole it for you.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgia_State_House_of_Representatives" title="Georgia State House of Representatives">State House</a> declined requests to impeach Talmadge but agreed to sue him to recover state funds spent on the hog price manipulation scheme.  When Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_B._Russell_Jr." title="Richard B. Russell Jr.">Richard B. Russell Jr.</a> referred the suit to the state attorney general, however, the request to sue Talmadge was rejected.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor">Governor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Talmadge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Governor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1932, Governor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_B._Russell,_Jr." title="Richard B. Russell, Jr.">Richard B. Russell, Jr.</a> sought a seat in the United States Senate. Talmadge ran for governor, appealing to rural Georgia by idealizing the small farmer, and preaching what he said were the true values of rural America, such as rugged individualism, frugality, governmental economy, segregation, limited government, and low taxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>  Talmage won a majority of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_Unit_System" title="County Unit System">county unit votes</a> in the primary, and winning the nomination of the Democratic Party was tantamount to automatic victory in the general election.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>  The County Unit System gave power to the rural counties, which were Talmadge's base. He boasted, “I can carry any county that ain't got street cars.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>  He made twelve campaign promises, the most controversial of which was to lower the price of an automobile license to 3 dollars, putting them within reach of the poorest farmers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The state legislature intensely debated the $3 license fee issue, but did not pass it. After it adjourned, Talmadge fixed the $3 fee by proclamation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Talmadge was re-elected in 1934, carrying every county but three in the state's Democratic primary, although he was often tied to both controversy and corruption. When the Public Service Commission, a body elected by the voters, refused to lower utility rates, he appointed a new board to get it done.  When the Highway Board resisted his efforts to control it, Talmadge declared <a href="/wiki/Martial_law" title="Martial law">martial law</a> and appointed more cooperative members to the board. When the state treasurer and comptroller general refused to cooperate, the governor ordered state police to physically remove them from their offices in the state Capitol. Critics denounced him as a <a href="/wiki/Dictator" title="Dictator">dictator</a>, a demagogue, and a threat to the tranquility of the state. His supporters considered him to be a friend of the “common man” and one of the state's most outstanding governors.
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Textile_workers_strike_(1934)" title="Textile workers strike (1934)">textile workers went on strike</a> on September 1, 1934, Talmadge declared martial law during the third week of the strike, and directed four thousand <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Guard_of_the_United_States" title="National Guard of the United States">National Guard</a> troops to arrest all picketers throughout the state. He ordered the prisoners to be held behind the barbed wire of a former <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> camp for trial by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_tribunal" title="Military tribunal">military tribunal</a>. While the state interned about one hundred or so picketers, the show of force effectively ended picketing throughout most of the state. When Talmadge discovered that one of the employers had hired the notorious strikebreaker <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Bergoff" title="Pearl Bergoff">Pearl Bergoff</a>, he had Bergoff and his two hundred men deported to New York City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Democrat Talmadge governed as a Southern conservative, vehemently attacking the nationalization of President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a>. He objected to policies favorable to black people (President Roosevelt did not introduce any Civil Rights measures),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> the farm programs, and relief-work programs such as the <a href="/wiki/Works_Progress_Administration" title="Works Progress Administration">Works Progress Administration</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civilian_Conservation_Corps" title="Civilian Conservation Corps">Civilian Conservation Corps</a>. Talmadge tried to build a region-wide coalition, making a national speaking tour in preparation for a challenge to FDR in 1936. His ‘Southern Committee to Uphold the Constitution’ organized a convention in <a href="/wiki/Macon,_Georgia" title="Macon, Georgia">Macon, Georgia</a>, in January 1936 that brought together fragments of the old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huey_P._Long" title="Huey P. Long">Huey P. Long</a> coalition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Talmadge pledged to defend the “sovereignty of our states and local self-government” at the upcoming Democratic National Convention. However, Roosevelt, who visited Georgia often because of his past polio, was more popular with the poor farmers. Unable to run for re-election in 1936, Talmadge chose to challenge Senator Russell in the <a href="/wiki/Primary_election" title="Primary election">primary</a>, but Russell defeated Talmadge by a landslide<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telfeyan-thesis_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-telfeyan-thesis-19">[19]</a></sup> and Talmadge's presidential hopes collapsed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (February 2018)">page needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Talmadge's handpicked candidate for governor, Charles Redwine, lost the 1936 Georgia gubernatorial election to pro-New Deal Democrat <a href="/wiki/Eurith_D._Rivers" title="Eurith D. Rivers">Eurith D. Rivers</a> by an overwhelming margin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telfeyan-thesis_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-telfeyan-thesis-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1936, according to a United Press (UP) article printed in the Atlanta Constitution on August 21, 1936 titled "Gene Selects Hitler as Favorite 'Author'", Talmadge reportedly told a Los Angeles newspaper that while he didn't have time to read many books, he read Adolf Hitler's "My Struggle", or Mein Kampf, seven times. In response, the Nazi publication Die Bewegung reprinted the interview with Talmadge and praised him, stating that "Governor Eugene Talmadge, of Georgia, is obviously a very intelligent man."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1938 Talmadge challenged Senator <a href="/wiki/Walter_F._George" title="Walter F. George">Walter George</a>. Though George had sided with 34 of Roosevelt's 44 New Deal proposals,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> he refused to support some of the proposals in Roosevelt's second term. The president believed George had now been “put out to pasture.” Roosevelt tried to purge George and campaigned for his own candidate, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lawrence_Camp&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lawrence Camp (page does not exist)">Lawrence Camp</a>. George, however, refused to criticize Roosevelt during the campaign and blamed the purge on Roosevelt's advisers.  Despite the divide among the New Deal vote, George easily won the renomination, securing 141,922 popular votes and a majority of 246 unit votes, while Talmadge won just 102,464 popular votes and 148 unit votes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telfeyan-thesis_19-2"><a href="#cite_note-telfeyan-thesis-19">[19]</a></sup> Talmadge's victory over Roosevelt's candidate Camp, who secured just 78,223 popular votes and 16 unit votes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telfeyan-thesis_19-3"><a href="#cite_note-telfeyan-thesis-19">[19]</a></sup> surprised his critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-telfeyan-thesis_19-4"><a href="#cite_note-telfeyan-thesis-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Georgia">University of Georgia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Talmadge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: University of Georgia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cocking_affair" title="Cocking affair">Cocking affair</a></div>
<p>Talmadge returned to the governor's office in 1940, emerging as the leader of racist and segregationist elements in Georgia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>  Responding to reports that <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cocking" title="Walter Cocking">Walter Cocking</a>, a dean at the University of Georgia, had advocated bringing black and white students together in the classroom, he launched an attack on the university, charging elitism, and called for the regents to remove Cocking and purge the university of Communists, "foreigners" (non-Georgians), and subscribers to racial equality. The <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Board_of_Regents" title="Georgia Board of Regents">university board of regents</a>  at first refused Talmadge's demands, but after the governor restructured the board, the dismissals took place.
</p><p>This intervention into academic affairs caused the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Association_of_Colleges_and_Schools" title="Southern Association of Colleges and Schools">Southern Association of Colleges and Schools</a> to remove accreditation from the Georgia state universities. It also contributed to Talmadge's defeat by <a href="/wiki/Ellis_Arnall" title="Ellis Arnall">Ellis Arnall</a> in 1942.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>During Arnall's term, the state legislature lengthened his term to four years and prohibited him from seeking re-election in 1946. Talmadge ran for governor and used the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Smith_v._Allwright" title="Smith v. Allwright">Smith v. Allwright</a></i> decision, ruling that the closed white primary was unconstitutional, as his main red flag issue. Talmadge promised that if he were to be elected, he would restore the 'Equal Primary.'
</p><p>Talmadge lost the popular vote in the Democratic primary to <a href="/wiki/James_V._Carmichael" title="James V. Carmichael">James V. Carmichael</a> but won a majority of the "county unit votes". He died in December 1946, before he could be sworn in for his fourth term. His  death precipitated the 1947 "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Three_Governors_Controversy" title="The Three Governors Controversy">Three Governors Controversy</a>" among Arnall, <a href="/wiki/Melvin_E._Thompson" title="Melvin E. Thompson">Melvin E. Thompson</a>, and Talmadge's son <a href="/wiki/Herman_Talmadge" title="Herman Talmadge">Herman</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The "<a href="/wiki/Cocking_affair" title="Cocking affair">Cocking affair</a>" later became the subject of Michael Braz's <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">opera</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Scholar_Under_Siege" title="A Scholar Under Siege">A Scholar Under Siege</a></i>, composed for the centenary of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_Southern_University" title="Georgia Southern University">Georgia Southern University</a> and premiered in 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Talmadge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1941, Talmadge received an honorary degree in Doctor of Laws from <a href="/wiki/Oglethorpe_University" title="Oglethorpe University">Oglethorpe University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Talmadge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Talmadge_Memorial_Bridge" title="Talmadge Memorial Bridge">Talmadge Memorial Bridge</a> in Savannah, Georgia, is named after Eugene Talmadge and connects downtown <a href="/wiki/Savannah,_Georgia" title="Savannah, Georgia">Savannah, Georgia</a> with the <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina_Lowcountry" title="South Carolina Lowcountry">Carolina Low Country</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Savannah_River" title="Savannah River">Savannah River</a>. Renaming of the bridge has been suggested due to the Talmadge's history as a white supremacist.
</p>
