<p>The <b>Polynesian Voyaging Society</b> (PVS) is a non-profit research and educational corporation based in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Honolulu,_Hawaii" title="Honolulu, Hawaii">Honolulu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiʻi</a>. PVS was established to research and perpetuate traditional <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesian</a> <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_navigation" title="Polynesian navigation">voyaging methods</a>. Using replicas of traditional double-hulled canoes, PVS undertakes voyages throughout Polynesia navigating  without modern instruments.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polynesian_Voyaging_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The society was founded in 1973 by nautical <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">anthropologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ben_Finney" title="Ben Finney">Ben Finney</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiian" title="Native Hawaiian">Hawaiian</a> artist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herb_Kawainui_Kane" title="Herb Kawainui Kane">Herb Kawainui Kane</a>, and sailor Charles Tommy Holmes. The three wanted to show that ancient Polynesians could have purposely settled the <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_Triangle" title="Polynesian Triangle">Polynesian Triangle</a> using non-instrument navigation. The first PVS project was to build a replica of a double-hulled voyaging canoe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SteeleCostaHanaHou_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SteeleCostaHanaHou-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the genesis of the Society, the <a href="/wiki/East%E2%80%93West_Center" title="East–West Center">East–West Center</a> was instrumental in convincing the UN authorities in the Pacific of the necessity of the project. The navigator Pius "Mau" Piailug, master of the techniques of traditional navigation, was named Special Fellow by the Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eastwest_relationship_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-eastwest_relationship-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="H.C5.8Dk.C5.ABle.CA.BBa"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hōkūleʻa">Hōkūleʻa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polynesian_Voyaging_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hōkūleʻa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dk%C5%ABle%CA%BBa" title="Hōkūleʻa">Hōkūleʻa</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="716" data-file-width="955" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg/220px-Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg/330px-Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg/440px-Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hokule%27aSailing2009.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Hokuleʻa</i> off the coast of Honolulu, January 2009</div></div></div>
<p>On March 8, 1975, the first voyaging canoe to be built in the Hawaiian Islands in over 600 years was launched  with captain <a href="/wiki/Kawika_Kapahulehua" title="Kawika Kapahulehua">Kawika Kapahulehua</a> and crew.  Named the <i><a href="/wiki/H%C5%8Dk%C5%ABle%CA%BBa" title="Hōkūleʻa">Hōkūleʻa</a></i>, it left Hawaiʻi on May 1, 1976 for <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> in an attempt to retrace the ancient voyaging route.  <a href="/wiki/Micronesia" title="Micronesia">Micronesian</a> navigator <a href="/wiki/Mau_Piailug" title="Mau Piailug">Mau Piailug</a>, using no instruments, successfully navigated the canoe to Tahiti, arriving there on June 3, 1976.
</p><p>After an attempted voyage to <a href="/wiki/Tahiti" title="Tahiti">Tahiti</a> in 1978 was aborted when the <i>Hokuleʻa</i> capsized near <a href="/wiki/Lanai" title="Lanai">Lānaʻi</a> and crew member <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Aikau" title="Eddie Aikau">Eddie Aikau</a> was lost at sea, Piailug trained <a href="/wiki/Nainoa_Thompson" title="Nainoa Thompson">Nainoa Thompson</a> in the ancient navigation methods.  Two years later in 1980, Thompson replicated the successful 1976 voyage to Tahiti, becoming the first modern Hawaiian to master the art of Micronesian navigation.
</p><p>Since that voyage, the <i>Hokuleʻa</i> and her sister canoe the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawaiiloa" title="Hawaiiloa">Hawaiʻiloa</a></i> have undertaken voyages to other islands in Polynesia, including <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>.
</p><p>From 2014 to 2017, Nainoa Thompson and the Mālama Honua Worldwide Voyage engaged on a 3-year trip to visit 13 marine <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Sites</a> around the world. <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> and the Polynesian Voyaging Society had signed a partnership agreement to promote those historical sites through PVS' sailing adventures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alingano_Maisu">Alingano Maisu</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polynesian_Voyaging_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alingano Maisu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On January 23, 2007 the <i>Hokuleʻa</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Alingano_Maisu" title="Alingano Maisu">Alingano Maisu</a></i> set sail on a voyage to Micronesia and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. In March 2007, the canoes arrived at Piailug's home island of <a href="/wiki/Satawal" title="Satawal">Satawal</a> where five native Hawaiians and sixteen others were inducted into <a href="/wiki/Pwo" title="Pwo">Pwo</a> as master navigators. The event was the first Pwo ceremony on Satawal in 50 years and the <i>Alingano Maisu</i> was presented to Piailug as a gift for his contribution in reviving <a href="/wiki/Wayfinding" title="Wayfinding">wayfinding</a> navigation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polynesian_Voyaging_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

The funding was targeted by <a href="/wiki/John_McCain" title="John McCain">John McCain</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pork-barrel" title="Pork-barrel">pork-barrel</a>-funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
