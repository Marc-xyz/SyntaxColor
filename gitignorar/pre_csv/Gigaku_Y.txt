		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><i><b>Gigaku</b></i> <span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">伎楽</span>)</span>, also known as  <b><i>kure-gaku</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">呉楽</span>)</span></b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup> refers to an extinct genre of masked drama-dance performance, imported into Japan during the <a href="/wiki/Asuka_period" title="Asuka period">Asuka period</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Records state that it was introduced during the 20th year of reign of <a href="/wiki/Empress_Suiko" title="Empress Suiko">Empress Suiko</a> (612.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-2">[2]</a></sup>) by a certain <b>Mimaji</b> (Mimaji<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">味摩之</span>)</span>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heibonsha_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-heibonsha-3">[3]</a></sup> from Kudara kingdom (<b><a href="/wiki/Baekje" title="Baekje">Baekje</a></b>), one of the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms of Korea</a> arrived in Sakurai and taught gigaku to the Japanese youth <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>. It is said that he had studied in <a href="/wiki/Wu_(region)" title="Wu (region)">Wu</a> (China) and learned gigaku, showing that ties of gigaku can be traced back to China as during the time period the Japanese admired Chinese and Korean cultures and were heavily influenced by them <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>. The regent at the time, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Sh%C5%8Dtoku" title="Prince Shōtoku">Shotoku</a>, played a decisive role in allowing and diffusing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buddhist" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> culture within Japan, this spread of culture allowed gigaku to be performed and viewed by many Japanese individuals as it promoted the religion <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>. Gigaku peaked during the first half of the eighth century but began to disappear when bugaku took over as the official entertainment of the imperial palace, though gigaku was still performed and taught in areas far from the capital and continued to play a role in Japanese entertainment until up to the fourteenth century <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>. Many wooden gigaku masks were painted at this time, most dating from the Nara Period (710-84), and are now preserved at  Hōryūji and Tōdaiji temples and the imperial treasure house (Shōsōin), all in Nara <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>. Masks were an integral aspect of gigaku theatre and represented various characteristics and properties and later influenced other parts of Japanese theatre. The history of gigaku is often widely debated as there is no documentation of gigaku in mainland Asia aside from a few delicate masks that remain, this lack of strong evidence often makes it difficult for researchers to decipher the true origins of gigaku and its processes, characters, plots, and performances.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performance">Performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The masked dance was performed in silent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mime" title="Mime">mime</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup> to the accompaniment of music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup> The flute, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Waist_drum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Waist drum (page does not exist)">waist drum</a> (or hip drum;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup> (<i>yōko</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">腰鼓</span>)</span>), also known as <i>kuretsuzumi</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">呉鼓</span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>"Wu(China) drum"</i>)</span><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup>), and <i>shōban</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">鉦盤</span>)</span>, a type of <a href="/wiki/Gong" title="Gong">gong</a>, were the three instruments used in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nara_Period" title="Nara Period">Nara Period</a>, though the gong was superseded by a type of cymbal (<i>dobyōshi</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">銅鈸子</span>)</span>) in the early <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heian_Period" title="Heian Period">Heian Period</a> (9th century).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tokyomus_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tokyomus-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>About the only surviving description of the performance comes from the musical treatise forming a part of the <i><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ky%C5%8Dkunsh%C5%8D&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kyōkunshō (page does not exist)">Kyōkunshō</a><sup class="noprint" style="font-style: normal;"> [<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%95%99%E8%A8%93%E6%8A%84" title="ja:教訓抄">ja</a>]</sup></b></i> (『教訓抄』; "Selections for Instructions and Admonition" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup>) authored by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Koma_no_Chikazane&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Koma no Chikazane (page does not exist)">Koma no Chikazane</a><sup class="noprint" style="font-style: normal;"> [<a class="extiw" href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koma_no_Chikazane" title="ja:Koma no Chikazane">ja</a>]</sup> (died 1242).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-1">[1]</a></sup> According to this, the <i>netori</i> or tuning of instruments signals the start, followed by a prelude of instruments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup> Then there is a parading of the whole cast, both dancers and instrumentalists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-heibonsha_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-heibonsha-3">[3]</a></sup> It has been speculated that the character mask named <i><b>Chidō<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">治道</span>)</span></b></i> "Govern the way" probably took position at the front of the parade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-chido_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-chido-11">[11]</a></sup> especially as this mask is listed first off in the assets ledgers (Shizaichō<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">資材帳</span>)</span>) for some of the temples that house gigaku masks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-chido_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-chido-11">[11]</a></sup> The program opens with the Lion Dance (<a href="/wiki/Lion_dance" title="Lion dance">Shishimai</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup> and solo dances by the Duke of Wu,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup> wrestler, the birdman <a href="/wiki/Karura" title="Karura">karura</a>, and the Brahman priest.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archetypes">Archetypes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Archetypes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are two wrestler archetype characters, the <b>Kongō</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">金剛</span>)</span> or "Vajra-yakṣa" who is open-mouthed,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-kongo_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-kongo-12">[12]</a></sup> and the <b><a href="/wiki/Rikishi" title="Rikishi">Rikishi</a></b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">力士</span>)</span> who is closed mouthed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-kongo_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-kongo-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayashiya_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-hayashiya-13">[13]</a></sup>  These two are said to be analogous to the two <a href="/wiki/Nio" title="Nio">Niō</a> or guardian gate statues, who respectively form the open and closed <a href="/wiki/A-un" title="A-un">A-un</a> shapes in their mouths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-kongo_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-kongo-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayashiya_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-hayashiya-13">[13]</a></sup> Rikishi and Konron masks are often mixed up due to their similar features, they possess a darker complexion, bulging eyes, large mouths and jutting teeth <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>. These masks can be differentiated through their facial expressions as the Konron is less aggressive than Rikishi.   
</p><p><br/>
With the exaggerated features of many of the masks, the content of the play is described as being farcical.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup>  Indeed, the two-part play of the <b>Kuron</b><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">崑崙</span>)</span> (or Konron; Chinese:<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kunlun_nu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kunlun nu (page does not exist)">Kunlun nu</a> which denotes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_man" title="Black man">black man</a> or <a href="/wiki/Negrito" title="Negrito">negrito</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>) and the Rikishi (wrestler or "Strong Man") is outright obscene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the ribald performance, the lascivious Kuron falls in lust for the Gojo (Wu woman or Chinese maiden), and expresses his desire by holding up his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phallic" title="Phallic">phallic</a> prop called <i>marakata</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">陽物(マラカタ)</span>)</span>, and beating it with his <a href="/wiki/Hand_fan" title="Hand fan">hand fan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayashiya_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-hayashiya-13">[13]</a></sup>  The comic dance maneuvers are referred to as <i>marafuri-mai</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">マラフリ舞</span><span style="display:none">,</span> <i>"phallus-swinging dance"</i>)</span>. In subsequent development, the Kuron is subdued by the Rikishi who binds the Kuron by his equipment (<i>marakata</i>), and drags him along by the noose around his manhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hayashiya_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-hayashiya-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Masks">Masks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Masks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Masque_01.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="445" data-file-width="290" decoding="async" height="338" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Masque_01.jpg/220px-Masque_01.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/02/Masque_01.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Masque_01.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Gigaku masks from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horyuji" title="Horyuji">Horyuji</a> temple</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Chidō<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">治道</span>)</span> "Govern the way" - Leads the procession part. This mask has been suggested as perucursor of the depiction of <a href="/wiki/Tengu" title="Tengu">Tengu</a> masks<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-chido_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-chido-11">[11]</a></sup> <a class="external text" href="http://webarchives.tnm.jp/imgsearch/show/C0000666" rel="nofollow">TNM (ex-Horyuji)</a> <a class="external text" href="http://bunka.nii.ac.jp/SearchDetail.do?heritageId=208209&amp;imageNum=1" rel="nofollow">quarter view</a> It was a red headed mask with a wide mouth, long nose, wide bulging eyes, dark brows and sometimes contained few whiskers on the chin <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>.</li>
<li>Shishi<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">師子</span>)</span> "lion" - Lion mask with movable jaw, ear, eyes,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-shishi_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-shishi-17">[17]</a></sup> similar in appearance to the mask from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shishimai" title="Shishimai">Shishimai</a> lion dance. The mask contained tiny ears applied to a large circular face, a red tongue and snout, white teeth, brown, red, or green <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>.</li>
<li>Shishiko<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">師子児</span>)</span> "lion tamers" - Usually two tamers accompany each lion<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shincho-shishiko_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-shincho-shishiko-19">[19]</a></sup><a class="external text" href="http://webarchives.tnm.jp/imgsearch/show/C0007649" rel="nofollow">TNM (ex-Horyuji)</a>  (ex-Horyuji) and <a class="external text" href="http://webarchives.tnm.jp/imgsearch/show/C0007695" rel="nofollow">another tamer, TNM (ex-Horyuji)</a></li>
<li>Gokō<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">呉公</span>)</span> "Duke of Wu"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-araki_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-araki-9">[9]</a></sup> - <a class="external text" href="http://webarchives.tnm.jp/imgsearch/show/C0000663" rel="nofollow">TCM (ex-Horyuji)</a></li>
<li>Kongō<span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span lang="ja">金剛</span>)</span> "Vajra-yakṣa)" - Topknotted wrestler, wide-eyed and flexed eyebrows, open-mouthed. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gigaku&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many of these masks also influenced other Japanese theatre forms, Noh, for example particularly has masks very similar to the gigaku masks of goko and gojo <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>. The well resemblance of  gojo can be seen in the well known <a href="/wiki/Noh" title="Noh">Noh</a> mask of Koomote as well as Chido and Konron to the ghost and demon masks with their stark, exaggerated, and frightening features <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>. Though these masks share similarities it is important to note that there are also differences with them, for example the masks of Noh are much smaller in comparison to gigaku, this is also the case with bugaku (the emerging theatre form after gigaku).
</p>
