<p><a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> is a country situated in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southwest_Asia" title="Southwest Asia">Southwest Asia</a>, bordering the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman" title="Gulf of Oman">Gulf of Oman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>, between <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> (UAE). The coast of Oman was an important part in the Omani empire and sultanate.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Oman is located in the southeastern quarter of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> and covers a total land area of 309,500 km<sup>2</sup> (119,500 sq mi). The land area is composed of varying <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topographic</a> features: <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valleys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> account for 82 percent of the land mass; mountain ranges, 15 percent; and the coastal plain, 3 percent. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanate" title="Sultanate">sultanate</a> is flanked by the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman" title="Gulf of Oman">Gulf of Oman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Rub%27_al_Khali" title="Rub' al Khali">Rub' al Khali</a> (Empty Quarter) of <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, all of which contributed to Oman's isolation. Historically, the country's contacts with the rest of the world were by sea, which not only provided access to foreign lands but also linked the coastal towns of Oman. The Rub' al-Khali, difficult to cross even with modern desert transport, formed a barrier between the sultanate and the Arabian interior. The <a href="/wiki/Al_Hajar_Mountains" title="Al Hajar Mountains">Al Hajar Mountains</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen2016_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Allen2016-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish2007_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish2007-2">[2]</a></sup> which form a belt between the coast and the desert from the <a href="/wiki/Musandam_Peninsula" title="Musandam Peninsula">Musandam Peninsula</a> (Ras Musandam) to the city of <a href="/wiki/Sur,_Oman" title="Sur, Oman">Sur</a>, almost at Oman's easternmost point,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AF_EHajar_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-AF_EHajar-3">[3]</a></sup> formed another barrier. These geographic barriers kept the interior of Oman free from foreign military encroachments.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_regions">Geographical regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographical regions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oman_Map_FBOI.gif"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="326" decoding="async" height="268" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Oman_Map_FBOI.gif/250px-Oman_Map_FBOI.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/81/Oman_Map_FBOI.gif 1.5x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oman_Map_FBOI.gif" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Oman</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="1601" decoding="async" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg/250px-Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg/375px-Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg/500px-Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oman_1996_CIA_map.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Detailed map of Oman</div></div></div>
<p>Natural features divide the country into six distinct areas: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ru%27us_al-Jibal" title="Ru'us al-Jibal">Ru'us al-Jibal</a>, including the northern Musandam Peninsula;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancaster&amp;Lancaster2011_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lancaster&amp;Lancaster2011-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Al_Batinah_Region" title="Al Batinah Region">the Batinah</a> plain running southeast along the Gulf of Oman coast;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish2007_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish2007-2">[2]</a></sup> the Oman interior behind the Batinah coast comprising the Hajar Mountains, their foothills, and desert fringes; the coast from <a href="/wiki/Muscat" title="Muscat">Muscat</a>-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matrah" title="Matrah">Matrah</a> around the point of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ras_Al_Hadd" title="Ras Al Hadd">Ras Al Hadd</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish2007_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish2007-2">[2]</a></sup> and down the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>; the offshore island of Masirah; and finally the barren coastline south to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhofar" title="Dhofar">Dhofar</a> region in the south.
</p><p>Except for the foggy and fertile Dhofar, all of the coast and the lowlands around the Hajar mountains are part of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman_desert_and_semi-desert" title="Gulf of Oman desert and semi-desert">Gulf of Oman desert and semi-desert</a> ecoregion, while the mountains themselves are a distinct habitat.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Musandam_Governorate">Musandam Governorate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Musandam Governorate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The northernmost area, <a href="/wiki/Musandam_Governorate" title="Musandam Governorate">Musandam</a>, extends from the tip of the Musandam Peninsula to the boundary with the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> (UAE) at <a href="/wiki/Dibba_Al-Hisn" title="Dibba Al-Hisn">Hisn al-Dibba</a>. It borders the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz" title="Strait of Hormuz">Strait of Hormuz</a>, which links the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Oman" title="Gulf of Oman">Gulf of Oman</a>, and is separated from the rest of the sultanate by a strip of territory belonging to the UAE. This area consists of low mountains forming the northernmost extremity of the Western Hajar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lancaster&amp;Lancaster2011_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lancaster&amp;Lancaster2011-4">[4]</a></sup> Two inlets, Elphinstone (<i>Khawr ash-Shamm</i>) and Malcom (<i>Ghubbat al-Ghazirah</i>), cleave the coastline about one third of the distance from the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz" title="Strait of Hormuz">Strait of Hormuz</a> and at one point are separated by only a few hundred meters of land. The coastline is extremely rugged, and the Elphinstone Inlet, 16 kilometres (9.9 miles) long and surrounded by cliffs 1,000 to 1,250 m (3,280 to 4,100 ft) high, has frequently been compared with <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Al-Batinah">Al-Batinah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Al-Batinah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Al_Batinah_Region" title="Al Batinah Region">Al Batinah Region</a></div>
<p>The UAE territory separating Ru'us al Jibal from the rest of Oman extends almost as far south as the coastal town of <a href="/wiki/Shinas" title="Shinas">Shinas</a>. A narrow, well-populated coastal plain known as <i>Al-Batinah</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish2007_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish2007-2">[2]</a></sup> runs from the point at which the sultanate is re-entered to the town of As-Sib, about 140 km (87 mi) to the southeast. Across the plains, a number of <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadis</a>, heavily populated in their upper courses, descend from the Western Hajar Mountains to the south. A ribbon of oases, watered by wells and <a href="/wiki/Qanat" title="Qanat">underground channels (<i>aflaj</i>)</a>, extends the length of the plain, about 10 km (33,000 ft) inland.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muscat-Matrah_coastal_area">Muscat-Matrah coastal area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Muscat-Matrah coastal area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/As_Sib" title="As Sib">As Sib</a>, the coast changes character. For about 175 km (109 mi), from As-Sib to Ras al-Hadd, it is barren and bounded by cliffs almost its entire length; there is no cultivation and little habitation. Although the deep water off this coast renders navigation relatively easy, there are few natural harbors or safe anchorages. The two best are at Muscat and Matrah, where natural harbors facilitated the growth of cities centuries ago. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Al-Sharqiyah">Al-Sharqiyah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Al-Sharqiyah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Al Sharqiyah is the northeastern region of the Sultanate of Oman and overlooks the Arabian Sea to the east and includes the inner side of the Eastern Hijr Mountains.<br/>
The region consists of the following states:<br/>
South Al Sharqiyah - The state of Sur is its administrative capital in addition to the states of Jalan Bani Bu Ali and Jalan Bani Bu Hassan, Kamel and Alwafi and Masirah.<br/>
North Al Sharqiyah - The state of Ibra is its administrative capital in addition to the states of Bidiyah, Al-Mudhaibi, Qabil, Wadi Bani Khalid, Damma and Al-Tayyeen.<br/>
</p>
<h3><span id="Coastal_tract.2C_and_island_of_Masirah"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Coastal_tract,_and_island_of_Masirah">Coastal tract, and island of Masirah</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coastal tract, and island of Masirah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The desolate coastal tract from Jalan to Ras Naws has no specific name. Low hills and wastelands meet the sea for long distances. Midway along this coast and about fifteen kilometers offshore is the barren <a href="/wiki/Masirah_Island" title="Masirah Island">Masirah Island</a>. Stretching about 70 km (43 mi), the island occupies a strategic location near the entry point to the Gulf of Oman from the Arabian Sea. Because of its location, it became the site of military facilities used first by the British and then by the United States, following an access agreement signed in 1980 by the United States and Oman.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oman_Governorate">Oman Governorate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Oman Governorate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>West of the coastal areas lies the tableland of central Oman. The Wadi <a href="/wiki/Samail" title="Samail">Samail</a> (the largest <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadi</a> in the mountain zone),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> a valley that forms the traditional route between Muscat and the interior divides the Hajar range into two subranges: <i>Al-Ḥajar Al-Gharbī</i> (The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Hajar_Mountains" title="Western Hajar Mountains">Western Hajar</a>) and <i>Al-Ḥajar Ash-Sharqī</i> (The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Hajar_Mountains" title="Eastern Hajar Mountains">Eastern Hajar</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cavendish2007_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Cavendish2007-2">[2]</a></sup> At the same time, mountains in the central region, where the highest of the Hajar are located, are recognised as the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Hajar_Mountains" title="Central Hajar Mountains">Central Hajar</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CullenKusky2010_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-CullenKusky2010-5">[5]</a></sup> The general elevation is about 1,200 m (3,900 ft), but the peaks of the high ridge known as <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar_(Oman)" title="Jebel Akhdar (Oman)">Jebel Akhdar</a> ("Green Mountain"), rise to more than 3,000 m (1.9 mi). Jabal Akhdar is a home of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_tahr" title="Arabian tahr">Arabian tahr</a>, a unique species of wild goat. In the hope of saving this rare animal, Sultan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qabus_ibn_Said" title="Qabus ibn Said">Qabus ibn Said</a> has declared part of the mountain a national park. Behind the Western Mountains are two inland regions, Az-Zahirah and Inner Oman, separated by the lateral range of the Rub al Khali. Adjoining the Eastern Hajar Mountains are the sandy regions of Ash-Sharqiyah and Jalan, which also border the desert.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dhofar_Governorate">Dhofar Governorate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dhofar Governorate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhofar" title="Dhofar">Dhofar</a></div>
<p>Dhofar region extends from Ras ash-Sharbatat to the border of Yemen and north to the clearly defined border with Saudi Arabia. Its capital, <a href="/wiki/Salalah" title="Salalah">Salalah</a>, was the permanent residence of Sultan <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Said_Bin_Taimur_of_Muscat" title="Said Bin Taimur of Muscat">Said ibn Taimur Al Said</a> and the birthplace of the present sultan, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qaboos_ibn_Said" title="Qaboos ibn Said">Qaboos ibn Said</a>. The highest peak of the <a href="/wiki/Dhofar_Mountains" title="Dhofar Mountains">Dhofar Mountains</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jabal_Samhan" title="Jabal Samhan">Jabal Samhan</a>, is about 2,000 metres (1.2 miles).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OmanTourismSamhan_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-OmanTourismSamhan-6">[6]</a></sup> The coast of Dhofar is fertile, being watered by monsoonal fogs from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> and is part of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula_coastal_fog_desert" title="Arabian Peninsula coastal fog desert">Arabian Peninsula coastal fog desert</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a>.
</p><p>Al Dharerah region consists of three parts: Dhank, <a href="/wiki/Ibri" title="Ibri">Ibri</a> and Yanqul.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2806" data-file-width="2445" decoding="async" height="287" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg/250px-Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg/375px-Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg/500px-Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dust_storm_in_Oman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/SeaWiFS" title="SeaWiFS">SeaWiFS</a> captured this dust cloud blowing out over the Arabian Sea from Oman. March 12, 2000</div></div></div>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen" title="Köppen">Köppen</a> climate classification Oman has three different climates (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BWh" title="BWh">BWh</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSk" title="BSk">BSk</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BSh" title="BSh">BSh</a>) and is dominated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BWh" title="BWh">BWh</a>.
</p><p>With the exception of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhofar" title="Dhofar">Dhofari</a> region, which has a strong monsoon climate and receives warm winds from the Indian Ocean, the climate of Oman is extremely hot and dry most of the year.
</p><p>Summer begins in mid-April and lasts until October. The highest temperatures are registered in the interior, where readings up to a maximum of  50.8 °C (123.4 °F) have been recorded. On the Batinah coastal plain, summer temperatures seldom exceed 47 °C (116.6 °F), but, because of the low elevation, the humidity may be as high as 90 percent. The mean summer temperature in Muscat is 33 °C (91.4 °F), but the <i>gharbī</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="ar">غَرْبِي</span>‎, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a> </small>'western'), a strong wind that blows from the Rub' al-Khali, can raise temperatures from the towns on the Gulf of Oman by 6 °C (10.8 °F) to 10 °C (18 °F). 
</p><p>Winter temperatures are mild and pleasant, ranging between 18 and 26 °C (64.4 and 78.8 °F). 
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">Precipitation</a> on the coasts and on the interior plains ranges from 20 to 100 millimeters (0.8 to 3.9 in) a year and falls during mid- and late winter. Rainfall in the mountains, particularly over Jebel Akhdar, is much higher and may reach 900 millimeters (35.4 in). 
</p><p>Because the <a href="/wiki/Plateau" title="Plateau">plateau</a> of Jebel Akhdar is porous limestone, rainfall seeps quickly through it, and the vegetation, which might be expected to be more lush, is meager. However, a huge reservoir under the plateau provides springs for low-lying areas. In addition, an enormous <a href="/wiki/Wadi" title="Wadi">wadi</a> channels water to these valleys, making the area agriculturally productive in years of good rainfall. 
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dhofar" title="Dhofar">Dhofar</a>, benefiting from a southwest <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> between June and September, receives heavier rainfall and has constantly running streams, which make the region Oman's most fertile area.
</p><p>Occasionally, a cyclone from the North Indian Ocean makes landfall, bringing with it heavy rain, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Kelia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cyclone Kelia (page does not exist)">Cyclone Kelia</a> did in 2011. Oman was hit by <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu" title="Cyclone Gonu">Cyclone Gonu</a> on June 6. Large areas in the capital area region in the Governorate of Muscat and in Amerat and <a href="/wiki/Qurayyat,_Oman" title="Qurayyat, Oman">Quriyat</a> were severely affected. Gonu first hit the southern city of Sur late on June 5, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_of_Oman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Natural hazards: Summer winds often raise large sandstorms and dust storms in the interior during periodic droughts. Following rain, wadis can fill with rainwater water and vast tracts of land can be flooded. A cyclone making landfall can severely flood large areas, or blow sand all over the place.</li>
<li>Environment - current issues: Soil salinity is rising. There is beach pollution from oil spills. There are very limited natural fresh water resources.</li>
<li>Geographical note: Oman is in a strategic location on <a href="/wiki/Musandam_Peninsula" title="Musandam Peninsula">Musandam Peninsula</a> adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz" title="Strait of Hormuz">Strait of Hormuz</a>, a vital transit point for world <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crude_oil" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a>.</li></ul>
