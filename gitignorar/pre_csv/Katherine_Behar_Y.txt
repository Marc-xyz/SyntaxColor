<p><b>Katherine Behar</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/New_media" title="New media">new media</a> and performance artist and writer based in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a>, New York. Her work uses <a href="/wiki/Materialism" title="Materialism">materialism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> to explore contemporary digital culture and is unified by an approach she calls "object-oriented feminism." Behar's art practice encompasses interactive <a href="/wiki/Installation_art" title="Installation art">installation</a>, performance art, <a href="/wiki/Public_art" title="Public art">public art</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Video_art" title="Video art">video art</a>. In addition to her acclaimed artwork, Behar writes on various topics including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> media theory, technologized labor, and objecthood.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Katherine Behar was born in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, Massachusetts. She earned her <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Fine_Arts" title="Bachelor of Fine Arts">Bachelor of Fine Arts</a> from <a href="/wiki/School_of_the_Art_Institute_of_Chicago" title="School of the Art Institute of Chicago">School of the Art Institute of Chicago</a> in 2000. In 2006, she attained a Master of Arts degree from <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">New York University</a>, and in 2009 received a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Fine_Arts" title="Master of Fine Arts">Master of Fine Arts</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hunter_College,_City_University_of_New_York" title="Hunter College, City University of New York">Hunter College, City University of New York</a>.
</p><p>Behar currently lives and works in New York City. She is associate professor at <a href="/wiki/Baruch_College" title="Baruch College">Baruch College, City University of New York</a> where she heads the New Media Arts undergraduate program and teaches classes in video art and exhibition practices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cuny.edu_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cuny.edu-1">[1]</a></sup> She runs the New Media Artspace, a teaching exhibition space at Baruch College.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Behar's work was the subject of a museum survey exhibition at <a href="/wiki/Pera_Museum" title="Pera Museum">Pera Museum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, in 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Behar has been honored with residencies and fellowships including <a href="/wiki/MacDowell_Colony" title="MacDowell Colony">The MacDowell Colony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Works" title="Pioneer Works">Pioneer Works</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Johannesburg Workshop in Theory and Practice,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Cannonball, <a href="/wiki/The_Wassaic_Project" title="The Wassaic Project">The Wassaic Project</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Art_Journal_(College_Art_Association_journal)" title="Art Journal (College Art Association journal)">Art Journal</a></i> Digital Fellow, and 7.stock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> She received a <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Furnace_Archive" title="Franklin Furnace Archive">Franklin Furnace</a> Award in 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Behar is also a feminist theorist of technology, and has been particularly influential in developing an approach called "object-oriented feminism." She is additionally known for introducing the concept of "decelerationist aesthetics.”
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Behar's art questions how objecthood, <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">gender</a>, <a href="/wiki/Race_(human_categorization)" title="Race (human categorization)">race</a>, and labor play out in the digital realm, and often combines sculpture and performance.
</p><p>New media and performance art scholar Anna Watkins Fisher calls Behar's artwork an "original contribution to the field of <a href="/wiki/New_media_art" title="New media art">new media art</a>." She writes that "Behar's work forces a different kind of interaction between the digital and the aesthetic than what is regularly shown and promoted under the banner of new media art . . . Behar makes new media and performance speak to each other."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Behar's art mixes objecthood and personhood, playing with our understandings of care. For art critic Nathan Sharratt, “Behar’s artwork raises questions about parental responsibility. As we create more and more disposable ‘children,’ what effect will that have on us? On our children?”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anonymous_Autonomous">Anonymous Autonomous</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anonymous Autonomous">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Behar’s interactive installation <i>Anonymous Autonomous</i> (2018) consists of three robotic office chairs that have been transformed to behave like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driverless_cars" title="Driverless cars">driverless cars</a>. Relying on simplified versions of the <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">lidar</a> sensors and <a href="/wiki/Computer_vision" title="Computer vision">computer vision</a> systems used by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_vehicles" title="Autonomous vehicles">autonomous vehicles</a>, the chairs roam through the gallery, following paper "lanes" that viewers arrange on the floor. With this installation, Behar shows how <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deskilling" title="Deskilling">deskilling</a>, and algorithmic decision-making are changing activities like driving and office work. The driverless chairs’ tentative, constrained movements suggest that technological innovations like these may produce more restrictions than freedoms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maritime_Messaging:_Red_Hook">Maritime Messaging: Red Hook</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Maritime Messaging: Red Hook">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Maritime Messaging: Red Hook</i> (2017) is a public performance that took place on <a href="/wiki/NYC_Ferry" title="NYC Ferry">NYC Ferry</a> boats crossing between <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Red_Hook,_Brooklyn" title="Red Hook, Brooklyn">Red Hook</a>. The performance on October 29, 2017 marked the five-year anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a>. It was co-produced with <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_Works" title="Pioneer Works">Pioneer Works</a> and PortSide New York, two organizations based in Red Hook, a Brooklyn neighborhood that was hard hit by the storm.
</p><p><i>Maritime Messaging</i> considers how water witnesses and absorbs traces of human activity throughout history. In the words of art critic Laila Pedro, the project invites the water to "talk back," by "draw[ing] on the accumulated memories the water might hold."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> In the performance, a mock conversation unfolds between the Atlantic Ocean and an AI digital assistant app that Pedro explains, “sends messages into the water and then translates its sounds — waves, splashes — creating a mysterious, evocative ‘dialogue.’”
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roomba_Rumba">Roomba Rumba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roomba Rumba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Roomba Rumba</i> (2015) is an interactive installation in which two <a href="/wiki/Roomba" title="Roomba">Roombas</a> carrying potted rubber tree plants vacuum a bright green carpet. The Roombas appear to be dancing with each other and with visitors. They mirror the "little old ant" in the song "<a href="/wiki/High_Hopes_(Frank_Sinatra_song)" title="High Hopes (Frank Sinatra song)">High Hopes</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>, trying against all odds to "move that rubber tree plant.”
</p><p>Curators Fatma Çolakoğlu and Ulya Soley describe how the installation "draws uncanny similarities between the plants and exploited workers, as well as machine production and human labor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> For Behar, Roomba Rumba provides opportunities for human/nonhuman solidarities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The piece has also been exhibited under the alternate title, <i>High Hopes (Deux)</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="E-Waste">E-Waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: E-Waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>E-Waste</i> (2014) is a series of <a href="/wiki/USB" title="USB">USB</a> sculptures that stages a <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science-fictional</a> post-apocalyptic future in which humans are extinct and only devices populate the Earth.
</p><p>Art critic Meredith Kooi describes how "<i>E-Waste</i> encourage[s] the viewer to affectively engage with techno-detritus." Kooi writes that "Behar's future dystopia—in which digital entities persist, even thrive, beyond human extinction—by extension creates conditions in which viewers can witness a world they cannot inhabit, but in which the human hand remains discernible, through its own technological waste. . . . But the earth that swaddles these gadgets is composed of mainly inorganic material—the soil itself has become aplastic replica of its failed self."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Art critic Kaya Genç and curator Ulya Soley describe <i>E-Waste</i> as "a timeless dystopia" related to Nicolas Bourriaud's concept of "the exform." In <i>E-Waste,</i> "handmade bits and pieces of machine-made electronic sculptures in the installation are both futuristic and aged, analog and digital, animate and inanimate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modelling_Big_Data">Modelling Big Data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modelling Big Data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Modeling Big Data</i> (2014) is a six-channel video installation in which the artist performs as an obese data body. Parodying the overproduction of so-called <a href="/wiki/Big_data" title="Big data">big data</a>, Behar repeats movements that she calls <i>data gestures.</i> In four scenes she mimics four common computer routines: clicking, buffering, caching, and pinging.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Theorist Tung-Hui Hu describes the moving figure's undulating motion as resembling “that of a natural organism, but the costume and mise-en-scène of the video appears inorganic; the combination is disconcerting because we expect body or data, and yet we see both at once. [… T]he literal data-body appears so lethargic that it is barely able to move, producing a choreographed awkwardness, or a virtuous antivirtuosity." As a result, he explains that Behar allows "the self to become more like an indistinct mass of data […] by accepting rather than resisting its objectification."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Street.s.2Fwall.ing.2Fin"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Street.s/wall.ing/in">Street.s/wall.ing/in</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Street.s/wall.ing/in">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Street.s/wall.ing/in</i> (2013) is a performance designed for the building at <a href="/wiki/32_Old_Slip" title="32 Old Slip">32 Old Slip</a> in Manhattan's Wall Street district.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Orange shapes, inspired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jersey_barriers" title="Jersey barriers">Jersey barriers</a>, contain dancers that travel around the building over a period of two and a half hours. The forms eventually reach a Jersey barrier lining the Northwest corner of the building, and convene in the barrier's furthermost nook.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Autoresponder.exe">Autoresponder.exe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Autoresponder.exe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Autoresponder.exe</i> (2012) is a <a href="/wiki/4K_resolution" title="4K resolution">4K</a> video that slowly scans to reveal an image of an executive desk standing on end in a disheveled office. The video compares a scene of managerial power to automated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E-Mail" title="E-Mail">e-mail scripts</a>, implying that like the e-mail reply software, corporate culture is impersonal, ineffective, and tone-deaf.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compositions_for_Bit">Compositions for Bit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Compositions for Bit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Compositions for Bit</i> (2010) is Behar's farewell concert for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney" title="Disney">Disney</a>’s original cult-classic <a href="/wiki/Tron" title="Tron">Tron</a> that took place at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judson_Church" title="Judson Church">Judson Church</a> in New York, NY. The interactive concert featured the polyhedron sidekick Bit as three larger than life sculptures—each holding a dancer, lasers, video mixes, original sound scores, and other components of arcade culture that were brought together as an immersive environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="3G56k">3G56k</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 3G56k">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>3G56k</i> (2009) is an interactive installation and <a href="/wiki/BDSM" title="BDSM">BDSM</a>-themed performance involving a twelve-foot touchscreen "iPhone" and a ten-foot tower computer. The iPhone requires a human "dialer" to input a number to call the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dial-up" title="Dial-up">dial-up</a> modem inside the pink computer tower.  Each call causes an analog thermal fax to slowly print out an image of the rubber hose connecting the two machines through an opening in the tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_Blocks">Building Blocks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Building Blocks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Building Blocks</i> (2008) is a public performance and video project made for the Camouflash exhibition, concerning the 2007 demolition of the Centrum Warenhaus in <a href="/wiki/Dresden" title="Dresden">Dresden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Four dancers each inhabit a silver shape modeled after the Centrum Warenhaus’ aluminum "honey-comb" facade. The pieces briefly meet at the building's original site and try to reassemble themselves, disperse, and roll away towards the exhibition site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primaries">Primaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Primaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Primaries</i> (2008) is a three-channel video installation of three circular projections, each seven and a half feet tall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The circles rotate while gaining and losing momentum equal to the sounds of the fans of the motors. The projections are saturated in red, yellow, and blue, each with footage of a distorted figure that is edited to counteract the "gravity" of the rotations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pipecleaner">Pipecleaner</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pipecleaner">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Pipecleaner</i> (2007) is a cross-platform <a href="/wiki/Screensaver" title="Screensaver">screensaver</a> that shows a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pole_dancer" title="Pole dancer">pole dancer</a> invading the kitschy late-90s Microsoft screensaver "3D Pipes." The dancer wears a dress made of cleaning gloves and attempts to climb, polish, and clean the pipes while her actions are constantly overwritten by the growing pipe maze.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Behar compares the maintenance work of the pipe cleaning pole dancer to the maintenance work that screensaver software does to keep screens in good condition. She suggests <i>Pipecleaner</i> is an example of nonanthropocentric art because unlike most art, screensavers serve machines and are not intended for humans to look at.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".28de.29bugging_Loop.28de.29Loop"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="(de)bugging_Loop(de)Loop">(de)bugging Loop(de)Loop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: (de)bugging Loop(de)Loop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>(de)bugging Loop(de)Loop</i> (2006) is the first installment of a series in which Behar performs <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_bugs" title="Computer bugs">computer bugs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> The video features repeated logical errors of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_viruses" title="Computer viruses">computer viruses</a>, preventing a computer program from running to completion. The DVD explores the infinite loop through five short video loops using a hula hoop and <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> video tape, each between 16 and 28 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaborations">Collaborations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Collaborations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disorientalism">Disorientalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Disorientalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Disorientalism</i> (2005–present) is a long-term multimedia performance art collaboration between Behar and artist and professor Marianne M. Kim. The duo studies the disorienting effects of technologized labour, junk culture, and <a href="/wiki/Consumerism" title="Consumerism">consumerism</a>. Through performance, video, and photographic projects, they explore how these mediate race, gender, and bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="RSI">RSI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: RSI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 2009 to 2013 Behar collaborated with Silvia Ruzanka and Ben Chang under the moniker RSI (also known as "Resynplement"), an art and technology team that advocated irrational <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human-computer_interface" title="Human-computer interface">human-computer interfaces</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibitions">Exhibitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Exhibitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Behar's artwork has appeared in galleries, museums, festivals, and art spaces all over the globe. Some noted exhibitions include:
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pera_Museum" title="Pera Museum">Pera Museum</a> in Istanbul;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Sector 2337 in Chicago; Boston Cyberarts Gallery; Tuska Center for Contemporary Art at University of Kentucky in Lexington;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> SPRING/BREAK Art Fair in New York;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> Museum of Contemporary Art North Miami, <a href="/wiki/Judson_Memorial_Church" title="Judson Memorial Church">Judson Memorial Church</a> in New York; UNOACTU in Dresden; The Girls Club Collection in Miami; Feldman Gallery + Project Space in Portland; <a href="/wiki/De_Balie" title="De Balie">De Balie Centre</a> for Culture and Politics in Amsterdam; the Mediations Biennale in Poznan; the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Cultural_Center" title="Chicago Cultural Center">Chicago Cultural Center</a>; the Swiss Institute in Rome; the National Museum of Art in Cluj-Napoca.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writings">Writings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Writings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In tandem with her creative work, Behar is also a writer and theorist. She is published in many different books, journals, and catalogues. Behar writes on subjects concerning embodiment and technology, automation and labor, cyborgian human/machine ethics, emerging and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obsolete" title="Obsolete">obsolete</a> technologies, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a> media critique.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Object-Oriented_Feminism">Object-Oriented Feminism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Object-Oriented Feminism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, Behar established "object-oriented feminism" (OOF), an interdisciplinary field of feminist materialist analysis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Her edited collection <i>Object-Oriented Feminism</i> “explores the political and ethical potential of being an object.” It was published in 2016 by University of Minnesota Press, which describes the book as “a feminist intervention into recent philosophical discourses—like speculative realism, object-oriented ontology, and new materialism—that take objects, things, stuff, and matter as primary.” Patricia Ticineto Clough states that Behar is "not shy in reestablishing feminist theory as a primary resource for thinking about objects, things and environments."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> For reviewer Jesse Bordwin, “Object-oriented criticism and feminist theory are not obviously compatible—their commitments and assumptions seldom overlap—but that tension is precisely what energizes the new collection <i>Object-Oriented Feminism.</i>”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="And_Another_Thing:_Nonanthropocentrism_and_Art">And Another Thing: Nonanthropocentrism and Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: And Another Thing: Nonanthropocentrism and Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016, Behar and Emmy Mikelson, coedited <i>And Another Thing: Nonanthropocentrism and Art,</i> an expanded exhibition catalog for their 2011 co-curated exhibition at the CUNY Graduate Center's James Gallery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> This book shows “how artists have figured and prefigured nonanthropocentric ideas strikingly similar to those expounded in various ‘new’ realist, materialist, and speculativist philosophies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bigger_than_You:_Big_Data_and_Obesity">Bigger than You: Big Data and Obesity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Katherine_Behar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Bigger than You: Big Data and Obesity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Also in 2016, Behar published an essay chapbook, <i>Bigger than You: Big Data and Obesity.</i> In this book she coins the term “decelerationist aesthetics” to name “the aesthetic properties, proclivities, and performances of objects come to defy the accelerationist imperative to be nimbly individuated.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> This imaginative research work coincided with Behar's video series <i><a class="external autonumber" href="https://vimeo.com/199454178" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>,</i> which puts her decelerationist theory into practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p>
