<p><b>Palisades Charter High School</b>  (usually colloquially known as "Pali High,"or "Pali," abbreviated as "PCHS") is an independent charter secondary school in Los Angeles, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, United States. The public high school serves the neighborhoods of <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Palisades,_Los_Angeles" title="Pacific Palisades, Los Angeles">Pacific Palisades</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palisades_Highlands,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Palisades Highlands, Los Angeles, California">Palisades Highlands</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenter_Canyon,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Kenter Canyon, Los Angeles, California">Kenter Canyon</a>, and portions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brentwood,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Brentwood, Los Angeles, California">Brentwood</a> (including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brentwood_Circle,_Los_Angeles,_California" title="Brentwood Circle, Los Angeles, California">Brentwood Circle</a>). Residents in <a href="/wiki/Topanga,_California" title="Topanga, California">Topanga</a>, an unincorporated section of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County" title="Los Angeles County">Los Angeles County</a>, may attend Palisades or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taft_High_School_(Los_Angeles)" title="Taft High School (Los Angeles)">Taft High School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Excess_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Excess-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The school serves grades 9 through 12. Formerly directly administered by the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Unified_School_District" title="Los Angeles Unified School District">Los Angeles Unified School District</a> school, the school is now an independent <a href="/wiki/Charter_school" title="Charter school">charter school</a>. Its current enrollment numbers 2,903 students.  Many students travel long distances to attend Palisades Charter High, which is one of the most highly ranked public high schools in the Los Angeles area.
</p><p>In 2005, Palisades was recognized as a <a href="/wiki/California_Distinguished_School" title="California Distinguished School">California Distinguished School</a>. In 2015, Palisades was named one of America's Best High Schools by <i>Newsweek</i> and <i>U.S. News &amp; World Report</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Revere_Charter_Middle_School" title="Paul Revere Charter Middle School">Paul Revere Charter Middle School</a> students are allowed to attend as a feeder school.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palisades_Charter_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The school was founded in 1961.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-begindate_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-begindate-1">[1]</a></sup> It was built for $6,000,000 on a filled-in canyon. At the time it was the most expensive high school in the LA City Schools. The founding principal was Herbert L. Aigner (died in 2000).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Class of 2012 was the 50th graduating class. 
</p><p>Prior to the founding, the property was called All Hallows Farm and for many years was owned by the Conway family: Hollywood film director <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jack_Conway_(film-maker)" title="Jack Conway (film-maker)">Jack Conway</a>; his wife, actress Virginia Conway — daughter of <a href="/wiki/Silent_film" title="Silent film">silent screen</a> star <a href="/wiki/Francis_X._Bushman" title="Francis X. Bushman">Francis X. Bushman</a> — and their two sons, one of whom, Pat Conway became an actor as well. This property was subsequently rented to actress <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Reynolds" title="Debbie Reynolds">Debbie Reynolds</a> and her husband, singer <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Fisher_(singer)" title="Eddie Fisher (singer)">Eddie Fisher</a>. It was then taken, some years later, by the State by <a href="/wiki/Eminent_domain" title="Eminent domain">eminent domain</a> to build the high school.
</p><p>Several members of the class of 1965 were profiled in a <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine article, which led to a best-selling 1976 book by class members <a href="/wiki/David_Wallechinsky" title="David Wallechinsky">David Wallechinsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michael_Medved" title="Michael Medved">Michael Medved</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/What_Really_Happened_to_the_Class_of_%2765%3F" title="What Really Happened to the Class of '65?">What Really Happened to the Class of '65?</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nat-rev_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nat-rev-8">[8]</a></sup> The book featured interviews with several members of the class, whose experiences were recounted both individually and in groupings around shared themes such as the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_draft" title="The draft">the draft</a>, drug experimentation, and sex. Various teachers from the school also were interviewed, among them English teachers Miss Jean O'Brien, history teacher Mr. Johnson, and Mrs. Rose "Mama G" Gilbert, who retired during 2012-2013 after 63 years of teaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> At age 94, Mrs. Gilbert was the oldest active teacher in the LAUSD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The success of the book later inspired a short-lived television dramatic anthology series of the same title, which ran from December 1977 to July 1978 on <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvg_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-tvg-12">[12]</a></sup> The character of <a href="/wiki/Maz_Kanata" title="Maz Kanata">Maz Kanata</a> was based on her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1989 <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/20/20_(U.S._TV_program)" title="20/20 (U.S. TV program)">20/20</a></i> aired an episode about the students of Palisades High School. Howard Rosenberg of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote that "Palisades High School is characterized here as both an institution of high academic performance and high drug and alcohol use. What "20/20" doesn't ask tonight is how both are possible at the same school."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>Circa 1992 there were so few students that LAUSD was considering closing the school. Pali High parents, principal Merle Price, and Pali High staff decided to advocate for making the school into a charter school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAmagHSp66_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAmagHSp66-15">[15]</a></sup> In 1993 the school, along with three feeder elementary schools, received approval from the Los Angeles Board of Education to become a charter school. This was the first time a group of schools in California became charter schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The school asked students to abide by a behavior code and instituted new academic programs. By 1998 student enrollment recovered: it had 200 students previously attending private schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAmagHSp66_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAmagHSp66-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>This school was the focus of a false email chain letter started around 2002. The message falsely claimed that a satiric message to parents about student truancies and homework problems was actually on the school's answering machine. The message was originally written in response to parent outrage that students who skipped class more than ten days per 90-schoolday semester (not counting legitimate absences, like sickness) could receive a failing grade in that class. This was reported on several web sites, including TruthOrFiction.com,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Snopes" title="Snopes">Snopes</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and BreakTheChain.org.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campus">Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palisades_Charter_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Campus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The campus is bounded by Temescal Canyon Road to the east, <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Boulevard" title="Sunset Boulevard">Sunset Boulevard</a> to the north, El Medio Street to the west, and Temescal Academy (formerly known as first Temescal Canyon Continuation School and later Temescal High School) to the south.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> It is bisected by Bowdoin Street, which runs between the school's football field and the academic center of the school. Located only a mile from <a href="/wiki/Will_Rogers_State_Beach" title="Will Rogers State Beach">Will Rogers State Beach</a>, the football stadium is called "Stadium by the Sea."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many movies have been filmed at Palisades. One of the first major motion pictures to be shot at Pali High was <i><a href="/wiki/Carrie_(1976_film)" title="Carrie (1976 film)">Carrie</a></i>.  Directors <a href="/wiki/George_Lucas" title="George Lucas">George Lucas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_De_Palma" title="Brian De Palma">Brian De Palma</a> held a joint audition for <i>Carrie</i> and Lucas's <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">Star Wars</a></i> (1977) on the Palisades campus. Other movies filmed on site include <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_(TV_series)" title="Popular (TV series)">Popular</a></i> (1999), <i><a href="/wiki/Crazy/Beautiful" title="Crazy/Beautiful">Crazy/Beautiful</a></i> (2001), <i><a href="/wiki/The_Glass_House_(2001_film)" title="The Glass House (2001 film)">The Glass House</a></i> (2001), <i><a href="/wiki/Old_School_(film)" title="Old School (film)">Old School</a></i> (2003), <i><a href="/wiki/Freaky_Friday_(2003_film)" title="Freaky Friday (2003 film)">Freaky Friday</a></i> (2003), <i><a href="/wiki/Havoc_(2005_film)" title="Havoc (2005 film)">Havoc</a></i> (2005), and <i><a href="/wiki/Project_X_(2012_film)" title="Project X (2012 film)">Project X</a></i> (2012). The TV series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Family_(TV_series)" title="Modern Family (TV series)">Modern Family</a></i> (2013), <i><a href="/wiki/Teen_Wolf_(2011_TV_series)" title="Teen Wolf (2011 TV series)">Teen Wolf</a></i> (2011),  the Netflix series <i><a href="/wiki/American_Vandal" title="American Vandal">American Vandal</a> (2017)</i> and <a href="/wiki/Dude_(film)" title="Dude (film)">Dude (film)</a> (2017) were also filmed at the school.
</p><p>Pali High was also used for the <a href="/wiki/Sweet_Valley_High" title="Sweet Valley High">Sweet Valley High</a> book, <i>Party Weekend</i>.
</p><p>As of 2010, approximately 43% of the student body, 1,180 out of 2,742 students, were bused to Palisades Charter High School from more than 100 Los Angeles zip codes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In 1994, approximately 70% of the student body, 1,176 out of 1,680 students, were bused from South-Central and East Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Times_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Times-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>As of 1998<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Palisades_Charter_High_School&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup> most of its classes are university preparatory in nature, and it hosts a mathematics, science, and technology magnet program. The school also has humanities and marine biology courses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAmagHSp66_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-LAmagHSp66-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The school includes a New Media Academy that was, as of 1998, one of three in LAUSD. Pali High uses its charter school status to increase the visibility of this program. By that year <a href="/wiki/DreamWorks_Pictures" title="DreamWorks Pictures">DreamWorks</a> had financed it with $100,000 ($153715.8 according to inflation). The location fees generated by <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Spelling" title="Aaron Spelling">Aaron Spelling</a><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'s</span> use of the school for <i><a href="/wiki/Malibu_Shores" title="Malibu Shores">Malibu Shores</a></i> were used to, by 1998, install a new <a href="/wiki/Computer_lab" title="Computer lab">computer lab</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAmagHSp66_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-LAmagHSp66-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2002 Palisades High School offers a surfing class that can be taken for physical education credit. It was established around 1998 by Ray Millette, a marine biology teacher and surfer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palisades Charter High School is known for having a great music program. Its marching band has medaled 3 years in a row at the SCSBOA championships: in 2013 getting silver, in 2014 winning the gold for 3A and in 2015 getting bronze. The school also has a winter Drumline that competes in both SCPA and WGI and is also the 2016 SCPA "A class" silver medalist.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Discrimination_and_lawsuits">Discrimination and lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Palisades_Charter_High_School&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discrimination and lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2016, extensive hate graffiti was found at the campus including references to the Ku Klux Klan, Jews, African-Americans, and LGBTQ people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2017, Palisades Charter High School gained national media attention after an African-American student was depicted in a student blog with a noose drawn around her neck.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>
</p>
