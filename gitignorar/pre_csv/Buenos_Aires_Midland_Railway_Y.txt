<p>The <b>Buenos Aires Midland Railway</b> (BAM) was a British-owned railway company which operated in <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, where it was known as <b>Ferrocarril Midland de Buenos Aires</b>. The company built and operated the <span class="nowrap">1,000 mm</span> (<span class="nowrap">3 ft <span class="frac">3<span class="visualhide"> </span><sup>3</sup>⁄<sub>8</sub></span> in</span>) gauge (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre_gauge" title="Metre gauge">metre gauge</a>) line between <a href="/wiki/Puente_Alsina_railway_station" title="Puente Alsina railway station">Puente Alsina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carhu%C3%A9" title="Carhué">Carhué</a> in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Province" title="Buenos Aires Province">Buenos Aires Province</a>.
</p><p>In 1948, the railways of Argentina were nationalised, the BAM becoming part of <a href="/wiki/Belgrano_Sur_Line" title="Belgrano Sur Line">Belgrano Sur Line</a>; the company closed as a result. Nowadays, services on the line are operated by the state-owned company <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trenes_Argentinos" title="Trenes Argentinos">Trenes Argentinos</a>, the concessionary of <a href="/wiki/Puente_Alsina_railway_station" title="Puente Alsina railway station">Puente Alsina</a>-Marinos del Crucero General Belgrano branch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SOFSE_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SOFSE-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 1904, the <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Province" title="Buenos Aires Province">Buenos Aires Province</a> granted entrepreneur Enrique Lavalle a concession to build a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre_gauge_railway" title="Metre gauge railway">metre gauge</a> railway line between <a href="/wiki/Puente_Alsina_railway_station" title="Puente Alsina railway station">Puente Alsina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carhu%C3%A9" title="Carhué">Carhué</a>. The company and line were named "Buenos Aires Midland Railway", so the Lacroze Brothers registered the Spanish form "Ferrocarril Central de Buenos Aires". Works were led by ideologue <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Casey" title="Eduardo Casey">Eduardo Casey</a>, who had founded the city of <a href="/wiki/Venado_Tuerto" title="Venado Tuerto">Venado Tuerto</a> some time before.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buenos_Aires_Midland_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Construction of the line began in 1907, with works carried out by Argentine company <i>Hume Hnos.</i>, hired by Lavalle. By November that year the line had extended to Puente de la Noria (now Ingeniero Budge station). The service was operated initially using a Koppel <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotive</a> and a unique coach.
</p><p>The BAM soon entered into conflict with the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a>-owned company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferrocarril_Compa%C3%B1%C3%ADa_General_de_Buenos_Aires" title="Ferrocarril Compañía General de Buenos Aires">Ferrocarril Compañía General de Buenos Aires</a> (CGBA), which was also building railways in the same part of the Province, a situation with potential to bankrupt both BAM and CGBA. The legal situation was also complicated: while National Law 2.793 gave priority to the company that had been granted concession first, BAM in this case, the concession granted to BAM had been given by a Province; therefore the CGBA also had rights to claim.
</p><p>Both companies initially continued expanding their lines across the Province, the conflict reaching such a stage that BAM asked CGBA to cease construction work, while CGBA threatened to take the case to the Supreme Court of Justice. Eventually an understanding was reached between the two companies, establishing joint use of Plomer station, among other issues. The BAM lost several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embankment_(transportation)" title="Embankment (transportation)">embankments</a> because of the deal, so the company requested financial compensation, alleging that they had invested too much money in the construction.
</p><p>In mid 1908, works had to be suspended as the company was unable to raise capital from Europe. Other companies such as <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Great_Southern_Railway" title="Buenos Aires Great Southern Railway">Great Southern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Western_Railway" title="Buenos Aires Western Railway">Western</a> Railways took over BAM and committed to finish the line. Some of the changes made by the consortium were the replacement of contractor Hume Hnos. by Clarke, Bradbury and Co. (owned by Great Southern's manager's brother). The Puente Alsina - San Sebastián section was finally opened on 15 June 1909, the complete line to Carhué opening on 1 July 1911.
</p><p>As the BAM line terminated at Puente Alsina, the company could not reach the capital city of <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, which was part of its strategy to increase business. Extending the line to the capital was not legally possible, since the concession had been granted by a Provincial body and the capital city was under the jurisdiction of the National Government. In 1912, an agreement was signed with the Western Railway, which opened a station (named "Intercambio Midland") that allowed Midland railway passengers to change for Western Railway trains to Sola station in the <a href="/wiki/Parque_Patricios" title="Parque Patricios">Parque Patricios</a> district of Buenos Aires. However, this situation did not last long, Puente Alsina becoming the terminus again soon after.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_locomotives">Steam locomotives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buenos_Aires_Midland_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Steam locomotives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first locomotive was a 21-<a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">ton</a> class A built by <a href="/wiki/Vulcan_Iron_Works" title="Vulcan Iron Works">Vulcan Iron Works</a>. The second one was the <a href="/wiki/Orenstein_%26_Koppel" title="Orenstein &amp; Koppel">Orenstein &amp; Koppel</a> locomotive that made the first trip from Puente Alsina to Puenta La Noria. The latter was sold in 1913, some accounts stating that it is currently exhibited at a <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazilian</a> museum. Another early locomotive was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerr_Stuart" title="Kerr Stuart">Kerr, Stuart</a>, manufactured in 1901 exclusively for the Government of Argentina, and sold in 1935 to another company in Buenos Aires Province. Another Kerr, Stuart machine was used on short trips until the 1930s.
</p><p>Once the entire line had been inaugurated, the company acquired six Kerr, Stuart class Es that served until 1948 when the railway was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway_Nationalisation_in_Argentina" title="Railway Nationalisation in Argentina">nationalised</a>, at which time these locomotives were sold. The company bought 20 class Fs by the same company, which were operated until the 1970s (latterly by <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarriles_Argentinos" title="Ferrocarriles Argentinos">Ferrocarriles Argentinos</a>).
</p><p>The BA Midland also used <a href="/wiki/Hunslet_Engine_Company" title="Hunslet Engine Company">Hunslet</a> class G locomotives until they were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">railcars</a> in 1939, being cascaded to other services. The <a href="/wiki/Beyer,_Peacock_and_Company" title="Beyer, Peacock and Company">Beyer Peacock</a> H class locomotives proved too big for the line, and were rented to Ferrocarril Santa Fe and other companies. Sentinel-Cammell locomotives, constructed jointly by <a href="/wiki/Sentinel_Waggon_Works" title="Sentinel Waggon Works">Sentinel Waggon Works</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metro_Cammell" title="Metro Cammell">Metro Cammell</a>, operated suburban services on the main line.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernisation:_railcars">Modernisation: railcars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buenos_Aires_Midland_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modernisation: railcars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 1936, railways in Argentina faced increasing competition from <a href="/wiki/Road_transport" title="Road transport">road transport</a>. Some British owned companies (such as <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Great_Southern_Railway" title="Buenos Aires Great Southern Railway">Great Southern Railway</a>) responded by acquiring <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">railcars</a> from <a href="/wiki/Drewry_Car_Co." title="Drewry Car Co.">Drewry Car Co.</a>. By this time, BAM carried very few passengers, and was losing money. In order to increase the number of passengers carried and to compete against Southern, Western and GCBA railways, BAM acquired 10 <a href="/wiki/Railcar" title="Railcar">railcars</a> from <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Railway_Carriage_and_Wagon_Company" title="Birmingham Railway Carriage and Wagon Company">Birmingham Railway Carriage &amp; Wagon Company</a>, powered with <a href="/wiki/L._Gardner_and_Sons" title="L. Gardner and Sons">Gardner</a> engines. The railcars entered service on 1 January 1939, replacing old steam locomotives on all services passenger. The railcars reduced the journey time to Carhué to 8 hours; the same journey took 14 hours on the Great Southern Railway.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nationalisation">Nationalisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Buenos_Aires_Midland_Railway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nationalisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the entire Argentine railway network was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Railway_Nationalisation_in_Argentina" title="Railway Nationalisation in Argentina">nationalised</a> in 1948 during <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">Juan Perón</a>'s presidency, the BAM line became part of <a href="/wiki/General_Manuel_Belgrano_Railway" title="General Manuel Belgrano Railway">Belgrano Railway</a>, one of the six divisions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government-owned_corporation" title="Government-owned corporation">State-owned company</a> <a href="/wiki/Ferrocarriles_Argentinos" title="Ferrocarriles Argentinos">Ferrocarriles Argentinos</a>.
</p>
