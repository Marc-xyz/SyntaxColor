<p>The <b>Elwha River</b> is a 45-mile (72 km) river on the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Peninsula" title="Olympic Peninsula">Olympic Peninsula</a> in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>. From its source at <a href="/wiki/Elwha_snowfinger" title="Elwha snowfinger">Elwha snowfinger</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Mountains" title="Olympic Mountains">Olympic Mountains</a>, it flows generally north to the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Juan_de_Fuca" title="Strait of Juan de Fuca">Strait of Juan de Fuca</a>. Most of the river's course is within the Olympic National Park.
</p><p>The Elwha is one several rivers in the Pacific Northwest that hosts all five species of native <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_salmon" title="Pacific salmon">Pacific salmon</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chinook_salmon" title="Chinook salmon">chinook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coho_salmon" title="Coho salmon">coho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chum_salmon" title="Chum salmon">chum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sockeye_salmon" title="Sockeye salmon">sockeye</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pink_salmon" title="Pink salmon">pink salmon</a>), plus four <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">anadromous</a> <a href="/wiki/Trout" title="Trout">trout</a> species (<a href="/wiki/Rainbow_trout" title="Rainbow trout">steelhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coastal_cutthroat_trout" title="Coastal cutthroat trout">coastal cutthroat trout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bull_trout" title="Bull trout">bull trout</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolly_Varden_char" title="Dolly Varden char">Dolly Varden char</a>). From 1911 to 2014, dams blocked fish passage on the lower Elwha River. Before the dams, 400,000 adult salmon returned yearly to spawn in 70 miles (110 km) of river habitat. Prior to dam removal, fewer than 4,000 salmon returned each year in only 4.9 miles (7.9 km) of habitat below the lower dam.  The <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> removed the two dams as part of the $325 million <a href="/wiki/Elwha_Ecosystem_Restoration" title="Elwha Ecosystem Restoration">Elwha Ecosystem Restoration Project</a>. Dam removal work began in September 2011 and was completed in August 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leach_estuary_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-leach_estuary-4">[4]</a></sup> The river has already carried sediment to its mouth, creating 70 acres of estuary habitat at the Strait of Juan de Fuca.
</p><p>The first documented use of the name Elwha River dates to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Kellett" title="Henry Kellett">Henry Kellett</a>'s 1846 map.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parratt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-parratt-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Course">Course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwha_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Elwha River begins at the <a href="/wiki/Elwha_snowfinger" title="Elwha snowfinger">Elwha snowfinger</a> near Mount Barnes and Mount Queets in the Olympic Range within Olympic National Park, in <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_Washington" title="Jefferson County, Washington">Jefferson County, Washington</a>. The river flows southeast, then curves northward for the great majority of its course, with its mouth at the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Major peaks near the Elwha's source include <a href="/wiki/Mount_Christie_(Washington)" title="Mount Christie (Washington)">Mount Christie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Carrie" title="Mount Carrie">Mount Carrie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Meany" title="Mount Meany">Mount Meany</a>, and Mount Seattle.
</p><p>After receiving the tributaries Delabarre and Godkin creeks, the Elwha River flows northward. The Hayes River joins in Press Valley, where the Hayes River Ranger Station is located. Lost River joins near the northern end of Press Valley, after which the Elwha crosses into <a href="/wiki/Clallam_County,_Washington" title="Clallam County, Washington">Clallam County, Washington</a>.
</p><p>Just after the county line, the Elwha River passes the Elkhorn Ranger Station and enters the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon_of_the_Elwha" title="Grand Canyon of the Elwha">Grand Canyon of the Elwha</a>. As the river leaves the Grand Canyon, it is crossed by <a href="/wiki/Dodger_Point_Bridge" title="Dodger Point Bridge">Dodger Point Bridge</a>. <a href="/wiki/Humes_Ranch_Cabin" title="Humes Ranch Cabin">Humes Ranch Cabin</a> is located near the river along the <a href="/wiki/Geyser_Valley_trail" title="Geyser Valley trail">Geyser Valley trail</a>. After passing <a href="/wiki/Krause_Bottom" title="Krause Bottom">Krause Bottom</a>, the river enters Rica Canyon at <a href="/wiki/Goblins_Gate" title="Goblins Gate">Goblins Gate</a>. Prior to dam removal, the river fanned out into a delta below Rica Canyon, at the head of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Mills_(Washington)" title="Lake Mills (Washington)">Lake Mills</a>, the reservoir behind <a href="/wiki/Glines_Canyon_Dam" title="Glines Canyon Dam">Glines Canyon Dam</a>. Below the dam site, the Elwha is paralleled by <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Hot_Springs" title="Olympic Hot Springs">Olympic Hot Springs</a> Road. The river receives a tribunate from the Madison creek. The trailhead for <a href="/wiki/Madison_Creek_Falls" title="Madison Creek Falls">Madison Creek Falls</a> is also along the river. Campgrounds and the <a href="/wiki/Elwha_Ranger_Station" title="Elwha Ranger Station">Elwha Ranger Station</a> are located along the river before it exits <a href="/wiki/Olympic_National_Park" title="Olympic National Park">Olympic National Park</a>. Until early 2012, when <a href="/wiki/Lake_Aldwell" title="Lake Aldwell">Lake Aldwell</a> was drained, the river entered this reservoir behind <a href="/wiki/Elwha_Dam" title="Elwha Dam">Elwha Dam</a>.
</p><p>Downriver from the former site of the Elwha Dam, the Elwha River flows several miles north through the Elwha Canyon and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_Elwha_Indian_Reservation" title="Lower Elwha Indian Reservation">Lower Elwha Indian Reservation</a>, to enter the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Juan_de_Fuca" title="Strait of Juan de Fuca">Strait of Juan de Fuca</a> at Angeles Point, just west of the city of <a href="/wiki/Port_Angeles,_Washington" title="Port Angeles, Washington">Port Angeles, Washington</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dams">Dams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elwha_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Elwha_Ecosystem_Restoration" title="Elwha Ecosystem Restoration">Elwha Ecosystem Restoration</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elwha_Dam.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Elwha_Dam.jpg/220px-Elwha_Dam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Elwha_Dam.jpg/330px-Elwha_Dam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Elwha_Dam.jpg/440px-Elwha_Dam.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elwha_Dam.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Elwha Dam with Lake Aldwell behind. The power house can be seen in the center.</div></div></div>
<p>The river is the site of the largest <a href="/wiki/Dam_removal" title="Dam removal">dam removal</a> project in history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Elwha_Ecosystem_Restoration" title="Elwha Ecosystem Restoration">Elwha Ecosystem Restoration</a> project is the second largest ecosystem restoration project the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> has attempted, after the <a href="/wiki/Everglades" title="Everglades">Everglades</a>. The Elwha River Ecosystem and Fisheries Restoration Act of 1992 was signed by the first <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">President Bush</a> after it was passed by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a>. The project was projected to cost $350 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup> The act authorized the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Interior" title="United States Secretary of the Interior">Secretary of the Interior</a> to acquire and remove the two dams on the river and restore the ecosystem and native <a href="/wiki/Fish_migration" title="Fish migration">anadromous</a> fisheries.
</p><p>The removal of the 108-foot (33 m) tall <a href="/wiki/Elwha_Dam" title="Elwha Dam">Elwha Dam</a> and the 210-foot (64 m) tall <a href="/wiki/Glines_Canyon_Dam" title="Glines Canyon Dam">Glines Canyon Dam</a> began in September 2011. Two downstream water treatment facilities were completed in early 2010 to protect the water supply for the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Port_Angeles" title="Port Angeles">Port Angeles</a> and the fish hatcheries from silt and sediment that would wash downstream once the dams were removed. In order to protect fish stocks below the dams during removal, the dams were taken out over a three-year process, pausing to ensure there would be no silt in the river while salmon spawned downstream.
</p><p>The Elwha Dam was completely dismantled in March 2012. Restoration of the area around the dam followed, including tens of thousands of native plants started in local greenhouses. The removal of the Glines Canyon Dam was completed in August 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leach_estuary_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-leach_estuary-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwartz_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-schwartz-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Salmon will naturally recolonize the 70 miles (110 km) of habitat in <a href="/wiki/Olympic_National_Park" title="Olympic National Park">Olympic National Park</a>. The area once under the reservoirs is being revegetated to prevent erosion and speed up ecological restoration of the area. Because almost all of the Elwha's watershed is in a national park, the river should become relatively pristine, with few of the issues of agricultural runoff and water heating that affect other salmon river habitat in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>. Model projections by the Park Service show that up to 392,000 fish will fill 70 miles of habitat, theoretically matching the "pre-dam peak".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>By late December 2012, about 10 percent of the estimated 25,000,000 cubic yards (19,000,000 m<sup>3</sup>) of sediment that had been caught behind the river's two dams had collected at the Elwha's mouth, forming sandbars. With the Elwha Dam removed, the sediment had been pushed downstream as heavy rainfall produced faster-moving flows in the free-running river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schwartz_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-schwartz-10">[10]</a></sup> By November 2014, 30 percent of the stored sediment had been carried to the mouth of the river, creating 70 acres (28 hectares) of new estuary habitat for a wide variety of shellfish and other species.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leach_estuary_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-leach_estuary-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
