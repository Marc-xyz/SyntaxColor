<p>The <b>Federal Detention Center (FDC Philadelphia)</b> is a United States <a href="/wiki/Federal_prison" title="Federal prison">Federal prison</a> in <a href="/wiki/Center_City,_Philadelphia" title="Center City, Philadelphia">Center City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> which holds male and female inmates prior to or during court proceedings, as well as inmates serving brief sentences. It is operated by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bureau_of_Prisons" title="Federal Bureau of Prisons">Federal Bureau of Prisons</a>, a division of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The jail, across from the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_J._Green_Jr._Federal_Building&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William J. Green Jr. Federal Building (page does not exist)">William J. Green Jr. Federal Building</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavisNeeded_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-DavisNeeded-2">[2]</a></sup> is on a 1-acre (0.40 ha) site the southwest corner of Arch Street and 7th Street, across from the <a href="/wiki/African_American_Museum_in_Philadelphia" title="African American Museum in Philadelphia">African American Museum in Philadelphia</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Mall_(Philadelphia)" title="Independence Mall (Philadelphia)">Independence Mall</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HineWouldDet_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-HineWouldDet-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The jail is 12 stories tall. It has 628 cells for United States Marshal Service pre-trial inmates, primarily from the Eastern District of Pennsylvania, the District of New Jersey and the District of Delaware. Federal Detention Center Philadelphia is also a United States Parole Commission Revocation Site. 120 prisoners, already sentenced, serve as staff. The prison is connected to a tunnel that allows people to travel to and from the <a href="/wiki/James_A._Byrne_United_States_Courthouse" title="James A. Byrne United States Courthouse">James A. Byrne United States Courthouse</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slobodziandebut_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Slobodziandebut-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Detention_Center,_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The proposal to build the jail its current site, an at the time mostly vacant plot of land, was made public in February 1992.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HineWouldDet_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-HineWouldDet-3">[3]</a></sup> Spokespersons for the museum and the businesses at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=East_Market_Street&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="East Market Street (page does not exist)">East Market Street</a> expressed opposition to the proposal,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> as did businesses in <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Philadelphia" title="Chinatown, Philadelphia">Chinatown</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._House_of_Representatives" title="U.S. House of Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</a> member <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Foglietta" title="Thomas Foglietta">Thomas Foglietta</a>. The East Market Street businesses did not want prisoners close to their businesses. The African-American museum objected to a reminder of the incarceration of black men. Chinatown businesses stated that the prison may drive away customers and block expansion of Chinatown from the Vine Street Expressway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavisNeeded_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-DavisNeeded-2">[2]</a></sup> Edmund Bacon wrote in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="Philadelphia Inquirer">Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i> that he believed that this jail would damage efforts to revitalize portions of Center City.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>As of April 1993, prior to the opening of the detention center, there were 18 federal prisons throughout the U.S. that housed pretrial inmates awaiting proceedings in Philadelphia. FDC Philadelphia, scheduled to cost $85 million, was built so the pretrial federal inmates could be housed in Philadelphia itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The federal government pursued building the prison at its selected site, with the legal processes for condemning structures on the site and acquiring the site beginning in March 1995 and with groundbreaking at a former parking lot on the tract in January 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavisNeeded_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-DavisNeeded-2">[2]</a></sup> Its formal opening was scheduled for June 1, 2000. Ultimately its construction cost was $68 million. 120 prisoners whose sentences were about to end served as a work cadre from April 1 until the prison's opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slobodziandebut_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Slobodziandebut-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Mark Davis of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="Philadelphia Inquirer">Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i> wrote that the tunnel directly connecting the jail with the courthouse "appeared to appease nearly every critic" against the prison's construction, and that the prison did not visually appear like one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DavisNeeded_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-DavisNeeded-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facility">Facility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Detention_Center,_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Each 96-square-foot (8.9 m<sup>2</sup>) prison cell has slit windows, a bunk bed, a toilet, twin lockers, a writing table, a basin, and drains at the perimeters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slobodziandebut_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Slobodziandebut-4">[4]</a></sup> The prison includes a caged recreation area with basketball and handball facility. The prison has facilities for reheating meals meant to be served to prisoners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_incidents">Notable incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Detention_Center,_Philadelphia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 19, 2012, Richard Spisak, 35, pleaded guilty to engaging in a sexual act with a male prisoner over whom he had disciplinary authority while Spisak was a Senior Corrections Specialist at FDC Philadelphia in 2010. Spisak further admitted to threatening to set off his body alarm and falsely report that the inmate had attacked him if the inmate did not perform oral sex on him. The victim acquiesced after Spisak's threats. Two other inmates testified at Spisak's sentencing hearing that Spisak victimized them in a similar manner. Spisak was sentenced to 32 months in federal prison on June 28, 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
