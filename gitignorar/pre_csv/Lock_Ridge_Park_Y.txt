<p><b>Lock Ridge Park</b> is a park built around a historic <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> <a href="/wiki/Blast_furnace" title="Blast furnace">blast furnace</a> just outside <a href="/wiki/Alburtis,_Pennsylvania" title="Alburtis, Pennsylvania">Alburtis, Pennsylvania</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Lehigh_Valley" title="Lehigh Valley">Lehigh Valley</a> region of the state.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lock_Ridge_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The park preserves portions of the former Lock Ridge Iron Works. The first furnace at the site was built in 1868 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lock_Ridge_Iron_Company" title="Lock Ridge Iron Company">Lock Ridge Iron Company</a>, on the line of the <a href="/wiki/Catasauqua_and_Fogelsville_Railroad" title="Catasauqua and Fogelsville Railroad">Catasauqua and Fogelsville Railroad</a> near its junction with the <a href="/wiki/East_Pennsylvania_Railroad" title="East Pennsylvania Railroad">East Pennsylvania Railroad</a>. The company was taken over the next year by the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Iron_Company" title="Thomas Iron Company">Thomas Iron Company</a>, and a second furnace put in blast shortly thereafter. The iron industry in the <a href="/wiki/Lehigh_Valley" title="Lehigh Valley">Lehigh Valley</a> was then flourishing, and the Lock Ridge furnaces (designated No. 7 and No. 8 by Thomas Iron) were typical of the time. They used a <a href="/wiki/Hot_blast" title="Hot blast">hot blast</a> to burn <a href="/wiki/Anthracite" title="Anthracite">anthracite</a> fuel (rather than the hitherto common <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a>) a technology brought to the United States by the company's founder, <a href="/wiki/David_Thomas_(industrialist)" title="David Thomas (industrialist)">David Thomas</a>, in 1839. However, by the early 20th century, the furnaces were, despite sporadic improvements, technologically out-of-date,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and were reputedly the last <a href="/wiki/Anthracite_iron" title="Anthracite iron">anthracite iron</a> furnaces to operate in the United States. President B.F. Fackenthal, of Thomas Iron, recommended they be decommissioned upon his resignation in 1913.
</p><p>However, Fackenthal's successor, Ralph Sweetser, pursued the opposite course. No. 8 furnace stack was rebuilt in 1914, and No. 7 the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Concurrently, anthracite was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> as a fuel for the furnaces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, modernization was not enough to save the Lock Ridge furnaces, and iron was last made there in 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The furnaces were sold to William Butz, who dismantled most of the buildings for scrap iron and lumber. However, the masonry structures were allowed to remain, albeit in a decayed condition. The slag dumps on the property were crushed, screened, and used for roofing and fill by the Lehigh Slag Company until 1958. Little-used since the closing of the furnaces, the Catasauqua and Fogelsville's line through the site was torn up in the 1940s. With industrial operations on the property long complete, it was donated by the Butz family to <a href="/wiki/Lehigh_County,_Pennsylvania" title="Lehigh County, Pennsylvania">Lehigh County</a> for use as a park.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Park_Today">The Park Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lock_Ridge_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Park Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lock Ridge Park was opened in August 1976 as a 59.2 acres (24.0 ha) park. Today it is operated as a partnership between the County of Lehigh, which owns and maintains the site, and the Lehigh County Historical Society, which provides public tours of the furnace complex.  The park is made up of the remains of the old furnace complex, now the <b>Lock Ridge Furnace Museum</b>, and the land surrounding it. <a href="/wiki/Swabia_Creek" title="Swabia Creek">Swabia Creek</a>, which flows through the park grounds, offers limited fishing. The park also features a <a href="/wiki/Little_League_Baseball" title="Little League Baseball">Little League Baseball</a> field and a picnic pavilion with restrooms and a fireplace. The park also features a self-guided walking tour of the site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
