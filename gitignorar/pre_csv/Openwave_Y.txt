<p><b>Openwave</b> (formerly <b>software.com</b>, <b>phone.com</b>, and <b>Libris, Inc</b>) has changed its name to Unwired Planet, and two of its former products have launched as private companies; Openwave Mobility and Openwave Messaging. Openwave is historically significant in its introduction of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_Internet" title="Mobile Internet">Mobile Internet</a> and its expansion into a large successful mobile software supplier in the mobile telecom sector.
</p><p>Openwave pioneered <a href="/wiki/Handheld_Device_Markup_Language" title="Handheld Device Markup Language">HDML</a>, a precursor to <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Markup_Language" title="Wireless Markup Language">WML</a>. Openwave was a founding member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WAP_Forum" title="WAP Forum">WAP Forum</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Openwave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The company started in 1996 as Libris, Inc. and focused on developing mobile client software for "pull" services while the general mobile market was rapidly growing "push" services based on <a href="/wiki/SMS" title="SMS">SMS</a>.  In 1996, it changed its name to Unwired Planet, Inc. and launched its proprietary end-to-end mobile network solution<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (August 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup> for Internet access and web browsing, known as up.link (browser and network server/gateway).
</p><p>In 1999, with the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Wireless_Application_Protocol" title="Wireless Application Protocol">WAP</a> standards, it acquired Apiion, Ltd. of <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> (formerly Aldiscon Northern Ireland, Ltd.), changed its name to Phone.com and went public on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a>.  In 2000, amidst huge growth in revenue and stock price, Phone.com acquired several high tech <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Startup_companies" title="Startup companies">startup companies</a> with niche products to integrate across its end-to-end solution.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Use_plain_English#Buzzwords" title="Wikipedia:Use plain English"><span title="The material near this tag may use buzzwords designed solely to impress you or obscure meaning. (August 2019)">buzzword</span></a></i>]</sup>  In 2001, it merged with Software.com and changed the company name again to Openwave Systems, Inc.  With Software.com's large installed base of email servers at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISP" title="ISP">ISPs</a>, Openwave expanded by providing its mobile operator customers with software infrastructure for mobile email applications and other multimedia messaging (<a href="/wiki/Multimedia_Messaging_Service" title="Multimedia Messaging Service">MMS</a>) applications.
</p><p>The company's mobile <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbrowser" title="Microbrowser">browser</a> (written by Bruce Schwartz) software shipped on over one billion handsets,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> at one point approx 49% of the global browser-capable device shipments, over 70 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mobile_operator" title="Mobile operator">mobile operators</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opwv_about_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-opwv_about-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002 it acquired SignalSoft Corp., a developer of location-based services, who developed the first platforms to provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/E911" title="E911">E911</a> services as per the first FCC (<a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">Federal Communications Commission</a>) requirements.
</p><p>In 2004 Openwave acquired Nombas Inc., the developer of Cmm (a scripting language with C-like syntax)  which was later enhanced to support <a href="/wiki/ECMAScript" title="ECMAScript">ECMAScript</a> and renamed ScriptEase.
</p><p>In January 2006, Openwave closed the $120 million acquisition of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Musiwave" title="Musiwave">Musiwave</a>, a French music application services provider for mobile phones founded by <a href="/wiki/Gilles_Babinet" title="Gilles Babinet">Gilles Babinet</a>. In November 2007, the company sold Musiwave to <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> for $46 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In the same year, Openwave was alleged of violating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Securities_law" title="Securities law">securities laws</a> by issuing misleading financial statements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2008, Openwave launched a contextual advertising system for mobile phones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2008, the <i>San Francisco Business Journal</i> reported that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NASDAQ" title="NASDAQ">NASDAQ</a> had given Openwave a delisting warning for failing to properly report some financial results.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 30, 2008, Openwave issued a press release stating: "that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Purple_Labs" title="Purple Labs">Purple Labs</a> has acquired the Openwave mobile phone software business, which develops and markets its browser and messaging client technologies.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 18, 2010, Openwave announced: "that it licensed certain patents to Mobixell/724 Solutions Inc" as part of an intellectual property protection arrangement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 1, 2012, Openwave announced: "the completion of the sale of its Mediation and Messaging product businesses to Marlin Equity Partners." Openwave changed its name back to Unwired Planet. They are now exclusively focused on licensing and enforcement of IP that they claim is “foundational to mobile communications.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ip_reference_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-ip_reference-9">[9]</a></sup> In response to claims that the company has become a <a href="/wiki/Patent_troll" title="Patent troll">patent troll</a>, Unwired Planet's general counsel Noah Mesel states: "We happen to be at the point in our business cycle where what’s left is a <a href="/wiki/Patent_portfolio" title="Patent portfolio">patent portfolio</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ip_reference_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-ip_reference-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The former product businesses have also re-launched as two privately held companies, <a class="external text" href="http://owmobility.com" rel="nofollow">Openwave Mobility Inc.</a> and <a class="external text" href="http://owmessaging.com" rel="nofollow">Openwave Messaging Inc.</a>
</p><p>In 2013 Openwave Messaging acquired Critical Path.
</p><p>On 2 March 2016 Openwave Messaging was acquired by Synchronoss Technologies and the company started trading under Synchronoss Messaging. 
As of April 2016 Openwave Mobility continues to operate as a Marlin Equity Company.
</p>
