	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Garfield Building (New York City)</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>Garfield Building</b> was located at 26<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-united_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-united-1">[1]</a></sup> Court Street<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and Remsen Street<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remsen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-remsen-3">[3]</a></sup> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brooklyn,_New_York" title="Brooklyn, New York">Brooklyn, New York</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> It was built by contractor William Lamb, who was originally from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glasgow,_Scotland" title="Glasgow, Scotland">Glasgow, Scotland</a>. Together with
his brother Thomas, the builders began their careers with the firm W&amp;T Lamb, starting in 1861.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The seven-story Garfield Building was offered in an auction by the Brooklyn Real Estate Exchange in January 1906. The lot covered 100 feet by 137.6 feet. It was near <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Borough_Hall" title="Brooklyn Borough Hall">Borough Hall</a>, the court house, Temple Bar, Hall of Records, the first subway station, and the heart of the financial center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_edifice">History of edifice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garfield_Building_(New_York_City)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of edifice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the late 19th century the structure often served as a meeting place for railroad officials and the Brooklyn Republican Campaign Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-remsen_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-remsen-3">[3]</a></sup> Colonel Charles L. Fincke, of the 23rd Regiment of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Guard" title="United States National Guard">United States National Guard</a>, maintained an office at the Garfield Building. He was found unconscious there prior to resigning in April 1887.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-united_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-united-1">[1]</a></sup> It also was home to lawyers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Garfield Building was located in the rear of a building at 198 Montague Street, which burned following an explosion of unknown origin, on March 3, 1895. The conflagration occurred inside a four-story edifice which was occupied by a restaurant and cafe'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demolition">Demolition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Garfield_Building_(New_York_City)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demolition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Garfield building was razed after a period of building inactivity following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. City departments were moved to a new Municipal Building and the Court Remsen Building. The latter was completed on May 1, 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
