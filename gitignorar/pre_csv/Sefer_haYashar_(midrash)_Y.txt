<p>The <b>Sefer haYashar</b> (first edition 1552) is a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Midrash" title="Midrash">midrash</a></i> also known as the <b>Toledot Adam</b> and <b>Dibre ha-Yamim be-'Aruk</b>. The Hebrew title may be translated <i>Sefer haYashar</i> (ספר הישר) - "Book of the Correct Record" - but it is known in English translation mostly as <i><b>The Book of Jasher</b></i> following English tradition.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_books_of_the_same_name">Other books of the same name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Other books of the same name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book is named after the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sefer_HaYashar_(Biblical_references)" title="Sefer HaYashar (Biblical references)">Book of Jasher</a> mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Joshua" title="Book of Joshua">Joshua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Books_of_Samuel" title="Books of Samuel">2 Samuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although it is presented as the original "Book of Jasher" in translations such as that of Moses Samuel (1840), it is not accepted as such in rabbinical Judaism, nor does the original Hebrew text make such a claim.  It should not be confused with the very different <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Jasher_(Pseudo-Jasher)" title="Book of Jasher (Pseudo-Jasher)">Book of Jasher (Pseudo-Jasher)</a></i> printed by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Ilive" title="Jacob Ilive">Jacob Ilive</a> in 1751, which was purported to have been translated by the English monk <a href="/wiki/Alcuin" title="Alcuin">Alcuin</a>. It should also not be confused with an ethical text by the same name, which, according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclopaedia_Judaica" title="Encyclopaedia Judaica">Encyclopaedia Judaica</a></i>, Volume 14, p. 1099, was "probably written in the 13th century."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The book covers biblical history from the creation of <a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Adam and Eve</a> until a summary of the initial <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israelite" title="Israelite">Israelite</a> conquest of <a href="/wiki/Canaan" title="Canaan">Canaan</a> in the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Judges" title="Book of Judges">book of Judges</a>. It contains references that fit those cited in the Biblical texts, both the reference about the sun and moon found in Joshua, and also the reference in 2 Samuel (in the Hebrew but not in the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a>) to teaching the Sons of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judah_(biblical_figure)" title="Judah (biblical figure)">Judah</a> to fight with the bow. This appears in Jasher 56:9 among the last words of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a> to his son Judah:
</p>
<dl><dd>Only teach thy sons the bow and all weapons of war, in order that they may fight the battles of their brother who will rule over his enemies. (MCR)</dd></dl>
<p>But the book in its entirety cannot be so old - as shown by chapter 10, covering the descendants of <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a>, but containing <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> names for territories and countries, most obviously <i>Franza</i> for France and <i>Lumbardi</i> in <i><a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italia</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardy</a>. The text of this chapter closely follows the beginning of <i><a href="/wiki/Josippon" title="Josippon">Josippon</a></i>, a tenth-century rabbinic text that lists the various peoples living in Europe in ca. 950.
</p><p>Most of its extra-Biblical accounts are found in nearly the same form in other medieval compilations, or in the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>, other <i>midrash</i> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> sources. For example, it includes the common tale that <a href="/wiki/Lamech_(descendant_of_Cain)" title="Lamech (descendant of Cain)">Lamech</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Jabal_(Bible)" title="Jabal (Bible)">Jabal</a> accidentally killed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cain" title="Cain">Cain</a>, thus requiting Cain's wickedness for slaying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abel" title="Abel">Abel</a>.
</p><p>There are five discrepancies when comparing it with chapter 5 of Genesis: When the Sefer relates that a son of Seth died "in the eighty-fourth year of the life of Noah", it calls that son Enoch instead of Enosh. Enoch actually was Jared's son. Other than the confusion of the names, the date agrees with Genesis. The Sefer also relates that Jared died in the as "336th year of the life Noah" (instead of the "336th year", as in Genesis) and that Lamech died in the "195th year of the life of Noah (instead of the 595th year). It also gives different lifespans for Lamech (770 instead of 777) and Methuselah (960 instead of 969).
</p><p>In its genealogy of Abram (7:19), it makes no mention of the <a href="/wiki/Cainan" title="Cainan">Cainan</a> between Arpachsad and Shelah, in congruence with the Masoretic Text and Samaritan Pentateuch, but in conflict with the Septuagint (LXX) and Luke's genealogy in chapter 3 of his Gospel.
</p><p>In its highly interpolated account of the God's testing of Abraham concerning Isaac, it says in 23:50-51: 'And when they were going along Isaac said to his father, Behold, I see here the fire and wood, and where then is the lamb that is to be the burnt offering before the Lord? And Abraham answered his son Isaac, saying, The Lord has made choice of thee my son, to be a perfect burnt offering instead of the lamb.', this conflicts with the biblical account which says Abraham's response was this: 'My son, God will provide himself a lamb for a burnt offering'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scholars have proposed various dates between the 9th and 16th century for its composition.
</p><p>The earliest extant version of this Hebrew <i>midrash</i> was printed in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1625, and the introduction refers to an earlier 1552 edition in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, of which neither trace nor other mention has been found. The printer Yosèf ben Samuel claimed the work was copied by a scribe named Jacob the son of Atyah, from an ancient manuscript whose letters could hardly be made out.
</p><p>The Venice 1625 text was heavily criticised as a forgery by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Modena" title="Leon Modena">Leon Modena</a>, as part of his criticisms of the <i><a href="/wiki/Zohar" title="Zohar">Zohar</a></i> as a forgery, and of <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalah</a> in general. Modena was a member of the Venetian rabbinate that supervised the Hebrew press in Venice, and Modena prevented the printers from identifying <i>Sefer ha-Yashar</i> with the Biblical lost book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Behold, it [the Zohar] is like Sefer ha-Yashar, which they printed (without my knowledge and without the knowledge of the sages here in Venice, about twenty years ago). Although I removed the fantasies and falsehoods from it, [e.g.,] that it is the Sefer ha-Yashar mentioned in Scripture, there are still those who claim that it was discovered during the time of the destruction [of the temple]. But who can stop those who imagine in their minds whatever they wish.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Leon Modena, <i>Ari Nohem</i>, before 1648<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Despite Modena's intervention, the preface to the 1625 version still claims that its original source book came from the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> in AD 70, where a <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> officer named Sidrus allegedly discovered a Hebrew scholar hiding in a hidden library. The officer Sidrus reportedly took the scholar and all the books safely back to his estates in <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">Seville</a>, Spain (in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispalis" title="Hispalis">Hispalis</a>, the provincial capital of <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Baetica" title="Hispania Baetica">Hispania Baetica</a>). The 1625 edition then claims that at some uncertain point in the history of <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">Islamic Spain</a>, the manuscript was transferred or sold to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">Jewish</a> college in <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Córdoba, Spain">Cordova</a>. The 1625 edition further claims that scholars preserved the book until its printings in <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a> in 1552 and in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> in 1625. Apart from the preface to the 1625 work, there is no evidence to support any of this story. The work was used extensively, but not especially more than many other sources, in <a href="/wiki/Louis_Ginzberg" title="Louis Ginzberg">Louis Ginzberg</a>'s <i>Legends of the Jews</i>.
</p><p>Although there remains doubt about whether the 1552 "edition" in Naples was ever truly printed, the study of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Dan" title="Joseph Dan">Joseph Dan</a>, professor of Kabbalah at the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_University_of_Jerusalem" title="Hebrew University of Jerusalem">Hebrew University of Jerusalem</a>, in the preface to his 1986 critical edition of the 1625 text<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> concludes, from the Hebrew used and other indicators, that the work was in fact written in Naples in the early 16th century. The Arabic connections suggest that if the preface to the 1625 version is an "exaggeration", it was then probably written by a Jew who lived in Spain or southern Italy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations">Translations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Translations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Johann_Abicht.27s_Latin_translation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Johann_Abicht's_Latin_translation">Johann Abicht's Latin translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Johann Abicht's Latin translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Johann_Georg_Abicht" title="Johann Georg Abicht">Johann Georg Abicht</a>, professor of theology at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Halle-Wittenberg" title="University of Halle-Wittenberg">University of Halle-Wittenberg</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> translated the 1625 text into Latin as <i>Dissertatio de Libro recti</i> (Leipzig, 1732).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thomas_Ilive_English_translation">Thomas Ilive English translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Thomas Ilive English translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1750 the London printer <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Ilive" title="Jacob Ilive">Jacob Ilive</a> published book with the same name, claiming that this is the real "Book of Jasher" mentioned in the Bible and that the translation was done by <a href="/wiki/Alcuin" title="Alcuin">Alcuin</a>, who had found the original manuscript. This book described the same period as midrsah "Sefer haYashar", but the contents was different. Reprint publication of 1829 became known as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-Jasher" title="Pseudo-Jasher">Pseudo-Jasher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Encyclopedia" title="Jewish Encyclopedia">Jewish Encyclopedia</a> links the publishing to Thomas Ilive<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Moses_Samuel.27s_English_translation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Moses_Samuel's_English_translation">Moses Samuel's English translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Moses Samuel's English translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first translation of the 1625 Venice edition into English was that published by <a href="/wiki/Mordecai_Manuel_Noah" title="Mordecai Manuel Noah">Mordecai Manuel Noah</a> and A. S. Gould in 1840. Mordecai Noah was a prominent Jewish newspaper editor and publisher, as well as playwright, diplomat, journalist, and utopian. The translator of the 1840 edition was not published, but indicated as an eminent Jewish scholar in Britain in the comments of one of the four certificating Hebraist scholars to the publisher in the preface to the 2nd editions:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To Mssrs Noah and Gould. Gentlemen - I am acquainted with the 'Book of Jasher,' having read a considerable part of it while in the hands of the translator in England. The Hebrew is very purely written, and the translator is an eminent scholar.</p><div class="templatequotecite">— <cite>Rabbi H. V. Nathan, Kingston Synagogue, Jamaica, April 14, 1840</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>Subsequently, the translator identified himself as <a href="/wiki/Moses_Samuel" title="Moses Samuel">Moses Samuel</a> of Liverpool (1795–1860), who obtained a copy of the 1625 Hebrew edition and became convinced that the core of this work truly was the self-same <i>Book of the Upright</i> referenced in Hebrew scriptures. He translated it into English, and in 1839 sold it to <a href="/wiki/Mordecai_Manuel_Noah" title="Mordecai Manuel Noah">Mordecai Manuel Noah</a>. Samuel gave the reason his name did not appear on the translation thus: "I did not put my name to it as my Patron and myself differed about its authenticity" — the NYC publisher Noah having had a lower opinion of the work's authenticity than Samuel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Samuel had in fact originally tried to persuade The Royal Asiatic Society at Calcutta to publish the work, a fact alluded to obliquely in the preface to Noah's 1840 edition, but eventually Samuel sold the work to Noah for £150. Even so, Noah in his promotional materials did enthusiastically claim that the historian <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a> had said of the <i>Book of Jasher</i>: "by this book are to be understood certain records kept in some safe place on purpose, giving an account of what happened among the Hebrews from year to year, and called Jasher or the upright, on account of the fidelity of the annals." No such statement is found in Josephus' works. Noah's 1840 preface contained endorsements by Hebrew scholars of the day, all of whom praised the quality of the translation, but these said nothing to indicate they believed it to be the work referred to in Joshua and 2 Samuel. In fact one of them, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_H._Turner" title="Samuel H. Turner">Samuel H. Turner</a> (1790–1861), of the General Theological Seminary, NYC, referred to the "Rabbinical writer" in this way: "The work itself is evidently composed in the purest Rabbinical Hebrew, with a large intermixture of the Biblical idiom, ..." indicating that Turner was not of the opinion that it was an ancient text.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edward_B.M._Browne_English_translation">Edward B.M. Browne English translation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Edward B.M. Browne English translation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another translation of this book exist, created by Reform rabbi and editor, Dr. Edward B.M. Browne, known as “Alphabet” Browne, and published in New York in 1876.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Acceptance_by_Latter-day_Saints">Acceptance by Latter-day Saints</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sefer_haYashar_(midrash)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Acceptance by Latter-day Saints">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith" title="Joseph Smith">Joseph Smith</a>, founder of the <a href="/wiki/Latter_Day_Saint_movement" title="Latter Day Saint movement">Latter Day Saint movement</a>, acquired a copy in 1841 or 1842 and wrote in the September 1, 1842 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Times_and_Seasons" title="Times and Seasons">Times and Seasons</a></i>, in reference to the patriarch <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>: "the book of Jasher, which has not been disproved as a bad author, says he was cast into the fire of the Chaldeans".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1886, Joseph Hyrum Parry of <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> acquired the rights to the translation from Mordecai Noah's estate. It was published by J. H. Parry &amp; Company in <a href="/wiki/Salt_Lake_City" title="Salt Lake City">Salt Lake City</a> in 1887.
</p><p>A number of <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormon</a> scholars<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> consider this Book of Jasher to be of authentic ancient Hebrew origin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Some of these scholars suggest that the book likely contains many original portions of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sefer_HaYashar_(Biblical_references)" title="Sefer HaYashar (Biblical references)">Sefer HaYashar</a> referenced in the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> but also has a number of added interpolations. This Joseph Hyrum Parry edition of the Book of Jasher continues to be held in high repute by many <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormons</a>. A number of Mormons<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2011)">who?</span></a></i>]</sup> have pointed to certain portions of the book that have commonalities to parts of the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Smith_Translation_of_the_Bible" title="Joseph Smith Translation of the Bible">Joseph Smith Translation of the Bible</a>, particularly those parts dealing with the <a href="/wiki/Antediluvian" title="Antediluvian">antediluvian</a> period. The Bible has only scant information about pre-flood times, but both the Book of Jasher and parts of the Joseph Smith Translation of the Bible contain additional information, some of which is strikingly similar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The LDS Church does not officially endorse this Book of Jasher.
</p>
