<p><b>Cyprenorphine</b> (<b>M-285</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Opioid" title="Opioid">opioid</a> <a href="/wiki/Drug" title="Drug">drug</a>. It is related to more well-known opioids such as <a href="/wiki/Buprenorphine" title="Buprenorphine">buprenorphine</a>, which is used as an <a href="/wiki/Analgesic" title="Analgesic">analgesic</a> and for the treatment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opioid_addiction" title="Opioid addiction">opioid addiction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Diprenorphine" title="Diprenorphine">diprenorphine</a>, which is used as an <a href="/wiki/Antidote" title="Antidote">antidote</a> to reverse the effects of other opioids.
</p><p>Cyprenorphine has mixed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agonist%E2%80%93antagonist" title="Agonist–antagonist">agonist–antagonist</a> effects at opioid receptors, like those of buprenorphine. However the effects of cyprenorphine are somewhat different, as it produces pronounced <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dysphoric" title="Dysphoric">dysphoric</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hallucinogenic" title="Hallucinogenic">hallucinogenic</a> effects which limit its potential use as an analgesic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cyprenorphine also has been shown to suppress the intake of sweet solution <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but doesn't suppress the increase in food consumption that's produced by the alpha-2-adrenoceptor antagonist idazoxan. Idazoxan may lead to the release of endogenous opioid peptides and increase food intake, this effect is attenuated by (-)-naloxone but not by the mu/delta-antagonist cyprenorphine. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
