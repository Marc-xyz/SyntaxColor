	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Middle years of Rabindranath Tagore</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p> 
The <b>middle years of <a href="/wiki/Rabindranath_Tagore" title="Rabindranath Tagore">Rabindranath Tagore</a></b> were spent primarily in Santiniketan, although they included extensive travels throughout Asia, Europe, and Japan.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Santiniketan">Santiniketan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_years_of_Rabindranath_Tagore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Santiniketan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1901, Tagore left Shelaidaha and moved to <a href="/wiki/Shantiniketan" title="Shantiniketan">Shantiniketan</a>, about one hundred miles to Calcutta's northwest in what is now <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a>. Shantiniketan, a spread of relatively arid and eroded red soil of seven acres bought in the 1860s by Debendranth, was made the home of Tagore's new ashram, a marble-floored prayer hall ("The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mandir" title="Mandir">Mandir</a>"), experimental school, groves of trees, gardens, and a library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_133_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_133-1">[1]</a></sup> Unfortunately, his wife along with two of his children, Renuka (in 1903) and Samindranath (in 1907), died in this period, leaving Tagore distraught. When Tagore's father, aged 87, also died on 19 January 1905, Tagore began receiving 1,250–1,500 <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_rupee#British_India_issues" title="History of the rupee">rupees</a> (Rs.) monthly as an inheritance. This combined with income from the Maharaja of <a href="/wiki/Tripura" title="Tripura">Tripura</a>, sales of jewelry owned by him and his late wife, his bungalow at the seaside <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puri,_Orissa" title="Puri, Orissa">Puri</a>, and mediocre royalties (Rs. 2,000) gleaned from the licensed publishing of thousands of copies of his works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_139-140_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_139-140-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Thus he gained a large following among Bengali readers. He published such works as <i>Naivedya</i> (1901) and <i>Kheya</i> (1906). Non-Bangla translations were also published, but these were frequently of mediocre quality. In response to requests by admirers (including painter <a href="/wiki/William_Rothenstein" title="William Rothenstein">William Rothenstein</a>), Tagore began translating his poems into free verse.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Travels">Travels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_years_of_Rabindranath_Tagore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Travels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1913, he went to England while carrying a sheaf of his translated works. At readings there, these works impressed a number of Englishmen, including English missionary and Gandhi protégé Charles F. Andrews, Anglo-Irish poet <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats">William Butler Yeats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bridges" title="Robert Bridges">Robert Bridges</a>, Ernest Rhys, and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Sturge_Moore" title="Thomas Sturge Moore">Thomas Sturge Moore</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_179_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_179-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_178_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_178-4">[4]</a></sup> Indeed, Yeats later wrote the preface to the English translation of <i><a href="/wiki/Gitanjali" title="Gitanjali">Gitanjali</a></i> (published by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/India_Society" title="India Society">India Society</a>), while Andrews joined Tagore in India to work with him. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:167px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tagore-THU.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="550" decoding="async" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Tagore-THU.jpg/165px-Tagore-THU.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Tagore-THU.jpg/248px-Tagore-THU.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Tagore-THU.jpg/330px-Tagore-THU.jpg 2x" width="165"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tagore-THU.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tagore (center, at right) visits with academics at <a href="/wiki/Tsinghua_University" title="Tsinghua University">Tsinghua University</a> (清華大學) during an extended journey to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> in 1924. (泰戈尔在清华大学讲学).</div></div></div>
<p>On 10 November 1912, Tagore traveled to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, speaking at a <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarian</a> church in <a href="/wiki/Urbana,_Illinois" title="Urbana, Illinois">Urbana, Illinois</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TFC_2006_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TFC_2006-5">[5]</a></sup> In that year, Tagore also toured the United Kingdom, meeting <a href="/wiki/William_Rothenstein" title="William Rothenstein">William Rothenstein</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Butler_Yeats" title="William Butler Yeats">William Butler Yeats</a>, who read his <i>Gitanjali</i>. Later, he stayed in <a href="/wiki/Butterton" title="Butterton">Butterton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a> with C.F. Andrews' clergymen friends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakravarty_1961_1-2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chakravarty_1961_1-2-6">[6]</a></sup> On 14 November 1913, he received word that he had won the Nobel Prize in Literature; the award stemmed from the idealistic and accessible (for Western readers) nature of a small body of translated material, including the 1912 <i><a href="/wiki/Gitanjali" title="Gitanjali">Gitanjali: Song Offerings</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hjarne_1913_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hjarne_1913-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Together with Mukul Dey, Charles F. Andrews and W. W. Pearson, Tagore again set off by boat on 3 May 1916, embarking on a lecturing circuit of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> that was to last until April 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_206_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_206-8">[8]</a></sup> During a four-month layover in Japan, Tagore authored "On the Way to Japan" and "In Japan", which were later compiled into the book <i>Japanyatri</i> ("A Sojourn to Japan"),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakravarty_1961_2_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chakravarty_1961_2-9">[9]</a></sup> which detailed his admiration for the Japanese aesthetic. Yet Tagore also denounced nationalism, particularly that of the Japanese and Americans. He wrote the essay "Nationalism in India", attracting both derision and praise (the latter from pacifists and fellow internationalists like <a href="/wiki/Romain_Rolland" title="Romain Rolland">Romain Rolland</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakravarty_1961_182_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chakravarty_1961_182-10">[10]</a></sup> Yet these views also endangered him: during his stay in a <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> hotel, Tagore narrowly escaped being assassinated by a pair of Indian expatriates — the plot failed only because the would-be assassins fell into argument concerning whether they should carry through with the murder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_204_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_204-11">[11]</a></sup> The following morning, Tagore left for <a href="/wiki/Santa_Barbara,_California" title="Santa Barbara, California">Santa Barbara</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_204-205_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_204-205-12">[12]</a></sup> There, Tagore meditated among orange groves and conceived of a new type of university, desiring to "make Shantiniketan the connecting thread between India and the world ... [and] a world center for the study of humanity ... somewhere beyond the limits of nation and geography."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_204_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_204-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visva-Bharati_University">Visva-Bharati University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_years_of_Rabindranath_Tagore&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Visva-Bharati University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The foundation stone of the school, which he named <a href="/wiki/Visva-Bharati_University" title="Visva-Bharati University">Visva-Bharati</a><sup class="reference" id="ref_Visva-Bharatinone"><a href="#endnote_Visva-Bharatinone">[θ]</a></sup>, was ceremonially laid on 22 December 1918 and the school was later inaugurated on 22 December 1921.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_220_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_220-13">[13]</a></sup> Tagore's duties as steward and mentor at Santiniketan kept him busy; he taught classes in mornings and wrote textbooks for his students in afternoons and evenings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakravarty_1961_27_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chakravarty_1961_27-14">[14]</a></sup> Of this routine, he wrote that "I long to discover some fairyland of holidays ... where all duties look delightfully undutiful, like clouds bearing rain appearing perfectly inconsequential".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chakravarty_1961_27_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Chakravarty_1961_27-14">[14]</a></sup> Tagore was also occupied with fundraising between 1919 and 1921, undertaking trips to Europe and the U.r<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutta_1995_221_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutta_1995_221-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
