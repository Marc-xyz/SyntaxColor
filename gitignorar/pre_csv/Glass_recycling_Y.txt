<p><b>Glass recycling</b> is the processing of waste glass into usable products. Glass that is crushed and ready to be remelted is called <b>cullet</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There are two types of cullet: internal and external. <b>Internal cullet</b> is composed of defective products detected and rejected by a quality control process during the industrial process of glass manufacturing, transition phases of product changes (such as thickness and colour changes) and production offcuts. <b>External cullet</b> is waste glass that has been collected or reprocessed with the purpose of recycling. External cullet (which can be pre- or post-consumer) is classified as waste. The word "cullet", when used in the context of end-of-waste, will always refer to external cullet.
</p><p>To be recycled, glass waste needs to be purified and cleaned of contamination. Then, depending on the end use and local processing capabilities, it might also have to be separated into different colors. Many recyclers collect different colors of glass separately since glass retains its color after <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>. The most common colours used for consumer containers are clear (flint) glass, green glass, and brown (amber) glass. Glass is ideal for recycling since none of the material is degraded by normal use.
</p><p>Many collection points have separate bins for clear (flint), green and brown (amber). Glass re-processors intending to make new glass containers require separation by color, because glass tends to retain its color after recycling. If the recycled glass is not going to be made into more glass, or if the glass re-processor uses newer optical sorting equipment, separation by color at the collection point may not be required. Heat-resistant glass, such as <a href="/wiki/Pyrex" title="Pyrex">Pyrex</a> or <a href="/wiki/Borosilicate_glass" title="Borosilicate glass">borosilicate glass</a>, must not be part of the glass recycling stream, because even a small piece of such material will alter the viscosity of the fluid in the <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnace</a> at remelt.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnn_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-mnn-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Processing_of_external_cullet">Processing of external cullet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Processing of external cullet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To be able to use external cullet in production, any contaminants should be removed as much as possible. Typical contaminations are:
</p>
<ul><li>Organics: Paper, plastics, caps, rings, PVB foils for flat glass</li>
<li>Inorganics: Stones, ceramics, porcelains</li>
<li>Metals: Ferrous and non-ferrous metals</li>
<li>Heat resistant and lead glass</li></ul>
<p>Manpower or machinery can be used in different stages of purification. Since they melt at higher temperatures than glass, separation of inorganics, the removal of heat resistant glass and lead glass is critical. In the modern recycling facilities, dryer systems and <a href="/wiki/Optical_sorting" title="Optical sorting">optical sorting machines</a> are used. The input material should be sized and cleaned for the highest efficiency in automatic sorting. More than one free fall or conveyor belt sorter can be used, depending on the requirements of the process. Different colors can be sorted by optical sorting machines.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling_into_glass_containers">Recycling into glass containers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Recycling into glass containers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="100" decoding="async" height="240" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg/240px-Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg/360px-Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg/480px-Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg.png 2x" width="240"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tidyman-glass-recycling.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A variant of the "Tidyman" symbol, intended to encourage people to recycle glass</div></div></div>
<p>Glass bottles and jars are infinitely recyclable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The use of recycled glass in manufacturing conserves raw materials and reduces energy consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpi.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-gpi.org-4">[4]</a></sup> Because the chemical energy required to melt the raw materials has already been expended, the use of cullet can significantly reduce energy consumption compared with manufacturing new glass from silica (SiO2), soda ash (Na2CO3), and lime (CaCO3). Soda lime glass from virgin raw materials theoretically requires approximately 2.671 GJ/tonne compared to 1.886 GJ/tonne to melt 100% glass cullet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> As a general rule, every 10% increase in cullet usage results in an energy savings of 2–3% in the melting process, with a theoretical maximum potential of 30% energy saving.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">[5]</a></sup> Every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metric_ton" title="Metric ton">metric ton</a> (1,000 kg) of waste glass recycled into new items saves 315 kilograms (694 lb) of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from being released into the atmosphere during the manufacture of new glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recycling_into_other_products">Recycling into other products</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Recycling into other products">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of the recycled glass as aggregate in <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> has become popular, with large-scale research on that application being carried out at Columbia University in New York. Recycled glass greatly enhances the aesthetic appeal of the concrete. Recent research has shown that concrete made with recycled glass aggregates have better long-term strength and better thermal insulation, due to the thermal properties of the glass aggregates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Glass which is not recycled, but crushed, reduces the volume of waste sent to <a href="/wiki/Landfill" title="Landfill">landfill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mnn_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-mnn-2">[2]</a></sup> Waste glass may also be kept out of landfill by using it for roadbed aggregate or landfill cover.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpi.org_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-gpi.org-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Glass <a href="/wiki/Construction_aggregate" title="Construction aggregate">aggregate</a>, a mix of colors crushed to a small size, is substituted for pea gravel or crushed rock in many construction and utility projects, saving municipalities, such as the City of <a href="/wiki/Tumwater,_Washington" title="Tumwater, Washington">Tumwater, Washington</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Works" title="Public Works">Public Works</a>, thousands of dollars (depending on the size of the project). Glass aggregate is not sharp to handle. In many cases, the state Department of Transportation has specifications for use, size and percentage of quantity for use.  Common applications are as pipe bedding—placed around sewer, storm water or drinking water pipes, to transfer weight from the surface and protect the pipe.  Another common use is as fill to bring the level of a concrete floor even with a foundation.
</p><p>Other uses for recycled glass include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiberglass_insulation" title="Fiberglass insulation">Fiberglass insulation</a> products</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">Ceramic</a> <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sanitary_ware" title="wikt:sanitary ware">sanitary ware</a> production</li>
<li>As a <a href="/wiki/Ceramic_flux" title="Ceramic flux">flux</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">brick manufacture</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astroturf" title="Astroturf">Astroturf</a></li>
<li>Agriculture and landscape applications, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Top_dressing" title="Top dressing">top dressing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Root" title="Root">root zone</a> material or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bunker_(golf)" title="Bunker (golf)">golf bunker</a> sand</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Recycled_glass_countertops" title="Recycled glass countertops">Recycled glass countertops</a></li>
<li>As <a href="/wiki/Sand_filter" title="Sand filter">water filtration media</a></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abrasives" title="Abrasives">Abrasives</a></li></ul>
<p>Mixed waste streams may be collected from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Materials_recovery_facilities" title="Materials recovery facilities">materials recovery facilities</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_biological_treatment" title="Mechanical biological treatment">mechanical biological treatment</a> systems. Some facilities can sort mixed waste streams into different colours using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electro-optical_sorting" title="Electro-optical sorting">electro-optical sorting</a> units.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_country">By country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: By country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2019, many Australian cities after decades of poor planning and minimum investment are winding back their glass recycling programmes in favour of plastic usage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>For many years, there was only one state in Australia with a return deposit scheme on glass containers. Other states had unsuccessfully tried to lobby for glass deposit schemes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> More recently this situation has changed dramatically, with the original scheme in South Australia now joined by <a href="/wiki/Container_deposit_legislation_in_Australia" title="Container deposit legislation in Australia">legislated container deposit schemes</a> in New South Wales,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Queensland,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Australian Capital Territory,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and the Northern Territory, with schemes planned in Western Australia (2020) and Tasmania (2022). Victoria is the only state not yet planning to introduce such a scheme.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2004, Germany recycled 2.116 million tons of glass. Reusable glass or plastic (PET) bottles are available for many drinks, especially beer and carbonated water as well as soft drinks (<i>Mehrwegflaschen</i>). The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Container_deposit_legislation" title="Container deposit legislation">deposit per bottle</a> (<i>Pfand</i>) is €0.08-€0.15, compared to €0.25 for recyclable but not reusable plastic bottles. There is no deposit for glass bottles which do not get refilled.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Altglasleerung.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Altglasleerung.JPG/220px-Altglasleerung.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Altglasleerung.JPG/330px-Altglasleerung.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Altglasleerung.JPG/440px-Altglasleerung.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Altglasleerung.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vehicle emptying a glass recycling container in Vienna</div></div></div>
<p>Glass collection points, known as <i>bottle banks</i> are very common near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shopping_centre" title="Shopping centre">shopping centres</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Civic_amenity_site" title="Civic amenity site">civic amenity sites</a> and in local neighborhoods in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The first bottle bank was introduced by Stanley Race <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CBE" title="CBE">CBE</a>, then president of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=British_Glass&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="British Glass (page does not exist)">Glass Manufacturers' Federation</a> and Ron England in <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a> on 6 June 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Development work was done by the DoE at Warren Springs laboratory, Stevenage, (now AERA at Harwell) and Nazeing Glass Works, <a href="/wiki/Broxbourne" title="Broxbourne">Broxbourne</a> to prove if a usable glass product could be made from over 90% recycled glass. It was found necessary to use magnets to remove unwanted metal closures in the mixture.
</p><p>Bottle banks commonly stand beside collection points for other recyclable waste like <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plastics</a>. Local, municipal <a href="/wiki/Waste_collector" title="Waste collector">waste collectors</a> usually have one central point for all types of waste in which large glass containers are located. There are now over 50,000 bottle banks in the United Kingdom,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK-15">[15]</a></sup> and 752,000 tons of glass are now recycled annually.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-UK-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>The waste recycling industry in the UK cannot consume all of the recycled container glass that will become available over the coming years, mainly due to the colour imbalance between that which is manufactured and that which is consumed. The UK imports much more green glass in the form of <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a> bottles than it uses, leading to a surplus amount for recycling.
</p><p>The resulting surplus of green glass from imported bottles may be exported to producing countries, or used locally in the growing diversity of secondary end uses for recycled glass.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> As of 2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Cory_Environmental" title="Cory Environmental">Cory Environmental</a> were shipping glass cullet from the UK to <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_of_America">United States of America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_recycling&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States of America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rates of recycling and methods of waste collection vary substantially across the United States because laws are written on the state or local level and large municipalities often have their own unique systems. Many cities do <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curbside_recycling" title="Curbside recycling">curbside recycling</a>, meaning they collect household recyclable waste on a weekly or bi-weekly basis that residents set out in special containers in front of their homes and transported to a <a href="/wiki/Materials_recovery_facility" title="Materials recovery facility">materials recovery facility</a>. This is typically <a href="/wiki/Single-stream_recycling" title="Single-stream recycling">single-stream recycling</a>, which creates an impure product and partly explains why, as of 2019, the US has a recycling rate of around 33% versus 90% in some European countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>Apartment dwellers usually use shared containers that may be collected by the city or by private recycling companies which can have their own recycling rules.  In some cases, glass is specifically separated into its own container because broken glass is a hazard to the people who later manually sort the co-mingled recyclables.  Sorted recyclables are later sold to companies to be used in the manufacture of new products.
</p><p>In 1971, the <a href="/wiki/Oregon_Bottle_Bill" title="Oregon Bottle Bill">state of Oregon passed a law</a> requiring buyers of carbonated beverages (such as beer and soda) to pay five cents per container (increased to ten cents in April 2017) as a deposit which would be refunded to anyone who returned the container for recycling. This law has since been copied in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bottle_Bill" title="Bottle Bill">nine other states</a> including New York and California. The abbreviations of states with deposit laws are printed on all qualifying bottles and cans. In states with these container deposit laws, most supermarkets automate the deposit refund process by providing <a href="/wiki/Reverse_vending_machine" title="Reverse vending machine">machines</a> which will count containers as they are inserted and then print credit vouchers that can be redeemed at the store for the number of containers returned. Small glass bottles (mostly beer) are broken, one-by-one, inside these deposit refund machines as the bottles are inserted. A large, wheeled hopper (very roughly 1.5 m by 1.5 m by 0.5 m) inside the machine collects the broken glass until it can be emptied by an employee. Nationwide bottle refunds recover 80% of glass containers that require a deposit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gpi.org_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-gpi.org-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Major companies in the space include Strategic Materials, which purchases post-consumer glass for 47 facilities across the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Strategic Materials has worked to correct misconceptions about glass recycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Glass manufacturers such as <a href="/wiki/Owens-Illinois" title="Owens-Illinois">Owens-Illinois</a> ultimately include recycled glass in their product. The Glass Recycling Coalition is a group of companies and stakeholders working to improve glass recycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
