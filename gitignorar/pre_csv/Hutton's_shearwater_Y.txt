<p><b>Hutton's shearwater</b> (<i>Puffinus huttoni</i>) or <b>kaikoura tītī</b> is a medium-sized ocean-going <a href="/wiki/Seabird" title="Seabird">seabird</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Procellariidae" title="Procellariidae">Procellariidae</a>. Its range is Australian and New Zealand waters, but it breeds only in mainland New Zealand, in just two remaining alpine colonies in the <a href="/wiki/Kaikoura_Ranges" title="Kaikoura Ranges">Seaward Kaikoura Range</a>. Because six other colonies have been wiped out by introduced pigs, a protected artificial colony has been established near the town of <a href="/wiki/Kaikoura" title="Kaikoura">Kaikoura</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hutton%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The bird's name commemorates <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Wollaston_Hutton" title="Frederick Wollaston Hutton">Frederick Wollaston Hutton</a>, a former curator of the <a href="/wiki/Canterbury_Museum,_Christchurch" title="Canterbury Museum, Christchurch">Canterbury Museum</a> in Christchurch, New Zealand. A medium-sized (350 g) seabird,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> with a 75 cm wingspan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> it is brown with a white underbelly and brown collar, dark borders to the underwing, dark grey bill, and pinkish dark-webbed feet; it can be distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Fluttering_shearwater" title="Fluttering shearwater">fluttering shearwater</a> by its dark grey "armpits".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> At a breeding colony it has a loud cackling call.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hutton%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hutton's shearwater feeds in the open ocean largely on small fish and krill, diving up to 20 m.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Puffinus huttoni</i> have long bills, which are adapted to catch prey more or less underwater by plunging from a few meters above the surface or by paddling slowly forwards searching with their head submerged, then diving using partly opened wings for propulsion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hutton%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg/220px-Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg/330px-Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg/440px-Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Boat_with_Hutton%27s_shearwaters.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A raft of Hutton's shearwaters feeding off the <a href="/wiki/Kaikoura" title="Kaikoura">Kaikoura</a> coast</div></div></div>
<p>These birds live entirely at sea except when breeding. During the September–March (spring and summer) breeding season, adults migrate to New Zealand waters; there have been individual sightings around the entire New Zealand coast,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> but most birds are feeding off the eastern <a href="/wiki/South_Island" title="South Island">South Island</a>, especially between <a href="/wiki/Cook_Strait" title="Cook Strait">Cook Strait</a> and <a href="/wiki/Banks_Peninsula" title="Banks Peninsula">Banks Peninsula</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Large flocks can be seen off the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kaikoura_Coast" title="Kaikoura Coast">Kaikoura Coast</a> during summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> Outside the breeding season, they are mostly found in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> waters. Geo-locators fitted on young birds revealed some circumnavigate Australia in an anti-clockwise direction in the 4–5 years leading up to sexual maturity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Breeding">Breeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hutton%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Breeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3360" data-file-width="2240" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg/220px-Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg/330px-Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg/440px-Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hutton%27s_shearwater_habitat.jpeg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hutton's shearwater breeding colony, Shearwater Stream, <a href="/wiki/Kaikoura_Ranges" title="Kaikoura Ranges">Seaward Kaikoura Range</a></div></div></div>
<p>Uniquely amongst seabirds, Hutton's shearwater breed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sub-alpine" title="Sub-alpine">sub-alpine</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alpine_climate" title="Alpine climate">alpine</a> zones, in burrows at an altitude of 1200–1800 m. They formerly bred in both the Seaward and Inland Kaikoura mountains in historic times, and <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> collected the young "<a href="/wiki/Muttonbirding" title="Muttonbirding">muttonbirds</a>" for food.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Their breeding is restricted to only two remaining colonies in the Seaward Kaikoura Range, one of over 100,000 pairs at the head of the Kowhai River, and one small (8000 pair) colony on private land at Shearwater Stream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> Although the species was scientifically described in 1912, its breeding colonies were only rediscovered in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> Burrows are dug into steep <a href="/wiki/Tussock_grassland" title="Tussock grassland">tussock</a> slopes at a density of 1 per 2 m<sup>2</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-:2-3">[3]</a></sup> One white egg is laid in November and incubated for 50 days; chicks take around 80 days to fledge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conservation">Conservation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hutton%27s_shearwater&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conservation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The eight breeding colonies discovered in 1964 have been reduced to two, the other lower-altitude colonies destroyed by feral <a href="/wiki/Introduced_species" title="Introduced species">introduced</a> pigs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-:3-5">[5]</a></sup> Their main predators are introduced <a href="/wiki/Stoat" title="Stoat">stoats</a>, which kill about 0.25% of adults and 12% of chicks each year in their nesting <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burrow_nest" title="Burrow nest">burrows</a>. The overall growth rate is still positive, though, so stoats are not considered a major threat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stoat_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-stoat-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some parts of the colonies are in steep, unstable sites. The <a href="/wiki/Browsing_(herbivory)" title="Browsing (herbivory)">browsing</a> of deer, goats, and pigs in these steep areas has contributed to <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, which has damaged the Hutton's shearwater burrows and the population. However, control of pigs has led to better vegetation cover at the colonies and lessened destruction of burrows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although there are an estimated 114,000 breeding pairs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> both breeding colonies are vulnerable to predators or erosion, so a new colony (Te Rae o Atiu) was established on the <a href="/wiki/Kaikoura_Peninsula" title="Kaikoura Peninsula">Kaikoura Peninsula</a> in 2005. First, a small transfer of 10 nestlings was sent in April 2005. After that, roughly 100 additional nestlings were moved annually each March in 2006, 2007, 2008, 2012 and 2013. Chicks translocated from the Kowhai colony were hand-fed in artificial burrows to ensure they would <a href="/wiki/Imprinting_(psychology)" title="Imprinting (psychology)">imprint</a> on the new colony, and since 2010 have been returning there to breed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup> A predator-proof fence was built around the 2 hectares (4.9 acres) site in February 2010 by the Hutton's Shearwater Charitable Trust.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Fledgling shearwaters are disoriented by bright lights, and problems with crash-landing birds have prompted the Kaikoura District Council to try new streetlighting which can be adjusted during fledging times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
