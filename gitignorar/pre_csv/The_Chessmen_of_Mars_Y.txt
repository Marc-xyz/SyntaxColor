<p><i><b>The Chessmen of Mars</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Science_fantasy" title="Science fantasy">science fantasy</a> novel by American writer  <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Rice_Burroughs" title="Edgar Rice Burroughs">Edgar Rice Burroughs</a>, the fifth of his  <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a> series. Burroughs began writing it in January, 1921, and the finished story was first published in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argosy_All-Story_Weekly" title="Argosy All-Story Weekly">Argosy All-Story Weekly</a></i> as a six-part serial in the issues for February 18 and 25 and March 4, 11, 18 and 25, 1922. It was later published as a complete novel by <a href="/wiki/A._C._McClurg" title="A. C. McClurg">A. C. McClurg</a> in November 1922.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="960" decoding="async" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg/150px-Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg/225px-Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg/300px-Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Martian_Family_of_John_Carter_from_Edgar_Rice_Burroughs_Barsoom_Novels.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Carter's descendants</div></div></div>
<p>In this novel Burroughs focuses on a younger member of the family established by <a href="/wiki/John_Carter_of_Mars" title="John Carter of Mars">John Carter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dejah_Thoris" title="Dejah Thoris">Dejah Thoris</a>, protagonists of the first three books in the series. The heroine this time is their daughter Tara, princess of Helium, whose hand is sought by the gallant Gahan, Jed (prince) of Gathol. Both Helium and Gathol are prominent Barsoomian city states.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tara meets Prince Gahan of Gathol, and is initially unimpressed, viewing him as something of a <a href="/wiki/Dandy" title="Dandy">popinjay</a>. Later she takes her flier into a storm and loses control of the craft, and the storm carries her to an unfamiliar region of Barsoom. After landing and fleeing from a pack of ferocious Banths (Martian lions), she is captured by the horrific <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldanes</a>, who resemble large heads with small, crab-like legs. The Kaldanes have bred a symbiotic race of headless human-like creatures called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rykor" title="Rykor">Rykors</a>, which they can attach themselves to and ride like a horse. The Kaldanes imprison Tara, intending to fatten her up, then eat her. While imprisoned, Tara manages to win over one of the Kaldanes, Ghek, with her lovely singing voice.
</p><p>Gahan, who has fallen in love with Tara, sets out to find her, only to find himself caught up in the same storm, and he falls overboard while attempting to rescue one of his crew. He stumbles upon Bantoom, realm of the Kaldanes, and manages to rescue Tara, and together with Ghek they flee in Tara's crippled flier. Tara doesn't recognize Gahan as the prince she met earlier, as he is worn from his ordeals and no longer dressed in his fancy clothes. In light of her earlier reaction to him, Gahan decides to keep his identity secret, and identifies himself instead as a Panthan (warrior) called Turan.
</p><p>The three of them manage to reach the isolated city of Manator. Gahan ventures into the city seeking food and water, but is tricked and taken prisoner by the inhabitants. Tara and Ghek are also captured. In Manator, captives are forced to a fight to the death in the arena, in a modified version of <a href="/wiki/Jetan" title="Jetan">Jetan</a>, a popular Barsoomian board game resembling <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">Chess</a>; the living version uses people as the game pieces on a life-sized board, with each taking of a piece being a duel to the death.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novel was written during 1921, from January 7 to November 12. It was a particularly inventive and broad piece of imagination, including many details of the traditions, beasts and characters featuring in the novel. While working on the piece, Burroughs created a worksheet with 70 entries relating to architecture, locations, equipment and geographical locations. Of these, probably the most remarkable is his creation of <a href="/wiki/Jetan" title="Jetan">Jetan</a>, the Martian version of Chess, played with living people. Burroughs was a keen chess player and played games with his assistant, John Shea, while writing the novel, which games he invariably won. Burroughs, as he had done in prior <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a> novels, cast himself as John Carter's nephew, entrusted with Carter's manuscript of another Martian tale. He actually mentions these games with Shea in the opening pages of the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges348_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-porges348-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre">Genre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The novel can be classed as a <a href="/wiki/Planetary_romance" title="Planetary romance">planetary romance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westfahl_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-westfahl-3">[3]</a></sup> This genre is a subset of <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Sword_and_sorcery" title="Sword and sorcery">sword and sorcery</a>, but including scientific elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harrisfain_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-harrisfain-4">[4]</a></sup> Most of the action in a planetary romance is on the surface of an alien world, usually includes sword fighting, monsters, supernatural elements as telepathy rather than magic, and involves civilizations echoing those on Earth in pre-technological eras, particularly composed of kingdoms or theocratic nations. Spacecraft may appear, but are usually not central to the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-westfahl_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-westfahl-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_characters">Major characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Major characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gahan_of_Gathol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gahan of Gathol (page does not exist)">Gahan of Gathol</a></b>: A prince of the Martian kingdom of Gathol who falls in love with Tara of Helium and is initially spurned by her when revealing his feelings. He later disguises himself as Turan, a mercenary, after rescuing her from the valley of the Kaldanes, revealing his true identity at the conclusion of the tale, by which time Tara has fallen in love with his assumed identity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tara_of_Helium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tara of Helium (page does not exist)">Tara of Helium</a></b>: A Princess of Helium, daughter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Carter_(character)" title="John Carter (character)">John Carter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dejah_Thoris" title="Dejah Thoris">Dejah Thoris</a>.</li>
<li><b>Ghek</b>: A Kaldane, unusual among his kind in his ability to appreciate emotion, dissatisfied with <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldane</a> society who is charmed by Tara's singing, and joins Gahan of Gathol and Tara of Helium in their escape from the valley of the Kaldanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Setting">Setting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Setting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_basis">Scientific basis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientific basis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Burroughs' vision of Mars was loosely inspired by <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomical</a> speculation of the time, especially that of <a href="/wiki/Percival_Lowell" title="Percival Lowell">Percival Lowell</a>, who saw the planet as a formerly <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earthlike</a> world now becoming less hospitable to life due to its advanced age,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baxter_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-baxter-5">[5]</a></sup>  whose inhabitants had built canals to bring water from the polar caps to irrigate the remaining arable land.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baxter_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-baxter-5">[5]</a></sup>  Lowell was influenced by Italian astronomer, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Virginio_Schiaparelli" title="Giovanni Virginio Schiaparelli">Giovanni Virginio Schiaparelli</a>, who in 1878, had observed features on Mars he called canali (Italian for "channels"). Mistranslation of this into English as "canals" fuelled belief the planet was inhabited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seed_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-seed-6">[6]</a></sup>  The theory of an inhabited planet with flowing water was disproved by data provided by Russian and American probes such as the two <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">Viking missions</a> which found a dead, frozen world where water could not exist in a fluid state.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-baxter_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-baxter-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_of_Barsoom">World of Barsoom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: World of Barsoom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a></div>
<p>A million years before the narrative commences, Mars was a lush world with oceans. As the oceans receded, and the atmosphere grew thin, the planet has devolved into a landscape of partial barbarism;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bainbridge132_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-bainbridge132-7">[7]</a></sup> living on an aging planet, with dwindling resources, the inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a> have become hardened and warlike, fighting one another to survive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sharp_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-sharp-8">[8]</a></sup>  Barsoomians distribute scarce water supplies via a worldwide system of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martian_canals" title="Martian canals">canals</a>, controlled by quarreling city-states. The thinning Martian atmosphere is artificially replenished from an "atmosphere plant".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slotkin_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-slotkin-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a world with clear territorial divisions between White, Yellow, Black, Red and Green skinned races. Each has particular traits and qualities, which seem to define the characters of almost every individuals within them.  Burroughs' concept of race in <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a>, is more similar to species than ethnicity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slotkin2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-slotkin2-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_settings">Specific settings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Specific settings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Bantoom.2C_Valley_of_the_Kaldanes"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bantoom,_Valley_of_the_Kaldanes">Bantoom, Valley of the Kaldanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bantoom, Valley of the Kaldanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A hidden valley. Dwelling place of the Kaldanes, which are mostly brain, and the Rykors, headless bodies that the Kaldanes use as vehicles. The Kaldanes mostly live in tunnels below the ground. It is ruled by a king <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldane</a>, with exceptional telepathic powers. Tara of Helium ends up in Bantoom after her flier is blown off course by a massive storm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Manator">Manator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Manator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A technologically backward Red Martian civilization, which has no firearms or fliers, survives by raiding caravans and prevents anyone from leaving their society. They have two distinctive traditions, firstly a habit of displaying the dead, covered in ornaments, and secondly playing a live version of the Martian chess, <a href="/wiki/Jetan" title="Jetan">Jetan</a>. Gahan of Gathol, Tara of Helium and Kaldane, Ghek, discover the location while fleeing the valley of the Kaldanes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Excessive_intellectualism_as_a_theme">Excessive intellectualism as a theme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Excessive intellectualism as a theme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While Burroughs is generally seen as a writer who produced work of limited philosophical value, Burroughs wrote two <a href="/wiki/Barsoom" title="Barsoom">Barsoom</a> novels, which appear to explore, or parody the limits of excessive intellectual development at the expense of bodily or physical existence. The first was <a href="/wiki/Thuvia,_Maid_of_Mars" title="Thuvia, Maid of Mars">Thuvia, Maid of Mars</a>, in which Thuvia and Carthoris discover a remnant of ancient White Martian civilization, the Lotharians. The Lotharians have mostly died out, but maintain the illusion of a functioning society through powerful telepathic projections, and have formed two factions, which appear portray the excesses of pointless intellectual debate - one faction, the realists believes in imagining meals to provide sustenance, another, the etherealists, believes in surviving without eating. The Chessmen of Mars is the second example of this trend.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholes13-14_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-scholes13-14-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Chessmen of Mars</i> introduces the <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldanes</a> of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bantoom&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bantoom (page does not exist)">Bantoom</a>. Their form is almost all head but for six spiderlike legs and a pair of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelae" title="Chelae">chelae</a>. Their racial goal is to evolve towards pure <a href="/wiki/Intellect" title="Intellect">intellect</a> and away from bodily existence. In order to function in the physical realm, they have bred the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rykor" title="Rykor">Rykors</a>, a complementary species composed of a body similar to that of a perfect specimen of Red Martian but lacking a head. When the <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldane</a> places itself upon the neck of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rykor" title="Rykor">Rykor</a>, a bundle of tentacles connects with the Rykor's <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, allowing the brain of the <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldane</a> to interface with the body of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rykor" title="Rykor">Rykor</a>. Should the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rykor" title="Rykor">Rykor</a> become damaged or die, the <a href="/wiki/Kaldane" title="Kaldane">Kaldane</a> merely climbs upon another as an earthling might change a horse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kaldanes have sacrificed their bodies to become pure brain, but although they can interface with Rykor bodies, their ability to function compared to a normal people, with an integrated mind and body, is ineffectual and clumsy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scholes13-14_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-scholes13-14-11">[11]</a></sup> The Kaldanes, almost pure brain, are also, while highly intelligent, ugly ineffectual creatures when not interfaced with a Rykor body. Tara of Helium compares them to effete intellectuals from her home city, with a self-important sense of superiority, and Gahan of Gathol muses that it might be better to find a balance between the intellect and bodily passions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-holtsmark29-30_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-holtsmark29-30-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jetan_and_its_influence">Jetan and its influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Chessmen_of_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jetan and its influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jetan" title="Jetan">Jetan</a></div>
<p>The game of Jetan is a Martian variety of <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a>. The board consists of 100 black and orange squares, laid in an alternating pattern. Both black and orange players are given 20 pieces. In the novel it is played at life size, at the arena in the Barsoomian location of Manator, with actual living people, who are dressed to appear the same as the pieces they represent. The game is a fight to the death, when a warrior is moved into one of the squares, and a warrior of the opposing color occupies the space, the two must engage in mortal combat.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges348-9_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-porges348-9-13">[13]</a></sup>  The game is intended to portray a battle between the Black Martians of the south and the Yellow Martians of the North and hence the board is traditionally orientated to these directions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges351_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-porges351-14">[14]</a></sup> While criminals and slaves are usually used as pieces, nobles also participate on occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bleiler99_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-bleiler99-1">[1]</a></sup> It is played in Manator to win a beautiful woman, usually an exceptionally comely slave. It undoubtedly originates from Burrough's own fascination with <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">Chess</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges348-9_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-porges348-9-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>On August 6, 1922 Burroughs received a letter from a prisoner, Elston B. Sweet, an inmate of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leavenworth_Prison" title="Leavenworth Prison">Leavenworth Prison</a>.  Sweet and another inmate had used the references to <a href="/wiki/Jetan" title="Jetan">Jetan</a> in the novel to make a <a href="/wiki/Jetan" title="Jetan">Jetan</a> set, with carved pieces, after which they had played numerous games, and the game had become popular with other prisoners. Burroughs wrote back on August 16, 1922 to inform the pair they had created the first ever actual set. Interest in the game from fans was high; consequently, he included the rules in an appendix,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges348-9_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-porges348-9-13">[13]</a></sup> entitled "Jetan, or Martian Chess",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-reneau_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-reneau-15">[15]</a></sup> when the novel version was published.  A chapter on the game was included in the 1968 book <i>Chess Variations</i> by John Gollon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges348-9_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-porges348-9-13">[13]</a></sup> Gollon made a Jetan set, curious about the game principally as a novelty and played with it; he was surprised by how much he enjoyed the game, and even described it as a 'respectable game'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-porges351_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-porges351-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept inspired imitation by authors of later <a href="/wiki/Planetary_romance" title="Planetary romance">planetary romances</a> influenced by Burroughs, each of whom felt compelled to invent their own extraterrestrial version of chess to be fought with human beings.  Instances of such homage include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lin_Carter" title="Lin Carter">Lin Carter</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Darza_(game)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Darza (game) (page does not exist)">Darza</a>, from <i><a href="/wiki/Renegade_of_Callisto" title="Renegade of Callisto">Renegade of Callisto</a></i>, 8th volume in his <i><a href="/wiki/Callisto_series" title="Callisto series">Callisto series</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Bulmer" title="Kenneth Bulmer">Kenneth Bulmer</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jikaida&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jikaida (page does not exist)">Jikaida</a>, from <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=A_Life_for_Kregen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="A Life for Kregen (page does not exist)">A Life for Kregen</a></i>, 19th volume in his <i><a href="/wiki/Dray_Prescot_series" title="Dray Prescot series">Dray Prescot series</a></i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Norman" title="John Norman">John Norman</a>'s <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kaissa_(fictional_game)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kaissa (fictional game) (page does not exist)">Kaissa</a>, from his <i><a href="/wiki/Gor" title="Gor">Gor</a></i> series, mentioned many times, though never fully described</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/S._M._Stirling" title="S. M. Stirling">S. M. Stirling</a>'s Atanj, from <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Courts_of_the_Crimson_Kings" title="In the Courts of the Crimson Kings">In the Courts of the Crimson Kings</a></i>, his own Burroughs-influenced novel of an alternate Mars, also not fully described</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone">Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone</a></i> provides a rare instance of its use in fantasy, in a scene in which her three protagonists Harry, Hermionie, and Ron must play as pieces in a lifelike game of <a href="/wiki/Magical_objects_in_Harry_Potter#Wizard's_Chess" title="Magical objects in Harry Potter">Wizard's Chess</a>.
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dan_Simmon" title="Dan Simmon">Dan Simmon</a>'s horror novel <i><a href="/wiki/Carrion_Comfort" title="Carrion Comfort">Carrion Comfort</a></i> has Nazi chess masters with psychic powers that permits them to enter into the minds of concentration camp prisoners allowing them to be completely controlled as pawns in deadly games of chess.
</p>
