<p><i><b>Lemna gibba</b></i>, the <b>gibbous duckweed</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <b>swollen duckweed</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> or <b>fat duckweed</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BSBI07_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BSBI07-3">[3]</a></sup> is a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Lemna" title="Lemna">Lemna</a></i> (duckweed).  It has a simple plant body, known as a thallus, which floats on the surface of the water and measures 3 – 5 mm in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> A single root hangs down into the water. Found in a wide range of still or slow-flowing water bodies, this common duckweed can also grow on mud or damp rocks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lemna_gibba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Distribution is in temperate areas in Europe, including <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a>, to the <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. This duckweed is one of Britain's most common small water plants, which forms familiar green mats covering stagnant water bodies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Widespread throughout Great Britain, but is absent from much of Scotland and Shetland. In Ireland, it is found mainly in the north and east.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Elsewhere, the species has a very wide global distribution, absent only from polar areas and the tropics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Distribution information for this species can be accessed via the <a class="external text" href="http://www.mobot.org/jwcross/duckweed/Habitat/geography.html" rel="nofollow">Charms of Duckweed</a> (worldwide) and <a class="external text" href="https://data.nbn.org.uk/Taxa/NBNSYS0000002359/Grid_Map" rel="nofollow">National Biodiversity Network Gateway</a> (Britain only).
</p><p>This species spreads mainly through vegetative reproduction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> but flowers are occasionally produced in shallow water exposed to full sun. When covering the entire surface of a pond, it can make the water appear solid, and in parts of the north-west of England, children were scared away from such ponds by the myth of Jenny Green-teeth, a pond elf or monster whose presence was indicated by duckweed; she was said to lure children into ponds and drown them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
