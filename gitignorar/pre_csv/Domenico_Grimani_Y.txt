<p><b>Domenico Grimani</b> (19 February 1461 – 27 August 1523) was an Italian nobleman, theologian  and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_(Catholicism)" title="Cardinal (Catholicism)">cardinal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MirandaDomGrim_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-MirandaDomGrim-1">[1]</a></sup> Like most noble churchman of his era Grimani was an ecclesiastical pluralist, holding numerous posts and <a href="/wiki/Benefice" title="Benefice">benefices</a>. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus" title="Desiderius Erasmus">Desiderius Erasmus</a> dedicated to Grimani his <i>Musica</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domenico_Grimani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, he was the eldest of five sons of <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Grimani" title="Antonio Grimani">Antonio Grimani</a>, the oldest elected <a href="/wiki/Doge_of_Venice" title="Doge of Venice">Doge of Venice</a>, and his wife Catarina Loredan. Antonio was elected doge in 1521, when Domenico was already a cardinal.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>He exhibited an early predilection for <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">humanist studies</a>, which was encouraged by  teachers in his native <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and then in the ambit of the Medicean academy in <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">Florence</a>, where he moved in the circle of <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">Lorenzo de' Medici</a> among scholars like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pico_della_Mirandola" title="Pico della Mirandola">Pico della Mirandola</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poliziano" title="Poliziano">Poliziano</a>. He obtained a doctorate in <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Padua" title="University of Padua">University of Padua</a> on 23 October 1487 and was elected a Senator of Venice that same year.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>He became a cardinal in 1493, an appointment paid by his father with a sum of around 25,000/30,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ducat_(Italian_coin)" title="Ducat (Italian coin)">ducats</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>  Grimani was not ordained a priest until 1498.  After the election of Pope <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julius_II" title="Julius II">Julius II</a> he became cardinal priest of San Marco.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p><p>After a period as apostolic administrator in <a href="/wiki/Nicosia" title="Nicosia">Nicosia</a>, starting from 1498 he was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Aquileia" title="Patriarch of Aquileia">Patriarch of Aquileia</a>, a position he abandoned in favor of his nephew <a href="/wiki/Marino_Grimani" title="Marino Grimani">Marino</a> (later also cardinal) in 1517. In 1508 he was named cardinal bishop of Albano,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and was  also administrator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Urbino" title="Diocese of Urbino">diocese of Urbino</a> (from 1514) and  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ceneda" title="Bishop of Ceneda">bishop of Ceneda</a> (1517-1520).  Grimani was already ill in the 1521 conclave, and died one year and a half later; he was buried in the church of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santi_Giovanni_e_Paolo,_Rome" title="Santi Giovanni e Paolo, Rome">Santi Giovanni e Paolo, Rome</a>, but later his remains were moved to <a href="/wiki/San_Francesco_della_Vigna" title="San Francesco della Vigna">San Francesco della Vigna</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Domenico_Grimani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grimani was a large collector, owning works by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giorgione" title="Giorgione">Giorgione</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titian" title="Titian">Titian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hans_Memling" title="Hans Memling">Hans Memling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Bosch" title="Hieronymus Bosch">Hieronymus Bosch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raphael" title="Raphael">Raphael</a> and others: his collection now forms part of the Museo d'Antichità in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doge%27s_Palace,_Venice" title="Doge's Palace, Venice">Doge's Palace</a> in <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, while several of his codexes are in the Archbishop's Library at <a href="/wiki/Udine" title="Udine">Udine</a>.  The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grimani_Breviary" title="Grimani Breviary">Grimani Breviary</a>, now in the <a href="/wiki/Biblioteca_Marciana" title="Biblioteca Marciana">Biblioteca Marciana</a> of Venice, is a key work in the late history of <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminated manuscripts</a>.  It was produced in <a href="/wiki/Ghent" title="Ghent">Ghent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bruges" title="Bruges">Bruges</a> ca 1515-1520 and by 1520 owned, though possibly not originally commissioned, by Domenico Grimani. Several leading artists, including <a href="/wiki/Simon_Bening" title="Simon Bening">Simon Bening</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerard_David" title="Gerard David">Gerard David</a> contributed some of their finest work to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Grimani also wrote several theological treatises, and translated <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">John Chrysostom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Homily" title="Homily">homilies</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>
</p>
