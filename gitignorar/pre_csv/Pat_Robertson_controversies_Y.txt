<h2><span id="Criticism_of_Robertson.27s_faith_healing"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_Robertson's_faith_healing">Criticism of Robertson's faith healing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Criticism of Robertson's faith healing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 1970s and 1980s Robertson was a <a href="/wiki/Faith_healing" title="Faith healing">faith healer</a>. <a href="/wiki/James_Randi" title="James Randi">James Randi</a> devoted a chapter of his book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Faith_Healers" title="The Faith Healers">The Faith Healers</a></i> to criticizing Robertson's faith healing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandi1989197–206_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandi1989197–206-1">[1]</a></sup>  Randi commented that "in 1986, soon after the full importance of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> epidemic began to become evident, Robertson was attempting to cure it by proclaiming people cured after prayer."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandi1989199–200_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandi1989199–200-2">[2]</a></sup> Randi also noted, "Gerry Straub, a former associate of Pat Robertson and his television producer, pointed out in his book <i>Salvation for Sale</i> the astonishing fact that God seemed to time miracles to conform with standard television format," and "God would stop speaking to Pat and stop healing exactly in time with the theme music."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandi1989200_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandi1989200-3">[3]</a></sup>  Randi explained that "in 1979, it appeared to Robertson's staff that their boss had been taking lessons from <a href="/wiki/Oral_Roberts" title="Oral Roberts">Oral Roberts</a>" and "proposed to film the <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">Second Coming</a>!".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandi1989204_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandi1989204-4">[4]</a></sup>  The project was eventually publicly dropped, but "budget allocations [CBN] are made for their development."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERandi1989205_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERandi1989205-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Efficacy_of_Robertson.27s_prayers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Efficacy_of_Robertson's_prayers">Efficacy of Robertson's prayers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Efficacy of Robertson's prayers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson prayed to God to steer hurricanes away from his company's <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Beach,_Virginia" title="Virginia Beach, Virginia">Virginia Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> headquarters. He credited his prayers for steering the course of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gloria" title="Hurricane Gloria">Hurricane Gloria</a> in 1985.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The storm instead hit the Mid-Atlantic states and New England, causing $900 million in damage and eight deaths.
</p>
<h2><span id="Calling_non-Christians_.22termites.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Calling_non-Christians_"termites"'>Calling non-Christians "termites"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: Calling non-Christians "termites"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an August 1986 <a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)"><i>New York</i> magazine</a> article Robertson is quoted saying, "It is interesting, that termites don't build things, and the great builders of our nation almost to a man have been Christians, because Christians have the desire to build something. He is motivated by love of man and God, so he builds.  The people who have come into [our] institutions [today] are primarily termites.  They are into destroying institutions that have been built by Christians, whether it is universities, governments, our own traditions, that we have ... The termites are in charge now, and that is not the way it ought to be, and the time has arrived for a godly fumigation."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_child_conceived_out_of_wedlock">First child conceived out of wedlock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First child conceived out of wedlock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During Robertson's unsuccessful presidential bid in 1987, Robertson told a Wall Street Journal reporter that his wedding date was actually five months after the date he had always maintained.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assails_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-assails-8">[8]</a></sup>  Reporters said that the actual wedding date meant that his first son was conceived out of wedlock and that Robertson had lied about the date of his marriage in an attempt to cover the truth up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assails_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-assails-8">[8]</a></sup> While conceding the reports were accurate, Robertson said that begetting his son out of wedlock occurred before Jesus Christ had entered his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assails_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-assails-8">[8]</a></sup> Robertson denounced the media choosing to report on the issue as "outrageous" and "reprehensible."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_other_Christian_denominations">Comments on other Christian denominations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Comments on other Christian denominations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 14, 1991, on <i>The 700 Club</i>, Pat Robertson attacked a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> denominations when he declared: "You say you're supposed to be nice to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_in_the_United_States_of_America" title="Episcopal Church in the United States of America">Episcopalians</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presbyterians" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodists" title="Methodists">Methodists</a> and this, that, and the other thing.  Nonsense.  I don't have to be nice to the spirit of the <a href="/wiki/Antichrist" title="Antichrist">Antichrist</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_Islam">Comments on Islam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Comments on Islam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson frequently denounces the religion of <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> people. During a 1995 taping of <i>The 700 Club</i>, he called the religion a "Christian <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> During a September 19, 2002 episode of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_News_Channel" title="Fox News Channel">Fox News Channel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hannity_%26_Colmes" title="Hannity &amp; Colmes">Hannity &amp; Colmes</a></i>, Robertson claimed that the Muslim prophet <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> was "an absolute wild-eyed fanatic ... a robber and a brigand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> On the July 14, 2005 broadcast of <i>The 700 Club</i>, he claimed that "Islam, at its core, teaches violence."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the March 13, 2006 broadcast of <i>The 700 Club</i> Robertson stated that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> want global domination and that the outpouring of rage elicited by <a href="/wiki/Jyllands-Posten_Muhammad_cartoons_controversy" title="Jyllands-Posten Muhammad cartoons controversy">cartoon drawings</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Islamic Prophet Muhammad</a> "just shows the kind of people we're dealing with. These people are crazed fanatics, and I want to say it now: I believe it's motivated by <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demonic</a> power. It is <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satanic</a> and it's time we recognize what we're dealing with."  He finished by stating "by the way, Islam is not a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Religion_of_Peace" title="Religion of Peace">religion of peace</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>  The Rev. <a href="/wiki/Barry_W._Lynn" title="Barry W. Lynn">Barry W. Lynn</a>, executive director of <a href="/wiki/Americans_United_for_Separation_of_Church_and_State" title="Americans United for Separation of Church and State">Americans United for Separation of Church and State</a>, slammed Robertson's comments as "grossly irresponsible". Lynn went on to say, "At a time when inter-religious tensions around the world are at an all-time high, Robertson seems determined to throw gasoline on the fire."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the September 25, 2006 broadcast of <i>The 700 Club</i> Robertson stated "It's amazing how the <a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a> deal with history and the truth with violence. They don't understand what reasoned dialogue is ..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>On the November 9, 2009 edition of <i>The 700 Club</i> Robertson stated "Islam is a violent – I was going to say, 'religion', but it's not a religion; it's a political system. It's a violent political system bent on the overthrow of the governments of the world, and world domination. That is the ultimate aim. And they talk about infidels and all this, but the truth is that's what the game is. So, you're dealing with a – not a religion – you're dealing with a political system. And I think we should treat it as such, and treat its adherents as such, as we would members of the Communist Party, or members of some fascist group."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_Hinduism">Comments on Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comments on Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On March 23, 1995, Pat Robertson led a television program in which he attacked Hinduism, calling it "demonic". He said that they worship "idols" and "hundreds of millions of deities," which "has put a nation in bondage to spiritual forces that have deceived many for thousands of years." He spoke against the doctrines of <a href="/wiki/Karma" title="Karma">karma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reincarnation" title="Reincarnation">reincarnation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>These and other remarks have been repudiated by some <a href="/wiki/Hindus" title="Hindus">Hindus</a>. Dr. Kusumita Pedersen, Director for the Project on Human Rights and Religion, commented that Robertson has employed "almost every <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-Hinduism" title="Anti-Hinduism">negative image and cliché</a> that has been used about <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> since the 18th century."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson-Hindus_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-Hindus-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his book <i>The New World Order</i>, Robertson wrote: "When I said during my presidential bid that I would bring only Christians and Jews into the government, I hit a firestorm. 'What do you mean?' the media challenged me. 'You're not going to bring atheists into the government?  How dare you maintain that those who believe in Christian values are better qualified to govern America than Hindus and Muslims?' My simple answer is, 'Yes, they are.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Cantor" title="David Cantor">David Cantor</a>, Senior Research Analyst of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League" title="Anti-Defamation League">Anti-Defamation League</a>, points out that such "religious tests for office are unconstitutional. It's not just a purely a religious statement. It's a political statement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Robertson-Hindus_19-1"><a href="#cite_note-Robertson-Hindus-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Feminism.2C_homosexuality.2C_abortion_and_liberalism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Feminism,_homosexuality,_abortion_and_liberalism">Feminism, homosexuality, abortion and liberalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Feminism, homosexuality, abortion and liberalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson is opposed to <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>  He has described <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">feminism</a> as a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist" title="Socialist">socialist</a>, anti-<a href="/wiki/Family_values" title="Family values">family</a> political movement that encourages women to <a href="/wiki/Divorce" title="Divorce">leave their husbands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">kill their children</a>, practice <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft" title="Witchcraft">witchcraft</a>, destroy <a href="/wiki/Capitalism" title="Capitalism">capitalism</a> and become <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbians</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many of Robertson's views mirror those of fellow evangelical activist pastor <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Falwell" title="Jerry Falwell">Jerry Falwell</a>, who made frequent appearances on <i>The 700 Club</i>. He agreed with Falwell when Falwell stated that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/September_11,_2001_attacks" title="September 11, 2001 attacks">September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks</a> were caused by "<a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagans</a>, abortionists, feminists, gays, lesbians, the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People_For_the_American_Way" title="People For the American Way">People For the American Way</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup>
</p><p>The June 8, 1998 edition of his show, where Robertson denounced <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney_World" title="Disney World">Disney World</a> for allowing a privately sponsored "Gay Days" weekend, also drew criticism from Americans United for Separation of Church and State. Robertson stated that the acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Homosexuality" title="Homosexuality">homosexuality</a> could result in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">earthquakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Domestic_terrorism_in_the_United_States" title="Domestic terrorism in the United States">terrorist bombings</a> and "possibly a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">meteor</a>," prompting Americans United to criticize Robertson, saying it was "deplorable that Robertson is using the tragedy of these fires to promote his religious and political agenda."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The resulting outcry prompted Robertson to return to the topic on June 24, where he quoted the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> to support his claims. The first hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1998_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1998 Atlantic hurricane season">1998 Atlantic hurricane season</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc.noaa.gov_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-nhc.noaa.gov-27">[27]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Bonnie_(1998)" title="Hurricane Bonnie (1998)">Hurricane Bonnie</a>, actually turned away from Florida and instead damaged the rest of the East Coast. The area hardest hit by the hurricane was the Hampton Roads region,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> which includes Virginia Beach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> the place of origin of Robertson's <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup>  While other hurricanes did hit Florida,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nhc.noaa.gov_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-nhc.noaa.gov-27">[27]</a></sup> none of them hit Disney World.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p><p>While discussing the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_Foley_congressional_page_incident" title="Mark Foley congressional page incident">Mark Foley scandal</a> on the October 5, 2006 broadcast of the show, Robertson condemned <a href="/wiki/Mark_Foley" title="Mark Foley">Foley</a>, saying he "does what gay people do" and claiming that it would not hurt Republican chances in the elections, as "the church people understand forgiveness, they understand sin."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court</a>'s decision in <i><a href="/wiki/Obergefell_v._Hodges" title="Obergefell v. Hodges">Obergefell v. Hodges</a></i> that legalized same-sex marriage in the United States, Robertson stated, on the June 29, 2015 episode of <i>The 700 Club</i>, in response to the ruling that, "You're gonna say that you like anal sex, you like oral sex, you like bestiality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSM_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-SSM-33">[33]</a></sup> He went on to say, "Sooner or later, you're going to have to conform your religious beliefs to the group of some abhorrent thing. It won't stop at homosexuality."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSM_33-1"><a href="#cite_note-SSM-33">[33]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_Scotland">Comments on Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comments on Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1999, <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Scotland" title="Bank of Scotland">Bank of Scotland</a> announced a joint venture with Robertson to establish banks in the United States that conduct all business by telephone, a business model that had been successful in the United Kingdom.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-furor_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-furor-34">[34]</a></sup>  Robertson said that the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Scotland" title="Church of Scotland">Church of Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Episcopal_Church" title="Scottish Episcopal Church">Scottish Episcopal Church</a> ignored the teachings of Saint Paul because they have ordained gays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-furor_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-furor-34">[34]</a></sup> Robertson also said that Scotland was "a dark land" overrun by gays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drops_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-drops-35">[35]</a></sup> In response the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Scotland" title="Bank of Scotland">Bank of Scotland</a> dropped their plans for a business operation with Robertson, following customer complaints.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drops_35-1"><a href="#cite_note-drops-35">[35]</a></sup> In a press release, Robertson said that "media comments about him had made it impossible to proceed."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-drops_35-2"><a href="#cite_note-drops-35">[35]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_ties_to_politicians">Financial ties to politicians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Financial ties to politicians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An investigation by the Commonwealth of Virginia's Office of Consumer Affairs determined that Robertson "willfully induced contributions from the public through the use of misleading statements and other implications" and called for a criminal prosecution against Robertson in 1999. However, Virginia Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Mark_Earley" title="Mark Earley">Mark Earley</a>, a Republican whose largest campaign contributor two years earlier was Robertson himself, intervened, accepting that Robertson had made deceptive appeals but overruling the recommendation for his prosecution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nationkatrinacash_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-nationkatrinacash-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_Chinese_abortions">Comments on Chinese abortions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Comments on Chinese abortions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a 2001 interview with <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Wolf_Blitzer" title="Wolf Blitzer">Wolf Blitzer</a>, he said that the Chinese were "doing what they have to do," regarding <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">China</a>'s <a href="/wiki/One-child_policy" title="One-child policy">one-child policy</a>, sometimes enforced with compulsory abortions, though he said that he did not personally agree with the practice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup>  The statement drew criticisms from a variety of groups, including the <a href="/wiki/Family_Research_Council" title="Family Research Council">Family Research Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Values_Coalition" title="Traditional Values Coalition">Traditional Values Coalition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Concerned_Women_for_America" title="Concerned Women for America">Concerned Women for America</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Racehorse_ownership">Racehorse ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Racehorse ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 2002, Robertson acknowledged buying a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_horse" title="Race horse">race horse</a>, named "Mr. Pat", for $520,000.  He told a <i>New York Times</i> reporter that his interest in the horse was based purely on its aesthetics. "I don't bet and I don't gamble. I just enjoy watching horses running and performing." Christian scholar <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Martin_Marty" title="Martin Marty">Martin Marty</a> criticised this statement by Robertson, stating "The whole culture of horse racing involves gambling, and all the money comes from people trying to hit it big gambling. This is like saying you're investing in a <a href="/wiki/Brothel" title="Brothel">bordello</a> but aren't in favor of prostitution."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Financial_ties_to_African_leaders">Financial ties to African leaders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Financial ties to African leaders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson repeatedly supported former <a href="/wiki/President_of_Liberia" title="President of Liberia">President</a> of <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_G._Taylor" title="Charles G. Taylor">Charles Taylor</a>, in various episodes of his <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i> program during the United States' involvement in the <a href="/wiki/Second_Liberian_Civil_War" title="Second Liberian Civil War">Second Liberian Civil War</a> in June and July 2003.  Robertson accuses the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._State_Department" title="U.S. State Department">U.S. State Department</a> of giving <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">President Bush</a> bad advice in supporting Taylor's ouster as president, and of trying "as hard as they can to destabilize Liberia."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p><p>Robertson was criticized for failing to mention in his broadcasts his $8,000,000 (USD) investment in a Liberian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gold_mine" title="Gold mine">gold mine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Taylor had been indicted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_crimes" title="War crimes">war crimes</a> at the time of Robertson's support, and was found guilty of <a href="/wiki/Crimes_against_humanity" title="Crimes against humanity">crimes against humanity</a>, including murder, rape, slavery and the use of child soldiers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p><p>Prosecutors also said that Taylor had harbored members of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Qaeda" title="Al Qaeda">Al Qaeda</a> responsible for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998_U.S._embassy_bombings" title="1998 U.S. embassy bombings">1998 U.S. embassy bombings</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.  According to Robertson, the Liberian gold mine Freedom Gold was intended to help pay for humanitarian and evangelical efforts in Liberia, when in fact the company was allowed to fail leaving many debts both in Liberia and in the international mining service sector.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>  Regarding this controversy, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Land" title="Richard Land">Richard Land</a>, head of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Baptist_Convention" title="Southern Baptist Convention">Southern Baptist Convention</a>'s public policy said, "I would say that Pat Robertson is way out on his own, in a leaking life raft, on this one."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_Blessing">Operation Blessing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Operation Blessing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson has also been accused of using his tax-exempt, nonprofit organization, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Blessing_International" title="Operation Blessing International">Operation Blessing International</a>, as a front for his own financial gain, and then using his influence in the Republican Party to cover his tracks. After Robertson made emotional pleas in 1994 on <i>The 700 Club</i> for cash donations to Operation Blessing to support airlifts of refugees from Rwanda to Zaire, a reporter from <i>The Virginian-Pilot</i> stated that Operation Blessing's planes were transporting diamond-mining equipment for the Robertson-owned African Development Corporation, a venture Robertson had established in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Zaire" title="Zaire">Zaire</a>'s dictator, <a href="/wiki/Mobutu_Sese_Seko" title="Mobutu Sese Seko">Mobutu Sese Seko</a>, whom Robertson had befriended earlier in 1993. According to Operation Blessing documents, Robertson personally owned the planes used for Operation Blessing airlifts. A documentary exploring the Operation Blessing controversies, entitled <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mission_Congo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mission Congo (page does not exist)">Mission Congo</a></i>, premiered at the <a href="/wiki/2013_Toronto_International_Film_Festival" title="2013 Toronto International Film Festival">2013 Toronto International Film Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-44">[44]</a></sup>
</p><p>Robertson continues to state that Operation Blessing was largely responsible for providing aid to Rwanda following the 1994 genocide, even after an official investigation into Operation Blessing described it as a "fraudulent and deceptive" operation that provided almost no aid. According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, all Operation Blessing volunteers did was recite Bible passages at dying refugees. Robertson was accused of taking credit for work that was actually done by <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res" title="Médecins Sans Frontières">Médecins Sans Frontières</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-44">[44]</a></sup> On December 12, 2013, <i>The Guardian</i> issued an apology to Operation Blessing over false claims in the article "Mission Congo: how Pat Robertson raised millions on the back of a non-existent aid project" and agreed to make a contribution to Operation Blessing to be used in its relief efforts for victims of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a> in the Philippines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> A 1999 report concluded that whilst Robertson's requests for donations to Operation Blessing had been misleading, they were not an intentional attempt to commit fraud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian_Newspaper_Apologizes_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_Newspaper_Apologizes-46">[46]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_the_US_State_Department">Comments on the US State Department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Comments on the US State Department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On his <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i> television program, Pat Robertson has sharply criticized elements of the United States government and "special interest" groups that do not share his views. In October 2003 interviews with author Joel Mowbray about his book "Dangerous Diplomacy", a book critical of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">United States Department of State</a>, Robertson made suggestions that the explosion of a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">nuclear weapon</a> at State Department Headquarters would be good for the country, and repeated those comments on the air.  "What we need is for somebody to place a small nuke at <a href="/wiki/Foggy_Bottom" title="Foggy Bottom">Foggy Bottom</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iraq_War">Iraq War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Iraq War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pat Robertson claimed in 2004 that President Bush told him before he led the United States into the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>, that he expected there to be no casualties. He made this claim in an interview with <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a>, on October 19, 2004. President Bush's then-press secretary <a href="/wiki/Scott_McClellan" title="Scott McClellan">Scott McClellan</a> denied the allegation.  Mike McCurry, press secretary for Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Kerry" title="John Kerry">John Kerry</a> of Massachusetts, who was Bush's opponent for the presidency in the 2004 election, said that Bush deserved the benefit of the doubt, but he should say whether or not Robertson was telling the truth or lying.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Robertson also said that Chávez was "going to make <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> a launching pad for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist" title="Communist">communist</a> infiltration and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> extremism all over the continent" and called the leader an "out-of-control dictator ... a dangerous enemy to our south, controlling a huge pool of oil that could hurt us very badly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chavez_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-chavez-49">[49]</a></sup>
</p><p>Assassinations of heads of state have been against U.S. policy since an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" title="Executive order (United States)">executive order</a> against them was issued in 1976; in response, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Secretary_of_Defense" title="U.S. Secretary of Defense">Defense Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Donald_Rumsfeld" title="Donald Rumsfeld">Donald Rumsfeld</a> said that "our department doesn't do that kind of thing." <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernardo_%C3%81lvarez" title="Bernardo Álvarez">Bernardo Álvarez</a>, Venezuela's <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassador</a> to the U.S., demanded a stronger condemnation from the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> and that the United States "respect our country and its president."
</p><p>On the August 24 edition of <i>The 700 Club, </i>Robertson asserted that he hadn't actually called for Chávez's assassination, but that there were other ways of "taking him out", such as having <a href="/wiki/Special_forces" title="Special forces">special forces</a> carry out a <a href="/wiki/Kidnapping" title="Kidnapping">kidnapping</a>.  Robertson explicitly denied having used the word "assassination", though the word "assassinate" was present in his initial statement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Later that day, he issued a written statement in which he said, "Is it right to call for assassination? No, and I apologize for that statement. I spoke in frustration that we should accommodate the man who thinks the U.S. is out to kill him". However, he continued to justify his original stance on the potential threat Chávez posed to U.S. interests,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> even reiterating his support for Chavez's assassination on the February 2, 2006 edition of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hannity_and_Colmes" title="Hannity and Colmes">Hannity and Colmes</a></i>, replying "[N]ot now, but one day, one day, one day," when asked whether Robertson wanted Chavez taken out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>On Sunday, August 28, 2005, Chávez called on the U.S. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Justice Department</a> to investigate the matter: "My government is going to take legal action in the United States," he said in a televised speech. "If the U.S. government does not take the necessary steps, we will denounce the U.S. government at the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Attributing_statement_to_Barry_W._Lynn">Attributing statement to Barry W. Lynn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Attributing statement to Barry W. <p>On the November 5, 2005 airing of <i>The 700 Club</i>, Robertson claimed that Reverend <a href="/wiki/Barry_W._Lynn" title="Barry W. Lynn">Barry W. Lynn</a>, director of <a href="/wiki/Americans_United_for_Separation_of_Church_and_State" title="Americans United for Separation of Church and State">Americans United for Separation of Church and State</a>, said that "if a church is burning down, the local community could not send the fire engine to put the fire out because that would violate, quote, separation of church and state."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>  Rev. </p>

<p>On his November 10, 2005 broadcast of <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i>, Robertson told citizens of <a href="/wiki/Dover,_Pennsylvania" title="Dover, Pennsylvania">Dover, Pennsylvania</a> that they had rejected God by voting out of office all seven members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_board" title="School board">school board</a> who support <a href="/wiki/Intelligent_design" title="Intelligent design">intelligent design</a>.

<h2><span id="Comments_on_Ariel_Sharon.27s_health"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_Ariel_Sharon's_health">Comments on Ariel Sharon's health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Comments on Ariel Sharon's health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lead story on the January 5, 2006, edition of <i>The 700 Club</i> was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Israeli_Prime_Minister" title="Israeli Prime Minister">Israeli Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Sharon" title="Ariel Sharon">Ariel Sharon</a>'s hospitalization for a severe stroke.  After the story, Robertson said that Sharon's illness was possibly retribution from God for his recent drive to give more land to the <a href="/wiki/Palestinians" title="Palestinians">Palestinians</a>. He also claimed former prime minister <a href="/wiki/Yitzhak_Rabin" title="Yitzhak Rabin">Yitzhak Rabin</a>'s 1995 assassination may have occurred for the same reason.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p><p>The remarks drew criticism from all sides, even from other evangelicals.  For instance, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Land" title="Richard Land">Richard Land</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Baptist_Convention" title="Southern Baptist Convention">Southern Baptist Convention</a>'s Ethics and Religious Liberty Commission, said that Robertson "ought to know better" than to say such things.  He added, "...the arrogance of the statement shocks me almost as much as the insensitivity of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ted_Haggard" title="Ted Haggard">Ted Haggard</a>, then president of the <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Evangelicals" title="National Association of Evangelicals">National Association of Evangelicals</a>, said that "any doctor could have predicted (Sharon's) going to have health problems" and that his illness was medical, not divine retribution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The White House called Robertson's statement "wholly inappropriate and offensive".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>  Robertson was also chastised by Israeli officials and members of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Defamation_League" title="Anti-Defamation League">Anti-Defamation League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 11, Israel responded by announcing that Robertson would be banned from involvement in a project to build a Christian tourist attraction and pilgrimage site near the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Galilee" title="Sea of Galilee">Sea of Galilee</a> known as the Christian Heritage Center.  The plan had called for Israel leasing 35 acres (140,000 m<sup>2</sup>) of land to a group of evangelicals (including Robertson) for free to create several tourist attractions and pilgrimage sites in exchange for the evangelicals raising 50 million dollars in funding. A spokesman for the Tourism Ministry commented, "We cannot accept these statements, and we will not sign any contracts with Mr. Robertson."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>He added that the decision would not apply to all members of the evangelical community: "We want to see who in the group supports his (Robertson's) statements. Those who support the statements cannot do business with us. Those that publicly support Ariel Sharon's recovery ... are welcome to do business with us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 12, Robertson sent a letter to Sharon's son <a href="/wiki/Omri_Sharon" title="Omri Sharon">Omri</a>, apologizing for his comments. In the letter, Robertson called Ariel Sharon a "kind, gracious and gentle man" who was "carrying an almost insurmountable burden of making decisions for his nation." He added that his "concern for the future safety of your nation led me to make remarks which I can now view in retrospect as inappropriate and insensitive in light of a national grief experienced because of your father's illness ... I ask your forgiveness and the forgiveness of the people of Israel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Omri and the Israeli government accepted the apology, though it remained unclear whether the deal with Robertson would be rehabilitated.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p><p>While some observers were satisfied by the gesture, some reporters also accused Robertson of using the apology as a tactic allowing him to make such statements while promoting a public image among evangelicals as a leader who does not compromise on his values. Surprisingly, some of the harsher criticism of Robertson did not come from American or Israeli Jews, but from his fellow evangelicals and conservative Christians, who charged that Robertson's behavior did serious harm to evangelicals' image, and led to unfair generalizations and criticism of them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p><p>The fallout from Robertson's comments was still visible over a month after the event; after speaking with organizers of the <a href="/wiki/National_Religious_Broadcasters" title="National Religious Broadcasters">National Religious Broadcasters</a> February 2006 convention, Robertson wound up cancelling his planned keynote speech.
</p><p>A representative from Israel's Tourism Ministry diplomatically commented, "Pat Robertson has been a long-term friend of the state of Israel, and continues to be so."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2006, Robertson lost a bid for re-election to the board of directors of the National Religious Broadcasters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Comments_about_.22liberal_professors.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id='Comments_about_"liberal_professors"'>Comments about "liberal professors"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title='Edit section: Comments about "liberal professors"'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the March 21, 2006 broadcast of <i>The 700 Club</i>, while reviewing <i><a href="/wiki/The_Professors:_The_101_Most_Dangerous_Academics_in_America" title="The Professors: The 101 Most Dangerous Academics in America">The Professors: The 101 Most Dangerous Academics in America</a></i> by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Horowitz_(conservative_writer)" title="David Horowitz (conservative writer)">David Horowitz</a>, the subject of which is radical academics in American universities, Robertson went on to say that the 101 professors named in the book are only but a few of "thirty to forty thousand" <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">left-wing</a> professors in the United States, all of whom he accused of being "racists, murderers, sexual deviants and supporters of <a href="/wiki/Al-Qaeda" title="Al-Qaeda">Al-Qaeda</a>," further labeling them as "termites that have worked into the woodwork of our academic society."  Later in the broadcast, he went on to say, "these guys are out and out communists, they are radicals, they are, you know, some of them killers, and they are propagandists of the first order ... you don't want your child to be brainwashed by these radicals, you just don't want it to happen. Not only brainwashed but also beat up, they beat these people up, cower them into submission."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leg_press_claims">Leg press claims</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Leg press claims">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In May 2006 Robertson began claiming on his web site that through training and his "Age-Defying energy shake", he is able to <a href="/wiki/Leg_press" title="Leg press">leg press</a> 2,000 lb (907 kg) while others claim he is a liar, pushing a common energy formula.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> Two thousand pounds would be an exceptional accomplishment for a world-class athlete, to say nothing of Robertson, then in his seventies.  For comparative purposes, when <a href="/wiki/Dan_Kendra" title="Dan Kendra">Dan Kendra</a> set the Florida State University record of 1,335 lb (606 kg), the leg press machine required extensive modifications to hold the proper amount of weight, and the capillaries in both of Kendra's eyes burst during his successful attempt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Thus, Robertson's claimed achievement would add 665 lb (302 kg) to the best-ever total of Kendra, a top athlete in his physical prime, who later played in the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> and tried unsuccessfully to become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navy_SEAL" title="Navy SEAL">Navy SEAL</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to the skepticism about this claim, Robertson's website has claimed that his doctor is able to leg press 2,700 lb (1,225 kg), and that "It is not nearly as hard as the authors of these reports make it out to be."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> A video has also been provided supposedly demonstrating Robertson doing several reps with a weight of 1,000 lb (454 kg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup>  In the video Pat Robertson is seen using a 45-degree sled-type leg press machine, which reduces the effective weight to 707 lb (321 kg) (<i>sin</i>(45°) x 1000 lbs [454 kg]). He keeps the safety locks in place at the second step, which severely limits the range of possible motion.  The seat is positioned to allow approximately six inches of travel after the lock.  This setup gives Pat Robertson the maximum mechanical advantage at the last few inches of travel. In contrast, actual leg press technique is allowing the weight to slide down until the hip and knee joints are at significant flexion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-exrx_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-exrx-80">[80]</a></sup>  The video of Roberson's lift has also been criticized because it does not appear to verify his claim that he's lifting 1,000 lb (454 kg). Mike DeBonis of <i><a href="/wiki/Slate_(magazine)" title="Slate (magazine)">Slate</a></i> noted "It appears as if 16 plates are loaded on the machine. Four of them look like 100-pound [45 kg] plates, and the rest are 45s [20 kg]. That adds up to 940 pounds [425 kg]." DeBonis also noted that Robertson is using incorrect form as he "helps his legs by pushing on his knees with his arms.  That's a no-no.  He also achieves nowhere near the recommended full range of motion, which is to bring the knees to at least a 90-degree angle." DeBonis saw the claims made by Robertson and similar claims by <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Albright" title="Madeleine Albright">Madeleine Albright</a> (that she can press 400 lb (181 kg)) as yet another proof of the inferiority of that particular machine in comparison to the <a href="/wiki/Squat_(exercise)" title="Squat (exercise)">barbell squat</a>, "Most leg press machines are constructed as either a sled angled at 45 degrees or a lever. (There are some that use cables, too.) In all cases, some of the weight gets borne by the machine. You may be loading 400 pounds [180 kg], but your muscles are feeling only 200 [90 kg]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-81"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> The video has since been removed from the CBN website.
</p><p>Robertson responded to questions concerning the lift in an interview with <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>.  He maintained his claim to have done it but admitted "I didn't do it with the same form that these professional bodybuilders do, which is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_squat" title="Full squat">full squat</a>, and it's very difficult. But I did do it. I regularly can do 1,000 pounds [454 kg] and 1,200 pounds [544 kg]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_lift_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_lift-82">[82]</a></sup> He said he used an incline leg press and did "the full extension on that particular machine. They have a brake on it. I was told put the brake on.  When the professionals do it, they take the brake off and let the weight come all the way down on them. And if you don't have a lot of help, you've got a Volkswagen sitting on your hips. I didn't do that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_lift_82-1"><a href="#cite_note-CBS_lift-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2006, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Nutrition_Center" title="General Nutrition Center">General Nutrition Center</a>, a nutritional supplement retailer, announced without explanation that it would stop carrying Robertson's energy drink.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-83"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Racist_comments_about_Asian_appearance">Racist comments about Asian appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Racist comments about Asian appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the February 7, 2007 edition of <i>The 700 Club</i>, Robertson stated that people who have too much <a href="/wiki/Plastic_surgery" title="Plastic surgery">plastic surgery</a> "got the eyes like they're Oriental" and stretched his eyelids in a manner stereotypical of <a href="/wiki/Asian_people" title="Asian people">Asians</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Remarks_about_2010_Haiti_Earthquake"></span>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2010_Haiti_earthquake">2010 Haiti earthquake</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: 2010 Haiti earthquake">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the January 13, 2010 broadcast of <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i>, Robertson blamed the <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haitians</a> for making a <a href="/wiki/Deal_with_the_Devil" title="Deal with the Devil">deal with the Devil</a> during their <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution#1791_slave_rebellion" title="Haitian Revolution">1791 slave rebellion</a>, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">Haiti earthquake of January 12, 2010</a> and other misfortunes.  He told viewers of his <a href="/wiki/Christian_Broadcasting_Network" title="Christian Broadcasting Network">Christian Broadcasting Network</a>:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... something happened a long time ago in Haiti, and people might not want to talk about it, they were under the heel of the French, uh, you know, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon the third</a> and whatever, and they got together and swore a pact to the devil, they said, we will serve you, if you get us free from the French, true story.  And so the <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">devil</a> said, 'OK, it's a deal.' And they kicked the French out, the Haitians revolted and got themselves free, and ever since they have been cursed by one thing after the other, desperately poor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>He went on to state:
</p>
<link href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036" rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>That island of <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> is one island. It is cut down the middle; on the one side is Haiti on the other is the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>.  Dominican Republic is prosperous, healthy, full of resorts, etc.  Haiti is in desperate poverty. Same island. They need to have and we need to pray for them a great turning to God and out of this tragedy I'm optimistic something good may come. But right now we are helping the suffering people and the suffering is unimaginable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>The reference to a "pact to the devil" was later said by CBN to be a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">Vodou</a> ceremony conducted by <a href="/wiki/Dutty_Boukman" title="Dutty Boukman">Dutty Boukman</a> involving animal sacrifice in <a href="/wiki/Bois_Ca%C3%AFman" title="Bois Caïman">Bois Caïman</a> that began the Haitian revolution. Contrary to Robertson's claims however, this ceremony and the religion of Vodou in general does not involve <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a> or devil worship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thylefors_88-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thylefors-88">[88]</a></sup>  Robertson mistakenly identified the French government as that of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a>, as Haiti became independent (and the Revolution won) four years before <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_III" title="Napoleon III">Napoleon III</a> was born.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbsnews.com_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbsnews.com-89">[89]</a></sup>  In fact, it was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> (by far, the more famous of the two rulers), who sent a French army to try to recapture Haiti a decade after its revolution.  The force was destroyed by <a href="/wiki/Yellow_fever" title="Yellow fever">yellow fever</a> and the fierce resistance led by Haitian generals.
</p><p>Veteran Christian radio broadcaster Michael Ireland stated that this <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">Haitian Vodou</a> ceremony has long been erroneously referenced by various self-assoiling Christian sources as the "pact with the devil" or "pact to the devil" that began the Haitian revolution.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assistnews.net_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-assistnews.net-90">[90]</a></sup> This Vodou ceremony was a ceremonial impulsion to the liberation of thousands of abjectly enslaved peoples under French, mostly Christian, tyranny: and as such has been perverted over the following decades to besmear a righteous liberty-struggle as formed of wicked means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assistnews.net_90-1"><a href="#cite_note-assistnews.net-90">[90]</a></sup>  These Christians were influenced by <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_warfare" title="Spiritual warfare">spiritual warfare</a> theology and concerned that the <a href="/wiki/Jean-Bertrand_Aristide" title="Jean-Bertrand Aristide">Aristide</a> government had made efforts to incorporate the Vodou sector more fully into the political process. These Evangelicals developed a counter-narrative to the official national story. In this narrative, the ancestral spirits at the Vodou cemetery were re-cast as demons. In their view, the engagement with demons amounted to a pact that put Haiti under the rule of Satan. While some Haitian Evangelicals subscribe to this idea, most Haitian nationalists vehemently oppose it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> According to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothenburg_University" title="Gothenburg University">Gothenburg University</a> researcher Markel Thylefors, "The event of the Bois Caïman ceremony forms an important part of Haitian national identity as it relates to the very genesis of Haiti."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thylefors_88-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thylefors-88">[88]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his daily press briefing on January 14, 2010, <a href="/wiki/White_House_Press_Secretary" title="White House Press Secretary">White House Press Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Gibbs" title="Robert Gibbs">Robert Gibbs</a> said of Robertson's commentary:  "It never ceases to amaze that in times of amazing human suffering somebody says something that could be so utterly stupid."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbsnews.com_89-1"><a href="#cite_note-cbsnews.com-89">[89]</a></sup>
</p><p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mainline_Christianity" title="Mainline Christianity">Mainline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Evangelicalism" title="Evangelicalism">evangelical</a> <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christian</a> voices appeared mostly unanimous in criticizing Robertson's remarks as untrue, untimely and insensitive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_marijuana_laws">Comments on marijuana laws</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Comments on marijuana laws">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the December 16, 2010 broadcast of <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i> Pat Robertson condemned harsh sentences for people convicted of possession of <a href="/wiki/Cannabis_(drug)" title="Cannabis (drug)">cannabis</a>. Robertson stated, "We're locking up people that take a couple of puffs of marijuana, and the next thing you know they've got 10 years." He went on to say, "I'm not exactly for the use of drugs – don't get me wrong – but I just believe that criminalizing marijuana, criminalizing the possession of a few ounces of pot and that kind of thing, I mean, it's just costing us a fortune and it's ruining young people."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup>
</p><p>Robertson's remarks were applauded by the <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_the_Reform_of_Marijuana_Laws" title="National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws">National Organization for the Reform of Marijuana Laws</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Drug_Policy_Alliance" title="Drug Policy Alliance">Drug Policy Alliance</a>. Executive Director of the Drug Policy Alliance <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Nadelmann" title="Ethan Nadelmann">Ethan Nadelmann</a> said, "The people who are listening to him may roll their eyes when the Democrats say this, but when Pat Robertson says this he has credibility in the faith community."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>Appearing on <i><a href="/wiki/Good_Morning_America" title="Good Morning America">Good Morning America</a></i>, Vice President <a href="/wiki/Joe_Biden" title="Joe Biden">Joe Biden</a> condemned Robertson's commentary, saying, "I still believe it's a gateway drug.  I've spent a lot of my life as chairman of the Judiciary Committee dealing with this.  I think it would be a mistake to legalize."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_advocating_divorce">Comments advocating divorce</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Comments advocating divorce">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the September 14, 2011, broadcast of <i><a href="/wiki/The_700_Club" title="The 700 Club">The 700 Club</a></i>, a viewer talked about a friend who was bitter at God because his wife had <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer's disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, and the friend had started seeing another woman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cta_96-0"><a href="#cite_note-cta-96">[96]</a></sup> The viewer asked Robertson for his advice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cta_96-1"><a href="#cite_note-cta-96">[96]</a></sup> Robertson responded, "I know it sounds cruel, but if he's going to do something, he should divorce her and start all over again, but make sure she has custodial care and somebody looking after her."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-divorceok_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-divorceok-97">[97]</a></sup> He continued saying, "If you respect that vow, you say 'til death do us part. This is a kind of death."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-divorceok_97-1"><a href="#cite_note-divorceok-97">[97]</a></sup>
</p><p>In response, <a href="/wiki/Russell_D._Moore" title="Russell D. Moore">Russell D. Moore</a>, Dean of the School of Theology at the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Baptist_Theological_Seminary" title="Southern Baptist Theological Seminary">Southern Baptist Theological Seminary</a>, wrote that Robertson's comments were "a repudiation of the gospel of Jesus Christ" and that "Pat Robertson's cruel marriage statement is no anomaly. He and his cohorts have given us for years a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosperity_gospel" title="Prosperity gospel">prosperity gospel</a> with more in common with an <a href="/wiki/Asherah_pole" title="Asherah pole">Asherah pole</a> than a cross."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup>
</p><p>Following a backlash over Robertson's advice, Robertson said, "Basically I'm saying, adultery is not a good thing and you might as well straighten your life out and the only way to do it is to kind of get your affair with your wife in order. ... I was not giving advice to the whole world and nor was I counseling anybody to be unscriptural and leave their spouse. ... Please know that I believe the Bible. Please know that I never would tell anybody to leave their sick spouse. I never never would say such a thing because I need my spouse when I get sick and she needs me when she gets sick. In sickness and in health, I believe it!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cta_96-2"><a href="#cite_note-cta-96">[96]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_spousal_abuse">Comments on spousal abuse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Comments on spousal abuse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson was criticized in September 2012 after a man named Michael called <i>The 700 Club</i> asking for advice on what to do about his wife not respecting him. Robertson called the woman a "rebellious child" who did not want to "submit to any authority." He jokingly suggested that Michael become a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> and move to <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> where he could <a href="/wiki/Sharia#Domestic_violence" title="Sharia">beat her</a>. Critics noted that Robertson's comments about wife-beating were edited out of the episode when it appeared on the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Broadcasting_Network" title="Christian Broadcasting Network">Christian Broadcasting Network</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_Young_Earth_creationism">Comments on Young Earth creationism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Comments on Young Earth creationism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In November 2012, a viewer of <i>The 700 Club</i> wrote that one of her biggest fears was that her children and husband would not go to heaven "because they question why the Bible could not explain the existence of <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>."
</p><p>Before answering the question, Robertson acknowledged the statement was controversial by saying, "I know that people will probably try to lynch me when I say this." He then replied: "You go back in time, you've got radiocarbon dating. You got all these things, and you've got the carcasses of dinosaurs frozen in time out in the Dakotas. They're out there. So, there was a time when these giant reptiles were on the Earth, and it was before the time of the Bible. So, don't try and cover it up and make like everything was 6,000 years. That's not the Bible. If you fight science, you're going to lose your children, and I believe in telling them the way it was."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-100"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Young_Earth_creationism" title="Young Earth creationism">Young Earth creationist</a> and director of <a href="/wiki/Answers_in_Genesis" title="Answers in Genesis">Answers in Genesis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Ham" title="Ken Ham">Ken Ham</a> responded to Robertson's commentary by saying: "Not only do we have to work hard to not let our kids be led astray by the anti-God teaching of the secularists, we have to work hard to not let them be led astray by compromising church leaders like Pat Robertson. Pat Robertson gives more fodder to our enemies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.christianpost.com_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-www.christianpost.com-101">[101]</a></sup> Ham said, referring to <a href="/wiki/Secularism" title="Secularism">secularists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheists</a> who applauded Robertson online for his statements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> He went on to say that, "Such leaders — including Pat Robertson — have a lot to answer to the Lord for one day, such leaders are guilty of putting stumbling blocks in the way of kids and adults in regards to believing God's word and the Gospel."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-www.christianpost.com_101-1"><a href="#cite_note-www.christianpost.com-101">[101]</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2014, Robertson responded to a caller regarding the age of the earth: "You have to be deaf, dumb and blind to think that this Earth that we live in only has 6,000 years of existence, it just doesn't, I'm sorry ... To deny the clear record that's there before us makes us looks silly ... There's no way that all this that you have here took place in 6,000 years, it just couldn't have been done, couldn't possibly have been done."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup>
</p><p>Once again, Ham vociferously criticized Robertson's remarks saying: "Pat Robertson illustrates one of the biggest problems we have today in the church-people like Robertson compromise the Word of God with the pagan ideas of fallible men!" Ham went on to say: "Pat Robertson is not upholding the Word of God with his ridiculous statements -- he is undermining the authority of the Word."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_AIDS">Comments on AIDS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Comments on AIDS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the August 27, 2013 episode of <i>The 700 Club</i>, Robertson said that members of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_gay_community" title="San Francisco gay community">San Francisco gay community</a> would deliberately infect people with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AIDS" title="AIDS">AIDS</a> by cutting them with a sharp, infected ring while shaking hands. The comment was edited out when the clip was posted online, but as of September 5, 2013 the video was available from other outlets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-105"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> He also said that the homosexual community had put laws on the books prohibiting people from mentioning their <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the October 16, 2014 episode of <i>The 700 Club</i>, a viewer sent in an email saying she had been called by God to go on a mission trip to <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a> but friends and family had expressed fears that she could catch <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebola" title="Ebola">Ebola</a> in Kenya. In his answer, Robertson pointed out that there was not currently an outbreak of Ebola in Kenya, but warned of the risk of AIDS, stating: "You might get AIDS in Kenya, people have AIDS, you've got to be careful. I mean, the towels could have AIDS." Health experts subsequently stated that towels cannot have AIDS.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> The comment angered viewers in Kenya who took to Twitter to express their disapproval using the <a href="/wiki/Hashtag" title="Hashtag">hashtag</a> <i>someonetellPatRobertson</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> The show later apologized.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Comments_on_US-Saudi_Arabia_relations_after_the_death_of_Jamal_Khashoggi">Comments on US-Saudi Arabia relations after the death of Jamal Khashoggi</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pat_Robertson_controversies&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Comments on US-Saudi Arabia relations after the death of Jamal Khashoggi">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson received criticism after stating that "we've got an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2017_United_States-Saudi_Arabia_arms_deal" title="2017 United States-Saudi Arabia arms deal">arms deal</a> that everybody wanted a piece of ... it'll be a lot of jobs, a lot of money come to our coffers. It's not something you want to blow up willy-nilly" in the wake of the murder of <a href="/wiki/Jamal_Khashoggi" title="Jamal Khashoggi">Jamal Khashoggi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-109"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup>
</p>
