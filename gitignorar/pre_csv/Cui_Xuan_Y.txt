		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Cui Xuan</b> (崔鉉), <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> <b>Taishuo</b> (臺碩), formally the <b>Duke of Wei</b> (魏公), was an official of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">Chinese</a> dynasty <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a>, serving two terms as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Tang_Dynasty" title="Chancellor of Tang Dynasty">chancellor</a> during the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wuzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Wuzong of Tang">Emperor Wuzong</a> and Emperor Wuzong's uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Xu%C4%81nzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Xuānzong of Tang">Emperor Xuānzong</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cui_Xuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is not known when Cui Xuan was born.  He was from the "Elder Boling branch" of the prominent <a href="/wiki/Cui_clan_of_Boling" title="Cui clan of Boling">Cui clan of Boling</a> (博陵, in modern <a href="/wiki/Hengshui" title="Hengshui">Hengshui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebei" title="Hebei">Hebei</a>), which claimed ancestry from the ruling Jiang house of the <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a> state <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qi_(Shandong)" title="Qi (Shandong)">Qi</a>.  Cui Xuan's traceable ancestors included officials of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qin_Dynasty" title="Qin Dynasty">Qin Dynasty</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Han_Dynasty" title="Han Dynasty">Han Dynasty</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(265-420)" title="Jin Dynasty (265-420)">Jin Dynasty (265-420)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei" title="Northern Wei">Northern Wei</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tang_Dynasty" title="Tang Dynasty">Tang Dynasty</a>.  His grandfather Cui Jing (崔儆) served as a secretary general of the executive bureau of government (尚書省, <i>Shangshu Sheng</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBT72_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBT72-1">[1]</a></sup>  His father Cui Yuanlüe (崔元略) was a prominent official during the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xianzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Xianzong of Tang">Emperor Xianzong</a>, Emperor Xianzong's son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Muzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Muzong of Tang">Emperor Muzong</a>, and Emperor Muzong's sons <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Jingzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Jingzong of Tang">Emperor Jingzong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wenzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Wenzong of Tang">Emperor Wenzong</a>.  Cui Yuanlüe was considered a potential <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Tang_Dynasty" title="Chancellor of Tang Dynasty">chancellor</a> at times, but was never chancellor.  Cui Yuanlüe's younger brother <a href="/wiki/Cui_Yuanshi" title="Cui Yuanshi">Cui Yuanshi</a> served briefly as chancellor between the two terms that Cui Xuan would eventually serve as chancellor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>  Cui Xuan had at least one younger brother, Cui Zi (崔鎡).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBT72_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-NBT72-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Cui Xuan himself passed the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_examination" title="Imperial examination">imperial examinations</a> in the <i>Jinshi</i> class, and thereafter served three terms on the staffs of regional governors,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup> including, at one point, under the former chancellor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li_Shi_(Tang_Dynasty)" title="Li Shi (Tang Dynasty)">Li Shi</a>, the military governor (<i><a href="/wiki/Jiedushi" title="Jiedushi">Jiedushi</a></i>) of Jingnan Circuit (荊南, headquartered in modern <a href="/wiki/Jingzhou" title="Jingzhou">Jingzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBT160_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBT160-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="During_Emperor_Wuzong.27s_reign"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="During_Emperor_Wuzong's_reign">During Emperor Wuzong's reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cui_Xuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: During Emperor Wuzong's reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in the <i>Huichang</i> era (841-846) of Emperor Wenzong's brother and successor <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Wuzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Wuzong of Tang">Emperor Wuzong</a>, Cui Xuan was recalled to the capital <a href="/wiki/Chang%27an" title="Chang'an">Chang'an</a> to serve as <i>Zuo Shiyi</i> (左拾遺), a low-level advisory official at the examination bureau of government (門下省, <i>Menxia Sheng</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>  He was later made <i>Sixun Yuanwailang</i> (司勛員外郎), a low-level official at the ministry of civil service affairs (吏部, <i>Libu</i>) as well as imperial scholar (翰林學士, <i>Hanlin Xueshi</i>).  He was subsequently promoted to <i>Zhongshu Sheren</i> (中書舍人), a mid-level official at the legislative bureau (中書省, <i>Zhongshu Sheng</i>), as well as chief imperial scholar (翰林學士承旨, <i>Hanlin Xueshi Chengzhi</i>).  Emperor Wuzong favored polo and wrestling, and when Cui advised him against devotion to games, Emperor Wuzong agreed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBT160_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-NBT160-3">[3]</a></sup>  In 843, he thus made Cui <i>Zhongshu Shilang</i> (中書侍郎), the deputy head of the legislative bureau, as well as chancellor <i>de facto</i> with the designation <i>Tong Zhongshu Menxia Pingzhangshi</i> (同中書門下平章事).  It was said that, contrary to the previous years, when emperors felt compelled to discuss the commissions of chancellors with powerful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eunuch_(court_official)" title="Eunuch (court official)">eunuchs</a>, Cui's commission was made suddenly, with Emperor Wuzong only having informed the imperial scholar Wei Cong (韋悰) to have Wei draft the edict, without the prior knowledge by the other chancellors or the eunuch directors of palace communications (<i><a href="/wiki/Shumishi" title="Shumishi">Shumishi</a></i>) Liu Xingshen (劉行深) or Yang Qinyi (楊欽義).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, it was said that the lead chancellor <a href="/wiki/Li_Deyu" title="Li Deyu">Li Deyu</a> was jealous of Cui.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>  In 845, he was thus removed from chancellor post and made the minister of census (戶部尚書, <i>Hubu Shangshu</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ248_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ248-6">[6]</a></sup>  He was subsequently sent out of Chang'an to serve as the governor (觀察使, <i>Guanchashi</i>) of Shanguo Circuit (陝虢, headquartered in modern <a href="/wiki/Sanmenxia" title="Sanmenxia">Sanmenxia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="During_Emperor_Xu.C4.81nzong.27s_reign"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="During_Emperor_Xuānzong's_reign">During Emperor Xuānzong's reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cui_Xuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: During Emperor Xuānzong's reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emperor Wuzong died in 846 and was succeeded by his uncle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Xu%C4%81nzong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Xuānzong of Tang">Emperor Xuānzong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ248_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ248-6">[6]</a></sup>  Cui Xuan was then made the military governor of Hezhong Circuit (河中, headquartered in modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yuncheng,_Shanxi" title="Yuncheng, Shanxi">Yuncheng, Shanxi</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBT160_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-NBT160-3">[3]</a></sup> as well as the mayor of its capital Hezhong Municipality; he was also created the Viscount of Boling.  He was thereafter recalled to Chang'an to serve as chief imperial censor (御史大夫, <i>Yushi Daifu</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>  In 849, Emperor Xuānzong again made him chancellor with the designation <i>Tong Zhongshu Menxia Pingzhangshi</i> as well as <i>Zhongshu Shilang</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ248_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ248-6">[6]</a></sup>  He eventually came to carry the titles of <i>Zuo Pushe</i> (左僕射, one of the heads of the executive bureau (尚書省, <i>Shangshu Sheng</i>)), <i>Menxia Shilang</i> (門下侍郎, deputy head of the examination bureau), administrator of Daqing Palace (大清宮), chief scholar at Hongwen Pavilian (弘文館), and the Duke of Boling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 851, with Emperor Xuānzong tired of dealing with repeated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dangxiang" title="Dangxiang">Dangxiang</a> rebellions, Cui suggested that a paramount official be appointed to oversee the Dangxiang affairs.  Emperor Xuānzong thus made Cui's colleague <a href="/wiki/Bai_Minzhong" title="Bai Minzhong">Bai Minzhong</a> the supreme commander of the forces facing the Dangxiang.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ249_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ249-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 852, with agrarian rebels occupying Mount Ji (雞山, in modern <a href="/wiki/Nanchong" title="Nanchong">Nanchong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>), Emperor Xuānzong dispatched the general Wang Zhihong (王贄弘) to the region to deal with them.  Cui suggested that instead an imperial official be sent to persuade them to submit, so Emperor Xuānzong sent the official Liu Tong (劉潼), who was able to persuade the Mount Ji rebels to surrender.  (However, after Liu accepted their surrender, Wang and the eunuch monitor Sixian Yiyi (似先義逸) slaughtered the surrendered rebels anyway.)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ249_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ249-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 855, with Huainan Circuit (淮南, headquartered in modern <a href="/wiki/Yangzhou" title="Yangzhou">Yangzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a>) suffering from a famine, but the military governor <a href="/wiki/Du_Cong" title="Du Cong">Du Cong</a> not governing the circuit diligently, Emperor Xuānzong removed Du from the post and sent Cui out to Huainan to serve as its military governor, continuing to carry the <i>Tong Zhongshu Menxia Pingzhangshi</i> title as an honorary title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ249_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ249-7">[7]</a></sup>  He was also created the greater title of Duke of Wei.  As he departed for Huainan, Emperor Xuānzong held a great feast to send him off and personally wrote a poem to him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 858, when the Xuanshe Circuit (宣歙, headquartered in modern <a href="/wiki/Xuancheng" title="Xuancheng">Xuancheng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anhui" title="Anhui">Anhui</a>) officer Kang Quantai (康全泰) mutinied and expelled the governor Zheng Xun (鄭薰), who fled to Huainan's capital Yang Prefecture (揚州), Cui launched his troops to attack the Xuanshe rebels, so Emperor Xuānzong gave him the additional title of governor of Xuanshe.  Later in the year, he captured Xuanshe's capital Xuan Prefecture (宣州), killing Kang and his coconspirators.  He then resigned the additional post of governor of Xuanshe, which was then given to Wen Zhang (溫璋).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZZTJ249_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-ZZTJ249-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="During_Emperor_Yizong.27s_reign"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="During_Emperor_Yizong's_reign">During Emperor Yizong's reign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cui_Xuan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: During Emperor Yizong's reign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early in the <i>Xiantong</i> era (860-874) of Emperor Xuānzong's son and successor <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Yizong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Yizong of Tang">Emperor Yizong</a>, Cui Xuan was transferred to Shannan East Circuit (山南東道, headquartered in modern <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xiangfang" title="Xiangfang">Xiangfang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hubei" title="Hubei">Hubei</a>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup> and later to Jingnan Circuit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBT160_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-NBT160-3">[3]</a></sup>  In 868, with soldiers originally from Xusi Circuit (徐泗, headquartered modern <a href="/wiki/Xuzhou" title="Xuzhou">Xuzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a>) having launched a mutiny under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Pang_Xun" title="Pang Xun">Pang Xun</a> at their defensive post at Gui Prefecture (桂州, in modern <a href="/wiki/Guilin" title="Guilin">Guilin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a>) as they wanted to return home to Xusi, Cui launched his troops and prepared for a confrontation with them; as a result, the Xusi mutineers did not dare to enter Jingnan,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> and instead crossed into Jiangxi Circuit (江西, headquartered in modern <a href="/wiki/Nanchang" title="Nanchang">Nanchang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a>) and Huainan to head for Xusi.  It was said that the imperial government much approved of Cui's preparedness.  Cui eventually died at his post at Jingnan, but the year is not known.  His son <a href="/wiki/Cui_Hang" title="Cui Hang">Cui Hang</a> would serve as a chancellor during the reign of Emperor Yizong's son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xizong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Xizong of Tang">Emperor Xizong</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BT163_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-BT163-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
