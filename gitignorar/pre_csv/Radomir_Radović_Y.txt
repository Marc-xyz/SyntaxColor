<p><b>Radomir Radović</b> (1951 – 30 April 1984) was a civil engineering technician and trade unionist who advocated creating an independent trade union in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SFR_Yugoslavia" title="SFR Yugoslavia">SFR Yugoslavia</a>. He was among 28 members of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milovan_Djilas" title="Milovan Djilas">Milovan Đilas's</a> Open University arrested on 20 April 1984. He was found dead several days later, on 30 April, under unsolved circumstances.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_political_engagement">Early life and political engagement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radomir_Radovi%C4%87&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and political engagement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He graduated from a vocational technical high school. He soon became involved in trade union activism.
</p><p>He helped to organize a petition, circulated by the workers of the Belgrade engineering enterprise <i>Minela</i>, demanding the replacement and punishment of one of the directors, Radoje Stefanović, for misappropriation and theft. An investigation was started but soon dropped, the signatories being informed that Stefanović had been appointed to head the executive of the Belgrade City Council. Radović was sacked.
</p><p>He then found a job at <i>Hidrotehnika</i>, where he was also involved in a workers' petition against Mikaina Savić, a judge in the Court of Associated Labor. The petition led nowhere and the city's new top executive moved in to protect her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magaš_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Magaš-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1982. Radović wrote to the Ninth Congress of the Union of Syndicates criticizing the system and suggesting measures to stop the <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia#Collapse_of_the_Yugoslav_economy" title="Economy of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">financial crisis</a>. He requested that the trade union be free from government control and a free association of workers, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_strike" title="Right to strike">right to strike</a>, and punishment for those governing structures and individuals who contributed to the crisis. He requested that all government business be subject to control by the public. He advocated for the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_press" title="Freedom of the press">freedom of the press</a> and a public dialog on all burning issues. Radović especially requested that the Congress condemn the juntas in <a href="/wiki/1980_Turkish_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="1980 Turkish coup d'état">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martial_law_in_Poland" title="Martial law in Poland">Poland</a>. The ruling party treated this as "heretical" and connected it to support for <a href="/wiki/Lech_Wa%C5%82%C4%99sa" title="Lech Wałęsa">Lech Wałęsa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ivanović_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ivanović-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrest_and_death">Arrest and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Radomir_Radovi%C4%87&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arrest and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was arrested on 20 April 1984 together with the 28 members of the Open University led by Milovan Đilas. During the night between the 20th and the 21st, Radović was interrogated by Ranko Savić, the brother of Mikaina Savić.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Magaš_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Magaš-1">[1]</a></sup> He was released on the 21st, and then arrested again on the 22nd only to be released on the 23rd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was found dead in his weekend house in the village of <a href="/wiki/Ora%C5%A1ac_(Obrenovac)" title="Orašac (Obrenovac)">Orašac</a> near Obrenovac on 30 April 1984. Nineteen intellectuals, including <a href="/wiki/Dobrica_%C4%86osi%C4%87" title="Dobrica Ćosić">Dobrica Ćosić</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mi%C4%87a_Popovi%C4%87" title="Mića Popović">Mića Popović</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mihailo_Markovi%C4%87" title="Mihailo Marković">Mihailo Marković</a>, sent a letter regarding Radović's death to the State Secretary for Internal Affairs <a href="/wiki/Stane_Dolanc" title="Stane Dolanc">Stane Dolanc</a> on 6 May 1984, demanding an inquiry into the death of Radomir Radović. The letter was published in the <a href="/wiki/Index_on_Censorship" title="Index on Censorship">Index on Censorship</a> magazine on 1 August 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This was the first petition in postwar Yugoslavia calling on the Interior Minister to account for an unexplained death or accept responsibility for it and resign from office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The ruling party stated that Radović's death was an act of suicide by pesticides.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ivanović_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ivanović-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
