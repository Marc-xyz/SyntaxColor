<p><b>Joseph Benedict Chifley</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/tʃ/: 'ch' in 'China'">tʃ</span><span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">ɪ</span><span title="'f' in 'find'">f</span><span title="'l' in 'lie'">l</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 22 September 1885 – 13 June 1951) was an Australian politician who served as the 16th <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Australia" title="Prime Minister of Australia">Prime Minister of Australia</a>, in office from 1945 to 1949. He was leader of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Labor Party</a> from 1945 until his death.
</p><p>Chifley was born in <a href="/wiki/Bathurst,_New_South_Wales" title="Bathurst, New South Wales">Bathurst, New South Wales</a>. He joined the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Government_Railways" title="New South Wales Government Railways">state railways</a> after leaving school, eventually qualifying as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engine_driver" title="Engine driver">engine driver</a>. He was prominent in the trade union movement before entering politics, and was also a director of <i><a href="/wiki/The_National_Advocate" title="The National Advocate">The National Advocate</a></i>. After several previous unsuccessful candidacies, Chifley was elected to parliament <a href="/wiki/1928_Australian_federal_election" title="1928 Australian federal election">in 1928</a>. In 1931, he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Defence_(Australia)" title="Minister for Defence (Australia)">Minister for Defence</a> in the government of <a href="/wiki/James_Scullin" title="James Scullin">James Scullin</a>. He served in cabinet for less than a year before losing his seat at the <a href="/wiki/1931_Australian_federal_election" title="1931 Australian federal election">1931 election</a>, which saw the government suffer a landslide defeat.
</p><p>After his electoral defeat, Chifley remained involved in politics as a party official, siding with the federal Labor leadership against the <a href="/wiki/Lang_Labor" title="Lang Labor">Lang Labor</a> faction. He served on a <a href="/wiki/Royal_commission" title="Royal commission">royal commission</a> into the banking system in 1935, and in 1940 became a senior public servant in the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Munitions" title="Department of Munitions">Department of Munitions</a>. Chifley was re-elected to parliament later that year, on his third attempt since 1931. He was appointed <a href="/wiki/Treasurer_of_Australia" title="Treasurer of Australia">Treasurer</a> in the new <a href="/wiki/Curtin_Government" title="Curtin Government">Curtin Government</a> in 1941, as one of the few Labor MPs with previous ministerial experience. The following year Chifley was additionally made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_for_Postwar_Reconstruction" title="Minister for Postwar Reconstruction">Minister for Postwar Reconstruction</a>, making him one of the most powerful members of the government. He became prime minister following Curtin's death in office in 1945, defeating caretaker prime minister <a href="/wiki/Frank_Forde" title="Frank Forde">Frank Forde</a> in <a href="/wiki/1945_Australian_Labor_Party_leadership_election" title="1945 Australian Labor Party leadership election">a leadership ballot</a>.
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/1946_Australian_federal_election" title="1946 Australian federal election">1946 election</a>, Chifley was re-elected with a slightly reduced majority – the first time that an incumbent Labor government had won re-election. The war <a href="/wiki/Victory_over_Japan_Day" title="Victory over Japan Day">had ended</a> a month after he took office, and over the following four years his government embarked on an ambitious program of social reforms and nation-building schemes. These included the expansion of the <a href="/wiki/Welfare_state" title="Welfare state">welfare state</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Post-war_immigration_to_Australia" title="Post-war immigration to Australia">large-scale immigration program</a>, and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASIO" title="ASIO">ASIO</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Mountains_Scheme" title="Snowy Mountains Scheme">Snowy Mountains Scheme</a>. Some of the new legislation was successfully challenged in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court</a>, and as a result <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_referendum,_1946_(Social_Services)" title="Australian referendum, 1946 (Social Services)">the constitution was amended</a> to give the federal government extended powers over social services.
</p><p>Some of Chifley's more interventionist economic policies were poorly received by Australian business, particularly an attempt to nationalise banks. His government was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/1949_Australian_federal_election" title="1949 Australian federal election">1949 election</a>, which brought <a href="/wiki/Robert_Menzies" title="Robert Menzies">Robert Menzies</a>' <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">Liberal Party</a> to power for the first time. He stayed on as <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(Australia)" title="Leader of the Opposition (Australia)">Leader of the Opposition</a> until his death, which came a few months after the <a href="/wiki/1951_Australian_federal_election" title="1951 Australian federal election">1951 election</a>; Labor did not return to government until 1972. For his contributions to post-war prosperity, Chifley is often regarded as one of Australia's greatest prime ministers. He is held in particularly high regard by the Labor Party, with in his "<a href="/wiki/The_light_on_the_hill" title="The light on the hill">light on the hill</a>" speech seen as seminal in both the history of the party and the broader <a href="/wiki/Australian_labour_movement" title="Australian labour movement">Australian labour movement</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_and_family_background">Birth and family background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birth and family background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Joseph Benedict Chifley was born at 29 Havannah Street, <a href="/wiki/Bathurst,_New_South_Wales" title="Bathurst, New South Wales">Bathurst, New South Wales</a>, on 22 September 1885. He was the first of three sons born to Mary Anne (née Corrigan) and Patrick Chifley II. His father – a blacksmith – was born in Bathurst to Irish immigrants from <a href="/wiki/County_Tipperary" title="County Tipperary">County Tipperary</a>, while his mother was born in <a href="/wiki/County_Fermanagh" title="County Fermanagh">County Fermanagh</a>, in present-day <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp19612_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp19612-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the age of five, Chifley was sent to live with his widowed grandfather, Patrick Chifley I, who had a small farm at <a href="/wiki/Limekilns,_New_South_Wales" title="Limekilns, New South Wales">Limekilns</a>. An aunt, Mary Bridget Chifley, kept house for them. Chifley began his education at the local state school, which was known as a "half-time school" due to it being too small to offer daily classes; it shared a single teacher with a neighbouring community.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp19613_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp19613-2">[2]</a></sup> He moved back to his parents' home at the age of 13, following his grandfather's death in January 1899, and attended a <a href="/wiki/Patrician_Brothers" title="Patrician Brothers">Patrician Brothers</a> school for about two years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp19615_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp19615-3">[3]</a></sup> He was a voracious reader from a young age, and would later supplement his limited formal education by attending classes at night schools or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanics%27_institutes" title="Mechanics' institutes">mechanics' institutes</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp19616_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp19616-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railwayman">Railwayman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Railwayman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After leaving school, Chifley's first job was as a cashier's assistant at a local department store. He later worked at a tannery for a period, and then in September 1903 joined the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_Government_Railways" title="New South Wales Government Railways">New South Wales Government Railways</a> as a "shop boy" at the Bathurst <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Locomotive_shed" title="Locomotive shed">locomotive shed</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp19617_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp19617-5">[5]</a></sup> Over the following decade, he was promoted through the ranks to engine-cleaner and <a href="/wiki/Fireman_(steam_engine)" title="Fireman (steam engine)">fireman</a>, and then finally in March 1914 to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Engine-driver" title="Engine-driver">engine-driver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196111_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196111-6">[6]</a></sup> The position of driver was considered relatively prestigious, and Chifley had to sit various examinations before being certified. He developed an intimate technical understanding of his locomotives, and became a lecturer and instructor at the Bathurst Railway Institute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196112_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196112-7">[7]</a></sup> Chifley drove both goods trains and passenger trains. He was based in Bathurst and worked on the <a href="/wiki/Main_Western_railway_line,_New_South_Wales" title="Main Western railway line, New South Wales">Main Western line</a>, except for a few months in 1914 when he drove on the <a href="/wiki/Main_Southern_railway_line,_New_South_Wales" title="Main Southern railway line, New South Wales">Main Southern line</a> and worked out of <a href="/wiki/Harden,_New_South_Wales" title="Harden, New South Wales">Harden</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196111_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196111-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_unionist">Trade unionist</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trade unionist">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Chifley became involved with the <a href="/wiki/Australian_labour_movement" title="Australian labour movement">labour movement</a> as a member of the Locomotive Enginemen's Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[a]</a></sup> He never held executive office, preferring to work as an <a href="/wiki/Union_organizer" title="Union organizer">organiser</a>, but did serve as a divisional delegate to state and federal conferences. He developed a reputation for compromise, maintaining good relations with both the railway management and the more militant sections of the union.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196113_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196113-9">[8]</a></sup> However, Chifley was one of the local leaders of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_general_strike,_1917" title="Australian general strike, 1917">1917 general strike</a>, and as a result was dismissed from the railway. He and most of the other strikers were eventually reinstated, but lost seniority and related privileges; Chifley was demoted from engine-driver to fireman.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196123_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196123-10">[9]</a></sup> Despite repeated lobbying, their pre-1917 benefits were not restored until 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196128_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196128-11">[10]</a></sup> After the strike, the state government of <a href="/wiki/William_Holman" title="William Holman">William Holman</a> also de-registered their union, placing it at a severe disadvantage against <a href="/wiki/Railway_trade_unions_in_Australia" title="Railway trade unions in Australia">other railway unions</a>. Chifley worked to secure its re-registration, which occurred in 1921, and was also involved in the formation of a national union – the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Federated_Union_of_Locomotive_Enginemen" title="Australian Federated Union of Locomotive Enginemen">Australian Federated Union of Locomotive Enginemen</a> – in 1920.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196126_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196126-12">[11]</a></sup> He appeared as an expert witness before the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Court_of_Conciliation_and_Arbitration" title="Commonwealth Court of Conciliation and Arbitration">Commonwealth Arbitration Court</a> in 1924, which subsequently implemented a new federal <a href="/wiki/Industrial_award" title="Industrial award">award</a> for the enginemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196127_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196127-13">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_political_involvement">Early political involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early political involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chifley joined the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party" title="Australian Labor Party">Labor Party</a> at a young age, and was involved in state and federal election campaigns as an organiser.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196129_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196129-14">[13]</a></sup> In 1921, he replaced his father on the board of <i><a href="/wiki/The_National_Advocate" title="The National Advocate">The National Advocate</a></i>, a local newspaper that functioned as the mouthpiece of the labour movement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001171_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001171-15">[14]</a></sup> In 1922 and 1924, Chifley unsuccessfully contested Labor <a href="/wiki/Preselection" title="Preselection">preselection</a> for the state <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district_of_Bathurst" title="Electoral district of Bathurst">seat of Bathurst</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196130_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196130-16">[15]</a></sup> He was eventually chosen as the Labor candidate for the <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Macquarie" title="Division of Macquarie">Division of Macquarie</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1925_Australian_federal_election" title="1925 Australian federal election">1925 federal election</a>. Macquarie was a large and diverse electorate, covering an area from Bathurst east across the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Mountains_(New_South_Wales)" title="Blue Mountains (New South Wales)">Blue Mountains</a> to <a href="/wiki/Penrith,_New_South_Wales" title="Penrith, New South Wales">Penrith</a>, on the outskirts of Sydney; it included industrial, agricultural, and mining districts in virtually equal measure. It was one of the most <a href="/wiki/Marginal_seat" title="Marginal seat">marginal seats</a> in the country, and had last been won by Labor in 1919.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196131–32_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196131–32-17">[16]</a></sup> Lacking name recognition, Chifley lost the election to the incumbent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalist_Party_of_Australia" title="Nationalist Party of Australia">Nationalist</a> MP, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Manning" title="Arthur Manning">Arthur Manning</a>. However, he reprised his candidacy <a href="/wiki/1928_Australian_federal_election" title="1928 Australian federal election">in 1928</a>, mounting a campaign that focused on the <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Bruce" title="Stanley Bruce">Bruce Government</a>'s unpopular labour policies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001228–229_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001228–229-18">[17]</a></sup> He accused the government of  endangering the <a href="/wiki/White_Australia_policy" title="White Australia policy">White Australia policy</a> by allowing Southern European migrant workers into the country, claiming it had "allowed so many dagoes and aliens in Australia that today they are all over the country taking work which rightly belongs to all Australians". The Labor Party recorded a 6.2-point swing in Macquarie, with Chifley becoming one of three candidates in New South Wales to win seats from the government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001230–233_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001230–233-19">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scullin_Government">Scullin Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scullin Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Australian_Labor_Party_split_of_1931" title="Australian Labor Party split of 1931">Australian Labor Party split of 1931</a></div>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/1929_Australian_federal_election" title="1929 Australian federal election">1929 election</a>, Chifley was re-elected on a 10.7-point swing as Labor won a landslide victory. <a href="/wiki/James_Scullin" title="James Scullin">James Scullin</a> became the new prime minister, the fourth member of his party to hold the office. As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Backbench" title="Backbench">backbencher</a> with little parliamentary experience, Chifley did not stand for election to the <a href="/wiki/Scullin_Ministry" title="Scullin Ministry">new ministry</a>, but did join the Public Accounts Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196152–53_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196152–53-20">[19]</a></sup> As the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_Australia" title="Great Depression in Australia">Great Depression</a> worsened, he defended the government's economic response against criticism from two factions within his own party – economic conservatives led by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Lyons" title="Joseph Lyons">Joseph Lyons</a> and left-wing populists led by <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lang_(Australian_politician)" title="Jack Lang (Australian politician)">Jack Lang</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup> His loyalty paid off in March 1931, when the Labor caucus chose him to fill one of the vacancies in cabinet caused by the resignations of Lyons and <a href="/wiki/James_Fenton_(Australian_politician)" title="James Fenton (Australian politician)">James Fenton</a>. Scullin appointed him <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Defence_(Australia)" title="Minister for Defence (Australia)">Minister for Defence</a>, a portfolio that had been disregarded somewhat in the face of more pressing concerns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001258_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001258-22">[21]</a></sup> There was little appetite for policy development, and Chifley instead concentrated on finding savings in his department that could be redirected to unemployment relief. He opened up unused military camps to the homeless, and also distributed surplus military clothing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001262_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001262-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BenChifley2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="895" data-file-width="656" decoding="async" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BenChifley2.jpg/220px-BenChifley2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BenChifley2.jpg/330px-BenChifley2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BenChifley2.jpg/440px-BenChifley2.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BenChifley2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chifley in the 1930s</div></div></div>
<p>Chifley was somewhat reluctant in his support of the <a href="/wiki/Premiers%27_Plan" title="Premiers' Plan">Premiers' Plan</a> of June 1931, but believed there was no better alternative and felt bound by the principle of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabinet_solidarity" title="Cabinet solidarity">cabinet solidarity</a>. His endorsement of the plan, which required cuts to wages and pensions, was received poorly in his own constituency. Many in the local labour movement defected to the <a href="/wiki/Lang_Labor" title="Lang Labor">Lang Labor</a> faction, which opposed the plan, and his own union expelled him in August 1931.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196175–78_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196175–78-24">[23]</a></sup> Joseph Lyons reportedly offered Chifley the <a href="/wiki/Treasurer_of_Australia" title="Treasurer of Australia">treasurership</a> as an inducement to join the <a href="/wiki/United_Australia_Party" title="United Australia Party">United Australia Party</a> (UAP), the new party he had created; Chifley declined and remained a member of the Labor Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup> At the <a href="/wiki/1931_Australian_federal_election" title="1931 Australian federal election">1931 election</a>, Chifley suffered a negative swing of 16.2 points in Macquarie, losing his seat to <a href="/wiki/John_Lawson_(Australian_politician)" title="John Lawson (Australian politician)">John Lawson</a>, the UAP candidate, by just 456 votes on the final count. The Labor Party was reduced to 14 seats out of 75 in the House of Representatives, with five other ministers (including Treasurer <a href="/wiki/Ted_Theodore" title="Ted Theodore">Ted Theodore</a>) and future prime minister <a href="/wiki/John_Curtin" title="John Curtin">John Curtin</a> also losing their seats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrisp196184–85_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrisp196184–85-26">[25]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wilderness_years">Wilderness years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Wilderness years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the Great Depression, with no parliamentary salary and no chance of returning to the railway, Chifley survived on his wife's family's money and his part-ownership of the Bathurst newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_National_Advocate" title="The National Advocate">The National Advocate</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-before_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-before-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1938, Chifley and most other Labor supporters in Bathurst joined the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Labor_Party" title="Industrial Labor Party">Industrial Labor Party</a> (ILP), a breakaway organisation formed by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Heffron" title="Bob Heffron">Bob Heffron</a> and dedicated to thwarting the <a href="/wiki/Lang_Labor" title="Lang Labor">Lang Labor</a> faction that controlled the ALP in New South Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001337–338_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001337–338-28">[27]</a></sup> He was a delegate to the party's annual conference in Sydney in April 1939. After a unity conference in August 1939, the ILP members rejoined the ALP and ended Jack Lang's dominance. Chifley was subsequently elected to the ALP state executive.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001339_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001339-29">[28]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1935 the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Lyons" title="Joseph Lyons">Lyons</a> government appointed Chifley as a member of the Royal Commission on Banking, a subject on which he had become an expert. He submitted a minority report advocating that the private banks be nationalised. After an unsuccessful effort to win back Macquarie at the <a href="/wiki/1934_Australian_federal_election" title="1934 Australian federal election">1934 election</a>, Chifley finally won his seat back at the <a href="/wiki/1940_Australian_federal_election" title="1940 Australian federal election">1940 election</a> on a swing of 10 percent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-2"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-before_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-before-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Curtin_Government">Curtin Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Curtin Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chifley was appointed <a href="/wiki/Treasurer_of_Australia" title="Treasurer of Australia">Treasurer of Australia</a> (finance minister) when Labor leader <a href="/wiki/John_Curtin" title="John Curtin">John Curtin</a> formed a mid-term Labor government in 1941 following the collapse of the <a href="/wiki/Menzies_Government_(1939%E2%80%9341)" title="Menzies Government (1939–41)">first Menzies government</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-3"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-before_27-2"><a href="#cite_note-before-27">[26]</a></sup>
</p><p>Although deputy Labor leader <a href="/wiki/Frank_Forde" title="Frank Forde">Frank Forde</a> was nominally the number-two-man in the government, Chifley became the minister Curtin most relied on, controlling most domestic policy while Curtin was preoccupied with World War II. Of highest importance was war funding, followed by the strong desire to control inflation. In February 1942 he announced the pegging of wages and profits, the introduction of controls on production, trade and consumption to reduce private spending, and the transfer of surplus personal income to savings and war loans. On 15 April 1942 more price controls were introduced. On 23 July a uniform income tax, giving the Commonwealth a monopoly in this vital field, was attained when the States were defeated in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-4"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Dictionary_of_Biography" title="Australian Dictionary of Biography">Australian Dictionary of Biography</a> claims Chifley proved himself to be his country's greatest treasurer – fiscally responsible, able to transmit the necessity for a reasonable equality of sacrifice, and capable of managing a wartime economy of complexity and difficulty. Financing the war by increased taxation, loans from the Australian public, and central bank credit, he ensured that the nation did not become burdened with overseas debt, as it had been after World War I. Every budget was accompanied by his strictures on 'vigorous self-denial', labour discipline and restriction of consumer demand with the aim of controlling a huge accumulation of purchasing power.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-5"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_minister">Prime minister</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prime minister">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>When Curtin died in July 1945, Forde became Prime Minister for eight days. Chifley defeated him in the leadership ballot, replacing him as Prime Minister and Curtin as Labor leader. Once the war ended a month later, normal political life resumed, and Chifley faced <a href="/wiki/Robert_Menzies" title="Robert Menzies">Robert Menzies</a> and his new <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_of_Australia" title="Liberal Party of Australia">Liberal Party</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1946_Australian_federal_election" title="1946 Australian federal election">1946 election</a>, which Chifley won with 54 percent of the <a href="/wiki/Two-party-preferred_vote" title="Two-party-preferred vote">two-party-preferred vote</a>. It marked the first time that an incumbent federal Labor government was re-elected. In the post-war years, Chifley maintained wartime economic controls, including the highly unpopular petrol rationing. He did this partly to help Britain in its postwar economic difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-6"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inoffice_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-inoffice-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislative_achievements">Legislative achievements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legislative achievements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Prose plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Prose" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" data-file-height="48" data-file-width="48" decoding="async" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" width="40"/></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>is in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOS:LIST" title="MOS:LIST">list</a> format, but may read better as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MOS:PROSE" title="MOS:PROSE">prose</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit">converting this section</a>, if appropriate. <a href="/wiki/Help:Editing" title="Help:Editing">Editing help</a> is available.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2018</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>

<p>Feeling secure in an unprecedented second term of office, post-WW2 Labor under Chifley looked toward incremental policies friendly to the Labor platform objective of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialism</a>. According to a biographer of Chifley, his government embarked upon greater "general intervention and planning in economic and social affairs", with its policies directed towards better conditions in the workplace, full employment, and an improvement in the "equalisation of wealth, income and opportunity".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-31">[30]</a></sup> Chifley was successful in transforming the wartime economy into a peacetime economy, and undertook a number of social welfare initiatives,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aged1_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-aged1-32">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> as characterised by fairer pensions and unemployment and sickness benefits, the construction of new universities and technical colleges, and the building of 200,000 houses between 1945 and 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The radical reforming nature of the Chifley Government was such that, between 1946–49, the Australian Parliament passed 299 <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Acts</a>, a record up until then, and well beyond the previous record of the Labor Government of <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Fisher" title="Andrew Fisher">Andrew Fisher</a>, which passed 113 Acts from 1910–13.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> Among other measures, the Chifley government passed legislation to establish <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">universal health care</a> modelled on the British <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a>, including a free formulary of essential medicines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[36]</a></sup> This was successfully opposed as unconstitutional in the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a> by the <a href="/wiki/British_Medical_Association" title="British Medical Association">British Medical Association</a> (precursor of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Medical_Association" title="Australian Medical Association">Australian Medical Association</a>) in the <a href="/wiki/Attorney-General_(Vic)_ex_rel_Dale_v_Commonwealth" title="Attorney-General (Vic) ex rel Dale v Commonwealth"><i>First Pharmaceutical Benefits case</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1st_Pharmaceutical_case_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-1st_Pharmaceutical_case-38">[37]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chifley then organised one of the few successful constitutional referenda to insert a new <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Section_51(xxiiiA)_of_the_Constitution_of_Australia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Section 51(xxiiiA) of the Constitution of Australia (page does not exist)">section 51xxiiiA</a> which permitted federal legislation over pharmaceutical benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001443–44_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001443–44-39">[38]</a></sup> It authorised federal legislation over medical and dental services (but not so as to authorise any form of civil conscription), together with family allowances, benefits to students and hospital benefits, child endowment, widows' pensions, unemployment benefits, and maternity allowances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-john.curtin.edu.au_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-john.curtin.edu.au-40">[39]</a></sup> The subsequent federal legislation in relation to pharmaceutical benefits was deemed constitutional by the High Court.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2nd_Pharmaceutical_case_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-2nd_Pharmaceutical_case-41">[40]</a></sup> This paved the way for the <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_Benefits_Scheme" title="Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme">Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme</a> (PBS), an important component of Australia's modern public health system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the few successful <a href="/wiki/Referendums_in_Australia" title="Referendums in Australia">referendums</a> to modify the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_Constitution" title="Australian Constitution">Australian Constitution</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_referendum,_1946_(Social_Services)" title="Australian referendum, 1946 (Social Services)">1946 Social Services referendum</a>, took place during Chifley's term.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alp_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-alp-43">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asio_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-asio-44">[43]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-7"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup> The 1946 referendum made possible many of the Chifley Labor government's other legislative initiatives in social welfare and social provision, including the following:
</p>
<ul><li>in 1946, concessional rate radio licences were introduced for age and invalid pensioners. These concessions were later extended to widow pensioners and also to television licences;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup></li>
<li>in 1947, specific racial disqualifications other than those referring to Aboriginal Australians were removed, while the Wife's Allowance became payable to de facto wives who had lived with the pensioner for at least three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aged1_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-aged1-32">[31]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><br/>
That same year, eligibility for a Class D pension was extended to women whose husbands were imprisoned for six months or more and were over 50 years old;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li>from July 1947, a prepayment of Maternity Allowance of five pounds could be made up to four weeks before the expected date of delivery, while the Act was amended to provide for an allowance of five pounds for each child in excess of one born from a single confinement (rather than there being separate rates for twins and so on). In addition, eligibility was extended to a mother who was an alien if she had 12 months residence;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></li>
<li>in 1947, eligibility for Child's Allowance was extended to those wives whose husbands were in benevolent asylums and to single invalid pensioners of either sex who had the custody, care and control of a child aged under the age of 16. Additional Benefit of five shillings per week for the first child became available to a beneficiary making regular contributions of not less than five shillings towards the maintenance of such a child, in addition to the person having the control, care, and custody of the child.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> Amendments were also made to legislation on Child Endowment to allow Australians temporarily absent from Australia and newly arrived migrants to receive the benefit;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup></li>
<li>from July 1947, funeral benefits could be paid in respect of claimants for Age Pension or Invalid Pension who would have qualified had they lived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> Under the Social Services Consolidation Act of 1947, an additional benefit became payable in cases where a man with one or more dependent children under the age of 16 had a housekeeper who was substantially dependent on him but not employed by him, where he was not receiving benefit for his wife; a partial additional benefit became payable for a partially dependent spouse; and wives legally separated or likely to be permanently living apart from their husbands became eligible for benefit;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup></li>
<li>the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Employment_Service" title="Commonwealth Employment Service">Commonwealth Employment Service</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-31">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>the introduction of federal funds to the States for public housing construction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Federation_Press_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Federation_Press-52">[51]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Acoustic Laboratories Act, passed in 1948,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> established the Commonwealth Acoustic Laboratories to undertake scientific investigations into hearing and problems associated with noise as it affects individuals;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></li>
<li>although it failed in its attempts to establish a national health service, the Chifley Government was successful in making arrangements with the states to upgrade the quality and availability of hospital treatment. The Mental Institutions Benefits Act (1948) paid the states a benefit equal to the charges upon the relatives of mental hospital patients, in return for free treatment. This legislation marked the entry of the Commonwealth into mental health funding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[54]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The achievements of both Chifley's government and those of the previous Curtin Government in expanding Australia's social welfare services (as characterised by a tenfold increase in commonwealth expenditure on social provision between 1941 and 1949)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[55]</a></sup> were brought together under the Social Services Consolidation Act of 1947,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-john.curtin.edu.au_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-john.curtin.edu.au-40">[39]</a></sup> which consolidated the various social services benefits, liberalised some existing social security provisions, and increased the rates of various benefits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-57">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Among the government's other legislative achievements were:
</p>
<ul><li>the establishment of a separate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_citizenship" title="Australian citizenship">Australian citizenship</a> in 1948;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Mountains_Scheme" title="Snowy Mountains Scheme">Snowy Mountains Scheme</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_University_Press_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_University_Press-58">[57]</a></sup> The aim was to reduce the country's reliance on coal-fired electricity. Militant mining unions had caused industrial upsets for decades previously. The infamous coal strike of 1949 was ended by Army intervention;</li>
<li>nationalisation of <a href="/wiki/Qantas" title="Qantas">Qantas</a> in 1947 and establishment of <a href="/wiki/Trans_Australia_Airlines" title="Trans Australia Airlines">Trans Australia Airlines</a> in 1946. Small at first these two model-of-efficiency airlines were later greatly expanded by conservative governments, who kept a tight quota on the number of airliners purchased;</li>
<li>the reorganisation and enlargement of the <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a>;</li>
<li>the establishment of a civilian rehabilitation service;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-57">[56]</a></sup></li>
<li>the founding of the Australian Security Intelligence Organisation (<a href="/wiki/Australian_Security_Intelligence_Organisation" title="Australian Security Intelligence Organisation">ASIO</a>);</li>
<li>a Commonwealth Reconstruction Training Scheme was established to provide ex-servicemen with the opportunity to complete or undertake a university education. An interim five-year scholarship scheme was also established to encourage other able students to attend universities and annual grants to the universities to provide the staff and accommodation for the influx of assisted students and ex-servicemen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitlam_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-whitlam-59">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>tertiary education was also expanded through the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a> and the Commonwealth Education Office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-8"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup> The Australian National University Act was passed to provide post-graduate facilities in Australia and to augment the supply of staff for the universities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moadoph1_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-moadoph1-60">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whitlam_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-whitlam-59">[58]</a></sup></li>
<li>returned soldiers were also provided with a war gratuity and entitlement to special unemployment allowances, loans, vocational training, and preference in employment for seven years. Soldier settlement schemes were better organized than their earlier equivalents, which had brought about a great deal of hardship throughout the Twenties and thirties;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup></li>
<li>the Dairy Industry Fund was established in July 1948 with the purpose of stabilising returns from exports;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></li>
<li>in 1948, unmatched grants to the States were introduced to assist them in expanding their agricultural extension activities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[62]</a></sup></li>
<li>the establishment of a Coal Industry Tribunal and a Joint Coal Board (both in 1946) also brought significant gains for miners; and</li>
<li>life insurance came to be comprehensively regulated.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BenChifley3.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="328" decoding="async" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/BenChifley3.jpg/170px-BenChifley3.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/BenChifley3.jpg/255px-BenChifley3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/28/BenChifley3.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BenChifley3.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chifley in the 1940s</div></div></div>
<p>Among the Chifley Labor Government's legislation was the <a href="/wiki/Post-war_immigration_to_Australia#The_Chifley_years" title="Post-war immigration to Australia">post-war immigration scheme</a>, the establishment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_citizenship" title="Australian citizenship">Australian citizenship</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Mountains_Scheme" title="Snowy Mountains Scheme">Snowy Mountains Scheme</a>, the nationalisation of airlines <a href="/wiki/Qantas" title="Qantas">Qantas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trans_Australia_Airlines" title="Trans Australia Airlines">Trans Australia Airlines</a>, improvements in social services,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_University_Press_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge_University_Press-58">[57]</a></sup> the creation of the Commonwealth Employment Service,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_31-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-31">[30]</a></sup> the introduction of federal funds to the States for public housing construction,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Federation_Press_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Federation_Press-52">[51]</a></sup> the establishment of a Universities Commission for the expansion of university education,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup> the introduction of a <a href="/wiki/Pharmaceutical_Benefits_Scheme" title="Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme">Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme</a> (PBS) and free hospital ward treatment,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_31-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-31">[30]</a></sup> the reorganisation and enlargement of the <a href="/wiki/CSIRO" title="CSIRO">CSIRO</a>, and the establishment of a civilian rehabilitation service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-books.google.com_57-2"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.com-57">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>As noted by one historian, Chifley's government "balanced economic development and welfare support with restraint and regulation and provided the framework for Australia's post-war economic prosperity."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moadoph1_60-1"><a href="#cite_note-moadoph1-60">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bank_nationalisation_and_1949_coal_strike">Bank nationalisation and 1949 coal strike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Bank nationalisation and 1949 coal strike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1947, Chifley announced the government's intention to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nationalisation" title="Nationalisation">nationalise</a> the banks. This provoked massive opposition from the press, and middle-class opinion turned against Labor. The High Court found Chifley's legislation to be unconstitutional. The government <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_v_Bank_of_New_South_Wales" title="Commonwealth v Bank of New South Wales">appealed the decision in the Privy Council</a>, but it upheld the High Court's decision.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-9"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inoffice_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-inoffice-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, Chifley's government did succeed in passing the Banking and Commonwealth Bank Acts of 1945 which gave the government control over monetary policy and established the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Bank_of_Australia" title="Commonwealth Bank of Australia">Commonwealth Bank</a> as Australia's national bank.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moadoph1_60-2"><a href="#cite_note-moadoph1-60">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-10"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>A prolonged and bitter <a href="/wiki/1949_Australian_coal_strike" title="1949 Australian coal strike">strike in the coal industry</a> began in June 1949 and caused unemployment and hardship. Chifley saw the strike as a move by the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Australia" title="Communist Party of Australia">Communist Party</a> to challenge Labor's place as the party of the working class, and he sent in the army to break the strike.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-11"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inoffice_30-2"><a href="#cite_note-inoffice-30">[29]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite this, Menzies exploited the rising <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> hysteria to portray Labor as soft on Communism. These events, together with a perception that Chifley and Labor had grown increasingly arrogant in office, led to the Liberal election victory at the <a href="/wiki/1949_Australian_federal_election" title="1949 Australian federal election">1949 election</a>. While Labor won an additional four seats in a House of Representatives that had been expanded from 74 seats to 121 seats, Menzies and the Coalition won an additional 48. Labor retained a <a href="/wiki/Australian_Senate" title="Australian Senate">Senate</a> majority however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elections_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-elections-65">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition">Opposition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Opposition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chifley was now aged 64 and in poor health (like Curtin, he was a lifelong smoker), but he refused to retire from politics. Though out of government, having retained a Senate majority, Chifley continued as Labor leader and became <a href="/wiki/Leader_of_the_Opposition_(Australia)" title="Leader of the Opposition (Australia)">Leader of the Opposition</a>. The opposition Senate majority would frequently ensure the passing of Labor amendments, or outright blocking, of <a href="/wiki/Menzies_Government_(1949%E2%80%9366)" title="Menzies Government (1949–66)">Menzies Government</a> legislation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-12"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afteroffice_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-afteroffice-66">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Menzies responded by introducing a bill to ban the Communist Party of Australia in 1950. He expected Chifley to reject it and give him an excuse to call a <a href="/wiki/Double_dissolution" title="Double dissolution">double dissolution</a> election. Menzies apparently hoped to repeat his "soft-on-Communism" theme to win a majority in both chambers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-13"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afteroffice_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-afteroffice-66">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moadoph2_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-moadoph2-67">[66]</a></sup>
</p><p>However, Chifley let the bill pass after a redraft (it was ultimately <a href="/wiki/Australian_Communist_Party_v_Commonwealth" title="Australian Communist Party v Commonwealth">thrown out</a> by the High Court). However, when Chifley rejected Menzies' Commonwealth Banking Bill a few months later, Menzies called <a href="/wiki/1951_Australian_federal_election" title="1951 Australian federal election">a double dissolution election for April 1951</a>. Although Chifley managed to lead Labor to a five-seat swing in the House, Labor lost six seats in the Senate, giving the Coalition control of both chambers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-14"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afteroffice_66-2"><a href="#cite_note-afteroffice-66">[65]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elections_65-1"><a href="#cite_note-elections-65">[64]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="452" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg/220px-BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg/330px-BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg/440px-BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BenChifely_lyinginstate_1951.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chifley's coffin <a href="/wiki/Lying_in_state" title="Lying in state">lay in state</a> in Old Parliament House, June 1951.</div></div></div>
<p>A few weeks later on 13 June 1951, Chifley suffered a heart attack in his room at the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Kurrajong" title="Hotel Kurrajong">Hotel Kurrajong</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adb_21-15"><a href="#cite_note-adb-21">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-afteroffice_66-3"><a href="#cite_note-afteroffice-66">[65]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chifley at first made light of the sudden chest pains and attempted to dissuade his secretary and confidante, Phyllis Donnelly, who was making him a cup of tea, from calling a doctor. As his condition deteriorated, however, Donnelly called Dr. John Holt, who ordered Chifley's immediate removal to hospital. Chifley died in an ambulance on the way to the Canberra Community Hospital. He was pronounced dead at 10:45 pm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup>
</p><p>
Menzies heard of Chifley's demise while attending a parliamentary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ball_(dance)" title="Ball (dance)">ball</a> at King's Hall in Parliament House to celebrate the 50th Jubilee of Federation (Chifley was invited but had declined to attend). Menzies was deeply distressed and abandoned his normally impassive demeanour to announce in a halting subdued voice:</p><blockquote><p>It is my very sorrowful duty during this celebration tonight to tell you that Mr Chifley has died. I don't want to try to talk about him now because, although we were political opponents, he was a friend of mine and yours, and a fine Australian. You will all agree that in the circumstances the festivities should end. It doesn't matter about party politics on an occasion such as this. Oddly enough, in Parliament we get on very well. We sometimes find we have the warmest friendships among people whose politics are not ours. Mr Chifley served this country magnificently for years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chifley_House,_Bathurst.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1418" data-file-width="1621" decoding="async" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Chifley_House%2C_Bathurst.jpg/220px-Chifley_House%2C_Bathurst.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Chifley_House%2C_Bathurst.jpg/330px-Chifley_House%2C_Bathurst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Chifley_House%2C_Bathurst.jpg/440px-Chifley_House%2C_Bathurst.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chifley_House,_Bathurst.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ben_Chifley%27s_House" title="Ben Chifley's House">Ben Chifley's House</a> at 10 Busby Street, Bathurst, now a heritage site and house museum</div></div></div>
<p>Chifley married <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Chifley" title="Elizabeth Chifley">Elizabeth McKenzie</a> (known as "Lizzie") on 6 June 1914.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay200193_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay200193-70">[69]</a></sup> She was the daughter of a more senior railways employee, George McKenzie. The couple began courting in 1912, but had known each other since childhood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay200184_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay200184-71">[70]</a></sup> The McKenzies were Presbyterian, and Elizabeth did not want to convert to Chifley's Catholic faith. Due to the Catholic Church's opposition to mixed marriages, the couple chose to marry in a Presbyterian church in Glebe, Sydney. Their parents opposed the union and did not attend the ceremony, but they and their families were eventually reconciled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay200192–94_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay200192–94-72">[71]</a></sup> The McKenzies were relatively wealthy, and Chifley was seen as "marrying into money, or as much money as he could hope to marry into in the context of the relatively class-bound society of Bathurst".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay200185_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay200185-73">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>After their marriage, Chifley's father-in-law gave the couple a house on Busby Street, Bathurst, which they would occupy for the rest of their respective lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay200194_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay200194-74">[73]</a></sup> It is now listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_State_Heritage_Register" title="New South Wales State Heritage Register">New South Wales State Heritage Register</a> as "<a href="/wiki/Ben_Chifley%27s_House" title="Ben Chifley's House">Ben Chifley's House</a>", and has operated as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_museum" title="House museum">house museum</a> since 1973. Chifley and his wife had no children. She suffered a "serious health problem", probably a miscarriage, in about 1915,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001104_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001104-75">[74]</a></sup> and later developed chronic back pain that restricted her mobility. The couple lived mostly separate lives, initially because of her husband's work on the railways and later because of his political career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001155_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001155-76">[75]</a></sup> She rarely travelled outside Bathurst and never lived in Canberra, even while her husband was prime minister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001415–417_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001415–417-77">[76]</a></sup> She usually only visited the city for special occasions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001239,_439–440_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001239,_439–440-78">[77]</a></sup> Her health prevented from campaigning for her husband, and she was known to have little interest in politics. Nonetheless, the couple "seemingly enjoyed a close and caring relationship throughout his life".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001157_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001157-79">[78]</a></sup> She survived her husband by 11 years, dying in 1962.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001532_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001532-80">[79]</a></sup>
</p><p>According to his biographer <a href="/wiki/David_Day_(historian)" title="David Day (historian)">David Day</a>, Chifley engaged in an long-running extramarital affair with his private secretary Phyllis Donnelly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001296–297_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001296–297-81">[80]</a></sup> Day believed that their relationship began shortly after Chifley was elected in parliament in 1928,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001239_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001239-82">[81]</a></sup> and continued more or less uninterrupted until his death in 1951; she was present in his room at the <a href="/wiki/Hotel_Kurrajong" title="Hotel Kurrajong">Hotel Kurrajong</a> when he suffered his final heart attack.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001526_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001526-83">[82]</a></sup> She stayed at the same hotel, and they were known to spend their free time with each other while in Canberra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001239–240,_404_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001239–240,_404-84">[83]</a></sup> She also accompanied him on many of his travels. According to Frank Slavin, Chifley's campaign manager at the 1940 election, his wife was aware of the relationship and tolerated it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001297_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001297-85">[84]</a></sup> Day also speculated that Chifley may have had a similar relationship with Phyllis's older sister Nell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001212_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001212-86">[85]</a></sup> He assisted her financially in the 1930s, including buying her a house in Bathurst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001350_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001350-87">[86]</a></sup> Day based his conclusions on interviews conducted with the Donnelly family and other Bathurst residents who had known Chifley. His claims have been disputed by members of the Chifley family,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDay2001212_86-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDay2001212-86">[85]</a></sup> and some reviewers of his book felt there was insufficient evidence to conclude that Chifley's relationship with either of the Donnelly sisters was sexual in nature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[b]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="680" data-file-width="600" decoding="async" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg/170px-Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg/255px-Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg/340px-Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Elizabeth_Chifley.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mrs <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Chifley" title="Elizabeth Chifley">Elizabeth Chifley</a>, wife of Ben Chifley</div></div></div>
<p>More than 30 years after his death, Chifley's name still aroused partisan passions. In 1987 the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a> <a href="/wiki/Government_of_New_South_Wales" title="Government of New South Wales">Labor government</a> decided to name the planned new university in Sydney's western suburbs Chifley University. When, in 1989, a new Liberal government renamed it the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Western_Sydney" title="University of Western Sydney">University of Western Sydney</a>, controversy broke out. According to a debate on the topic, held in 1997 after the Labor Party had regained government, the decision to rename Chifley University reflected a desire to attach the name of Western Sydney to institutions of lasting significance, and that idea ultimately received the support of <a href="/wiki/Bob_Carr" title="Bob Carr">Bob Carr</a>, later the <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_New_South_Wales" title="Premier of New South Wales">Premier of New South Wales</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[90]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Chifley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" decoding="async" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg/170px-Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg/255px-Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg/340px-Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ben_Chifley_bust.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bust of sixteenth Prime Minister of Australia Ben Chifley by sculptor Ken Palmer located in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Minister%27s_Avenue" title="Prime Minister's Avenue">Prime Minister's Avenue</a> in the Ballarat Botanical Gardens</div></div></div>
<p>Places and institutions that have been named after Chifley include:
</p>
<ul><li>the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Chifley,_Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Chifley, Australian Capital Territory">Chifley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canberra" title="Canberra">Canberra</a></li>
<li>the suburb of <a href="/wiki/Chifley,_New_South_Wales" title="Chifley, New South Wales">Chifley</a> in Sydney</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Division_of_Chifley" title="Division of Chifley">Division of Chifley</a>, a federal electorate</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ben_Chifley%27s_House" title="Ben Chifley's House">his former house in Bathurst</a>, now the Chifley Home and Education Centre,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[91]</a></sup> listed on the <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_State_Heritage_Register" title="New South Wales State Heritage Register">NSW State Heritage Register</a></li>
<li>Chifley Library, the main library of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_National_University" title="Australian National University">Australian National University</a>, Canberra</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chifley_Tower" title="Chifley Tower">Chifley Tower</a> and Chifley Square in Sydney</li>
<li>Chifley Cave (formerly the Left Imperial Cave), one of the <a href="/wiki/Jenolan_Caves" title="Jenolan Caves">Jenolan Caves</a> in the Blue Mountains, New South Wales<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cave_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-cave-94">[92]</a></sup></li>
<li>several <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Government_schools_in_New_South_Wales" title="List of Government schools in New South Wales">public</a> <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">high schools</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Sydney" title="Western Sydney">Western Sydney</a> are now known as Chifley College.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[93]</a></sup></li>
<li>a grouping of <a href="/wiki/Dormitory" title="Dormitory">dormitories</a> at the Bathurst campus of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sturt_University" title="Charles Sturt University">Charles Sturt University</a> are collectively named as Chifley Halls</li></ul>
<p>In 1975 he was honoured on a postage stamp bearing his portrait issued by <a href="/wiki/Australia_Post" title="Australia Post">Australia Post</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[94]</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the locomotives driven by Chifley, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales_D50_class_locomotive" title="New South Wales D50 class locomotive">5112</a>, is preserved on a plinth at the eastern end of <a href="/wiki/Bathurst_railway_station,_New_South_Wales" title="Bathurst railway station, New South Wales">Bathurst railway station</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[95]</a></sup>
</p>
