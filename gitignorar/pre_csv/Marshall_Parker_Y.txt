<p><b>Marshall Joyner Parker</b> (April 25, 1922 – November 15, 2008)  was a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> from the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Seaboard,_North_Carolina" title="Seaboard, North Carolina">Seaboard</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northampton_County,_North_Carolina" title="Northampton County, North Carolina">Northampton County</a> in northeastern <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, Parker graduated in 1944 from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina</a> at <a href="/wiki/Chapel_Hill,_North_Carolina" title="Chapel Hill, North Carolina">Chapel Hill</a>.  In his first year of college, Parker received the Freshmen Athlete of the Year Award. Later, he lettered in boxing and football. Immediately following graduation, he entered the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">United States Marine Corps</a> and served in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific Theatre</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After military service, Parker moved briefly to <a href="/wiki/Danville,_Virginia" title="Danville, Virginia">Danville</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virginia_(U.S._state)" title="Virginia (U.S. state)">Virginia</a>, and then to <a href="/wiki/Seneca,_South_Carolina" title="Seneca, South Carolina">Seneca</a>, South Carolina.  His political career began in <a href="/wiki/Oconee_County,_South_Carolina" title="Oconee County, South Carolina">Oconee County</a>, South Carolina, where he served on the Seneca City Council and the Oconee County School Board.  He was thereafter elected as a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Carolina_State_Senate" title="South Carolina State Senate">South Carolina State Senate</a>, having represented Oconee County, which includes his hometown of Seneca. He remained in the state Senate from 1957 to 1967, in which capacity he was instrumental in the creation of his state's technical education system. He owned and operated Oconee Daries, a milk processing plant, which serviced the Golden Corner of South Carolina.
</p><p>In 1966, Parker <a href="/wiki/Party_switching_in_the_United_States" title="Party switching in the United States">switched</a> to <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> affiliation to run for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a>. The other Senate seat from South Carolina was held at the time by the Democrat-turned-Republican <a href="/wiki/Strom_Thurmond" title="Strom Thurmond">Strom Thurmond</a>, who had been elected as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_(politics)" title="Independent (politics)">Independent</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Write-in" title="Write-in">write-in</a> candidate in 1954 and as a Democrat in 1960 but switched to the GOP in 1964 to support <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barry_M._Goldwater" title="Barry M. Goldwater">Barry M. Goldwater</a> for the presidency. Parker did not challenge Thurmond in the Republican primary but instead attempted in a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_election" title="Special election">special election</a> for a two-year term to succeed former senator <a href="/wiki/Olin_D._Johnston" title="Olin D. Johnston">Olin D. Johnston</a>, who died in office in 1965. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_South_Carolina" title="Governor of South Carolina">Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Donald_S._Russell" title="Donald S. Russell">Donald S. Russell</a> appointed himself to the Johnston seat. However, Russell was unseated in the 1966 Democratic primary for the Senate by former governor <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Hollings" title="Fritz Hollings">Fritz Hollings</a>. Thereafter in the general election, Hollings narrowly defeated the Republican convert Marshall Parker.
</p><p>In this same 1966 election, the Democrat <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_E._McNair" title="Robert E. McNair">Robert E. McNair</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_South_Carolina" title="Lieutenant Governor of South Carolina">lieutenant governor</a> who succeeded Russell as governor in 1965, defeated the attorney  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joseph_O._Rogers,_Jr." title="Joseph O. Rogers, Jr.">Joseph O. Rogers, Jr.</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Manning,_South_Carolina" title="Manning, South Carolina">Manning</a>, the first Republican gubernatorial nominee of the 20th century in South Carolina.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Two years later in 1968, when Senator Hollings sought a full six-year term, he defeated Parker by a comfortable margin even though the Republican presidential nominee, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a>, had narrowly won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electoral_vote" title="Electoral vote">electoral votes</a> of South Carolina against <a href="/wiki/American_Independent_Party" title="American Independent Party">American Independent Party</a> nominee <a href="/wiki/George_Wallace" title="George Wallace">George C. Wallace</a> of <a href="/wiki/Alabama" title="Alabama">Alabama</a> and Democratic presidential nominee <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubert_H._Humphrey" title="Hubert H. Humphrey">Hubert H. Humphrey</a> of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>.
</p><p>Despite his twin defeats by Hollings for the U.S. Senate, Parker remained committed to the newly invigorated South Carolina Republican Party as well as the national GOP. He ran for Congress from <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina%27s_3rd_congressional_district" title="South Carolina's 3rd congressional district">South Carolina's 3rd congressional district</a> but was defeated. In later years, he served in the senior leadership at the <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Administration" title="Small Business Administration">Small Business Administration</a> under Presidents Nixon and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronald_W._Reagan" title="Ronald W. Reagan">Ronald W. Reagan</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Marshall_Parker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marshall Parker retired to his Oconee County farm, where he raised beef cattle.  He was a member of both the <a href="/wiki/Veterans_of_Foreign_Wars" title="Veterans of Foreign Wars">Veterans of Foreign Wars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Legion" title="American Legion">American Legion</a> and a former member of the Seneca Lions, Sertoma, and <a href="/wiki/Rotary_International" title="Rotary International">Rotary International</a> clubs.  He was a former trustee and a past president of the Capital Foundation of <a href="/wiki/Tri-County_Technical_College" title="Tri-County Technical College">Tri-County Technical College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pendleton,_South_Carolina" title="Pendleton, South Carolina">Pendleton</a>, South Carolina. The auditorium there is named in his honor.
</p><p>Parker was an active <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Methodist" title="United Methodist">United Methodist</a>. He and his wife of sixty-five years, Martha Parker, had four daughters, nine grandchildren, and one great-grandchild at the time of his death. He died at the age of eighty-six at  Oconee Medical Center in Seneca, South Carolina, after experiencing a year of declining health.
</p>
