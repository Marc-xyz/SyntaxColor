<p><b>Antonio "Tony" Manibusan Palomo</b> (June 13, 1931 – February 1, 2013) was a <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guamanian</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a>, <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalist" title="Journalist">journalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Columnist" title="Columnist">columnist</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academic" title="Academic">academic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup> Palomo served as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senator" title="Senator">senator</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Legislature_of_Guam" title="Legislature of Guam">Legislature of Guam</a> and the director of the <a href="/wiki/Guam_Museum" title="Guam Museum">Guam Museum</a> from December 1995 to June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pnc_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-pnc-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guampedia_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-guampedia-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Palomo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Palomo was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agana,_Guam" title="Agana, Guam">Agana, Guam</a>, (present-day <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hag%C3%A5t%C3%B1a" title="Hagåtña">Hagåtña</a>) on June 13, 1931, the oldest of nine children of Vicente Gogo Palomo and Dolores "Lydia" Mendiola Manibusan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pnc_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-pnc-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> He attended both Padre Palomo and Agana Elementary Schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He was ten years old when <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> forces attacked Guam on December 8, 1941, leading to the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Guam" title="Japanese occupation of Guam">occupation of the island during World War II</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup> Palomo graduated from Belmont Abbey College Prep School in <a href="/wiki/Charlotte,_North_Carolina" title="Charlotte, North Carolina">Charlotte, North Carolina</a>, in 1950.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guampedia_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-guampedia-3">[3]</a></sup> He received a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a> from the College of Journalism at <a href="/wiki/Marquette_University" title="Marquette University">Marquette University</a>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">Jesuit</a> university in <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee" title="Milwaukee">Milwaukee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, in 1954.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guampedia_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-guampedia-3">[3]</a></sup> He began his journalism career as a <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Sentinel" title="Milwaukee Sentinel">Milwaukee Sentinel</a></i> copy boy while attending Marquette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palomo returned to Guam following his graduation from Marquette.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> He married his wife, Margarita, in 1958 and the couple raised their ten children in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tamuning" title="Tamuning">Tamuning</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guampedia_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-guampedia-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Palomo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalism">Journalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Palomo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Journalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Palomo began his journalism career in Guam as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proofreader" title="Proofreader">proofreader</a> and general assignment <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reporter" title="Reporter">reporter</a> for the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guam_Daily_News" title="Guam Daily News">Guam Daily News</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> (The <i>Guam Daily News</i> is a predecessor to the modern-day <i><a href="/wiki/Pacific_Daily_News" title="Pacific Daily News">Pacific Daily News</a></i> newspaper).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup>  He served as the assistant <a href="/wiki/Managing_editor" title="Managing editor">managing editor</a> and sports editor of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guam_Daily_News" title="Guam Daily News">Guam Daily News</a></i> from 1954 until 1963.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition to his work for the <i>Pacific Daily News</i>, Palomo also worked as a Guam-based <a href="/wiki/Correspondent" title="Correspondent">correspondent</a> for the <i><a href="/wiki/Associated_Press" title="Associated Press">Associated Press</a></i> and a reporter for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>-edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Stars_and_Stripes_(newspaper)" title="Stars and Stripes (newspaper)">Stars and Stripes</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-6"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> reporting on the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> during the era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palomo was also involved with other magazines and newspapers as well. He edited the <a href="/wiki/Weekly_newspaper" title="Weekly newspaper">weekly newspaper</a>, <i>Pacifican</i>; served as both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publisher" title="Publisher">publisher</a> and editor of the monthly <a href="/wiki/Magazine" title="Magazine">magazine</a>, <i>Pacific Profile</i>; and worked as the editor of the daily newspaper, <i>Pacific Journal</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-7"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Palomo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1969, Palomo served as the President of the first Constitutional Convention of Guam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-8"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> He was also a member of the first Commission on Self-Determination for Guam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-9"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> He attended the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pacific_Conference&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="South Pacific Conference (page does not exist)">South Pacific Conference</a>, the predecessor of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretariat_of_the_Pacific_Community" title="Secretariat of the Pacific Community">Secretariat of the Pacific Community</a>, held in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noumea" title="Noumea">Noumea</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">New Caledonia</a>, in 1969 as Guam's official delegate to the conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-10"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> Palomo advised the delegation of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> to the South Pacific Commission.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-11"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> For a short time, Palomo was the <a href="/wiki/General_manager" title="General manager">general manager</a> of the Guam Tourist Commission, the forerunner of the modern-day <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guam_Visitors_Bureau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guam Visitors Bureau (page does not exist)">Guam Visitors Bureau</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-12"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Tony Palomo became the special assistant to the first elected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Guam" title="Governor of Guam">Governor of Guam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Camacho" title="Carlos Camacho">Carlos Camacho</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-13"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> Palomo was also the records manager and administrative director of the 8th Guam Legislature prior to running for elected office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-14"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palomo was elected as a Senator during the 12th, 14th, and 15th <a href="/wiki/Legislature_of_Guam" title="Legislature of Guam">Guam Legislatures</a> during the 1970s and early 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup> He served as the Chairman of the Committee on Rules and the Committee on Territorial and Federal Affairs during his tenure as a senator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-15"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>After leaving office, Palomo became a special assistant to the Assistant Secretary of the United States Department of Interior in 1982.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-16"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup> From 1986 until 1994, Palomo was a Department of the Interior desk officer for <a href="/wiki/American_Samoa" title="American Samoa">American Samoa</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a>, as well as an Interior Department's Guam field representative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-17"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kuam_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-kuam-5">[5]</a></sup> He held the position of acting Assistant Secretary of the Interior for Territorial and International Affairs for a time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-18"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historian">Historian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Palomo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historian</a> who specialized in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Guam" title="History of Guam">history of Guam</a>, Palomo taught history at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Guam" title="University of Guam">University of Guam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guam_Community_College" title="Guam Community College">Guam Community College</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1984, he published "An Island in Agony," a book documenting the <a href="/wiki/Chamorro_people" title="Chamorro people">Chamorro</a> experience during World War II and the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_Guam" title="Japanese occupation of Guam">Japanese occupation of Guam</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-pdn-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Palomo served as the Director of the <a href="/wiki/Guam_Museum" title="Guam Museum">Guam Museum</a> from December 1995 to June 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pnc_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-pnc-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guampedia_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-guampedia-3">[3]</a></sup> Palomo oversaw the opening of an exhibit of Guamanian history at the <a href="/wiki/Micronesia_Mall" title="Micronesia Mall">Micronesia Mall</a> beginning in April 2004, which has attracted more than 200,000 visitors to date.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guampedia_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-guampedia-3">[3]</a></sup> Palomo was a strong advocate for the construction of a permanent building for the Guam Museum. He retired as the museum's director on June 13, 2007.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loc_4-19"><a href="#cite_note-loc-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 5, 2013, just four days after Palomo's death, a groundbreaking ceremony was held for the new $27 million permanent museum, which was scheduled to open in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn2_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdn2-6">[6]</a></sup> The groundbreaking for the museum, which will be built at Skinner Plaza in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hag%C3%A5t%C3%B1a" title="Hagåtña">Hagåtña</a>, was attended by Palomo's widow, Margaret, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Governor_of_Guam" title="Governor of Guam">Governor</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eddie_Calvo" title="Eddie Calvo">Eddie Calvo</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn2_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-pdn2-6">[6]</a></sup> Gov. Eddie Calvo paid tribute to Palomo during the ceremony, as well as another prominent Guamanian historian, <a href="/wiki/Dirk_Ballendorf" title="Dirk Ballendorf">Dirk Ballendorf</a>, who died on February 4, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn2_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-pdn2-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tony_Palomo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tony Palomo died at Guam Memorial Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Tamuning,_Guam" title="Tamuning, Guam">Tamuning, Guam</a>, on February 1, 2013, at the age of 81.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pnc_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-pnc-2">[2]</a></sup> He was survived by his wife,  Margarita, and nine of their ten children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pnc_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-pnc-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/State_funeral" title="State funeral">state funeral</a> was held for Palomo at the Legislature of Guam on February 11, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn3_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdn3-7">[7]</a></sup> His <a href="/wiki/Eulogy" title="Eulogy">eulogy</a> was given by former Senator <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eddie_Duenas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eddie Duenas (page does not exist)">Eddie Duenas</a>, while singers Jesse Bias and Ruby Aquiningoc Santos sang <a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">The Star-Spangled Banner</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Guam_Hymn" title="The Guam Hymn">The Guam Hymn</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn3_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-pdn3-7">[7]</a></sup> Palomo, who was a member of St. Anthony/St. Victor Catholic Church, was buried at Pigo Cemetery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn3_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-pdn3-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
