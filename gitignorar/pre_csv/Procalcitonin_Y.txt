	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Procalcitonin</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold;background-color: lightblue">Figure 1: Immature Calcitonin</th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><i><a href="/wiki/Medical_diagnosis" title="Medical diagnosis">Medical diagnostics</a></i></td></tr><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound,_calcitonin.png"><img alt="A 3D cartoon of procalcitonin's parent compound, calcitonin.png" data-file-height="762" data-file-width="670" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound%2C_calcitonin.png/290px-A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound%2C_calcitonin.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound%2C_calcitonin.png/435px-A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound%2C_calcitonin.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound%2C_calcitonin.png/580px-A_3D_cartoon_of_procalcitonin%27s_parent_compound%2C_calcitonin.png 2x" width="290"/></a><div>A 3D cartoon of procalcitonin's parent compound, calcitonin</div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Procalcitonin</b> (<b>PCT</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptide</a> <a href="/wiki/Protein_precursor" title="Protein precursor">precursor</a> of the hormone <a href="/wiki/Calcitonin" title="Calcitonin">calcitonin</a>, the latter being involved with <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">calcium</a> <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>. It arises once preprocalcitonin is cleaved by <a href="/wiki/Endopeptidase" title="Endopeptidase">endopeptidase</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It was first identified by Leonard J. Deftos and Bernard A. Roos in the 1970s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It is composed of 116 <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a> and is produced by <a href="/wiki/Parafollicular_cell" title="Parafollicular cell">parafollicular cells</a> (C cells) of the <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid</a> and by the <a href="/wiki/Neuroendocrine_cell" title="Neuroendocrine cell">neuroendocrine cells</a> of the lung and the intestine.
</p><p>The level of procalcitonin in the blood stream of healthy individuals is below the limit of detection (0.01 µg/L) of clinical assays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid7989463_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid7989463-3">[3]</a></sup> The level of procalcitonin rises in a response to a pro-inflammatory stimulus, especially of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bacterium" title="Bacterium">bacterial</a> origin. It is therefore often classed as an <a href="/wiki/Acute-phase_protein" title="Acute-phase protein">acute phase reactant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Long_et_al_2012_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Long_et_al_2012-4">[4]</a></sup> The induction period for procalcitonin ranges from 4–12 hours with a half-life spanning anywhere from 22–35 hours.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> It does not rise significantly with viral or non-infectious inflammations. With the inflammatory cascade and systemic response that a severe <a href="/wiki/Infection" title="Infection">infection</a> brings, the blood levels of procalcitonin may rise multiple orders of magnitude with higher values correlating with more severe disease.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Remarkably the high procalcitonin levels produced during infections are not followed by a parallel increase in calcitonin or a decrease in serum calcium levels.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biochemistry">Biochemistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biochemistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="722" data-file-width="586" decoding="async" height="320" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png/260px-Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png/389px-Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png/519px-Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png 2x" width="260"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Production_Pathway_of_PCT_and_CT_in_healthy_and_infected_individuals.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Figure 2: Production pathway of PCT and CT in healthy and infected individuals via the induction of CALC-1 gene</div></div></div><p>PCT is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Calcitonin" title="Calcitonin">calcitonin</a> (CT) superfamily of <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a>. It is a peptide of 116 amino acid with an approximate molecular weight of 14.5 kDa, and its structure can be divided into three sections (<a class="external text" href="https://academic.oup.com/view-large/figure/92577605/174fig1.jpeg" rel="nofollow">see Figure 1</a>):<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> amino terminus (represented by the ball and stick model in Figure 1), immature calcitonin (shown in Figure 1 from PDB as the crystal structure of procalcitonin is not yet available), and calcitonin carboxyl-terminus peptide 1.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Under normal physiological conditions, active CT is produced and secreted in the C-cells of the <a href="/wiki/Thyroid" title="Thyroid">thyroid gland</a> after proteolytic cleavage of PCT, meaning, in a healthy individual, that PCT levels in circulation are very low (&lt;.05 ng/mL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> The pathway for production of PCT under normal and inflammatory conditions are shown in Figure 2.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-:7-9">[9]</a></sup> During inflammation, LPS, microbial toxin, and inflammatory mediators, such as IL-6 or TNF-α, induce the CALC-1 gene in adipoctyes, but PCT never gets cleaved to produce CT.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-:7-9">[9]</a></sup> In a healthy individual, PCT in endocrine cells is produced by CALC-1 by elevated calcium levels, glucocorticoids, CGRP, glucagon, or gastrin, and is cleaved to form CT, which is released to the blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-:7-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>PCT is located on the CALC-1 gene on <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosome</a> 11.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> Bacterial infections induce a universal increase in the CALC-1 gene expression and a release of PCT (&gt;1 μg/mL).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">[8]</a></sup> Expression of this hormone occurs in a site specific manner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-3"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> In healthy and non-infected individuals, transcription of PCT only occurs in <a href="/wiki/Neuroendocrine_cell" title="Neuroendocrine cell">neuroendocrine</a> tissue, except for the C cells in the thyroid. The formed PCT then undergoes post-translational modifications, resulting in the production small peptides and mature CT by removal of the C-terminal glycine from the immature CT by peptidylglycine α-amidating monooxygenase (PAM).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In a microbial infected individual, non-neuroendocrine tissue also secretes PCT by expression of CALC-1. A microbial infection induces a substantial increase in the expression of CALC-1, leading to the production of PCT in all differentiated cell types.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The function of PCT synthesized in nonneuroendocrine tissue due to a microbial infection is currently unknown, but, its detection aids in the differentiation of inflammatory processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-4"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_uses">Medical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medical uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sepsis">Sepsis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sepsis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Measurement of procalcitonin can be used as a marker of severe <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a> caused by bacteria and generally grades well with the degree of sepsis,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> although levels of procalcitonin in the <a href="/wiki/Blood" title="Blood">blood</a> are very low. PCT has the greatest <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(tests)" title="Sensitivity (tests)">sensitivity</a> (90%) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specificity_(tests)" title="Specificity (tests)">specificity</a> (91%) for differentiating patients with <a href="/wiki/Systemic_inflammatory_response_syndrome" title="Systemic inflammatory response syndrome">systemic inflammatory response syndrome</a> (SIRS) from those with sepsis, when compared with <a href="/wiki/Interleukin_2" title="Interleukin 2">IL-2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interleukin_6" title="Interleukin 6">IL-6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Interleukin_8" title="Interleukin 8">IL-8</a>, <a href="/wiki/C-reactive_protein" title="C-reactive protein">CRP</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TNF-alpha" title="TNF-alpha">TNF-alpha</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> Evidence is emerging that procalcitonin levels can reduce unnecessary antibiotic prescribing to people with <a href="/wiki/Lower_respiratory_tract_infection" title="Lower respiratory tract infection">lower respiratory tract infections</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuetz_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuetz-51">[51]</a></sup> Currently, procalcitonin assays are widely used in the clinical environment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yealy2009_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Yealy2009-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p><p>A meta-analysis reported a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sensitivity_(tests)" title="Sensitivity (tests)">sensitivity</a> of 76% and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Specificity_(tests)" title="Specificity (tests)">specificity</a> of 70% for bacteremia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2018 systematic review comparing PCT and <a href="/wiki/C-reactive_protein" title="C-reactive protein">C-reactive protein(CRP)</a> found PCT to have a sensitivity of 80% and a specificity of 77% in identifying septic patients.  In the study, PCT outperformed CRP in diagnostic accuracy of predicting sepsis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 2018 meta-analysis of randomized trials of over 4400 ICU patients with sepsis, researchers concluded that PCT led therapy resulted in lower mortality and lower antibiotic administration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Respiratory_illnesses">Respiratory illnesses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Respiratory illnesses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Given procalcitonin is a blood marker for bacterial infections, evidence shows that it is a useful tool in guiding the initiation and duration of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotics</a> in patients with <a href="/wiki/Bacterial_pneumonia" title="Bacterial pneumonia">bacterial pneumonia</a> and other acute respiratory infections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuetz_2017_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schuetz_2017-56">[56]</a></sup> The use of procalcitonin guided antibiotic therapy leads to lower mortality, less antibiotic usage, decreased side effects due to antibiotics and promotes good <a href="/wiki/Antimicrobial_stewardship" title="Antimicrobial stewardship">antibiotic stewardship</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuetz_2017_56-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schuetz_2017-56">[56]</a></sup>  The value in these protocols are evident since a high PCT level correlates with increased mortality in critically ill pneumonia patients especially those with a low <a class="external text" href="https://www.mdcalc.com/curb-65-score-pneumonia-severity" rel="nofollow">CURB-65 pneumonia risk factor</a> score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup>
</p><p>In adult patients with Acute Respiratory infections, a 2017 systematic review found that PCT-guided therapy reduced mortality, reduced antibiotic use(2.4 less days of antibiotics) and led to decreased <a href="/wiki/Adverse_drug_reaction" title="Adverse drug reaction">adverse drug effects</a> across a variety of clinical settings(ED, ICU, primary care clinic).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schuetz_2017_56-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schuetz_2017-56">[56]</a></sup>
</p><p>Procalcitonin-guided treatment limits antibiotic exposure with no increased mortality in patients with acute exacerbation of <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>
</p><p>Using procalcitonin to guide protocol in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asthma_exacerbation" title="Asthma exacerbation">acute asthma exacerbation</a> led to reduction in prescriptions of antibiotics in primary care clinics, emergency departments and during hospital admission.  This was apparent without an increase in ventilator days or risk of intubation.  Be that acute <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> exacerbation is one condition that leads to overuse of antibiotics worldwide, researchers concluded that PCT could help curb over-prescribing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiovascular_disease">Cardiovascular disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cardiovascular disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PCT serves a marker to help differentiate acute respiratory illness such as infection from an acute cardiovascular concern.  It also has value as a prognostic lab value in patients with <a href="/wiki/Atherosclerosis" title="Atherosclerosis">atherosclerosis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease" title="Coronary artery disease">coronary heart disease</a> as its levels correlate with the severity of the illness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/European_Society_of_Cardiology" title="European Society of Cardiology">European Society of Cardiology</a> recently released a PCT-guided algorithm for administering antibiotics in patients with <a href="/wiki/Shortness_of_breath" title="Shortness of breath">dyspnea</a> and suspected acute <a href="/wiki/Heart_failure" title="Heart failure">heart failure</a>. The guidelines use a cut off point of     .2 ng/mL and above as the point at which to give antibiotics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup>  This coincides with a 2017 review of literature which concluded that PCT can help reduce antibiotic overuse in patients presenting with acute heart failure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup>  In regards to mortality, a meta analysis of over 5000 patients with heart failure concluded that elevated PCT was reliable in predicting short term mortality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meningitis">Meningitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Meningitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blood procalcitonin levels can help confirm <a href="/wiki/Meningitis" title="Meningitis">bacterial meningitis</a> and. if negative, can effectively rule out bacterial meningitis.  This was shown in a review of over 2000 patients in which PCT had a sensitivity of 86% and a specificity of 80% for <a href="/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid" title="Cerebrospinal fluid">cerebrospinal fluid</a> PCT.  Blood PCT measurements proved superior to cerebrospinal fluid PCT with a sensitivity of 95% and a specificity of 97% as a marker for bacterial meningitis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup>
</p><p>In acute meningitis, serum PCT is useful as a biomarker for sepsis.  It can also be of use in determining viral meningitis versus bacterial meningitis. These findings are the result of a 2018 literature review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup>  This followed a 2015 meta analysis that showed that PCT had a sensitivity of 90% and a specificity of 98% in judging viral versus bacterial meningitis.  PCT also outperformed other biomarkers such as C-reactive protein.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gastrointestinal_disease">Gastrointestinal disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Gastrointestinal disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Evidence shows that an elevated PCT above .5 ng/mL could help diagnose infectious complications of <a href="/wiki/Inflammatory_bowel_disease" title="Inflammatory bowel disease">inflammatory bowel disease</a> such as abdominal abscesses, bacterial <a href="/wiki/Enterocolitis" title="Enterocolitis">enterocolitis</a> etc.  PCT can be effective in early recognition of infections in IBD patients and decisions on whether to prescribe antibiotics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kidney_disease">Kidney disease</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kidney disease">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Patients with chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal disease are at higher risk for infections, and procalcitonin has been studied in these populations, who often have higher levels. Procalcitonin can be dialyzed, and so levels are dependent upon when patients receive <a href="/wiki/Hemodialysis" title="Hemodialysis">hemodialysis</a>. While there is no formally accepted cutoff value for patients undergoing HD, using a value of greater or equal to 0.5 ng/mL yielded a sensitivity of 97-98% and a specificity of 70-96%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hepatitis">Hepatitis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hepatitis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PCT, possibly together with <a href="/wiki/C-reactive_protein" title="C-reactive protein">CRP</a>, is used to corroborate the <a href="/wiki/Model_for_End-Stage_Liver_Disease" title="Model for End-Stage Liver Disease">MELD</a> score.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid28840619_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid28840619-70">[70]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Septic_arthritis">Septic arthritis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Septic arthritis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>PCT at a cutoff  value of .5 ng/mL was effective at ruling in <a href="/wiki/Septic_arthritis" title="Septic arthritis">septic arthritis</a> in an analysis of over 8000 patients across 10 prospective studies.  PCT had a sensitivity of 54% and specificity of 95%.  The study also concluded that PCT outperforms <a href="/wiki/C-reactive_protein" title="C-reactive protein">C-reactive protein</a> in differentiating septic arthritis from non-septic arthritis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cancer">Cancer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cancer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A 2016 literature review showed that PCT has good value in diagnosing infections in oncologic patients. Moreso, it is especially effective in diagnosing major life threatening episodes in cancer patient such as bacteremia and sepsis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup>
</p><p>Procalcitonin is reliable to monitor recurrence of <a href="/wiki/Medullary_thyroid_cancer" title="Medullary thyroid cancer">Medullary Thyroid carcinoma</a>.  In detecting cancer recurrence, PCT had a sensitivity and specificity of 96% and 96% respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pediatrics">Pediatrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pediatrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a meta analysis of 17 studies, PCT had a sensitivity of 85% and a specificity of 54% in diagnosing sepsis in neonates and children.  The PCT cut off used was between 2-2.5 ng/mL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup>
</p><p>In children presenting with fever without an apparent source, a PCT level of .5 ng/mL had a sensitivity of 82% and specificity of 86%.  At a 5 ng/mL value, the sensitivity and specificity were 61% and 94%.  PCT can help the clinical decision making while identifying invasive bacterial infection in children with unexplained fever.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup>
</p><p>PCT levels correlate with the degree of illness in pediatric patients with sepsis or <a href="/wiki/Urinary_tract_infection" title="Urinary tract infection">urinary tract infections</a> making it effective as a prognostic lab value in these patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-76"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antibiotic_stewardship">Antibiotic stewardship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Antibiotic stewardship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Procalcitonin guided cessation of antibiotic use reduces duration of <a href="/wiki/Antibiotic" title="Antibiotic">antibiotic</a> exposure and lowers mortality in critically ill patients in the <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care_unit" title="Intensive care unit">Intensive Care Unit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup>
</p><p>In adult emergency department patients with respiratory tract illnesses,  PCT-guided treatment groups had reduced antibiotic use.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Procalcitonin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Excessive overdose on <a href="/wiki/Amphetamine" title="Amphetamine">amphetamine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Substituted_amphetamine" title="Substituted amphetamine">its analogs</a> can induce systemic inflammation; in a case report of amphetamine overdose, sans bacterial infection, significant elevations in procalcitonin were observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid24826347_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid24826347-79">[79]</a></sup>
</p>
