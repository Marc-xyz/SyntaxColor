<p>The <b>vampire squid</b> (<i>Vampyroteuthis infernalis</i>, lit. "vampire squid from Hell") is a small <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopod</a> found throughout temperate and tropical oceans in <a href="/wiki/Vampire_squid#Habitat_and_adaptations" title="Vampire squid">extreme deep sea conditions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Unique retractile sensory filaments justify the vampire squid's placement in its own <a href="/wiki/Order_(biology)" title="Order (biology)">order</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vampyromorphida" title="Vampyromorphida">Vampyromorphida</a>, as it shares similarities with both <a href="/wiki/Octopus" title="Octopus">octopuses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squid" title="Squid">squid</a>. As a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">phylogenetic</a> <a href="/wiki/Relict_(biology)" title="Relict (biology)">relict</a>, it is the only known surviving member of its order. The first specimens were collected on the <a href="/wiki/Valdivia_Expedition" title="Valdivia Expedition">Valdivia Expedition</a> and they were originally <a href="/wiki/Species_description" title="Species description">described</a> as an octopus in 1903 by German <a href="/wiki/Teuthology" title="Teuthology">teuthologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Chun" title="Carl Chun">Carl Chun</a>, but later assigned to a new order together with several extinct <a href="/wiki/Taxon" title="Taxon">taxa</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vampire_squid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vampire squid can reach a maximum total length around 30 cm (1 ft). Its 15-cm (6-in) gelatinous body varies in colour from velvety jet-black to pale reddish, depending on location and lighting conditions. A webbing of skin connects its eight arms, each lined with rows of fleshy spines or cirri; the inner side of this "cloak" is black. Only the distal halves (farthest from the body) of the arms have suckers. Its limpid, globular eyes, which appear red or blue, depending on lighting, are proportionately the largest in the animal kingdom at 2.5 cm (1 in) in diameter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellis_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ellis-2">[2]</a></sup> The name of the animal was inspired by its dark colour, cloaklike webbing, and red eyes, rather than habit—it feeds on <a href="/wiki/Marine_snow" title="Marine snow">detritus</a>, not blood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Seibel_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Seibel-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="988" data-file-width="1504" decoding="async" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg/220px-Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg/330px-Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg/440px-Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_dorsal_view.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dorsal view</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1500" data-file-width="1444" decoding="async" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg/220px-Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg/330px-Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg/440px-Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis_infernalis_arms.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Oral view</div></div></div>
<p>Mature adults have a pair of <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod_fin" title="Cephalopod fin">small fins</a> projecting from the lateral sides of the <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(mollusc)" title="Mantle (mollusc)">mantle</a>. These earlike fins serve as the adult's primary means of propulsion: vampire squid move through the water by flapping their fins. Their beaklike jaws are white. Within the webbing are two pouches wherein the tactile velar filaments are concealed. The filaments are analogous to a true squid's <a href="/wiki/Tentacle" title="Tentacle">tentacles</a>, extending well past the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cephalopod_arm" title="Cephalopod arm">arms</a>; but differ in origin, and represent the pair that was lost by the ancestral octopus.
</p><p>The vampire squid is almost entirely covered in light-producing organs called <a href="/wiki/Photophore" title="Photophore">photophores</a>, capable of producing disorienting flashes of light ranging in duration from fractions of a second to several minutes. The intensity and size of the photophores can also be modulated. Appearing as small, white discs, the photophores are larger and more complex at the tips of the arms and at the base of the two fins, but are absent from the undersides of the caped arms. Two larger, white areas on top of the head were initially believed to also be photophores, but are now identified as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_eyes_in_invertebrates" title="Simple eyes in invertebrates">photoreceptors</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Chromatophore" title="Chromatophore">chromatophores</a> (pigment organs) common to most <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod" title="Cephalopod">cephalopods</a> are poorly developed in the vampire squid. Although the animal is, therefore, incapable of changing its skin colour in the dramatic fashion of shallow-dwelling cephalopods, such ability would not be useful at the lightless depths where it lives.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitat_and_adaptations">Habitat and adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vampire_squid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Habitat and adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vampire squid is an extreme example of a <a href="/wiki/Deep_sea" title="Deep sea">deep sea</a> cephalopod, thought to reside at <a href="/wiki/Aphotic_zone" title="Aphotic zone">aphotic</a> (lightless) depths from 600 to 900 metres (2,000 to 3,000 ft) or more. Within this region of the world's oceans is a discrete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitat_(ecology)" title="Habitat (ecology)">habitat</a> known as the <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_minimum_zone" title="Oxygen minimum zone">oxygen minimum zone</a>. Within the zone, the <a href="/wiki/Saturation_(chemistry)" title="Saturation (chemistry)">saturation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> is too low to support aerobic <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a> in most complex organisms. Nonetheless, the vampire squid is able to live and breathe normally in the minimum zone at oxygen saturations as low as 3%, an ability that few other animals possess.
</p><p>To cope with life in the suffocating depths, vampire squids have developed several interesting adaptations. Of all deep-sea cephalopods, their mass-specific <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic rate</a> is the lowest. Their blue blood's <a href="/wiki/Hemocyanin" title="Hemocyanin">hemocyanin</a> binds and transports oxygen more efficiently than in other cephalopods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeibelChaussonLallierZal1999_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeibelChaussonLallierZal1999-5">[5]</a></sup> aided by <a href="/wiki/Gill" title="Gill">gills</a> with an especially large surface area. The animals have weak musculature, but maintain agility and buoyancy with little effort because of sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Statocyst" title="Statocyst">statocysts</a> (balancing organs akin to a human's <a href="/wiki/Inner_ear" title="Inner ear">inner ear</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStephensYoung2009_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStephensYoung2009-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">ammonium</a>-rich gelatinous tissues closely matching the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a> of the surrounding seawater.
</p><p>Like many deep-sea cephalopods, the vampire squid lacks <a href="/wiki/Cephalopod_ink" title="Cephalopod ink">ink</a> sacs. If disturbed, it will curl its arms up outwards and wrap them around its body, turning itself inside-out in a way, exposing spiny projections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> If highly agitated, it may eject a sticky cloud of bioluminescent mucus containing innumerable orbs of blue light from the arm tips. This luminous barrage, which may last nearly 10 minutes, would presumably serve to dazzle would-be predators and allow the vampire squid to disappear into the blackness without the need to swim far. The display is made only if the animal is very agitated because regenerating the mucus is metabolically costly.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vampire_squid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1545" data-file-width="2355" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Vampyroteuthis1.jpg/250px-Vampyroteuthis1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Vampyroteuthis1.jpg/375px-Vampyroteuthis1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Vampyroteuthis1.jpg/500px-Vampyroteuthis1.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dissected adult (center) and two immature specimens</div></div></div>
<p>Few specifics are known regarding the <a href="/wiki/Ontogeny" title="Ontogeny">ontogeny</a> of the vampire squid. Their development progresses through three morphologic forms: the very young animals have a single pair of fins, an intermediate form has two pairs, and the mature form again has one. At their earliest and intermediate phases of development, a pair of fins is located near the eyes; as the animal develops, this pair gradually disappears as the other pair develops.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPickford1949_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPickford1949-8">[8]</a></sup> As the animals grow and their surface area to volume ratio drops, the fins are resized and repositioned to maximize gait efficiency. Whereas the young propel themselves primarily by jet propulsion, mature adults find flapping their fins to be the most efficient means.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeibelThuesenChildress1998_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeibelThuesenChildress1998-9">[9]</a></sup> This unique ontogeny caused confusion in the past, with the varying forms identified as several species in distinct families.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung2002_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung2002-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>If hypotheses may be drawn from knowledge of other deep-sea cephalopods, the vampire squid likely reproduces slowly by way of a small number of large eggs. Growth is slow, as nutrients are not abundant at depths frequented by the animals. The vastness of their habitat and its sparse population make procreative encounters a fortuitous event. The female may store a male's hydraulically implanted <a href="/wiki/Spermatophore" title="Spermatophore">spermatophore</a> (a tapered, cylindrical satchel of <a href="/wiki/Sperm" title="Sperm">sperm</a>) for long periods before she is ready to fertilize her eggs. Once she does, she may need to brood over them for up to 400 days before they hatch. Their reproductive strategy appears to be of the <a href="/wiki/Semelparity_and_iteroparity" title="Semelparity and iteroparity">iteroparous</a> type, which is an exception amongst the otherwise semelparous Coleoidea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hatchlings are about 8 mm in length and are well-developed miniatures of the adults, with some differences. Their arms lack webbing, their eyes are smaller, and their velar filaments are not fully formed. The hatchlings are transparent and survive on a generous internal <a href="/wiki/Yolk" title="Yolk">yolk</a> for an unknown period before they begin to actively feed. The smaller animals frequent much deeper waters, perhaps feeding on <a href="/wiki/Marine_snow" title="Marine snow">marine snow</a> (falling organic detritus).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behaviour">Behaviour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vampire_squid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Behaviour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="431" data-file-width="675" decoding="async" height="140" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg/220px-Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg/330px-Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg/440px-Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vampyroteuthis_illustration.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vampire squid illustration</div></div></div>
<p>What behavioural data are known have been gleaned from ephemeral encounters with <a href="/wiki/Remotely_operated_underwater_vehicle" title="Remotely operated underwater vehicle">ROVs</a>; animals are often injured during capture, and survive up to two months in aquaria.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> An artificial environment makes reliable observation of non-defensive behaviour difficult. In May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Monterey_Bay_Aquarium" title="Monterey Bay Aquarium">Monterey Bay Aquarium</a> (California, United States) became the first to ever put this species on display.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>With their long velar filaments deployed, vampire squids have been observed drifting along in the deep, black ocean currents. If the filaments contact an entity, or if vibrations impinge upon them, the animals investigate with rapid acrobatic movements. They are capable of swimming at speeds equivalent to two body lengths per second, with an acceleration time of five seconds. However, their weak muscles limit stamina considerably.
</p><p>Unlike their relatives living in more hospitable climates, deep-sea cephalopods cannot afford to expend energy in protracted flight. Given their low metabolic rate and the low density of prey at such depths, vampire squids must use innovative predator avoidance tactics to conserve energy. Their aforementioned bioluminescent "fireworks" are combined with the writhing of glowing arms, erratic movements, and escape trajectories, making it difficult for a predator to identify a single target.
</p><p>In a threat response called the "pumpkin" or "pineapple" posture, the vampire squid inverts its caped arms back over the body, presenting an ostensibly larger form covered in fearsome-looking though harmless spines (called cirri).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The underside of the cape is heavily pigmented, masking most of the body's photophores. The glowing arm tips are clustered together far above the animal's head, diverting attack away from critical areas. If a predator were to bite off an arm tip, the vampire squid can regenerate it.
</p><p>They have eight arms but lack feeding tentacles, and instead use two retractile filaments in order to capture food. They combine the waste with mucus secreted from suckers to form balls of food. They feed on detritus, including the remains of <a href="/wiki/Gelatinous_zooplankton" title="Gelatinous zooplankton">gelatinous zooplankton</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Salp" title="Salp">salps</a>, <a href="/wiki/Larvacea" title="Larvacea">larvaceans</a>, and medusae <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellies</a>) and complete copepods, ostracods, amphipods, and isopods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHovingRobison2012_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHovingRobison2012-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Vampire squids have been found among the stomach contents of large, deepwater fish, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_grenadier" title="Giant grenadier">giant grenadiers</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> and deep-diving mammals, such as <a href="/wiki/Whale" title="Whale">whales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_lion" title="Sea lion">sea lions</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationships">Relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vampire_squid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vampylarge.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="860" data-file-width="1503" decoding="async" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Vampylarge.JPG/250px-Vampylarge.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Vampylarge.JPG/375px-Vampylarge.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Vampylarge.JPG/500px-Vampylarge.JPG 2x" width="250"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vampylarge.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pyritization" title="Pyritization">Pyritized</a> fossil of <i><a href="/wiki/Vampyronassa" title="Vampyronassa">Vampyronassa rhodanica</a></i> from the Lower <a href="/wiki/Callovian" title="Callovian">Callovian</a> of <a href="/wiki/La_Voulte-sur-Rh%C3%B4ne_(lagerst%C3%A4tte)" title="La Voulte-sur-Rhône (lagerstätte)">La Voulte-sur-Rhône</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The Vampyromorphida are characterized by derived characters such as the possession of photophores and of two velar filaments which are most probably modified arms. It also shares the inclusion of an internal <a href="/wiki/Gladius_(cephalopod)" title="Gladius (cephalopod)">gladius</a> with other <a href="/wiki/Coleoidea" title="Coleoidea">coleoids</a>, including squid, and eight webbed arms with <a href="/wiki/Cirrina" title="Cirrina">cirrate</a> octopods.
</p><p><i>Vampyroteuthis</i> shares its eight cirrate arms with the Cirrata, in which lateral cirri, or filaments, alternate with the suckers. <i>Vampyroteuthis</i> differs in that suckers are present only on the distal half of the arms while cirri run the entire length. In cirrate octopods suckers and cirri run and alternate on the entire length. Also, a close relationship between <i>Vampyroteuthis</i> and the Jurassic-Cretaceous <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Loligosepiina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Loligosepiina (page does not exist)">Loligosepiina</a> is indicated by the similarity of their gladiuses, the internal stiffening structure. However, the inclusion of <i><a href="/wiki/Vampyronassa" title="Vampyronassa">Vampyronassa rhodanica</a></i> from the middle Jurassic <a href="/wiki/La_Voulte-sur-Rh%C3%B4ne_(lagerst%C3%A4tte)" title="La Voulte-sur-Rhône (lagerstätte)">La Voulte-sur-Rhône</a> of France as a vampyroteuthid turns out to be rather doubtful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>The supposed vampyromorphids from the <a href="/wiki/Kimmeridgian" title="Kimmeridgian">Kimmeridgian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Tithonian" title="Tithonian">Tithonian</a> (156–146 mya) of <a href="/wiki/Solnhofen" title="Solnhofen">Solnhofen</a>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plesioteuthis_prisca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plesioteuthis prisca (page does not exist)">Plesioteuthis prisca</a></i>, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leptoteuthis_gigas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leptoteuthis gigas (page does not exist)">Leptoteuthis gigas</a></i>, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trachyteuthis_hastiformis" title="Trachyteuthis hastiformis">Trachyteuthis hastiformis</a></i>, cannot be positively assigned to this group; they are large species (from 35 cm in <i>P. prisca</i> to &gt; 1 m in <i>L. gigas</i>) and show features not found in vampyromorphids, being somewhat similar to the true squids, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teuthida" title="Teuthida">Teuthida</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFischerRiou2002_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFischerRiou2002-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p>
