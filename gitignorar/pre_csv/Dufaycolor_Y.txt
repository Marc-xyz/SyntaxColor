	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Dufaycolor</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dufaycolor_1956.jpg"><img alt="An elderly man, wearing a grey coat and holding a black hat sits in a garden in autumn." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3149" data-file-width="2400" decoding="async" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Dufaycolor_1956.jpg/220px-Dufaycolor_1956.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Dufaycolor_1956.jpg/330px-Dufaycolor_1956.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Dufaycolor_1956.jpg/440px-Dufaycolor_1956.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dufaycolor_1956.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A home-processed Dufaycolor 6x6 cm <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">transparency</a>, 1956</div></div></div>
<p><b>Dufaycolor</b> is an early <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">additive colour</a> <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">photographic film</a> process, introduced for <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">motion picture</a> use in 1932 and for <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">still photography</a> in 1935. It was derived from <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Dufay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Louis Dufay (page does not exist)">Louis Dufay</a>'s Dioptichrome <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">plates</a>, a glass-based product for colour still photography, introduced in France in 1909. Both Dioptichrome and Dufaycolor worked on the same principles as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autochrome" title="Autochrome">Autochrome</a> process, but achieved their results using a layer of tiny colour filter elements arrayed in a regular geometric pattern, unlike Autochrome's random array of coloured <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starch</a> grains.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-au_tint_tone_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-au_tint_tone-1">[1]</a></sup> The manufacture of Dufaycolor film ended in the late 1950s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Process">Process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dufaycolor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Color_photography" title="Color photography">photographic reproduction of natural colour</a> by means of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-and-white" title="Black-and-white">black-and-white</a> photograph taken and viewed through a <a href="/wiki/Mosaic" title="Mosaic">mosaic</a> of tiny colour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(optics)" title="Filter (optics)">filters</a> was an idea first patented and published by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Ducos_du_Hauron" title="Louis Ducos du Hauron">Louis Ducos du Hauron</a> in the late 1860s, but the incomplete colour sensitivity of contemporary photographic materials made it impractical at that time. <a href="/wiki/John_Joly" title="John Joly">John Joly</a> independently reinvented the concept in 1894 and attempted to commercialise it, but the first successful product based on this idea, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autochrome" title="Autochrome">Autochrome</a> <a href="/wiki/Photographic_plate" title="Photographic plate">plate</a>, did not reach the market until 1907. Several competing mosaic colour screen plate products soon appeared, including <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Louis_Dufay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Louis Dufay (page does not exist)">Louis Dufay</a>'s Dioptichrome plate, but the Autochrome plate remained by far the most popular. A film-based version of Autochrome was introduced in 1931, shortly before the first Dufaycolor product appeared.
</p><p>These plate and film products differed substantially only in the means used to manufacture the colour mosaic layer and its resulting pattern and fineness. Autochrome's mosaic was a random array of dyed <a href="/wiki/Potato_starch" title="Potato starch">potato starch</a> grains, too small to be individually visible without a microscope. Most competing products employed a coarser geometric pattern created by one of the many methods devised and patented during that era. Dufaycolor's filter layer was of the geometric type, but its proprietary manufacturing process produced an unusually fine-patterned mosaic.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1404" data-file-width="1684" decoding="async" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg/220px-Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg/330px-Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg/440px-Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dufay_Color_Matrix_LS01284.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Closeup of the color filter layer (réseau) embedded in the base of a Dufaycolor transparency</div></div></div>
<p>A very thin coating of <a href="/wiki/Collodion" title="Collodion">collodion</a> on one side of the <a href="/wiki/Film_base" title="Film base">film base</a> was dyed blue, printed with closely spaced fine lines using a water-repelling greasy ink, and bleached. The clear spaces created were then dyed green. The ink was removed, and new ink lines were printed at a 90-degree angle to the blue and green lines. The new gaps were bleached and dyed red, resulting in a colour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filter_(optics)" title="Filter (optics)">filter</a> mosaic, known as a <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9seau_plate" title="Réseau plate">réseau</a></i>, consisting of alternating green and blue squares between red lines, and having roughly one million colour filter elements per square inch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sowerby_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sowerby-2">[2]</a></sup> In very early years, different arrangements of the same colours were used, the lines being green or blue instead of red and sometimes intersecting the other colours diagonally. After a final ink removal and the application of an isolating varnish, the same side of the film base was coated with a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panchromatic" title="Panchromatic">panchromatic</a> black-and-white <a href="/wiki/Photographic_emulsion" title="Photographic emulsion">photographic emulsion</a>. When exposed to light through the base and its <i>réseau</i>, the bit of emulsion behind each colour element recorded only the amount of light of that primary colour striking the film at that point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brighton_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brighton-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Dufaycolor was normally a <a href="/wiki/Reversal_film" title="Reversal film">reversal film</a> which was <a href="/wiki/Photographic_processing" title="Photographic processing">processed</a> to produce the final positive image, instead of a <a href="/wiki/Negative_(photography)" title="Negative (photography)">negative</a>, on the original film. In the case of still photographs, the result, known as a <i>diapositive</i> or <i>transparency</i>, was usually viewed directly by means of a backlight, but it could also be bound up between cover glasses or mounted in a small frame for use in a projector, in which form it was commonly called a <i>slide</i>. Small-gauge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_movie" title="Home movie">home movie</a> films were also unique original positives, but to facilitate use for theatrical motion pictures, which required the production of numerous identical positive prints, a two-step negative-positive 35 mm version was introduced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pritchard_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pritchard-4">[4]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Upon projection, the <i>réseau</i> serves to filter the white projection light, so that the colours reaching the screen correspond to those in the recorded scene. For example, intensely red objects are represented by transparent areas behind the red filter elements and opaque areas behind the green and blue elements. The same principle operates with intensely green or blue objects. Less saturated tints, and non-primary colours such as orange, yellow, and purple, along with neutral grays and white, are reproduced by various proportions of red, green, and blue light blending together in the viewer's eye due to the tiny size and close spacing of the individual elements. Typical modern <a href="/wiki/LCD_television" title="LCD television">LCD video displays</a> work similarly, combining a backlit black-and-white image layer with an array of hair-thin red, green, and blue vertical filter stripes.
</p><p>Finished Dufaycolor films suffer from the two shortcomings inherent in all mosaic colour screen processes: the <i>réseau</i> absorbs most of the viewing or projection light, requiring the use of an unusually bright light for normal image brightness, and if too greatly magnified, the individual colour filter elements become disruptively visible.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_development">Product development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dufaycolor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Product development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Louis Dufay's interests were purchased by British paper manufacturing firm Spicers in 1926, which then funded research to produce a workable <a href="/wiki/Color_motion_picture_film" title="Color motion picture film">colour motion picture film</a>. In 1932, Spicers finally released Dufaycolor as a motion picture product.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfi_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-bfi-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Roll films for colour snapshots followed in 1935 and remained popular with some amateurs until manufacture ceased in the late 1950s. They were cheaper than the more sophisticated film types, some of which, most notably <a href="/wiki/Kodachrome" title="Kodachrome">Kodachrome</a>, were not available in the sizes used by typical snapshot cameras, and amateur <a href="/wiki/Darkroom" title="Darkroom">darkroom</a> enthusiasts could <a href="/wiki/Photographic_processing" title="Photographic processing">process</a> Dufaycolor at home almost as easily as black-and-white film. Medium and large format cut films for professional use were also made.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_motion_pictures">Use in motion pictures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dufaycolor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use in motion pictures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dufaycolor was used in only two British-made feature films: the two colour sequences in <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Parade_of_1935" title="Radio Parade of 1935">Radio Parade of 1935</a></i> (1934), and the all-colour <i><a href="/wiki/Sons_of_the_Sea_(film)" title="Sons of the Sea (film)">Sons of the Sea</a></i> (1939), directed by <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Elvey" title="Maurice Elvey">Maurice Elvey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bfi_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-bfi-5">[5]</a></sup> It was also used for a number of <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short films</a>; <a href="/wiki/Len_Lye" title="Len Lye">Len Lye</a>, for instance, used it for his films <i>Kaleidoscope</i> (1935), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Colour_Box" title="A Colour Box">A Colour Box</a></i> (1935), and <i>Swinging the Lambeth Walk</i> (1940). The <a href="/wiki/GPO_Film_Unit" title="GPO Film Unit">GPO Film Unit</a> used it for short documentaries such as <i>How the Teleprinter Works</i> (1940).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Dufaycolor was also used for the final minutes of the Italian <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Thrill_of_the_Skies" title="The Thrill of the Skies">The Thrill of the Skies</a></i> (1939).
</p><p>Perhaps the most widely seen example of Dufaycolor is the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Movietone_News" title="British Movietone News">British Movietone News</a> footage of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_George_V" title="King George V">King George V</a>'s 1935 <a href="/wiki/Silver_jubilee" title="Silver jubilee">silver jubilee</a> procession, as it has been excerpted in broadcast television documentaries.  
</p><p>Although less expensive than other colour films, Dufaycolor was still expensive compared to black-and-white film. As colour became more common in motion pictures, Dufaycolor was superseded by technologically superior processes, such as three-strip <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">Technicolor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastmancolor" title="Eastmancolor">Eastmancolor</a>. Dufaycolor remained the only successfully implemented <a href="/wiki/Additive_color" title="Additive color">additive</a> film stock for motion pictures until 1977, when <a href="/wiki/Polaroid_Corporation" title="Polaroid Corporation">Polaroid</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Polavision" title="Polavision">Polavision</a>, a system for making and viewing "instant" colour home movies that proved to be a spectacular commercial failure and was soon discontinued.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brighton_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Brighton-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
