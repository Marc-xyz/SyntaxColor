	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Carlota Matienzo</h1>

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Carlota Matienzo Román</b> (1881, <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">Barcelona</a> — 1926, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queens,_New_York" title="Queens, New York">Queens, New York</a>) was a <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rican</a> teacher and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feminist" title="Feminist">feminist</a>. She is credited with working for reform of the public school system in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, and as one of the founders in 1917 of the Puerto Rican Feminine League and in 1921 of the Suffragist Social League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b-miller_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-b-miller-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carlota_Matienzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Carlota Matienzo Román<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b-miller_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-b-miller-1">[1]</a></sup> was born in Barcelona, where her parents had met and married while her father was in law school. He was <a href="/wiki/Rosendo_Matienzo_Cintr%C3%B3n" title="Rosendo Matienzo Cintrón">Rosendo Matienzo Cintrón</a> from Puerto Rico. In the beginning of the 1880s, the family returned to Puerto Rico from Barcelona. They settled in Mayaguez, where her father built his law practice and became active in politics. In 1904 he was elected from the district as a member of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Puerto_Rico" title="House of Representatives of Puerto Rico">House of Representatives of Puerto Rico</a>.
</p><p>In 1907, Carlota Matienzo graduated from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="University of Puerto Rico">University of Puerto Rico</a>, among the first generation of students after the university was founded. She subsequently went to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> for graduate work, where she studied <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> She returned to Puerto Rico to become a teacher.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carlota_Matienzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In her professional life, Matienzo worked on reform in the public school system in Puerto Rico in order to extend education to all classes of children.
</p><p>She also became active in other political issues. The Puerto Rican Feminine League, the first organization of its type to work for women's rights, was founded in 1917 by women of the "propertied, professional and intellectual elite", including <a href="/wiki/Ana_Roque_de_Duprey" title="Ana Roque de Duprey">Ana Roque de Duprey</a>, Ángela Negrón Muñoz, and Matienzo, who were all teachers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b-miller_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-b-miller-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In this period women's rights groups were active throughout Latin America as well as in the United States.
</p><p>In 1921, Carlota Matienzo was one of the founders as the group changed its name to the Suffragist Social League and began to work directly for women's voting rights. Its members included medical doctors, writers and other intellectuals. She helped organize major conferences in <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce, Puerto Rico</a> and Arecibo. She was among the leaders who took women's concerns to the legislature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> After allying with the Popular Feminist Association of Working Women of Puerto Rico, the League supported universal suffrage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b-miller_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-b-miller-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Matienzo Román continued as one of the League's prominent members until 1924, when it divided into two organizations, split largely along grounds of political party affiliation of its leaders. Those who remained with the League favored the Pure Republican Party and the Republican-Socialist Coalition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b-miller_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-b-miller-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The dissidents recruited members from the Union Party and Republican-Unionist Alliance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b-miller_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-b-miller-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Matienzo Román next became a founding member of the Women's Aid Society of Puerto Rico. She worked directly to provide solutions to problems faced by women.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy_and_honors">Legacy and honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carlota_Matienzo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legacy and honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The University of Puerto Rico posthumously named a hall in her honor at the campus. In addition, it established the annual Carlota Matienzo Prize, which is awarded to graduates who demonstrate outstanding ability in teaching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brooklyn_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-brooklyn-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>In the late 20th century, Matienzo was one of 999 women selected by American artist <a href="/wiki/Judy_Chicago" title="Judy Chicago">Judy Chicago</a> for her installation, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dinner_Party" title="The Dinner Party">The Dinner Party</a></i> (1979), premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Museum" title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brooklyn_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-brooklyn-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
