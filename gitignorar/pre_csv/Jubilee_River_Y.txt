	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Jubilee River</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jubilee_weir.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="237" data-file-width="447" decoding="async" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Jubilee_weir.jpg/300px-Jubilee_weir.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Jubilee_weir.jpg 1.5x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Jubilee_weir.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Jubilee River at Slough Weir</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Jubilee River</b> is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic" title="Hydraulic">hydraulic</a> <a href="/wiki/Channel_(geography)" title="Channel (geography)">channel</a> in southern England. It is 11.6 km (7.2 mi) long<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is on average 45 metres (148 feet) wide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-2">[2]</a></sup> It was constructed in the late 1990s and early 2000s to take overflow from the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and so alleviate <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> to areas in and around the towns of <a href="/wiki/Maidenhead" title="Maidenhead">Maidenhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Windsor,_Berkshire" title="Windsor, Berkshire">Windsor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Eton,_Berkshire" title="Eton, Berkshire">Eton</a>  in the counties of <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>. It achieves this by taking water from the left (at this point eastern) bank of the Thames upstream of <a href="/wiki/Boulter%27s_Lock" title="Boulter's Lock">Boulter's Lock</a> near Maidenhead and returning it via the north bank downstream of <a href="/wiki/Eton,_Berkshire" title="Eton, Berkshire">Eton, Berkshire</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>  Although successful in its stated aims, residents of villages downstream claim it has increased flooding.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC2014-01-14_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2014-01-14-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jubilee_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dorney_bridge.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="264" data-file-width="469" decoding="async" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Dorney_bridge.jpg/220px-Dorney_bridge.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Dorney_bridge.jpg/330px-Dorney_bridge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Dorney_bridge.jpg/440px-Dorney_bridge.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dorney_bridge.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dorney Bridge</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency" title="Environment Agency">Environment Agency</a> commissioned the design and construction of the river which cost £110 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> When it was <a href="/wiki/Terraforming" title="Terraforming">formed</a>, the channel was the most expensive man-made river project undertaken in Britain in monetary terms (i.e. without adjusting for inflation), and the second largest<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> in Europe. Principal works were creation of the channel, various flow control mechanisms and bridges for road, rail and foot traffic. One of these, Dorney Bridge, takes the channel through a 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Isambard_Kingdom_Brunel" title="Isambard Kingdom Brunel">Brunel</a>-designed railway <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embankment_(transportation)" title="Embankment (transportation)">embankment</a>, and was built while the railway continued to carry arterial passenger and goods trains between London and destinations such as <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>. The Victorian 12 m (39 ft) high embankment was stabilised by <a href="/wiki/Ground_freezing" title="Ground freezing">freezing</a> over a period of three month using brine tubes cooled to −25 °C (−13 °F). At the same time as a tunnel was dug a pre-formed 50 m (160 ft) long box section was jacked through.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacked_Box_Tunnels_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacked_Box_Tunnels-6">[6]</a></sup> This created a 23 m (75 ft) wide by 9.5 m (31 ft) high tunnel into which a concrete <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">culvert</a> formed-to-fit was inserted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-2">[2]</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Black_potts_viaduct.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="288" data-file-width="384" decoding="async" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Black_potts_viaduct.jpg/220px-Black_potts_viaduct.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Black_potts_viaduct.jpg/330px-Black_potts_viaduct.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/Black_potts_viaduct.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Black_potts_viaduct.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Black Potts Viaduct</div></div></div>
<p>The channel also had to be taken through <a href="/wiki/Black_Potts_Railway_Bridge" title="Black Potts Railway Bridge">Black Potts Viaduct</a>, which carries a branch railway to Windsor. Protective structures<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> were put in place to preserve the viaduct's structural integrity.
</p><p>The channel involved complex <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">civil engineering</a> to deal with utility conduits, roads and railways, as well as ecological and social issues, entailing compulsory purchases, community lectures and consultations and a public enquiry. Conception to fruition took about twenty years.
</p><p>Defects in parts of the engineering emerged in the flood of January 2003, a serious test of the flood-relief main purpose of the channel. The channel saw flows well short of its designed maximum flow capacity, and some intended<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> weir damage, bed and bank erosion occurred.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The event prompted repairs and upgrades costing £3.5 million. The Environment Agency sued their lead design consultants for recovery of the remedial costs, and refunded the owner of the river £2.75 million in an out-of-court settlement.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jubilee_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name used during planning was the "Maidenhead, Windsor and Eton Flood Alleviation Scheme" (MWEFAS). The choice of a name for the river was put to the local population in a poll.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The result was a strong preference for 'Jubilee', as it was being completed in <a href="/wiki/Golden_Jubilee_of_Elizabeth_II" title="Golden Jubilee of Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth's Golden Jubilee</a> year of 2002 and one of the Queen's main residences is at <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle" title="Windsor Castle">Windsor Castle</a>, in one of the three towns being protected by the scheme.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_use">In use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jubilee_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On most analyses the Jubilee River looks and acts like a natural river.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Its banks have artificially constructed wildlife habitats intended to replace those lost from the banks of the Thames during urban expansion in the 19th and 20th centuries. During construction 38 hectares (94 acres) of reed beds and 5 hectares (12 acres) of wet woodland were laid down and about 250,000 trees were planted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicol_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Nicol-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The river is well used by walkers, runners, swimmers, wildlife enthusiasts and cyclists: a footpath combined with <a href="/wiki/List_of_cycle_routes_in_England" title="List of cycle routes in England">National Cycle Route 61</a> runs along virtually its entire length. A wide variety of bird life can be seen along the river, including green woodpeckers, cormorants, lapwing and red kites.
</p><p>During flooding in the early months of 2014, some residents of <a href="/wiki/Ham_Island" title="Ham Island">Ham Island</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Old_Windsor" title="Old Windsor">Old Windsor</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Wraysbury" title="Wraysbury">Wraysbury</a> said that the Jubilee River had increased the height of the flooded <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">Thames</a> in those villages which, along with much of the river upstream of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, saw water levels unprecedented since 1947. Wraysbury had also suffered significant flooding in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC2014-01-14_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC2014-01-14-4">[4]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Environment_Agency" title="Environment Agency">Environment Agency</a> in the 2010s is undertaking a widening and dredging programme of Thames works to assist with downstream flows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
