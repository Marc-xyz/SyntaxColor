<p><b>Papeloze Kerk</b> (catalogued as D49)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bakky1-1">[1]</a></sup> is a restored <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megalithic" title="Megalithic">megalithic</a> tomb (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">hunebed</i>) near <a href="/wiki/Schoonoord,_Coevorden" title="Schoonoord, Coevorden">Schoonoord</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papeloze_Kerk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The meaning of the name is disputed but it is generally thought to mean "priestless church" (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">paap</i> = priest) or "popeless church".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky1_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bakky1-1">[1]</a></sup> It is sometimes said that anti-Catholic (anti-"popish") sermons were held here in the 16th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dk-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Papeloze_Kerk&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The monument was heavily damaged in the 1860s by people using it as a quarry for building material.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-bakky2-3">[3]</a></sup> It was excavated in 1938 and again in 1958 by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Egges_van_Giffen" title="Albert Egges van Giffen">A. E. van Giffen</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-bakky2-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1959 he chose to restore it as a demonstration model of a Dutch "hunebed" tomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dk_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dk-2">[2]</a></sup> Some of the stones were taken from elsewhere, including eleven stones taken from another hunebed (D33).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky2_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-bakky2-3">[3]</a></sup> Half the tomb was covered in earth and the chamber was reconstructed with dry-stone walling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky2_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-bakky2-3">[3]</a></sup> The other half of the tomb was left in skeleton form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakky2_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-bakky2-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
