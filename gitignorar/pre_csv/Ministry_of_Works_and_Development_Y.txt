<p>The <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> <b>Ministry of Works and Development</b>, formerly the <b>Department of Public Works</b> and often referred to as the Public Works Department or PWD, was founded in 1876 and disestablished and privatised in 1988. The Ministry had its own <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Cabinet" title="New Zealand Cabinet">Cabinet</a>-level responsible minister, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Public_Works_(New_Zealand)" title="Minister of Public Works (New Zealand)">Minister of Works</a> or Minister of Public Works.
</p><p>Historically, the state has played an important part in developing the New Zealand economy. For many years the Public Works Department (which became the Ministry of Works in 1948 and the Ministry of Works and Development in 1974) undertook most major construction work in New Zealand, including roads, railways and power stations. After the reform of the state sector, beginning in 1984, the ministry disappeared and its remnants now have to compete for government work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_operations">Structure and operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Works_and_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure and operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Restored_building,_Napier_NZ.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" decoding="async" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Restored_building%2C_Napier_NZ.jpg/220px-Restored_building%2C_Napier_NZ.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Restored_building%2C_Napier_NZ.jpg/330px-Restored_building%2C_Napier_NZ.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Restored_building%2C_Napier_NZ.jpg/440px-Restored_building%2C_Napier_NZ.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Restored_building,_Napier_NZ.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former Ministry of Works Building in Napier, built in 1938 in <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stripped_Classical" title="Stripped Classical">Stripped Classical</a> style<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Head Office of the Ministry was in the Vogel Building in Wellington, named after former Premier Sir <a href="/wiki/Julius_Vogel" title="Julius Vogel">Julius Vogel</a>, who helped create the Public Works Department during his term in office, through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Statutes_of_New_Zealand_(1840%E2%80%9390)" title="List of Statutes of New Zealand (1840–90)">Immigration and Public Works Act 1870</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> This building held the <i>Vogel Computer</i>, one of the largest in New Zealand and used by several government departments for engineering work. The Ministry moved to the Vogel Building in about 1965 from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Government_Buildings,_Wellington" title="Old Government Buildings, Wellington">Old Government Building</a> on Lambton Quay.
</p><p>The ministry was renamed the <b>Ministry of Works</b> on 16 March 1943 under the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Statutes_of_New_Zealand_(1935%E2%80%9349)" title="List of Statutes of New Zealand (1935–49)">Ministry of Works Act</a>. This was to reflect the extended wartime functions, when the Minister explained it was, "to ensure that, whilst the building and constructional potential of the country is limited by war and immediate post-war conditions, it is assembled and utilized in the most efficient manner from the point of view of the national interest".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1944 the ministry was involved in the "great furniture scandal" when asked to order items of furniture for the new Legation in Moscow, to be headed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Boswell" title="Charles Boswell">Charles Boswell</a>. The list of items to be shipped from New Zealand to Moscow (via Tehran and Central Asia) included 40 armchairs, 10 couches, a billiard table, and palm stands. Apparently made after looking at furniture in Government House and ministerial houses, the order could have seated almost the entire House of Representatives; it was cancelled by Prime Minister <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Fraser_(New_Zealand_politician)" title="Peter Fraser (New Zealand politician)">Peter Fraser</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the latter years of the Ministry there were seven District Offices (Auckland, Hamilton, Wanganui, Napier, Wellington, Christchurch and Dunedin) each headed by a District Commissioner of Works. In each District there were a number of Residency Offices (headed by a Resident Engineer) and each had a number of Depots. In addition there were Project Offices set up for a particular purpose, such as to build a power project, airport, tunnel or irrigation scheme.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissolution">Dissolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Works_and_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dissolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the policy functions were either disestablished or passed on to other Government departments, the commercial operations were set up as Works and Development Services Corporation (a state-owned enterprise) and the computing bureau and the buildings maintenance units were sold. The corporation had two main subsidiaries, Works Consultancy Services and Works Civil Construction. These were sold in 1996 and became <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opus_International_Group" title="Opus International Group">Opus International Consultants</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Works_Infrastructure" title="Works Infrastructure">Works Infrastructure</a> respectively, and the corporation was disestablished.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Railways">Railways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Works_and_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Railways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ministry_of_Works_and_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Under the Public Works Act 1876, the Department of Public Works was responsible for the operation of <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport_in_New_Zealand" title="Rail transport in New Zealand">New Zealand's railway network</a> from 1876 until 1880, when operations were transferred to the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Railways_Department" title="New Zealand Railways Department">New Zealand Railways Department</a>. This transfer did not end the PWD's railway operations, as it still operated railway lines when under construction, sometimes providing revenue services prior to the official transfer of the line to the Railways Department. The PWD owned its own locomotives and rolling stock, some second-hand from the Railways Department, and it operated some small railway lines that were never transferred to the Railways Department. One example is a 6.4 km <a href="/wiki/Branch_line" title="Branch line">branch line</a> built in 1928 from near the terminus of the Railways Department's <a href="/wiki/Kurow_Branch" title="Kurow Branch">Kurow Branch</a> to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydro-electric_dam" title="Hydro-electric dam">hydro-electric dam</a> project on the <a href="/wiki/Waitaki_River" title="Waitaki River">Waitaki River</a>. This branch was not solely used to service the dam project; the PWD used its own rolling stock to provide a service for school children who attended school in <a href="/wiki/Kurow" title="Kurow">Kurow</a>, and occasionally special Railways Department trains operated on the line with PWD motive power, including a 1931 sightseeing excursion to view the under-construction dam. This line was removed in April 1937 as the PWD no longer required it.
</p>
