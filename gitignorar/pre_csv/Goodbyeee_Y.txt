	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Goodbyeee</h1>

<p>"<b>Goodbyeee</b>", or "<b>Plan F: Goodbyeee</b>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[nb 1]</a></sup> is the sixth and final episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Blackadder_Goes_Forth" title="Blackadder Goes Forth">Blackadder Goes Forth</a></i>, the fourth series of British historical <a href="/wiki/Television_comedy" title="Television comedy">sitcom</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Blackadder" title="Blackadder">Blackadder</a></i>. The episode was first broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC1</a> in the United Kingdom on 2 November 1989, shortly before <a href="/wiki/Armistice_Day" title="Armistice Day">Armistice Day</a>. Apart from the one-off short film <i><a href="/wiki/Blackadder:_Back_%26_Forth" title="Blackadder: Back &amp; Forth">Blackadder: Back &amp; Forth</a></i> made a decade later, it was the last episode of <i>Blackadder</i> to be produced and transmitted.
</p><p>The episode depicts its main characters' final hours before a British offensive on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Blackadder" title="Captain Blackadder">Captain Blackadder</a>'s attempts to escape his fate by feigning madness; after he fails to convince <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Melchett" title="General Melchett">General Melchett</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Haig,_1st_Earl_Haig" title="Douglas Haig, 1st Earl Haig">Field Marshal Haig</a>'s advice proves useless, he resigns himself to taking part in the push. "Goodbyeee" has a darker tone than other episodes in the series, culminating in its acclaimed ending in which the main characters are assumed to die in <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine-gun</a> fire. The episode's theme of death ties in with the series' use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallows_humour" title="Gallows humour">gallows humour</a>, its criticism and satire of war, and its depiction of authority figures contentedly sending their subordinates to face the enemy, while unwilling to do so themselves.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Curtis" title="Richard Curtis">Richard Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Elton" title="Ben Elton">Ben Elton</a> wrote the episode, and further material was provided by cast members. Its final sequence, which shows the main characters going "over the top", uses slow motion, as the programme's creators were unhappy with the result of the scripted ending. The enhanced scene has been described as bold and highly poignant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodbyeee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodbyeee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1566" data-file-width="1138" decoding="async" height="234" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg/170px-RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg/255px-RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg/340px-RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RowanAtkinsonMar07.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a> played Captain Blackadder in the series.</div></div></div>
<p>Each series of <i>Blackadder</i> depicts its protagonist, always a scheming and (except in the <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Adder" title="The Black Adder">first series</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[5]</a></sup>) witty man named <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Blackadder" title="Edmund Blackadder">Edmund Blackadder</a>, in different periods throughout history. In <i>Blackadder Goes Forth</i>, he is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain_Blackadder" title="Captain Blackadder">Captain Blackadder</a> (<a href="/wiki/Rowan_Atkinson" title="Rowan Atkinson">Rowan Atkinson</a>), an officer in the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> on the Western Front during the First World War.
</p><p>Joined by his colleagues—the <a href="/wiki/Poverty" title="Poverty">poor</a>, stupid and unhygienic <a href="/wiki/Baldrick#Blackadder_Goes_Forth" title="Baldrick">Private Baldrick</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tony_Robinson" title="Tony Robinson">Tony Robinson</a>), and the overly optimistic, <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">upper-class</a> and equally idiotic <a href="/wiki/George_(Blackadder)" title="George (Blackadder)">Lieutenant George St Barleigh</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hugh_Laurie" title="Hugh Laurie">Hugh Laurie</a>)—Blackadder tries constantly to escape his position and avoid the "big push", which he fears will result in his death. His efforts are hindered by the loud and intimidating <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Melchett" title="General Melchett">General Melchett</a> (<a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a>) and Melchett's strict, <a href="/wiki/Sardonicism" title="Sardonicism">sardonic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jobsworth" title="Jobsworth">jobsworth</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Staff_officer" title="Staff officer">staff officer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darling_(Blackadder)" title="Darling (Blackadder)">Captain Darling</a> (<a href="/wiki/Tim_McInnerny" title="Tim McInnerny">Tim McInnerny</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodbyeee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Captain Blackadder's trench receives a phone call from HQ: a full-scale attack has been ordered for the next day at dawn. Realising that this is likely to mean his death, Blackadder plans to <a href="/wiki/Desertion" title="Desertion">escape</a> by <a href="/wiki/Feigned_madness" title="Feigned madness">pretending to be mad</a>: he puts underpants on his head and sticks pencils in his nostrils. His plan is thwarted when General Melchett arrives to see what is happening and remarks that he shot an entire platoon that used this exact method; Blackadder overhears and narrowly escapes Melchett's punishment by pretending he is relating the story to Baldrick.
</p><p>Melchett leaves after George declines his offer not to participate in the push, and Baldrick suggests that Blackadder ask Field Marshal <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Haig,_1st_Earl_Haig" title="Douglas Haig, 1st Earl Haig">Douglas Haig</a> to get them out; remembering that Haig owes him a favour, Blackadder decides to call in the morning. George, Baldrick and Blackadder discuss the War and the friends they have lost—George mentions the <a href="/wiki/Christmas_truce" title="Christmas truce">Christmas truce</a> of 1914 (in which the belligerents stopped fighting to play <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a>) and realises he is the only "<a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity</a> <a href="/wiki/Tiddlywinks" title="Tiddlywinks">Tiddlers</a>" member still alive; this is paralleled in Baldrick's pets, who have all died. Back at HQ, Melchett surprises Captain Darling with a front-line <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Commissioned_officer" title="Commissioned officer">commission</a>. Darling's pleas to reconsider are misinterpreted, and Melchett insists that he go.
</p><p>The following morning, Blackadder calls Field Marshal Haig and reminds him of his debt; Haig reluctantly advises using the underpants method, and abruptly hangs up, sealing Blackadder's fate. Darling arrives, and his animosity with Blackadder dissolves as they are both put in the same situation. George tries to cheer everybody up, but finds himself as scared as the others. Darling states that he had hoped to live through the War, return to England and marry his fiancée.
</p>

<p>The men are called to the trench to prepare for the big push. There is a moment of hope when the British <a href="/wiki/Barrage_(artillery)" title="Barrage (artillery)">barrage</a> lifts, but Blackadder reminds his colleagues that they have stopped only to avoid hitting their own men. Baldrick tells Blackadder that he has a plan to escape certain death, but for the first time in any series, does not call it a "cunning plan". Blackadder replies Baldrick's idea will have to wait, but admits it couldn't fail to improve over his own plan to feign insanity because "who would have noticed another madman round here?"  The series concludes with Blackadder earnestly wishing his comrades good luck, and they charge over the top into thunderous <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine-gun</a> fire. The sequence enters <a href="/wiki/Slow_motion" title="Slow motion">slow motion</a> as a slow piano version of the <a href="/wiki/Blackadder#Theme_tune" title="Blackadder"><i>Blackadder</i> theme</a> is played. The series ends as the violent chaos of <a href="/wiki/No_man%27s_land" title="No man's land">no man's land</a> fades into a tranquil field of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poppy_(flower)" title="Poppy (flower)">poppies</a>, with only birdsong to be heard.
</p>

had co-written it since the <a href="/wiki/Blackadder_II" title="Blackadder II">second series</a>.</div></div></div></div>
<p>The episode was written by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Curtis" title="Richard Curtis">Richard Curtis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ben_Elton" title="Ben Elton">Ben Elton</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewisohn_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lewisohn-8">[7]</a></sup> who swapped <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">computer disks</a> to make edits. They stuck to a rule whereby they could not add back material the other had removed. The script was collaboratively edited by the cast members of <i>Blackadder Goes Forth</i> during <a href="/wiki/Read-through" title="Read-through">read-throughs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britainsbestsitcom_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-britainsbestsitcom-9">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrs_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-wrs-10">[9]</a></sup> Elton was primarily responsible for the sequence in which Blackadder explains <a href="/wiki/Causes_of_World_War_I" title="Causes of World War I">how the First World War started</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-lloyd_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-lloyd-11">[10]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Running_gag" title="Running gag">running gag</a> of Baldrick using mud and bodily fluids to make coffee throughout the episode was greatly expanded during rehearsals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rides_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-rides-12">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>The episode's title is a reference to the popular First World War song "<a href="/wiki/Good-bye-ee!" title="Good-bye-ee!">Good-bye-ee!</a>",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts2012312_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2012312-13">[12]</a></sup> which was based on a catchphrase of the comedian <a href="/wiki/Harry_Tate" title="Harry Tate">Harry Tate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bmh_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-bmh-14">[13]</a></sup> The song is also heard in an earlier episode — "<a href="/wiki/Major_Star" title="Major Star">Major Star</a>". The title is a departure from those of previous <i>Blackadder Goes Forth</i> episodes, which are <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">puns</a> on military ranks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts2012313_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2012313-15">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>During the filming of the episode, which took place before a <a href="/wiki/Studio_audience" title="Studio audience">studio audience</a> at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Television_Centre" title="BBC Television Centre">BBC Television Centre</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britainsbestsitcom_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-britainsbestsitcom-9">[8]</a></sup> Rowan Atkinson described sharing his character's dread of impending death and feeling a "knot in the pit of my stomach",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-atkinson_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-atkinson-17">[16]</a></sup> something that he had never experienced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-atkinson_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-atkinson-17">[16]</a></sup> Hugh Laurie said that filming was sad because "even for comic effect, we were representing the deaths of hundreds of thousands of people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrs_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-wrs-10">[9]</a></sup> Regarding guest star <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Palmer_(actor)" title="Geoffrey Palmer (actor)">Geoffrey Palmer</a>, the producer <a href="/wiki/John_Lloyd_(producer)" title="John Lloyd (producer)">John Lloyd</a> said "[We] probably could have given [him] more attention", calling him "a wonderful actor" who is "really just delivering three or four plot lines [pieces of dialogue essential to the <a href="/wiki/Plot_(narrative)" title="Plot (narrative)">plot</a>]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-lloyd_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-lloyd-11">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The slow motion and fade effects at the end of the episode were not scripted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uktvs4_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-uktvs4-18">[17]</a></sup> but the decision to use them was made in editing after the final scene was hastily filmed on an unconvincing <a href="/wiki/Polystyrene" title="Polystyrene">polystyrene</a> set, ruining the poignancy of the sequence; the episode's director <a href="/wiki/Richard_Boden" title="Richard Boden">Richard Boden</a> added the poppy field image.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wrs_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-wrs-10">[9]</a></sup> The piano version of the theme tune was performed by <a href="/wiki/Howard_Goodall" title="Howard Goodall">Howard Goodall</a> and recorded in a <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">gymnasium</a>, giving it what Lloyd described as a "liquid, lonely sound".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts2012320_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2012320-19">[18]</a></sup> The episode's <a href="/wiki/Closing_credits" title="Closing credits">end credits</a> were omitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britainsbestsitcom_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-britainsbestsitcom-9">[8]</a></sup> Tim McInnerny did not know about these changes before the episode aired, and has said that he found the ending particularly emotional.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britainsbestsitcom_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-britainsbestsitcom-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Themes">Themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodbyeee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the episode, Field Marshal Haig is shown casually sweeping away <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toy_soldiers" title="Toy soldiers">toy soldiers</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Dustpan" title="Dustpan">dustpan</a> and brush; <i>BBC News Magazine</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">'</span>s Finlo Rohrer called this a "visual allusion to his callousness", but quoted the historian <a href="/wiki/Gary_Sheffield_(historian)" title="Gary Sheffield (historian)">Gary Sheffield</a> as saying "The real Field Marshal Haig was certainly not a callous man. He was commanding the largest British army ever. Whatever he did, you ended up with lots and lots of casualties." Sheffield also noted that "Melchett is an amalgam of Haig and <a href="/wiki/John_French,_1st_Earl_of_Ypres" title="John French, 1st Earl of Ypres">John French</a> and the other generals", so Haig effectively "appears twice".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rohrer_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Rohrer-20">[19]</a></sup> The series, especially the storyline of "Goodbyeee", often depicts the "<a href="/wiki/Lions_led_by_donkeys" title="Lions led by donkeys">lions led by donkeys</a>" perception of the War, an element of <i>Blackadder Goes Forth</i> that has been criticised by historians.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newhistories_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-newhistories-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-badsey_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-badsey-21">[20]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his book <i>The Great War</i>, Ian F. W. Beckett also cited Sheffield: the latter commented that <i>Blackadder Goes Forth</i> was successful because "the characters and situations needed no explanation, so familiar was the audience with the received version of the war". Beckett noted the popularity of the episode's final scene, and compared it to a similarly popular scene from <i><a href="/wiki/Dad%27s_Army" title="Dad's Army">Dad's Army</a></i>. He said that this comparison demonstrates the observation made by historian <a href="/wiki/A._J._P._Taylor" title="A. J. P. Taylor">A. J. P. Taylor</a> that the Second World War has been regarded as a "good war" in comparison to the first; he opined that "television producers...have much to answer for in the perpetuation of the image of the Great War as one in which a generation of 'lions' were needlessly sacrificed by the 'donkeys'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beckett2007_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Beckett2007-22">[21]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blackadder_Goodbyeee_poppies.jpg"><img alt='A tranquil field of red poppies, and the text "Blackadder" with a copyright notice' class="thumbimage" data-file-height="231" data-file-width="300" decoding="async" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ea/Blackadder_Goodbyeee_poppies.jpg/220px-Blackadder_Goodbyeee_poppies.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ea/Blackadder_Goodbyeee_poppies.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blackadder_Goodbyeee_poppies.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ending shows a field of poppies to reflect on the deaths of soldiers; it was inspired by <a href="/wiki/John_McCrae" title="John McCrae">John McCrae</a>'s poem "<a href="/wiki/In_Flanders_Fields" title="In Flanders Fields">In Flanders Fields</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfaustin_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-sfaustin-23">[22]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Producer John Lloyd cited the episode's lack of another major character as the reason they had time to "explore the relationships of the five principal people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-lloyd_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-lloyd-11">[10]</a></sup> Atkinson said that the scene involving Darling's "ghastly realisation" of his commission was "very sad";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-atkinson_17-2"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-atkinson-17">[16]</a></sup> Lloyd commented "I love the fact that Captain Darling does have some compassion; he's not just a <a href="/wiki/Bureaucrat" title="Bureaucrat">bureaucrat</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-lloyd_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-lloyd-11">[10]</a></sup> They noted that "all the comedy just goes away" upon Darling's arrival in the trench,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-lloyd_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-lloyd-11">[10]</a></sup> and that "there are still funny moments, but dramatically, there is no comic content, it is just leading inexorably to the end."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd-atkinson_17-3"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-atkinson-17">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>Comparing the ending scene to those of previous series of <i>Blackadder</i>, in which the main characters were also killed, writer Curtis commented: "I think it was by chance that [previous series] ended with Blackadder being killed...but series four, we did do it very much on purpose." He said that he and Elton felt they could use the First World War as a setting if the characters died, considering "if we did do that...it would not be too disrespectful, and would actually represent some of the tragedy of the First World War".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfaustin_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-sfaustin-23">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Goodbyeee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following its original broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC1</a> at 9:30 pm on 2 November 1989,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lewisohn_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lewisohn-8">[7]</a></sup> "Goodbyeee" was praised for its powerful and memorable ending.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbccomedy_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-bbccomedy-24">[23]</a></sup> One journalist called the scene "a classy ending to a television classic",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanna2009133–134_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanna2009133–134-25">[24]</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">Sunday Times</a></i> said that it was "brave" and "properly responsible" of the writers to end the series poignantly, especially when the episode aired close to <a href="/wiki/Remembrance_Day" title="Remembrance Day">Remembrance Day</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanna2009133–134_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanna2009133–134-25">[24]</a></sup>
</p><p>"Goodbyeee" has also been the subject of more recent reviews: Rob Cromwell of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> listed the final scene among six "perfect <a href="/wiki/Series_finale" title="Series finale">end-of-show finales</a>", saying of <i>Blackadder</i>: "It was brilliantly funny throughout, right up until the last 60 seconds", and praising the writers and producer Lloyd for "deliver[ing] a perfectly pitched, poignant ending".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[25]</a></sup> Comparing <i>Blackadder Goes Forth</i> to the 2012 war serial <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birdsong_(TV_serial)" title="Birdsong (TV serial)">Birdsong</a></i>, Alison Graham of the <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Times" title="Radio Times">Radio Times</a></i> commented that "Nothing...evokes the terror of those unspeakable battlefields or leaves such an overwhelming sense of loss as [its characters] going over the top to their certain death".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[26]</a></sup> Den of Geek's Carley Tauchert placed the episode second on her list of "top 10 TV show endings", calling it "one of the greatest interpretations of the madness of war that has ever been put on film".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> Reviewing the episode for <a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a>, Kate Kulzick described it as "...a masterpiece, a hilarious and painful crystallization of everything Blackadder does well. It is without a doubt the best episode of the series and more than that, it is one of the best series finales in television history". She particularly praised it for "...its masterful balancing of comedy and tragedy. Both are interspersed throughout, with each character given moments of laugh out loud brilliance and poignant reflection", concluding that "...there is no such thing as a perfect television show or episode, but “Goodbyeee” comes dang close".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> The academic and theatre director Mary Luckhurst contrasted the regular British comedic treatment of the Second World War with the absence of comedies set in the First World War, until the <i>Blackadder</i> series, which she considered "an important British dramatic treatment" of the War. Of the final episode Luckhurst wrote:
</p>
<blockquote><p>"Goodbyeee" went a good deal further than any other sitcom or comedy, by terminally sending pretty much the entire cast over the top in 1917, into a silence that has...endured ever since. Many millions of viewers were shocked, and almost all taken aback by the abrupt realization of tragedy amid much-loved national television and after riotous laughter to that sudden and bitter end ...<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In a poll conducted by <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a></i> to determine television's one hundred most memorable moments, the final scene of "Goodbyeee" came ninth; it was one of only two entries in the top ten that was not news coverage (the other being a scene from <i><a href="/wiki/Only_Fools_and_Horses" title="Only Fools and Horses">Only Fools and Horses</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanna2009133–134_25-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanna2009133–134-25">[24]</a></sup> In 2001, <i>Radio Times</i> asked a panel of comedians, writers and producers to pick their ‘50 favourite sitcom moments’; "Goodbyeee" was the only episode of <i>Blackadder</i> that was included, where it ranked eleventh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Film_Institute" title="British Film Institute">British Film Institute</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Screenonline" title="Screenonline">Screenonline</a> called the episode's ending "unexpectedly moving", and noted that, unusually for a comedy programme, it was repeated as part of a serious commemoration of Armistice Day:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> for its 80th anniversary in 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHanna200923_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHanna200923-33">[32]</a></sup> The series' overview from the website of <a href="/wiki/UKTV" title="UKTV">UKTV</a>'s channel <a href="/wiki/Gold_(UK_TV_channel)" title="Gold (UK TV channel)">Gold</a>, which airs repeats of <i>Blackadder</i>, calls the final episode "a seamless blend of gallows humour and rich poignancy" and "a fitting end to an iconic series".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-uktvs4_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-uktvs4-18">[17]</a></sup> In his segment advocating for <i>Blackadder</i> to be voted <i><a href="/wiki/Britain%27s_Best_Sitcom" title="Britain's Best Sitcom">Britain's Best Sitcom</a></i>, broadcaster and journalist <a href="/wiki/John_Sergeant_(journalist)" title="John Sergeant (journalist)">John Sergeant</a> called the final sequence "the one sitcom moment with claims to immortality".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britainsbestsitcom_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-britainsbestsitcom-9">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some historians of the First World War have taken a different view. William Philpott referred to the series, by name, as "<a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bathetic" title="wikt:bathetic">bathetic</a>" and felt it part of a "post-facto generalisation of the nature of their war" that "sucked in" even veterans of the conflict; in other words, the First World War soldier had become a "victim" in the public consciousness, a circumstance at odds with the historical record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> The impact of <i>Blackadder</i> on the public consciousness was so pervasive that <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Corrigan" title="Gordon Corrigan">Gordon Corrigan</a> referenced it in his book cover copy when he published <i>Mud, Blood, and Poppycock</i>, which was an attempt to "dispel various myths" about the war.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup>
</p>
