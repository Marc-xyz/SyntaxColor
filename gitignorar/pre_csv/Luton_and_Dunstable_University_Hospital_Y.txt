<p><b>Luton and Dunstable University Hospital</b> is an acute hospital run by the <a href="/wiki/Luton_and_Dunstable_University_Hospital_NHS_Foundation_Trust" title="Luton and Dunstable University Hospital NHS Foundation Trust">Luton and Dunstable University Hospital NHS Foundation Trust</a>. It provides medical and surgical services for over 350,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire" title="Bedfordshire">Bedfordshire</a>, the north of <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>. The hospital is often abbreviated to the 'L&amp;D', and  employs 3,400 staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luton_and_Dunstable_University_Hospital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bute_Hospital,_Luton.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="304" data-file-width="473" decoding="async" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Bute_Hospital%2C_Luton.jpg/220px-Bute_Hospital%2C_Luton.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Bute_Hospital%2C_Luton.jpg/330px-Bute_Hospital%2C_Luton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Bute_Hospital%2C_Luton.jpg/440px-Bute_Hospital%2C_Luton.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bute_Hospital,_Luton.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The old Bute Hospital in Luton</div></div></div>
<p>The hospital has its origins in the Bute Hospital, which was built on land donated by the <a href="/wiki/John_Crichton-Stuart,_3rd_Marquess_of_Bute" title="John Crichton-Stuart, 3rd Marquess of Bute">Marquis of Bute</a> (who lived locally at <a href="/wiki/Luton_Hoo" title="Luton Hoo">Luton Hoo</a>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-war_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-war-3">[3]</a></sup> on Dunstable Road in <a href="/wiki/Luton" title="Luton">Luton</a> and which opened in September 1882.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> Although the facility was enlarged by two new wards in July 1902 and by a further extension in July 1912 there was little room for further expansion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-war_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-war-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ten acres of land, situated in the countryside between <a href="/wiki/Luton" title="Luton">Luton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dunstable" title="Dunstable">Dunstable</a> were purchased from <a href="/wiki/Electrolux" title="Electrolux">Electrolux</a> and a new hospital was built.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> The hospital was opened by <a href="/wiki/Mary_of_Teck" title="Mary of Teck">Queen Mary</a> on 14 February 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> The wards in the new hospital were named after Queen Mary, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lopes,_2nd_Baron_Ludlow" title="Henry Lopes, 2nd Baron Ludlow">Lady Ludlow</a> from Luton Hoo and Arthur Buckingham, a Dunstable grocer who had bequeathed £4,000 towards the cost of the hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-history-4">[4]</a></sup> The hospital joined the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Service</a> in 1948.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Birgitte,_Duchess_of_Gloucester" title="Birgitte, Duchess of Gloucester">Duchess of Gloucester</a> visited the Disability Resource Centre in July 1996<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Princess_Royal" title="Anne, Princess Royal">Princess Anne</a> opened the new St Mary's Wing Rehabilitation Centre in February 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the facility became a teaching hospital for <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> in March 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Duke of Edinburgh</a> opened a new state-of-the-art cardiac centre there in February 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patient_safety">Patient safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luton_and_Dunstable_University_Hospital&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Patient safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Luton and Dunstable has been a Safer Patient Initiative site since 2004. Reducing the hospital's <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality rate</a> was a priority for Chief Executive Stephen Ramsden, who believed that saving patient lives must be at the top of all chief executives' agendas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Stephen Ramsden was appointed the director of the National Patient Safety Campaign,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> after the hospital won the Health Foundation's Safer Patients Initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> He also received an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OBE" title="OBE">OBE</a> for services to healthcare.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>99% of A&amp;E patients are treated within the target 4 hours.  A <i><a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a></i> article maintains other hospitals can learn from Luton and Dunstable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was named by the <a href="/wiki/Health_Service_Journal" title="Health Service Journal">Health Service Journal</a> as one of the top hundred NHS trusts to work for in 2015.  At that time it had 3323 full-time equivalent staff and a sickness absence rate of 3.25%. 67% of staff recommend it as a place for treatment and 60% recommended it as a place to work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
