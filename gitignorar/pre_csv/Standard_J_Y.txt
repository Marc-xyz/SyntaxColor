<p>The <b>Standard J</b> is a two-seat basic trainer <a href="/wiki/Bracing_(aeronautics)#Bays" title="Bracing (aeronautics)">two-bay</a> biplane produced in the United States from 1916 to 1918, powered by a four-cylinder inline <a href="/wiki/Hall-Scott_A-7" title="Hall-Scott A-7">Hall-Scott A-7a</a> engine. It was constructed from wood with wire bracing and fabric covering. The J-1 was built as a stopgap to supplement the <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_JN-4" title="Curtiss JN-4">Curtiss JN-4</a> in production.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_J&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Healy Day had designed the preceding <a href="/wiki/Standard_H-2" title="Standard H-2">Sloan H</a> series of aircraft, and continued the line under the Standard Aero Corporation (later <a href="/wiki/Standard_Aircraft_Corporation" title="Standard Aircraft Corporation">Standard Aircraft Corporation</a>). Four companies, Standard, Dayton-Wright, Fisher Body, and Wright-Martin, delivered 1,601 J-1s between June 1917 and June 1918. The Standard J-1 can be differentiated from the Curtiss JN series by its slightly swept-back wing planform, triangular <a href="/wiki/King_post" title="King post">king posts</a> above the upper wings, and the front legs of the landing gear which were mounted behind the lower wing's leading edge, just about where the forward <a href="/wiki/Spar_(aeronautics)" title="Spar (aeronautics)">wing spar</a> of the lower wing panel attaches to the fuselage.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_J&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._(5684424286).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="580" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._%285684424286%29.jpg/220px-04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._%285684424286%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._%285684424286%29.jpg/330px-04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._%285684424286%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._%285684424286%29.jpg/440px-04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._%285684424286%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:04-00075_Carrying_passengers_at_Rockwell_Field._(5684424286).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Standard J-1 providing joyrides.</div></div></div>
<p>Although produced in large numbers, its four-cylinder <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall-Scott_A-7a" title="Hall-Scott A-7a">Hall-Scott A-7a</a> engine was unreliable and vibrated badly. While JN-4 production outnumbered J-1s by about two to one to June 1918, fatalities in JN-4s versus J-1s numbered about seven to one as a result of the limited use of the J-1s. Few later production J-1s left their delivery crates.
</p><p>In June 1918, all Standard J-1s were grounded, although training remained intensive. Sufficient JN-4s were available to meet training needs, and at $2,000 per aircraft it was not cost-effective to convert them to use <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_OX-5" title="Curtiss OX-5">Curtiss OX-5</a> engines. Contracts for 2,600+ JS-1s were canceled, and those not used for ground instruction by the US Army were sold as surplus or scrapped. <a href="/wiki/Curtiss_Aeroplane_and_Motor_Company" title="Curtiss Aeroplane and Motor Company">Curtiss</a>, which produced its competitor (the Curtiss JN) bought surplus J-1s which they modified with different powerplants, for resale.
</p><p>Many J-1s were flown by civilian flying schools, and for joy-riding and barnstorming operations, until they were worn out, or were forced into retirement by new air transport legislation in 1927 which banned passenger aircraft with wood structures due to a number of high-profile accidents.
</p>

<h2><span id="Specifications_.28SJ.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Specifications_(SJ)">Specifications (SJ)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Standard_J&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Specifications (SJ)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="584" data-file-width="1024" decoding="async" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg/220px-Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg/330px-Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg/440px-Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Standard_J-1_with_Hispano-Suiza_engine.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Standard J-1 with <a href="/wiki/Hispano-Suiza" title="Hispano-Suiza">Hispano-Suiza</a> engine</div></div></div>
<p style="font-size: 90%; margin:0;"><i>Data from</i> The Standard Aero Corporation Model J Training Tractor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p><p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>General characteristics</b></span>
</p><ul><li><b>Crew:</b> 2</li>
<li><b>Length:</b> 26 ft 7 in (8.10 m)</li>
<li><b>Upper wingspan:</b> 43 ft 11 in (13.39 m)</li>
<li><b>Lower wingspan:</b> 32 ft (9.8 m)</li>
<li><b>Height:</b> 10 ft 10 in (3.30 m)</li>
<li><b>Wing area:</b> 429 sq ft (39.9 m<sup>2</sup>)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Airfoil" title="Airfoil">Airfoil</a>:</b> R.A.F No 3</li>
<li><b>Empty weight:</b> 1,350 lb (612 kg)</li>
<li><b>Gross weight:</b> 1,950 lb (885 kg)</li>
<li><b>Fuel capacity:</b> 31 US gal (26 imp gal; 120 L)</li>
<li><b>Powerplant:</b> 1 × <a href="/wiki/Hall-Scott_A-7" title="Hall-Scott A-7">Hall-Scott A-7</a> water-cooled <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight-4" title="Straight-4">straight-4</a> engine, 100 hp (75 kW)</li></ul>
<p><span style="font-size: 120%;"><b>Performance</b></span>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Maximum speed:</b> 68 mph (109 km/h, 59 kn)</li>
<li><b>Stall speed:</b> 37 mph (60 km/h, 32 kn)</li>
<li><b>Range:</b> 350 mi (560 km, 300 nmi)</li>
<li><b>Time to altitude:</b> 10 minutes to 2,600 ft (790 m)</li></ul>
