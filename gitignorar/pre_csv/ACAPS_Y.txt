	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">ACAPS</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p><p><br/>
<b>ACAPS</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norwegian</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-profit" title="Non-profit">non-profit</a>, non-governmental project that provides international, independent humanitarian analysis. ACAPS was created in 2009. The project is currently funded by 5 donors.
</p><p>The analysis is based on evidence and needs and is free of operational agenda. It is freely provided to the <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">NGOs</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> agencies and donors to help them better understand the humanitarian crises, make more informed decisions and respond more effectively to disasters.
</p><p>ACAPS established the Syria Needs Analysis Project – SNAP in 2012. For about three years SNAP delivered over 40 products related to the humanitarian impact of the conflict.
</p><p>ACAPS provides of independent, multi-sectoral humanitarian analysis. ACAPS is also known for having developed a severity ranking of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanitarian_crises" title="Humanitarian crises">humanitarian crises</a>. Another ACAPS contribution to the humanitarian system is advocating for the importance of better collecting secondary data (instead of only collecting primary data) and the importance of better analysing fewer data rather than compiling a maximum of information without making sense of it.
</p><p>It employs around 30 professionals based in <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> and in the field.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>ACAPS (initially known as "The Assessment Capacities Project”) was established in 2009 to improve the assessment of humanitarian needs in complex emergencies and crises, at a time where there were no single approach, nor consensus on needs assessments and where evidence- and needs-based approaches were not standard. It was created by a consortium of three <a href="/wiki/International_non-governmental_organization" title="International non-governmental organization">INGOs</a>: <a href="/wiki/HelpAge_International" title="HelpAge International">HelpAge International</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_Refugee_Council" title="Norwegian Refugee Council">Norwegian Refugee Council</a> and Merlin, which then merged with <a href="/wiki/Save_the_Children" title="Save the Children">Save the Children</a>.
</p><p>Through the development of methodological tools, the delivery of training and operational support (such as Bangladesh in 2012 and <a href="/wiki/April_2015_Nepal_earthquake" title="April 2015 Nepal earthquake">Nepal Earthquake</a> in 2015) ACAPS contributed to shape norms and standards around coordinated needs assessment and advocated for more meaningful and evidence-based data.
</p><p>ACAPS was a contributor<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> to the MIRA process (Multi-Cluster/Sector Initial Rapid Assessment) which is a joint needs assessment tool that can be used in sudden-onset emergencies.
</p><p>Between December 2012 and 30 June 2015 ACAPS and <a href="/wiki/MapAction" title="MapAction">MapAction</a> ran the Strategic Needs Analysis Project — SNAP (initially the Syria Needs Analysis Project). The aim was to support the humanitarian response in Syria and the region with independent analysis and coordinated assessments. For almost three years, SNAP pursued these goals with a combination of independent information products, technical support and capacity building for humanitarian assessments. Its data and products were broadly used by humanitarian workers and also journalists, such as BBC<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the <i>New York Times</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>After the SNAP project ACAPS ran several other field projects, such as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebola" title="Ebola">Ebola</a> Needs Assessment Project (ENAP) in 2014-2015, the Refugee/Migrant Crisis in 2015-2016 , together with MapAction, the Caribbean response  to the Hurricane Irma and Maria, and more recently the support to the Rohingya crisis.
</p><p>In 2014 ACAPS together with the <a href="/wiki/ECB_Project" title="ECB Project">Emergency Capacity Building Project</a> (ECB) launched "Humanitarian Needs Assessment — The Good Enough Guide ”, a tool dedicated to ease field staff carrying out assessment after a humanitarian crisis. This guide received praise for enabling field-based staff and national responders ‘to provide the critical information needed to inform the wider humanitarian community.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016 ACAPS, together with six other INGOs, joined the Mixed Migration platform  (MMP),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> a joint NGO initiative providing quality mixed migration-related information for policy, programming and advocacy work, as well as critical information for people on the move. ACAPS contributes to the MMP with analytical capacity, scenario-building expertise, and online data collection and analysis.
</p><p>Over the years, ACAPS centred its core services on humanitarian analysis and the provision of secondary data. Although ACAPS still undertakes and supports field needs assessments (and primary data collection) and produce needs assessment tools, the majority of its work is dedicated to support the humanitarian community by providing up-to-date information on more than 40 key crises around the globe. The ACAPS website, and its related app, CrisisAlert,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> released in 2017, are the main platforms to present this analysis to the humanitarian community.
</p><p>At the recent <a href="/wiki/World_Humanitarian_Summit" title="World Humanitarian Summit">World Humanitarian Summit</a> in Istanbul, "joint and impartial needs assessments”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> were promoted as one of the 10 key commitments of the Grand Bargain giving ACAPS new opportunities to play a central role and contribute to change the humanitarian sector.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Activities">Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crisis_analysis">Crisis analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crisis analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ACAPS performs crisis analysis, monitoring and updating current crises on a daily basis. They rank the severity of world humanitarian crises on a weekly basis. Tailored reports, such as the Briefing Notes for the START Fund,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> are also released. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting">Forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2015 ACAPS has developed a range of products and services to better anticipate events and humanitarian developments. These services should contribute to inform timely contingency planning and preparedness measures. ACAPS writes risks reports, anticipatory briefing notes and organizes scenario building workshops, such as the one for IFRC, held in April 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The result of this exercise is a report with a set of key scenarios including an overview of events that could trigger the scenario, humanitarian consequences and impact, estimated caseload, operational constraints and recommended actions. Every year, in December, ACAPS releases its annual publication, the "Humanitarian Overview”,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> identifying key crises and corresponding needs to look at in the upcoming year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology_development">Methodology development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Methodology development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ACAPS has developed material around humanitarian assessments and analysis, which ranges from decision-making to initial planning of analysis work. The importance of better collecting <a href="/wiki/Secondary_data" title="Secondary data">secondary data</a> (instead of only collecting primary data) and the importance of better analysing fewer data rather than compiling a maximum of information without making sense of it are some of the changes ACAPS made into the humanitarian system. ACAPS is also known for having developed a severity ranking of humanitarian crises. Categories are: severe humanitarian crises, humanitarian crises and situation of concern. This ranking is based on 5 indicators.
</p><p>More than 100 methodological tools and guidance have been produced by ACAPS.
</p>
<h3><span id="API.2Fwebservices"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="API/webservices">API/webservices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: API/webservices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>ACAPS is currently openly sharing its data with various organizations, through an API service. Among them, the Crises App from <a href="/wiki/ReliefWeb" title="ReliefWeb">ReliefWeb</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> uses the ACAPS "Crisis Overview” section for each crisis displayed on its own app.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding">Funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=ACAPS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since its launch ACAPS has been funded by various institutional donors. The project is currently funded by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs" title="Norwegian Ministry of Foreign Affairs">Norwegian Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Agency_for_Development_and_Cooperation" title="Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation">Swiss Agency for Development and Cooperation</a> (SDC), the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_International_Development_Cooperation_Agency" title="Swedish International Development Cooperation Agency">Swedish International Development Cooperation Agency</a> (SIDA), the UK <a href="/wiki/Department_for_International_Development" title="Department for International Development">Department for International Development</a> (DFID), and The <a href="/wiki/Directorate-General_for_European_Civil_Protection_and_Humanitarian_Aid_Operations" title="Directorate-General for European Civil Protection and Humanitarian Aid Operations">Directorate-General for European Civil Protection and Humanitarian Aid Operations</a> (ECHO).
</p>
