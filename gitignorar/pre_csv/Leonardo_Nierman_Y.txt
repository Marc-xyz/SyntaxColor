	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Leonardo Nierman</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Leonardo Nierman</b> (born November 1, 1932), full name Leonardo Nierman Mendelejis, is a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mexican_artists" title="List of Mexican artists">Mexican artist</a> mostly known for his painting and sculpture. He at first wanted to be a violinist, but gave it up after twenty years when he compared a recording of his playing with that of <a href="/wiki/Yehudi_Menuhin" title="Yehudi Menuhin">Yehudi Menuhin</a>. However, his musical training has been a major influence on his painting and sculpture, reproducing movement and harmony as Nierman sees similarities between the two disciplines.  Nierman has had exhibitions in Mexico and abroad and over sixty recognitions of his work, half of which are from outside Mexico. His work is <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract</a> but still with discernible images from nature such as birds, water, lightning and more. His paintings are in pure colors while his sculptures are generally of metal, often silver-toned.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonardo_Nierman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1016" data-file-width="676" decoding="async" height="331" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg/220px-%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg/330px-%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg/440px-%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:%22_Angeles_testigos_de_la_beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza_%22.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngeles_testigos_de_la_Beatificaci%C3%B3n_de_Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza" title="Ángeles testigos de la Beatificación de Juan de Palafox y Mendoza">Ángeles testigos de la Beatificación de Juan de Palafox y Mendoza</a></i>, a sculpture commemorating the beatification of <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Palafox_y_Mendoza" title="Juan de Palafox y Mendoza">Juan de Palafox y Mendoza</a>, in the city of <a href="/wiki/Puebla_(city)" title="Puebla (city)">Puebla</a>, Mexico.</div></div></div>
<p>Leonardo Nierman Mendelejis was born in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> on November 1, 1932.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup> He is the only child of Clara Mendelejis, a bakery worker and Chanel Nierman, a bus inspector who later started a small jacket factory. Nierman's parents arrived to Mexico in the mid 1920s, his father from <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> and his mother from the <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, both poor. The two met in Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>When he was a child, he wanted to be a musician and dedicated himself to the violin for two decades.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup>  At that time, art did not attract him although he remembers seeing the murals of <a href="/wiki/Diego_Rivera" title="Diego Rivera">Diego Rivera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Clemente_Orozco" title="José Clemente Orozco">José Clemente Orozco</a> but with little reaction except anger upon seeing some vandalism on an Orozco mural.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup> To pursue music, he began with private violin lesson and attended  the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Conservatory_of_Music_of_Mexico" title="National Conservatory of Music of Mexico">National Conservatory of Music of Mexico</a> for a while. He even gave some recitals in the <a href="/wiki/Palacio_de_Bellas_Artes" title="Palacio de Bellas Artes">Palacio de Bellas Artes</a>.  He gave up the violin when he heard a recording of himself playing <a href="/wiki/Symphonie_espagnole" title="Symphonie espagnole">Symphonie espagnole</a> by <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lalo" title="Édouard Lalo">Édouard Lalo</a>, and then comparing his interpretation with that of Yehudi Menuhin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>At first he thought he had wasted his time with the violin but has since decided that it gave him his philosophy on life and prepared him for his painting and sculpture.  After he left music, he began to be attracted to color, but he remains attached to music especially artists such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bach" title="Bach">Bach</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debussy" title="Debussy">Debussy</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahler" title="Mahler">Mahler</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stravinsky" title="Stravinsky">Stravinsky</a>. However, he was still hesitant about painting. He remembers that one day he was walking in the <a href="/wiki/Historic_center_of_Mexico_City" title="Historic center of Mexico City">historic center of Mexico City</a> and found an artist's supply shop and thought about taking up painting as a hobby, but then thought he could not because of lack of preparation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite his affinity for the arts, Nierman's formal education was not in this field. He graduated from preparatory in 1951, with a concentration in physics and mathematics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venas_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-venas-3">[3]</a></sup> In 1953, he studied the psychology of color and form in static and moving bodies. He also began spending large amounts of time in museums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup> He completed a bachelor's in business administration at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universidad_Nacional_Aut%C3%B3noma_de_M%C3%A9xico" title="Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México">Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México</a> but never pursued this career because he had begun to paint, including a mural at his school. He began painting on his own in his bedroom in his parents’ home. He never thought he would become professional but little by little the activity began to consume most of his time, becoming a self-taught artist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonardo_Nierman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He began his career painting when he convinced the dean of the business school where he was a student, to paint a mural at the department's auditorium in 1956. To paint the mural, he made an appointment with <a href="/wiki/David_Alfaro_Siqueiros" title="David Alfaro Siqueiros">David Alfaro Siqueiros</a> to ask for advice, receiving such especially the mixing of colors. The mural was later destroyed when the wall was taken down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Nierman had been painting for a while when <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Raquel_Tibol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Raquel Tibol (page does not exist)">Raquel Tibol</a> invited him to exhibit his work at the Centro de Deportes Israeli in Mexico City. He told himself that if he did not sell a single painting, he would quit. Two paintings were purchased and were then seen by the owner of IFA Gallery in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>. Since 1959, this gallery has exhibited Nierman's work and opened doors for him internationally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Since then, he has had over 100 exhibitions in the Americas, Asia, Australia and Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origami_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-origami-4">[4]</a></sup>  More recent exhibitions include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universidad_Aut%C3%B3noma_de_Coahuila" title="Universidad Autónoma de Coahuila">Universidad Autónoma de Coahuila</a> in 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venas_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-venas-3">[3]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/International_Museum_of_Art_%26_Science" title="International Museum of Art &amp; Science">International Museum of Art &amp; Science</a>  in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mcallen,_Texas" title="Mcallen, Texas">Mcallen, Texas</a> in 2009,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imas_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-imas-5">[5]</a></sup> the MACAY museum in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Yucat%C3%A1n" title="Mérida, Yucatán">Mérida</a> in 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origami_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-origami-4">[4]</a></sup> the Francisco Cossío Museum in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD,_San_Luis_Potos%C3%AD" title="San Luis Potosí, San Luis Potosí">San Luis Potosí</a> in 2012,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sanluis_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sanluis-6">[6]</a></sup> and the art gallery of the Complejo Cultural Universitario in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puebla,_Puebla" title="Puebla, Puebla">city of Puebla</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puebla_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-puebla-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>His work can be found in museums and public buildings in Australia, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, the United States, Spain, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, Japan, Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">Monaco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origami_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-origami-4">[4]</a></sup> These include the gallery of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chispazo_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-chispazo-8">[8]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Art_Institute_of_Chicago" title="Art Institute of Chicago">Art Institute of Chicago</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Fine_Arts,_Boston" title="Museum of Fine Arts, Boston">Museum of Fine Arts in Boston</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Museo_de_Arte_Moderno" title="Museo de Arte Moderno">Museo de Arte Moderno</a> in Mexico City,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fednews_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-fednews-9">[9]</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Memorial_Art_Gallery" title="Memorial Art Gallery">Memorial Art Gallery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rochester,_New_York" title="Rochester, New York">Rochester</a>, The Art Modern Gallery of New York and <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Art_Museum" title="Phoenix Art Museum">Phoenix Art Museum</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmex_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-encmex-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>His monumental works can be found in many of Mexico's major cities and abroad.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup> In 1969 he painted a mural for the physics department at <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton</a> as well as designed the stained glass windows for Temple Beth Israel in <a href="/wiki/Lomas_de_Chapultepec" title="Lomas de Chapultepec">Lomas de Chapultepec</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-encmex_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-encmex-10">[10]</a></sup> His metal sculptures appear in places such as universities, concert halls, research centers, libraries, cultural centers, atriums and parks in countries such as <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, the United States, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origami_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-origami-4">[4]</a></sup> These include the Flame of the Millennium which is at the Ohio Street interchange of the Kennedy Expressway in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, Eternal Light at the Outpatiene Care Center of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_Medical_Center" title="University of Illinois Medical Center">University of Illinois Medical Center</a> in Chicago and Sensación de Vuelo at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambert-St._Louis_International_Airport" title="Lambert-St. Louis International Airport">Lambert-St. Louis International Airport</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fednews_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-fednews-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stlouis_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-stlouis-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997 he created a limited edition postage stamp for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servicio_Postal_Mexicano" title="Servicio Postal Mexicano">Mexican postal service</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>He has received over sixty recognitions, half of them from outside Mexico.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup> Recognitions including Honorific Mention at UNAM (1960), member of the Instituto de Artes in Mexico (1964), lifetime member of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_the_Arts" title="Royal Society of the Arts">Royal Society of the Arts</a> in London (1965), Palme d'Or des Beaux Arts from Monaco (1969), Royce Medal (New York, 1970), League of Art Gold Medal  (Chicago, 1980), Golden Centaur and honorary master's of painting from the Academy of Italy (1982), named European Academic by the Centro Studi Di Recerch L Accademia D Europa in Italy (1984) and the winner of the sculpture competition at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Central_Florida" title="University of Central Florida">University of Central Florida</a> (1988).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imas_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-imas-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palvarez_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-palvarez-13">[13]</a></sup> In 1993, he became a patron of the <a href="/wiki/Academy_of_St_Martin_in_the_Fields" title="Academy of St Martin in the Fields">Academy of St Martin in the Fields</a> in London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1995 he received an honorary doctorate from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concordia_University_(California)" title="Concordia University (California)">Concordia University in Irvine, California</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup> The city of Chicago named December 19 in his honor in 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup> In 2003 he received the Gloria Award from the International Latino Cultural Center in Chicago.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palvarez_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-palvarez-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2010 he received the Vasco de Quiroga Medal from the Mexico City government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quiroga_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-quiroga-14">[14]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNAM" title="UNAM">UNAM</a> named a classroom designed for cultural activities after the painter in 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aula_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-aula-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artistry">Artistry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Leonardo_Nierman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Artistry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His artistic production includes painting, <a href="/wiki/Tapestry" title="Tapestry">tapestry</a> design, sculpture, murals, engraving and glass work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origami_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-origami-4">[4]</a></sup> His first artwork was done in the 1950s, influenced by the work of <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky" title="Wassily Kandinsky">Kandinsky</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klee" title="Klee">Klee</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mir%C3%B3" title="Miró">Miró</a> and Chirico, as well as the abstract, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubist" title="Cubist">cubist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surrealist" title="Surrealist">surrealist</a> movements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup> However, much of his later work has been shaped by his interpretation of nature and a search for the relationship between abstract art and the cosmos, spurred by his studies of color and movement in the 1950s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sullivan_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-16">[16]</a></sup> The other major influence has been his musical background.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup> He stated Music and painting are very much alike — both have tonalities, rhythms, high-intensity areas and resting areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soundcolor_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-soundcolor-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origami_4-5"><a href="#cite_note-origami-4">[4]</a></sup> He has been called the <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Pollock" title="Jackson Pollock">Jackson Pollock</a> of Latin American art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>His work has been classified as “magical expressionism.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup> He paints abstract forms with movement and rhythm using bright colors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quiroga_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-quiroga-14">[14]</a></sup>  His work is not narrative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-puebla_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-puebla-7">[7]</a></sup> He describes his work as an interaction of colors which create a moment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup> While abstract, elements of nature are present in his works with elements such as wind, water, lightning, fire and volcanic eruptions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sullivan_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In his painting, he prefers to work with clean pigments as the mixing of colors tend to dull the effect of light.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-venas_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-venas-3">[3]</a></sup> When he paints, he says it is like going crazy. He does not know at that time if the work is good or not only that it makes him feel. It is not conscious or planned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sueño_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-sueño-11">[11]</a></sup> Nierman has said: Painting is to me the aperture through which it is possible to enter a certain world; in it the viewer may find an endless number of magic images, objects, remembrances, associations, fears, joys, hopes and dreams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sullivan_16-2"><a href="#cite_note-sullivan-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>His sculpture work has been made of marble, silver, gold, bronze and stainless steel, but it is usually silver-toned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palvarez_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-palvarez-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-subestima_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-subestima-17">[17]</a></sup> These often contain elements such as birds, angels, archangels, winged victories, flames, and musical instruments. His metal sculptures evoke movement and harmony usually through the use of spirals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tercer_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-tercer-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-palvarez_13-3"><a href="#cite_note-palvarez-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
