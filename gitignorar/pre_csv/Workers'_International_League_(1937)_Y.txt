<p>The WIL was formed in 1937 by members of the <a href="/wiki/Militant_Group" title="Militant Group">Militant Group</a>, who had split due to false allegations from the leadership of that group that Ralph Lee (born Raphael Levy),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> then a newly arrived <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_African" title="South African">South African</a> member, had misled a strike and used the strike funds to move to England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The split took around a third of the membership of the Militant Group and four of its branches,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born5_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-born5-3">[3]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Jock_Haston" title="Jock Haston">Jock Haston</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Grant" title="Ted Grant">Ted Grant</a>. The group remained in the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, where they published <i>Searchlight</i> edited by <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Healy" title="Gerry Healy">Gerry Healy</a>, which in September 1938 was replaced by the magazine <i>Youth for Socialism</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born5_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-born5-3">[3]</a></sup> which in its own turn was renamed <i>Socialist Appeal</i> in June 1941<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> as a result of the WIL's turn of focus away from the Labour Party.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The group also produced a theoretical journal <i>Workers International News</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born5_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-born5-3">[3]</a></sup>  The WIL grew with recruits from the Labour Party, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Great_Britain" title="Communist Party of Great Britain">Communist Party of Great Britain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Labour_Party" title="Independent Labour Party">Independent Labour Party</a> and the Militant Group.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fourth_International" title="Fourth International">Fourth International</a> was formed in 1938, and the WIL refused to merge into the newly formed official British affiliate, the <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Socialist_League_(UK,_1938)" title="Revolutionary Socialist League (UK, 1938)">Revolutionary Socialist League</a> itself a regroupment of the Militant Group and others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They requested either affiliate or sympathiser status to the International but were rejected.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Outbreak_of_World_War_II">Outbreak of World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Workers%27_International_League_(1937)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Outbreak of World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unlike the Revolutionary Socialist League, the WIL readily adopted the <a href="/wiki/Proletarian_Military_Policy" title="Proletarian Military Policy">Proletarian Military Policy</a> developed by Trotsky in his last writings and expanded upon and advocated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_P_Cannon" title="James P Cannon">James P Cannon</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Socialist_Workers_Party_(US)" title="Socialist Workers Party (US)">Socialist Workers Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> They campaigned for the creation of workers' militias instead of the <a href="/wiki/Home_Guard_(United_Kingdom)" title="Home Guard (United Kingdom)">Home Guard</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> deep air raid shelters for workers, and after 1941 against the pro-war, anti-strike position of the CPGB.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Question_of_the_Leadership">Question of the Leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Workers%27_International_League_(1937)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Question of the Leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to its adoption of the Proletarian Military Policy, the WIL argued that its members should go through the experience of the war with other members of their class by joining the army when called-up. But if this was applied to the whole membership it meant they could be dispersed and provide no real leadership and therefore the organisation took measures to preserve the leading cadres outside the forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fredjackson277_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-fredjackson277-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>With the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the WIL expected to be banned and so temporarily moved a few members (Tommy Reilly, Jock Haston, Gerry Healy, John Williams and George Noseda) to Dublin.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> It soon became obvious that the group would not be persecuted, and they were allocated paper for their publications.
</p><p>On their own initiative, Arthur Carford, who had gained employment as an orderly at the Medical Examination Centre, where potential conscripts fitness was reviewed, and three other members from Sheffield attempted to steal cards to exempt the bearer from military service for medical reasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born11_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-born11-12">[12]</a></sup> The four were arrested and charged at Sheffield Magistrates Court with "stealing, receiving, conspiring to steal and conspiring to receive these cards, to which later was added a fifth charge of doing those acts "with intent to assist the enemy".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born11_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-born11-12">[12]</a></sup> Realising the seriousness of these charges (especially the fifth charge which carried a charge of 20 years imprisonment),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fredjackson277_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-fredjackson277-10">[10]</a></sup> one of the defendants, Fred Jackson, defended himself arguing that by virtue of the political ideals it was impossible for Trotskyists to do anything deliberately to assist Nazi Germany, leading to no guilty verdicts on the fifth charge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born12_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-born12-13">[13]</a></sup> He was sentenced to 2 years imprisonment, with Carford receiving 18 months,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born12_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-born12-13">[13]</a></sup> however, given the irresponsibility of their initial action they were barred from holding office within the WIL for a year and reduced to supporter status respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born19_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-born19-14">[14]</a></sup> Of the other two defendants, Ward received 6 months and Beet was acquitted,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born12_13-2"><a href="#cite_note-born12-13">[13]</a></sup> but as both had given statements to the police that could have implicated the WIL they were expelled.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born19_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-born19-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trade_Union_Activities">Trade Union Activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Workers%27_International_League_(1937)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trade Union Activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The WIL began to orient towards the trade unions, and deprioritised <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entrism" title="Entrism">entrism</a> into the Labour Party. After the appointment of Roy Tearse as industrial organiser (replacing Gerry Healy),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> the WIL found itself involved through him in setting up a solidarity committee in support of victimised workers called the Clyde Workers Committee after the <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Workers%27_Committee" title="Clyde Workers' Committee">original organisation bearing that name</a> which had led the revolt on the Clyde during World War I.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born70_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-born70-16">[16]</a></sup> This body called a conference in Glasgow on 5–6 June 1943, which attracted delegates from "Yorkshire's West Riding, Newcastle, Nottingham, Huddersfield, Barrow and London" and set up the Militant Workers Federation with Tearse as Secretary and offices in Nottingham near the Royal Ordnance Factory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-born70_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-born70-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1944, the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_International" title="Fourth International">Fourth International</a> had realised that the WIL were far more effective and closer to the FI's policies than the RSL which had disintegrated into a set of warring factions, and so coordinated a unity conference.  This produced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revolutionary_Communist_Party_(1944-1949)" title="Revolutionary Communist Party (1944-1949)">Revolutionary Communist Party</a>, which adopted all the WIL's positions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p>
