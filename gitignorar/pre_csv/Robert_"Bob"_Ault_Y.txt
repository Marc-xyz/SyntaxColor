	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Robert "Bob" Ault</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Robert Ault</b> (1936–2008) was an <a href="/wiki/Art_therapy" title="Art therapy">art therapist</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a>, co-founder of  American Art Therapy Association, founder of the  Master's of Science in Art Therapy program at <a href="/wiki/Emporia_State_University" title="Emporia State University">Emporia State University</a>, and founder of the Kansas Art Therapy Association.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> He was a recipient of 10th Honorary Life Member of the American Art Therapy Association and the Kansas Outstanding Educator award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Childhood">Childhood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Childhood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bob Ault was born in 1936 in <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi,_Texas" title="Corpus Christi, Texas">Corpus Christi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> In the fifth grade, his teacher, Miss Love, noticed his talent for art and recommended art lessons. Ault was voted into the South Texas Art League at the age of 14.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feen_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Feen-3">[3]</a></sup> He was the youngest member ever allowed in.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ault went to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a> to receive his Bachelor of Fine Arts in painting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> He then chose to attend <a href="/wiki/Wichita_State_University" title="Wichita State University">Wichita State University</a> for his Master of Fine Arts, never planning to stay in Kansas once he completed it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Ault found that his graduate fellowship did not call for him to teach at the University, but at the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_of_Logopedics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute of Logopedics (page does not exist)">Institute of Logopedics</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> This institution specialized in communication disorders and was interested in setting up an art program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Having a graduate student do the work solved the problem. Ault was not immediately interested, but decided to persevere for the next two years while he completed his MFA.
</p><p>As it turned out, his experiences there helped shape the rest of his career. Many of the children at the institute had <a href="/wiki/Cerebral_palsy" title="Cerebral palsy">cerebral palsy</a> or other kinds of brain damage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Ault was sent to a classroom with another woman to help students make art and quickly found the work was enjoyable. He said that the art room was a place where he could use his creativity to generate meaningful interactions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>One boy in particular influenced Ault greatly. "Tom" was 14 and had suffered <a href="/wiki/Whooping_cough" title="Whooping cough">whooping cough</a> at a young age that had left him with severe expressive-receptive <a href="/wiki/Aphasia" title="Aphasia">aphasia</a>; it left him without the ability to understand or speak language.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-6"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> At first, Ault was unsure of how to handle the situation, so they just played and drew pictures of each other. Tom seemed to enjoy this, so Ault drew a cartoon of Tom getting up from the table, going out the door, and then coming back inside.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-7"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Upon seeing this, Tom got up and did the actions shown in the picture. Ault pointed out this moment as one that changed both of their lives forever; Ault became an art therapist and Tom was finally able to communicate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-8"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> They worked out a language of symbols to communicate by and distributed it to those who Tom knew. Soon, Tom was running errands for people throughout the institute. Years later, Ault found out that Tom’s family had moved him back to <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a>, where Tom gave everyone in town a set of his symbols. Ault believed experiences like this were what the <a href="/wiki/Art_therapy" title="Art therapy">art therapy</a> profession was all about.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-9"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ault met a young <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_therapist" title="Speech therapist">speech therapist</a>, Marilynn Miller, and fell in love.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Ault returned to <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> after graduation, but was miserable without her, so he followed her to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topeka" title="Topeka">Topeka</a> and they got married.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-10"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Marilynn was hired by the Institute of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logopedics" title="Logopedics">Logopedics</a> to run a field center in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topeka" title="Topeka">Topeka</a>, while Ault faced the possibility of being drafted by the army.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Ault decided to join the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Army_reserve" title="Army reserve">army reserve</a> in order to avoid the draft and served six months of active duty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-11"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> He had been married for two weeks before he had to report to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Leonard_Wood_(military_base)" title="Fort Leonard Wood (military base)">Fort Leonard Wood</a> in <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">Missouri</a>, but Marilynn brought him crayons and paper that he kept hidden so he could practice art in secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-12"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_Therapy_at_The_Menninger_Institute">Art Therapy at The Menninger Institute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Art Therapy at The Menninger Institute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1961, Ault returned from duty and applied for a position at the <a href="/wiki/Menninger_Foundation" title="Menninger Foundation">Menninger Foundation</a> in the activity department.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> He was hired and found a new friend in his colleague <a href="/wiki/Don_Jones_(arts)" title="Don Jones (arts)">Don Jones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-13"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Although he commonly used art to work with patients, Jones did not call himself an art therapist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-14"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Ault and Jones knew of people on the East Coast who practiced art analysis, but they saw themselves separate from this group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-15"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> They even discussed the potential of a national organization that focused on the curative powers of art. Jones eventually left Menninger’s and went to take a director position at <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Harding_Hospital&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Harding Hospital (page does not exist)">Harding Hospital</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, while Ault became the director of Menninger’s Creative Arts Clinic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_American_Art_Therapy_Association">The American Art Therapy Association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The American Art Therapy Association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soon after Jones left, Ault received word from <a href="/wiki/Psychologist" title="Psychologist">psychologist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_therapist" title="Art therapist">art therapist</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Myra_Levick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Myra Levick (page does not exist)">Myra Levick</a> that there was to be a meeting in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a> concerning the beginnings of a national <a href="/wiki/American_Art_Therapy_Association" title="American Art Therapy Association">art therapy association</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-16"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Levick organized the meeting despite major resistance. Ault was eventually elected to the committee to write the <a href="/wiki/American_Art_Therapy_Association" title="American Art Therapy Association">American Art Therapy Association’s</a> (AATA) constitution and bylaws.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-17"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1971, Ault became the second president of AATA.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_programs">Education programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Kansas" title="University of Kansas">University of Kansas</a> (KU) <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Kansas" title="Lawrence, Kansas">Lawrence</a> approached Ault in 1970 to form an art therapy master’s program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Although this may have come as good news, Ault was never allowed to teach a class in Lawrence and so he resigned in 1973.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-18"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> According to Ault, the KU psychology department saw those in the arts as a “bunch of hippies who shouldn’t be on campus”.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-19"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> Meanwhile, Ault taught classes at Menninger’s in Topeka until he decided that KU was not interested in starting an art therapy program, but preventing him from starting one at another university.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-20"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The same week that Ault resigned from KU, a group of students at <a href="/wiki/Emporia_State_University" title="Emporia State University">Emporia State University</a> (ESU) asked to speak with Ault about art therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-21"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> The next day, Ault received a call from ESU, and in a month’s time they had set up a Master of Science in Psychology with a specialty in Art Therapy. Ault led the program and taught halftime there until he retired in 1995.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Ault was still employed at Menninger’s but had mixed feelings about his work there. He loved the patients and colleagues, but was often frustrated by the lack of respect for art therapy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-22"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> He was never allowed to even have a business card until a secretary forged an order for a set.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> They had been judged not professional enough to deserve cards, although Ault carried a full case load and charged full fees for services .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-23"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> This frustration caused Ault to look for ways to cut ties with Menninger’s. 
In 1978, Ault established Ault’s Academy of Art in Topeka.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Feen_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Feen-3">[3]</a></sup> This was a small school and private practice in art therapy that intended to put people at ease and suit his clients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Ault also found time to work on his own art there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-24"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup> In fact, in 1993, he retired from Menninger’s and in 1995, resigned from ESU in order to pursue work at his studio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> Ault felt that it was time for him to become more selfish with his time and focus on his own practice in order to live a less stressful life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ault_2-25"><a href="#cite_note-Ault-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_accomplishments">Other accomplishments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_%22Bob%22_Ault&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Other accomplishments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ault faced occasional health problems but continued to work at his studio full-time until he was hospitalized in October 2007 for respiratory difficulties.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> He died on February 5, 2008. Ault was 71 years old and left behind his wife, two children, and four grandchildren.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ault also worked to found the Kansas Art Therapy Association (KATA) and worked toward getting state licensure for art therapists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup> He was honored with many awards, including the Kansas Outstanding Educator and the tenth Honorary Life Member of AATA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmanke_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-Schmanke-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
