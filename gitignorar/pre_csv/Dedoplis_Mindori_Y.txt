<p><b>Dedoplis Mindori</b> (<a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>: <span lang="ka" title="Georgian language text">დედოფლის მინდორი</span>, <span style="font-size: smaller;"><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Georgian" title="Romanization of Georgian">translit.</a>:</span> <span lang="ka-Latn" style="font-style: italic;">dedoplis mindori</span>, literally, "the queen's meadow") is an archaeological site in <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>'s east-central <a href="/wiki/Mkhare" title="Mkhare">region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Shida_Kartli" title="Shida Kartli">Shida Kartli</a>, at the confluence of eastern and western Prone, tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Kura_(Caspian_Sea)" title="Kura (Caspian Sea)">Kura</a>. A multi-layer site, it has yielded some <a href="/wiki/Acheulean" title="Acheulean">Acheulean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mousterian" title="Mousterian">Mousterian</a> stone tools, burials from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age" title="Late Bronze Age">Late Bronze</a> to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Iron_Age" title="Early Iron Age">Iron</a> ages, and several settlements and burials from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>. Of particular importance is a substantial complex of what once were religious buildings, dated to the 2nd–1st century BC, and inscribed on the list of the <a href="/wiki/Immovable_Cultural_Monuments_of_National_Significance" title="Immovable Cultural Monuments of National Significance">Immovable Cultural Monuments of National Significance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The Dedoplis Mindori plain is adjoined by a group of mounds, known as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aradetis_Orgora&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aradetis Orgora (page does not exist)">Aradetis Orgora</a>, where archaeological finds span several periods of local culture sequence, from the <a href="/wiki/Chalcolithic" title="Chalcolithic">Chalcolithic</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dic_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dic-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dedoplis_Mindori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dedoplis Mindori is a gently sloping plain at the confluence of eastern and western Prone, tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Kura_(Caspian_Sea)" title="Kura (Caspian Sea)">Kura</a>, occupying the area of approximately 25 sq. km, some 3 km west of the village of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aradeti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aradeti (page does not exist)">Aradeti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kareli_Municipality" title="Kareli Municipality">Kareli Municipality</a>. At its southern edge, where the river Ptsa joins the Kura near the village of <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Doghlauri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Doghlauri (page does not exist)">Doghlauri</a>, there is <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aradetis_Orgora&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aradetis Orgora (page does not exist)">Aradetis Orgora</a>, a group of three mounds, the dominant of which is known as Dedoplis Gora ("the queen's hill") and raises to 34 m above the river. A series of archaeological reconnaissance surveys explored both the plain and mounds in the 1950s and 1960s, but systematic archaeological study of the area was undertaken under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Iulon_Gagoshidze" title="Iulon Gagoshidze">Iulon Gagoshidze</a> from 1972 to 1982. The excavations revealed an extensive complex of religious buildings at Dedoplis Mindori and a palace complex at Dedoplis Gora. Digs at Aradetis Orgora are ongoing, as part of the Georgian–Italian Shida Kartli Archaelogical Project, a collaboration of the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_National_Museum" title="Georgian National Museum">Georgian National Museum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ca%27_Foscari_University_of_Venice" title="Ca' Foscari University of Venice">Ca' Foscari University of Venice</a> launched in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Dedoplis Mindori sanctuary is unknown from written history. According to its excavator, Iulon Gagoshidze, it might have served as one of the principal pre-Christian shrines, and probably a royal one, of the ancient kingdom of Kartli, known to the <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">Classical world</a> as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Iberia" title="Kingdom of Iberia">Iberia</a>. The temples are dated by archaeologists to the 2nd–1st century BC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IG_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-IG-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IR_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-IR-5">[5]</a></sup> In the later 1st century AD, the complex was destroyed by fire. This dating is supported by combined archaeological evidence from a palace complex at nearby Dedoplis Gora: pottery, imported bronze-ware from the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Radiocarbon_dating" title="Radiocarbon dating">radiocarbon analysis</a> of grain stores. Arrowheads found in the destruction deposits suggest the fire was the result of a military conflict.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Layout">Layout</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dedoplis_Mindori&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Layout">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The middle portion of the Dedoplis Mindori plain is occupied by a complex of religious buildings, with the total area of 4 ha (225 × 180 m). The complex consists of eight temples, two gateways, dwellings, and other accessory structures, built simultaneously according to a well-designed uniform rectangular plan. The buildings, oriented south-north, surround a central square courtyard, measuring 105 × 103 m. They had <a href="/wiki/Mudbrick" title="Mudbrick">mudbrick</a> walls, with cobblestone <a href="/wiki/Socle_(architecture)" title="Socle (architecture)">socles</a> and, with the exception of the main temple, were roofed with <a href="/wiki/Terracotta" title="Terracotta">terracotta</a> <a href="/wiki/Roof_tiles" title="Roof tiles">tiles</a>. Wooden columns of the temples and gateways were crowned with <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a> <a href="/wiki/Capital_(architecture)" title="Capital (architecture)">capitals</a>, adorned with elaborate carvings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dic_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dic-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IG_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-IG-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IR_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-IR-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The main temple is located at the southern end of the complex. With a columned hall as a central architectural element, its design is inspired by an <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_architecture" title="Achaemenid architecture">Achaemenid architectural tradition</a>. Its large open columned <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">portico</a> and square <a href="/wiki/Cella" title="Cella">cella</a> are surrounded by an <a href="/wiki/Ambulatory" title="Ambulatory">ambulatory</a> on three sides. At the north end of the building there is a two-column portico with a balcony. The cella, with the area of 300 sq. m, housed an altar under a cupola, supported by four columns; its mudbrick podium has survived. The walls of the temple had been plastered and adorned with murals. A smaller temple of similar design lies at the northern edge of the courtyard, with an ambulatory and a tiled pitched roof, which gives it a "<a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_art" title="Hellenistic art">Hellenistic</a>" appearance. Six more, architecturally simpler places of worship are nearby, each having an open portico connected to a square cella with a doorway. The religious buildings are surrounded by the contemporaneous dwellings, probably for those who served at the sanctuary. Further 400 m north-west, there are remnants of a settlement of construction workers and craftsmen as well as a <a href="/wiki/Clay_pit" title="Clay pit">clay pit</a>, with a number of discarded and broken pottery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dic_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dic-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IG_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-IG-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IR_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-IR-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
