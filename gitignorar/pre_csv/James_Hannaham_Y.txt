	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">James Hannaham</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>James Hannaham</b> (born 1968<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>) is a writer, performer, and visual artist. His novel <i>Delicious Foods</i> (2015), which deals with human trafficking, won the <a href="/wiki/PEN/Faulkner_Award_for_Fiction" title="PEN/Faulkner Award for Fiction">PEN/Faulkner Award</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hurston-Wright_Legacy_Award" title="Hurston-Wright Legacy Award">Hurston/Wright Legacy Award</a> and was named one of <i><a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">Publisher’s Weekly</a></i>’s top ten books of the year. <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> called it an “ambitious, sweeping novel of American captivity and exploitation.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Hannaham was born in the <a href="/wiki/The_Bronx" title="The Bronx">Bronx</a> and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Yonkers,_New_York" title="Yonkers, New York">Yonkers, NY,</a> where his mother was an investigative journalist. His early experience was marked by the legal battle to end segregation in the Yonkers schools, which his mother covered for the radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> His cousin is the artist <a href="/wiki/Kara_Walker" title="Kara Walker">Kara Walker</a>, who illustrated the cover of <i>Delicious Foods</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>He studied art at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a> and in 1992 began working in the art department of <a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a> as well as writing for the paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Later he studied creative writing at the <a href="/wiki/Michener_Center_for_Writers" title="Michener Center for Writers">Michener Center for Writers</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas</a>. His <a href="/wiki/Debut_novel" title="Debut novel">debut novel</a>, <i>God Says No</i> (2009)<i>,</i> was a <a href="/wiki/Lambda_Literary_Award" title="Lambda Literary Award">Lambda Literary Award</a> finalist.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> He has published short stories in <a href="/wiki/One_Story" title="One Story">One Story</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fence_(magazine)" title="Fence (magazine)">Fence</a>, <a href="/wiki/StoryQuarterly" title="StoryQuarterly">StoryQuarterly</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bomb_(magazine)" title="Bomb (magazine)">BOMB</a>. He reviews theater and art for 4Columns.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">[7]</a></sup> 
</p><p>He cofounded the New York–based performance group <a href="/wiki/Elevator_Repair_Service" title="Elevator Repair Service">Elevator Repair Service</a> and worked with them 1992–2002.  His text-based artworks often satirize the theoretical jargon that is used to describe visual art;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> his 2014 gallery show "Card Tricks" consisted of descriptive placards for fictive artworks, with titles such as "Planet" and "Nothing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Hannaham is an associate professor in the writing program at the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_Institute" title="Pratt Institute">Pratt Institute</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn, NY</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
