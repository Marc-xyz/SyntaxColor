	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Mirza Abutaleb Zanjani</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abutaleb_al-Zanjani.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="288" decoding="async" height="280" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Abutaleb_al-Zanjani.jpg/230px-Abutaleb_al-Zanjani.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7c/Abutaleb_al-Zanjani.jpg 1.5x" width="230"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abutaleb_al-Zanjani.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mirza Abutaleb Zanjani</div></div></div>
<p><b>Mirzā Abutāleb Zanjānī</b> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="fa">میرزا ابوطالب زنجانی</span>‎) also known as <b>Sayyid Fakhr al-Din Mohammad Abutāleb Mousavi al-Zanjānī</b> (10 December 1843 – 16 March 1911) Iranian <a href="/wiki/Jurist" title="Jurist">Jurist</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shia" title="Shia">Shia</a> <a href="/wiki/Scholar" title="Scholar">scholar</a>
</p><p>He was born on 10 December 1843, to an educated family in <a href="/wiki/Zanjan,_Iran" title="Zanjan, Iran">Zanjan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>. His paternal ancestors were all celebrated scholars. He started his education in his birthplace and continued in <a href="/wiki/Qazvin" title="Qazvin">Qazvin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> and <a href="/wiki/Najaf" title="Najaf">Najaf</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>, trained under <a href="/wiki/Morteza_Ansari" title="Morteza Ansari">Morteza Ansari</a>, Sheikh Razi and Sayyid Hossein Kooh-kamari. He returned to Iran at the age of 40 and stayed in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a>, where as a distinguished disciple of Koohkamari, became the centre of clerical circles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tahoor_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Tahoor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>He spent most of his time on teaching his students and writing religious books.
</p><p>He advocated <a href="/wiki/Persian_Constitutional_Revolution" title="Persian Constitutional Revolution">Persian Constitutional Revolution</a>, but later adhered to <a href="/wiki/Royalist" title="Royalist">royalists</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tahoor_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Tahoor-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>He died at the age of 69 on 16 March 1911, <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a> and was buried in <a href="/wiki/Mashhad" title="Mashhad">Mashhad</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiki_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-wiki-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Zanjani was among the few scholars of <a href="/wiki/Qajar_dynasty" title="Qajar dynasty">Qajar</a> period who used their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> knowledge to translate Arabic texts into <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>. In addition to Persian and <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_language" title="Azerbaijani language">Azari</a>, he had acquaintance with <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish language</a>. He also had knowledge of ideas of his contemporary European intellects such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thomas_Malthus" title="Thomas Malthus">Thomas Malthus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Charles Darwin</a>, and used their views in his essays.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiki_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-wiki-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Henry_Hardinge" title="Arthur Henry Hardinge">Arthur Henry Hardinge</a>, the British ambassador to Iran, wrote about him:"As I have previously mentioned, the most intellectual and enlightened Shia scholar that I've met in Tehran was Mirza Abutaleb Zanjani with whom we usually had debates about religion and politics. I personally think that Mirza Abutaleb worked on the same aspiration for Islamic unity as <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Hamid_II" title="Abdul Hamid II">Abdul Hamid II</a> ... although he himself had less faith to these principals".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiki_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-wiki-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>He married a daughter of <a href="/wiki/Bahram_Mirza" title="Bahram Mirza">Bahram Mirza</a> son of <a href="/wiki/Abbas_Mirza" title="Abbas Mirza">Abbas Mirza</a> and had 4 daughters. He is maternal grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Reza_Zanjani" title="Reza Zanjani">Reza Zanjani</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiki_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-wiki-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
