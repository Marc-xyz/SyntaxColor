<p><i><b>Tragopogon</b></i>, also known as <b>goatsbeard</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> or <b>salsify</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flowering_plant" title="Flowering plant">flowering plants</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Asteraceae" title="Asteraceae">sunflower family</a>. It includes the <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetable</a> known as <a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_porrifolius" title="Tragopogon porrifolius">salsify</a>, as well as a number of common wild flowers.
</p><p>Salsifies are <a href="/wiki/Forb" title="Forb">forbs</a> growing as <a href="/wiki/Biennial_plant" title="Biennial plant">biennial</a> or <a href="/wiki/Perennial_plant" title="Perennial plant">perennial plants</a>.  They have a strong taproot and milky <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plant_sap" title="Plant sap">sap</a>.  They generally have few branches, and those there are tend to be upright.  Their leaves are somewhat grass-like.  Flower colour varies within the genus, with some yellow species, and some bronze or purple.  Seeds are <a href="/wiki/Achene" title="Achene">achenes</a> and are borne in a globe like that of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dandelion" title="Dandelion">dandelion</a> but larger, and are dispersed by the wind.
</p><p>The salsifies are mostly natives of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, but several species have been introduced into <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and have spread widely there.  There is one species considered native to North America, <i><a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_mirus" title="Tragopogon mirus">Tragopogon mirus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the more common species of <i>Tragopogon</i> are known, in the regions where they are most common, by the common names goat's beard, goatsbeard, salsify, or common salsify, without further qualification.  These names are therefore inherently ambiguous, and best avoided, or reserved for the genus collectively.  In the species list below, the first common name given is the one that seems to be most widely used for that species and is not in significant use for any other species.
</p><p>The vegetable called salsify is usually the root of the purple salsify, <i><a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_porrifolius" title="Tragopogon porrifolius">Tragopogon porrifolius</a></i>; the root is described as having the taste of <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a> (hence the alternative common name "<a href="/wiki/Oyster_plant" title="Oyster plant">oyster plant</a>" for some species in this genus), but more  insipid with a touch of sweetness.  The young shoots of purple salsify can also be eaten, as well as young leaves <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.northernbushcraft.com/plants/salsify/notes.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>.  Other species are also used in the same way, including the black or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_salsify" title="Spanish salsify">Spanish salsify</a>, <i>Scorzonera hispanica</i>, which is closely related though not a member of the genus <i>Tragopogon</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybridization">Hybridization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tragopogon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hybridization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Salsifies are one example where <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_speciation" title="Hybrid speciation">hybrid speciation</a> has been observed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the early 1900s, humans introduced three species of goatsbeard into North America. These <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>, the western salsify (<i><a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_dubius" title="Tragopogon dubius">T. dubius</a></i>), the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meadow_salsify" title="Meadow salsify">meadow salsify</a> (<i>T. pratensis</i>), and the oyster plant (<i><a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_porrifolius" title="Tragopogon porrifolius">T. porrifolius</a></i>), are now common in urban areas. In the 1950s, botanists found two new species in the regions of <a href="/wiki/Idaho" title="Idaho">Idaho</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, where the three already known species overlapped. One new species, <i><a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_miscellus" title="Tragopogon miscellus">Tragopogon miscellus</a></i>, is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetraploid" title="Tetraploid">tetraploid</a> <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrid</a> of <i>T. dubius</i> and <i>T. pratensis</i>. The other species, <i><a href="/wiki/Tragopogon_mirus" title="Tragopogon mirus">Tragopogon mirus</a></i>, is also an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyploid" title="Polyploid">allopolyploid</a>, but its ancestors were <i>T. dubius</i> and <i>T. porrifolius</i>.  These new species are usually referred to as "the Ownbey hybrids" after the botanist who first described them.  The <i>T. mirus</i> population grows mainly by reproduction of its own members, but additional episodes of hybridization continue to add to the <i>T. mirus</i> population.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
