<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artes_Mundi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 2003 the Artes Mundi art prize has been held every two years at the <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_Cardiff" title="National Museum Cardiff">National Museum Cardiff</a>. The prize is the biggest art prize in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> with £40,000 for each year’s winner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Though the prize has included artists who use traditional media, like paint, this is usually only part of their practice, with the focus being very much on conceptual approaches. Though the exhibition takes place in Cardiff, the focus is on international artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014/15 Artes Mundi 6 expanded beyond the National Museum and shared the exhibition of shortlisted works with <a href="/wiki/Chapter_Arts_Centre" title="Chapter Arts Centre">Chapter Arts Centre</a>, Cardiff, and <a href="/wiki/Turner_House_Gallery" title="Turner House Gallery">Turner House Gallery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penarth" title="Penarth">Penarth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personnel">Personnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artes_Mundi&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Artes Mundi was founded in 2002 by Welsh artist <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Wilkins_(artist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Wilkins (artist) (page does not exist)">William Wilkins</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Its founding Artistic Director and CEO was <a href="/wiki/Tessa_Jackson" title="Tessa Jackson">Tessa Jackson</a>, formerly the Director of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Arts_Council" title="Scottish Arts Council">Scottish Arts Council</a>. In 2010, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ben_Borthwick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ben Borthwick (page does not exist)">Ben Borthwick</a> was appointed Artistic Director and CEO, after Jackson left to take the helm at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InIVA" title="InIVA">InIVA</a>. Borthwick's previous role was as Assistant Curator at <a href="/wiki/Tate_Modern" title="Tate Modern">Tate Modern</a>. In 2013 the director became Karen MacKinnon, formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Glynn_Vivian_Art_Gallery" title="Glynn Vivian Art Gallery">Glynn Vivian Gallery</a>, Swansea and Chapter, Cardiff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 2019, Nigel Prince was announced the new Director, leaving his post as Executive Director at Contemporary Art Gallery Vancouver <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
