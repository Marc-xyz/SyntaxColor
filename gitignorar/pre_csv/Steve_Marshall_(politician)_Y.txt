<p><b>Steve Marshall</b> (born October 26, 1964) is the 48th <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_of_Alabama" title="Attorney General of Alabama">Attorney General of Alabama</a>, having been appointed in February 2017 by Governor <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Bentley" title="Robert J. Bentley">Robert J. Bentley</a> to fill the vacancy caused by previous attorney general <a href="/wiki/Luther_Strange" title="Luther Strange">Luther Strange</a>'s appointment to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>. He previously served as district attorney in <a href="/wiki/Marshall_County,_Alabama" title="Marshall County, Alabama">Marshall County</a> for 16 years.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marshall was born in <a href="/wiki/Atmore,_Alabama" title="Atmore, Alabama">Atmore, Alabama</a>, the only child of Conrad Marshall, a representative for a sporting goods manufacturer, and Mary Jo Marshall, a secretary. He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Pinecrest_High_School" title="Pinecrest High School">Pinecrest High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Pines,_North_Carolina" title="Southern Pines, North Carolina">Southern Pines, North Carolina</a>, and earned a bachelor's degree in American Studies from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a> in 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> He then earned a law degree at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama_School_of_Law" title="University of Alabama School of Law">University of Alabama School of Law</a>, and was admitted to the Alabama State Bar in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Law">Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marshall practiced law in Birmingham and Montgomery in the firm of Maynard Cooper and Gale P.C. and then moved to <a href="/wiki/Guntersville,_Alabama" title="Guntersville, Alabama">Guntersville</a> where he started the firm of McLaughlin &amp; Marshall. He was district representative for Alabama and Georgia in the <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a>. In addition to private practice, he served as a legal analyst for the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_House_of_Representatives" title="Alabama House of Representatives">Alabama House of Representatives</a>, as prosecutor for the <a href="/wiki/Arab,_Alabama" title="Arab, Alabama">Arab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albertville,_Alabama" title="Albertville, Alabama">Albertville</a> municipal courts and as municipal attorney for Arab.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marshall_County_District_Attorney">Marshall County District Attorney</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marshall County District Attorney">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2001, on the retirement of Ronald Thompson, he was appointed District Attorney for Marshall County by Governor <a href="/wiki/Don_Siegelman" title="Don Siegelman">Don Siegelman</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup> the second youngest district attorney in the state at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-five-2">[2]</a></sup> Marshall was unopposed in three subsequent elections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gattis_2017_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gattis_2017-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huntsville_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Huntsville-4">[4]</a></sup> As district attorney, he assisted in passage of the Brody Act, which makes injury to a fetus an offense punishable in addition to any injury to the mother,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-five-2">[2]</a></sup> and of a state law requiring a driver's license for the purchase of <a href="/wiki/Ephedrine" title="Ephedrine">ephedrine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pseudoephedrine" title="Pseudoephedrine">pseudoephedrine</a>, ingredients used in manufacturing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_meth" title="Crystal meth">crystal meth</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attorney_General_of_Alabama">Attorney General of Alabama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Attorney General of Alabama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Marshall was appointed Attorney General of Alabama by Governor <a href="/wiki/Robert_J._Bentley" title="Robert J. Bentley">Robert J. Bentley</a> in February 2017, to fill the vacancy caused by <a href="/wiki/Luther_Strange" title="Luther Strange">Luther Strange</a>'s appointment to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gattis_2017_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-Gattis_2017-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huntsville_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Huntsville-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aiello_2017_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aiello_2017-5">[5]</a></sup> He was elected to a full term in 2018, defeating former attorney general <a href="/wiki/Troy_King" title="Troy King">Troy King</a> in a July <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Run-off_election" title="Run-off election">run-off election</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-five-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2017, after <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham</a> Mayor <a href="/wiki/William_A._Bell" title="William A. Bell">William A. Bell</a> draped a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Confederate_monuments_and_memorials" title="List of Confederate monuments and memorials">Confederate memorial</a> with plastic and surrounded it with plywood with the rationale "This country should in no way tolerate the hatred that the KKK, neo-Nazis, fascists and other hate groups spew", Marshall sued Bell and the city for violating the <a href="/wiki/Alabama_Memorial_Preservation_Act" title="Alabama Memorial Preservation Act">Alabama Memorial Preservation Act</a>, which prohibits the "relocation, removal, alteration, or other disturbance of any monument on public property that has been in place for 40 years or more".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-five-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2017, Texas Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Ken_Paxton" title="Ken Paxton">Ken Paxton</a> led a group of Republican Attorneys General from nine other states, including Steve Marshall, plus Idaho Governor <a href="/wiki/Butch_Otter" title="Butch Otter">Butch Otter</a>, in threatening the <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> administration that they would litigate if the president did not terminate the <a href="/wiki/Deferred_Action_for_Childhood_Arrivals" title="Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals">Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals</a> (DACA) policy that had been put into place by president <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Tennessee Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Slatery" title="Herbert Slatery">Herbert Slatery</a> subsequently reversed his position.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, Marshall's opponent, Troy King, accused him of violating campaign finance laws by accepting money from a banned political action committee. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, Attorneys General from all 50 U.S. states, the District of Columbia, and all four U.S. territories were urged by the National Association of Attorneys General to support a bill, the Secure and Fair Enforcement (SAFE) Banking Act (H.R. 1595), sponsored by U.S. Rep. Ed Perlmutter (D-Colo.), which would permit marijuana-related businesses in states and territories to use the banking system. The bill would facilitate collection of taxes levied on the $8.3 billion industry, reduce the danger of operating cash-only businesses and more effectively monitor the industry. Only Marshall and 16 other A.G.s did not support the measure.
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p><p>Marshall is co-chair of Alabama Governor <a href="/wiki/Kay_Ivey" title="Kay Ivey">Kay Ivey</a>'s Opioid Overdose and Addiction Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_party_affiliation">Political party affiliation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Political party affiliation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2011, after serving for ten years as Marshall County District Attorney, Marshall officially changed his political party affiliation from the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican Party</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steve_Marshall_(politician)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Marshall is an elder at LifePoint Church in Albertville and has participated in <a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> work in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-five-2">[2]</a></sup> He was married to the former Bridgette Gentry, who took her own life on June 24, 2018, after "a long struggle with mental illness" and addictions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> They have a daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bio_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-bio-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
