<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="746" data-file-width="959" decoding="async" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg/200px-Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg/300px-Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg/400px-Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kufstein_Almabtrieb_2005.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Almabtrieb</i> in <a href="/wiki/Kufstein" title="Kufstein">Kufstein</a>, Austria</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Almabtrieb</b> (in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>: <b>Alpabzug</b>, <b>Alpabfahrt</b>, or in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> speaking Switzerland: <b>Désalpes</b>; <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German language</a> literally: <i>drive from the mountain pasture</i>) is an annual event in the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">alpine regions</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, referring to a <a href="/wiki/Cattle_drive" title="Cattle drive">cattle drive</a> that takes place in late September or early October.
</p><p>During summer, all over the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">alpine regions</a> <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> herds feed on alpine <a href="/wiki/Pasture" title="Pasture">pastures</a> (<i>Almen</i> in Austria or Germany, <i>Alpen</i> in Switzerland) high up in the mountains, a practice known as <a href="/wiki/Transhumance" title="Transhumance">transhumance</a>. In numbers, these amount to about 500,000 in <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, 380,000 in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, and 50,000 in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.
</p><p>While there is often some movement of cattle between the <i>Almen</i> (sing.: <i>Alm</i>), or <i>Alpen</i> (sing.: <i>Alp</i>) respectively, during the summer, there is usually one concerted cattle drive in the autumn to bring the cattle to their barns down in the valley. If there were no accidents on the <i>Alm</i> during the summer, in many areas the cattle are decorated elaborately, and the cattle drive is celebrated with music, feasts and dance events in the towns and villages. Upon arrival in the valley, joint herds from multiple farmers are sorted in the <i>Viehscheid</i>, and each animal is returned to its owner.
</p><p>In many places this Alpine custom of <i>Almabtrieb</i> has today evolved into a major <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourist</a> attraction, with a public festival, and booths set up along the course for selling agricultural, as well as artisans', products along with alcoholic beverages.
</p><p>In the spring, the reverse cattle drive moves from the valley barns to the <i>Alp</i> (in Switzerland: <i>Alpaufzug</i>, <i>Alpfahrt</i>, <i>Alpauffahrt</i>; in Germany/Austria: <i>Almauftrieb</i>). It is celebrated in Switzerland, though less well known. It is not celebrated in Germany and Austria, however.
</p>
