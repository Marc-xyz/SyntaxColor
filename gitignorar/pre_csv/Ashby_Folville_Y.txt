<p><b>Ashby Folville</b> is a village in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Melton_(borough)" title="Melton (borough)">Melton</a> district of <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>, south west of <a href="/wiki/Melton_Mowbray" title="Melton Mowbray">Melton Mowbray</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> of Ashby Folville was abolished in 1936 and its 1,796 acres (727 ha) were merged with <a href="/wiki/Gaddesby" title="Gaddesby">Gaddesby</a> .
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashby_Folville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The village of 'Ashby' was recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> as consisting of twenty-four villagers, three smallholders, two slaves, one priest and being owned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countess_Judith" title="Countess Judith">Countess Judith</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the time of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Leicestershire_Survey&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Leicestershire Survey (page does not exist)">Leicestershire Survey</a> of 1124-29 the manor had passed from Judith to her daughter <a href="/wiki/Maud,_Countess_of_Huntingdon" title="Maud, Countess of Huntingdon">Maud, Countess of Huntingdon</a> and her husband King <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David I of Scotland</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFarnham1919–20_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFarnham1919–20-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Folville_Family">The Folville Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashby_Folville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Folville Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Folville_Arms.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="640" decoding="async" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/John_Folville_Arms.jpg/120px-John_Folville_Arms.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/John_Folville_Arms.jpg/180px-John_Folville_Arms.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/John_Folville_Arms.jpg/240px-John_Folville_Arms.jpg 2x" width="120"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Folville_Arms.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arms of Folville - <i>party per fess, argent &amp; or, a cross moline gules</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENichols1795_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENichols1795-3">[3]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Folville element of the placename comes from a family that had its seat here since at least 1137 when its lordship was held of the Honour of Huntingdon by Fulk de Folville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENichols1795_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENichols1795-3">[3]</a></sup> The family name, ultimately derived from <a href="/wiki/Folleville,_Somme" title="Folleville, Somme">Folleville</a> in the French region of <a href="/wiki/Picardy" title="Picardy">Picardy</a>, was attached to several other sites in Leicestershire, including the deserted village of Newbolt Folville.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPrimrose188917_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPrimrose188917-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>They seem to have gained most their estate at the beginning of the 12th century. Several of their possessions, such as Ashby and the manor at <a href="/wiki/Teigh" title="Teigh">Teigh</a> in Rutland, were in the hands of other parties at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday</a> survey, but had passed to the Folvilles by the reign of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_of_England" title="Stephen of England">Stephen</a> (1135-1154). The family were certainly well-established in Leicestershire by the mid 13th century. In 1240 a member of the family donated a large sum to the church at <a href="/wiki/Cranoe" title="Cranoe">Cranoe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoskins196482_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoskins196482-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Folvilles were rebels during both Barons Wars; Sir William Folville (died about 1240) had his lands seized for his part in the <a href="/wiki/First_Barons%27_War" title="First Barons' War">First Barons' War</a> in 1216<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENichols1795_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENichols1795-3">[3]</a></sup> and Sir Eustace Folville (murdered in 1274) was one of the knights appointed to enforce the <a href="/wiki/Provisions_of_Oxford" title="Provisions of Oxford">Provisions of Oxford</a> in 1258<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVCH_Rutland_II1935_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVCH_Rutland_II1935-6">[6]</a></sup> and stoutly defended <a href="/wiki/Kenilworth_Castle" title="Kenilworth Castle">Kenilworth Castle</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Evesham" title="Battle of Evesham">Battle of Evesham</a> in 1265.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENichols1795_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENichols1795-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The family gained renown during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_II_of_England" title="Edward II of England">Edward II</a>, when they ambushed and killed the <a href="/wiki/Baron_of_the_Exchequer" title="Baron of the Exchequer">Baron of the Exchequer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_de_Beler" title="Roger de Beler">Roger de Beler</a>.  <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_le_Despenser,_1st_Earl_of_Winchester" title="Hugh le Despenser, 1st Earl of Winchester">Hugh le Despenser, 1st Earl of Winchester</a>  had been stealing people's lands in Leicestershire, using Roger de Beler as an enforcer, and in 1325 de Beler had threatened the Folville family with violence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELumley1895_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELumley1895-7">[7]</a></sup> By the beginning of 1326 much of the country had turned against Edward and the Despencers and preparations for a rebellion led by Edward's wife, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_France" title="Isabella of France">Queen Isabella</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Mortimer,_1st_Earl_of_March" title="Roger Mortimer, 1st Earl of March">Roger Mortimer</a> had started.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFryde1979_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFryde1979-8">[8]</a></sup> The Folville family, headed by <a href="/wiki/Eustace_Folville" title="Eustace Folville">Eustace Folville</a>, and encouraged by Sir <a href="/wiki/Roger_la_Zouch" title="Roger la Zouch">Roger la Zouch</a>, Lord of <a href="/wiki/Lubbesthorpe" title="Lubbesthorpe">Lubbesthorpe</a> murdered de Beler before fleeing to Paris. Following Isabella and Mortimer's successful <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_England_(1326)" title="Invasion of England (1326)">invasion of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III</a> was crowned and rebels were pardoned including the Folville family who were celebrated locally with the Folville Cross, said to be located at the site of de Beler's murder.
</p><p>The Folville Gang  flitted in and out of outlawry for many years, but, apart from <a href="/wiki/Richard_Folville" title="Richard Folville">Richard Folville</a>, vicar of Teigh, who was beheaded in his own churchyard, they ended with their freedom intact.
</p><p>The manor of Ashby eventually passed via marriage from the Folvilles to the Woodfords and then Smiths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENichols1795_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENichols1795-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashby_Folville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After World War II, a resettlement camp for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Displaced_people" title="Displaced people">displaced people</a> from Poland  was established in a former US Army base in the grounds of <a href="/wiki/Ashby_Folville_Manor" title="Ashby Folville Manor">Ashby Folville Manor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_sites">Religious sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ashby_Folville&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religious sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The parish church of St Mary is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grade_I_listed_building" title="Grade I listed building">Grade I listed building</a>. The Ashby Folville estate was bought in 1890 by Herbert Smith-Carington, then mayor of <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a> (died 1917), who built cottages and a village institute and restored the church. The old wooden roofs of the nave and the new oak panels of the chancel and screen of the Woodford chapel are among the features of interest. In the chancel are memorials to Ralph Woodford (a descendant of the Folvilles) and Elizabeth Woodford. Monuments in the Woodford chapel include a stone knight known as "Old Folville" and the fine monument of Sir Francis Smith and his wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth1926272-5_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth1926272-5-10">[10]</a></sup> Stained glass windows by <a href="/wiki/Veronica_Whall" title="Veronica Whall">Veronica Whall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Woore" title="Edward Woore">Edward Woore</a> were erected in memory of members of the Smith-Carington family.
</p>
