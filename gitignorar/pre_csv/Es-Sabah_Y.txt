	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Es-Sabah</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Es-Sabah</b></i> ('The Morning') was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judeo-Arabic" title="Judeo-Arabic">Judeo-Arabic</a> daily newspaper, printed in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">Hebrew alphabet</a>, published from <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-p2-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g2_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-g2-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-af_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-af-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-br-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_profile">History and profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Es-Sabah&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Es-Sabah</i> was founded and directed by Jacob Cohen. The first issue came out on 1 November 1904. It was the most popular Jewish newspaper in the country at one point. The issues contained between four and sixteen pages. Politically, it served as an organ for philanthropic Zionism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-br-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Es-Sabah</i> carried the byline <i>Es-Sabah, seul quotidien israélite du Nord-Africain, le plus fort tirage des journaux israélites de Tunisie</i> ("<i>Es-Sabah</i>, the only Jewish daily in North Africa, with the highest circulation of the Jewish papers in Tunisia").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-br-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1930s <i>Es-Sabah</i> was the last remaining Judeo-Arabic newspaper in the country. At the turn of the century, however, Tunisia had hosted a vivid Judeo-Arabic press.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-af_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-af-3">[3]</a></sup> As of 1937 Simon Cohen was the director of <i>Es-Sabah</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-p2_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-p2-1">[1]</a></sup> <i>Es-Sabah</i>, along with other Jewish publications in Tunisia, was suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy regime</a> in October 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-g2_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-g2-2">[2]</a></sup> The last issue of <i>Es-Sabah</i> was published on 14 May 1940.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-br_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-br-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
