	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">List of webcomics in print</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Megatokyo_reader.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2742" data-file-width="1828" decoding="async" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Megatokyo_reader.jpg/220px-Megatokyo_reader.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Megatokyo_reader.jpg/330px-Megatokyo_reader.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Megatokyo_reader.jpg/440px-Megatokyo_reader.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Megatokyo_reader.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Someone reading volume 3 of <i><a href="/wiki/Megatokyo" title="Megatokyo">Megatokyo</a></i> in 2008.</div></div></div>
<p>Though <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webcomics" title="Webcomics">webcomics</a> are typically published primarily on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>, some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Webcartoonist" title="Webcartoonist">webcartoonists</a> may get <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing</a> deals in which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comic_books" title="Comic books">comic books</a> are created of their work. Sometimes, these books are published by mainstream comics publishers who are traditionally aimed at the <a href="/wiki/Direct_market" title="Direct market">direct market</a> of regional comic books.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Publishers_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Publishers-1">[1]</a></sup> Some webcartoonists may pursue <a href="/wiki/Print_syndication" title="Print syndication">print syndication</a> in established <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newspapers" title="Newspapers">newspapers</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magazines" title="Magazines">magazines</a>. In other cases, webcomic creators decide to <a href="/wiki/Self-publishing" title="Self-publishing">self-publish</a> their work. Crowdfunding through <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a> is often used in order to fund such projects.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_of_webcomics">Publication of webcomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_webcomics_in_print&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Publication of webcomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Business_of_webcomics" title="Business of webcomics">Business of webcomics</a></div>
<p>Though mainstream comic book publishers have typically been wary of licensing webcomics and adapting them into a print format, the rise of webcomics in the 2000s coincided with an American boom in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graphic_novels" title="Graphic novels">graphic novels</a>. Anna Baddeley, writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, stated that the established fanbase many webcomics have could give publishers a chance to attract new audiences to the print format, making webcomics an attractive focus for publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The traditional audience base for webcomics and print comics are vastly different, and webcomic readers do not necessarily go to bookstores. For some webcartoonists, a print release may be considered the "goal" of a webcomic series, while for others, comic books are "just another way to get the content out."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Caitlin Rosberg, writing for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paste_Magazine" title="Paste Magazine">Paste Magazine</a></i>, noted that <a href="/wiki/Digital_comic" title="Digital comic">"digital-first" comics</a>, as found on platforms such as <a href="/wiki/ComiXology" title="ComiXology">ComiXology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Unlimited" title="Marvel Unlimited">Marvel Unlimited</a> and <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>' Digital First, share more aspects with printed comics than with webcomics. With the exception of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Two-page_spread" title="Two-page spread">two-page spreads</a> and the occasional large-panel layout, the formatting of such digital comics are indistinguishable from their print counterparts. "Digital-first" comics can almost seamlessly transition from screen to print, as they are designed with this leap in platform in mind. Rosberg claimed that such comics are not webcomics, as webcomics are designed for consumption only on the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Web" title="World Wide Web">World Wide Web</a>, often using <a href="/wiki/Infinite_canvas" title="Infinite canvas">infinite canvas</a> techniques or uncommon page formats.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Similarly, Lauren Davis wrote for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comics_Alliance" title="Comics Alliance">Comics Alliance</a></i> that "webcomics are not print comics that happen to appear on the web. They're a distinct animal, offer a distinct reading experience, and should be evaluated accordingly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Webcomics have been seen by some artists as a potential new path towards syndication in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Newspapers" title="Newspapers">newspapers</a>, but attempts have rarely proven lucrative. According to <a href="/wiki/Jeph_Jacques" title="Jeph Jacques">Jeph Jacques</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Questionable_Content" title="Questionable Content">Questionable Content</a></i>), "there's no real money" in syndication for webcomic artists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-6">[6]</a></sup> For instance <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Rowland" title="Jeffrey Rowland">Jeffrey Rowland</a> uploaded his webcomics to the internet in order to gain constructive criticism after being rejected from various syndicates in 1999, but eventually found that he didn't need to get his work syndicated when he started selling merchandise of his webcomic <i>Wigu</i>. To <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i>, Rowland said that "if a syndicate came to me and offered me a hundred newspapers, I would probably say no ... I'd probably make less money, with more work." When <i><a href="/wiki/Diesel_Sweeties" title="Diesel Sweeties">Diesel Sweeties</a></i> found syndication by <a href="/wiki/United_Media" title="United Media">United Media</a> in 2007, its creator Richard Stevens still made 80% of his income through his website. Other webcomic creators, such as R. K. Milholland (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Something_Positive" title="Something Positive">Something Positive</a></i>) and Michael Terracciano (<i>Dominic Deegan</i>), wouldn't be able to syndicate their work in newspaper because they fill up a specific <a href="/wiki/Niche_market" title="Niche market">niche</a> and wouldn't be accepted by a broader audience.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boston_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Boston-6">[6]</a></sup> Some webcartoonists have proven more successful with newspaper syndication since: in 2015, <a href="/wiki/Dana_Simpson" title="Dana Simpson">Dana Simpson</a> syndicated her webcomic <i><a href="/wiki/Phoebe_and_Her_Unicorn" title="Phoebe and Her Unicorn">Phoebe and Her Unicorn</a></i> through <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Uclick" title="Universal Uclick">Universal Uclick</a> to over 100 newspapers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phoebe_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phoebe-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many authors opt to self-publish their webcomic in print. In order to do so, many comic artists may use the <a href="/wiki/Crowdfunding" title="Crowdfunding">crowdfunding</a> service <a href="/wiki/Kickstarter" title="Kickstarter">Kickstarter</a>, which successfully funded 994 comic and graphic novel projects in 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Published_webcomics">Published webcomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_webcomics_in_print&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Published webcomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996, David Allen launched <a href="/wiki/Plan_Nine_Publishing" title="Plan Nine Publishing">Plan Nine Publishing</a>, a small press American publisher focused on printing webcomics. The first webcomic Plan Nine published was <a href="/wiki/Bill_Holbrook" title="Bill Holbrook">Bill Holbrook</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Kevin_and_Kell" title="Kevin and Kell">Kevin and Kell</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Plan Nine published over 70 titles, printing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1995-99_in_webcomics" title="1995-99 in webcomics">late 1990s</a> and early 2000s webcomics such as <i><a href="/wiki/Sluggy_Freelance" title="Sluggy Freelance">Sluggy Freelance</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ozy_and_Millie" title="Ozy and Millie">Ozy and Millie</a></i>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greystone_Inn" title="Greystone Inn">Greystone Inn</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/College_Roomies_from_Hell!!!" title="College Roomies from Hell!!!">College Roomies from Hell!!!</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Since 1997, various webcomic creators worldwide have made book deals with larger publishing companies, resulting in their webcomics being adapted into comic books and distributed to retailers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collected_works">Collected works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_webcomics_in_print&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Collected works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 2004 to 2011, graphic novel author and illustrator <a href="/wiki/Kazu_Kibuishi" title="Kazu Kibuishi">Kazu Kibuishi</a> edited the <a href="/wiki/Comics_anthology" title="Comics anthology">comics anthology</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_(comics)" title="Flight (comics)">Flight</a></i>. Published by <a href="/wiki/Image_Comics" title="Image Comics">Image Comics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ballantine_Books" title="Ballantine Books">Ballantine Books</a>, <i>Flight</i> featured short comics by various artists who had varying audiences online.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Publishers_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Publishers-1">[1]</a></sup> The third book in <a href="/wiki/Ted_Rall" title="Ted Rall">Ted Rall</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Attitude:_The_New_Subversive_Cartoonists" title="Attitude: The New Subversive Cartoonists"><i>Attitude</i> series</a>, subtitled "The New Subversive Online Cartoonists" (2006), features interviews with and strips of 21 different webcartoonists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116">[113]</a></sup>
</p>
