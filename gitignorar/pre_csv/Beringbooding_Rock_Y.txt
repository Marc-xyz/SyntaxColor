	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Beringbooding Rock</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

<b>Berongbooding Rock</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> rock formation located approximately 65 km (40 mi) north east of <a href="/wiki/Mukinbudin,_Western_Australia" title="Mukinbudin, Western Australia">Mukinbudin</a> and approximately 80 km (50 mi) east of <a href="/wiki/Beacon,_Western_Australia" title="Beacon, Western Australia">Beacon</a> in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Wheatbelt_(Western_Australia)" title="Wheatbelt (Western Australia)">Wheatbelt</a> region of Western Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwvc_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-cwvc-1">[1]</a></sup> 
</p><p>The site features a large <a href="/wiki/Balancing_rock" title="Balancing rock">balancing boulder</a>, a large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gnamma" title="Gnamma">gnamma</a> hole and paintings of the <a href="/wiki/Kalamaia" title="Kalamaia">Kalamaia</a> people's hands in a cave at the rear of the rock.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwvc_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-cwvc-1">[1]</a></sup> There is also a camping site facilities including picnic tables, fire pits and a toilet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>Like other granite outcrops found in the Wheatbelt, Beringbooding displays the different forms of weathering from water and wind. A large vertical rock face is beginning to take the shape of a wave formation, caused by the same weathering patterns that created <a href="/wiki/Wave_Rock" title="Wave Rock">Wave Rock</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-sci-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 18-acre (7.3 ha) rock is used to collect water<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-sci-3">[3]</a></sup> for the largest rock water catchment tank in Australia, holding around 2,250,000 imp gal (10,200,000 l; 2,700,000 US gal) of water. The tank was built in 1937 using <a href="/wiki/The_Susso" title="The Susso">sustenance labour</a> at a cost of £10,000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Two early pioneer wells are also found nearby.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwvc_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-cwvc-1">[1]</a></sup>  
</p><p>The area was known to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australian" title="Indigenous Australian">Indigenous Australians</a> as a reliable water source. Following European settlement prior to 1902<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> the wells near the rock were equipped with pumps in 1929 as part of the <a href="/wiki/3500_Farms_Scheme" title="3500 Farms Scheme">3500 Farms Scheme</a> to increase the number of settlers in the area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-sci-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Surveys of Beringbooding were completed in 1936 to build a large scale water holding facility. Water is channelled from the rock surface catchment using a wall around the edge of the rock. It then flows into an <a href="/wiki/Aqueduct_(water_supply)" title="Aqueduct (water supply)">aqueduct</a> and fills the tank during rain events. A roof was installed to minimise water loss through evaporation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-sci-3">[3]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Some of the vegetation surrounding the rock include: <i><a href="/wiki/Leptospermum_erubescens" title="Leptospermum erubescens">Leptospermum erubescens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus_websteriana" title="Eucalyptus websteriana">Eucalyptus websteriana</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kunzea_pulchella" title="Kunzea pulchella">Kunzea pulchella</a></i>, as well as species of <i><a href="/wiki/Melaleuca" title="Melaleuca">Melaleuca</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Acacia" title="Acacia">Acacia</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Grevillea" title="Grevillea">Grevillea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hakea" title="Hakea">Hakea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Calothamnus" title="Calothamnus">Calothamnus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Quandong" title="Quandong">Quandongs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sandalwood" title="Sandalwood">Sandalwood</a> and native orchids which support a variety of bird life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cwvc_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-cwvc-1">[1]</a></sup> The area is also home to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudophryne_guentheri" title="Pseudophryne guentheri">Crawling toadlet</a> or Günther's Toadlet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sci_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-sci-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
