	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">James West Clark</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">"James W. Clarke" redirects here. For other people with similar names, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/James_Clark_(disambiguation)" title="James Clark (disambiguation)">James Clark (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p><b>James West Clark</b> (October 15, 1779 – December 20, 1843) was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Representative" title="United States Representative">United States Representative</a> from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>. Born in <a href="/wiki/Bertie_County,_North_Carolina" title="Bertie County, North Carolina">Bertie County</a> to Hannah and Christopher Clark, a successful sea captain and import/export merchant.  James Clark graduated from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princeton_College" title="Princeton College">Princeton College</a> in 1797, was a member of the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_General_Assembly" title="North Carolina General Assembly">State House of Commons</a> in 1802, 1803 and 1811, and was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presidential_elector" title="Presidential elector">presidential elector</a> on the <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">Madison</a> ticket in 1812. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Senate" title="North Carolina Senate">North Carolina Senate</a> from 1812 to 1814 and was elected as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic-Republican" title="Democratic-Republican">Republican</a> to the Fourteenth Congress, serving from March 4, 1815, to March 3, 1817. Clark was appointed chief clerk of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navy_Department" title="Navy Department">Navy Department</a> by Secretary <a href="/wiki/John_Branch" title="John Branch">John Branch</a> and served from 1829 to 1831.  He resigned his appointment in protest (as did John Branch) as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Petticoat_affair" title="Petticoat affair">Petticoat affair</a> (or Peggy Eaton affair), which rocked Washington society and the Jackson administration.
</p><p>In private life, Clark was a planter, businessman and slave owner.  He owned several hundred acres of lands in North Carolina and at least 5,000 acres (20 km<sup>2</sup>) in <a href="/wiki/Dyer_County,_Tennessee" title="Dyer County, Tennessee">Dyer County, Tennessee</a>.  In 1835 he moved the bulk of his male slaves to <a href="/wiki/Livingston,_Alabama" title="Livingston, Alabama">Livingston, Alabama</a>, where he made a substantial profit in the slave hire business.  Clark's son, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Toole_Clark" title="Henry Toole Clark">Henry Toole Clark</a>, helped his father manage the family plantations and their local business interests, which included a grist mill and operating a canal.  His son would later become governor of North Carolina during the Civil War from 1861 to 1862.
</p><p>He died at home in <a href="/wiki/Tarboro,_North_Carolina" title="Tarboro, North Carolina">Tarboro, North Carolina</a>, at the age of 64.
</p>
