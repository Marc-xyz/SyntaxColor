<p><b>Sebastian Virdung</b> (born c. 1465) was a <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> composer and theorist on <a href="/wiki/Musical_instrument" title="Musical instrument">musical instruments</a>. He is grouped among the composers known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colorists_(music)" title="Colorists (music)">Colorists</a>. He studied in <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> as a scholar  of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_von_Soest" title="Johannes von Soest">Johannes von Soest</a> at the chapel of the ducal court. After being ordained, he became chaplain at the court in <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>. Virdung sung in the choir as a male alto until 1505/1506. Around 1506 he became a singer in the chapel of the court of WÃ¼rttemberg in Stuttgart. The following year, in January 1507, he received one of nine succentorships at Konstanz Cathedral where he educated the choirboys until he was dismissed in 1508 presumably for his difficult temperament. 
</p><p>In 1511, he published his treatise <i>Musica getuscht und angezogen</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The text is described as the first printed book on the subject.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> It covered theory of music, counterpoint and composition. However, none of these subjects are to be found in the printed work that survives. Existing sections are based on instruments with illustrations and is notable for being the oldest printed source on this subject. The second is said to be <i>Musica instrumentalis deudsch</i> (1529) by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Agricola" title="Martin Agricola">Martin Agricola</a>. These works are both illustrated, and important for the history of European instruments. It classifies instruments into families. It is also a source on <a href="/wiki/Musical_notation" title="Musical notation">musical notation</a>.
</p>
