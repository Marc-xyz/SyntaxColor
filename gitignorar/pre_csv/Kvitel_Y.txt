<p><b>Kvitel</b> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a>: <span dir="rtl" lang="yi">קוויטל</span>‎, "little note"; plural קוויטלך, <b>kvitelach</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> refers to a practice developed by <a href="/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism" title="Hasidic Judaism">Hasidic Judaism</a> in which a Hasid (a follower of Hasidic Judaism) writes a note with a petitionary prayer and gives it to a <a href="/wiki/Rebbe" title="Rebbe">Rebbe</a> (Hasidic Jewish leader) in order to receive the latter's blessing. This prayer may be a general request for health, livelihood, or success,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gen_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-gen-2">[2]</a></sup> or a specific request such as recovery from illness, the ability to bear children, a wedding match, etc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The writing, giving and reading of a <i>kvitel</i> is treated very seriously by Hasid and Rebbe alike, and is executed according to specific protocols. Because of their inherent sanctity, kvitelach may not be thrown away after use; they are either burned or buried.
</p><p>The practice of giving <i>kvitelach</i> continues today in all the Hasidic courts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>Kvitelach</i> are also placed on the graves of Rebbes and <i><a href="/wiki/Tzadik" title="Tzadik">tzadikim</a></i> (plural of "tzadik," or Jewish holy man) with the hope that the soul of the deceased will intercede for the petitioner in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heaven_(Judaism)" title="Heaven (Judaism)">Heaven</a>. 
</p><p>It is a centuries-old custom for Jews to place <i>kvitelach</i> containing personal prayers to God <a href="/wiki/Placing_notes_in_the_Western_Wall" title="Placing notes in the Western Wall">between the stones of the Western Wall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-origin-5">[5]</a></sup> This practice has been also adopted by <a href="/wiki/Christian_pilgrimage" title="Christian pilgrimage">Christian pilgrims</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-abc-6">[6]</a></sup> and foreign dignitaries as well. More than a million prayer notes are placed in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Wall" title="Western Wall">Western Wall</a> each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leaked_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-leaked-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is unclear when the practice of writing and giving <i>kvitelach</i> began. This practice is not mentioned in the writings of the early <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">kabbalists</a>, nor in the works of the school of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Luria" title="Isaac Luria">Isaac Luria</a> (1534–1572), the father of modern Kabbalah.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The first time it is mentioned is during the time of the <a href="/wiki/Baal_Shem_Tov" title="Baal Shem Tov">Baal Shem Tov</a> (1698–1762), founder of Hasidism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-custom_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-custom-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scholars have suggested that the practice is based on Biblical commentator <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahmanides" title="Nahmanides">Nahmanides</a>'s (1194–1270) interpretation of the scriptural verse "And all those that were numbered of the children of Israel by their fathers' houses, from twenty years old and upward, all that were able to go forth to war in Israel" (<a class="external text" href="https://www.mechon-mamre.org/p/pt/pt0401.htm#45" rel="nofollow">Numbers 1:45</a>). In his commentary on the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a> (the Five Books of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a>), Nahmanides contends that Moses asked each Jew to come before him to be counted. This personal appearance of the Jew before Moses, the <i>tzadik</i> (Jewish holy man), foreshadowed the ceremony of the giving of a <i>kvitel</i> by the Hasid to his Rebbe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kvittel_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-kvittel-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Once the practice of giving <i>kvitelach</i> was established in Hasidic courts, it was treated very seriously. There were cases where Hasidic Jews hired a non-Jew to ride or travel to the Rebbe on <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">Shabbat</a> (the Jewish Sabbath) to deliver a <i>kvitel</i> for a Jew who was ill, a clear violation of the <a href="/wiki/Shabbat#Prohibited_activities" title="Shabbat">Shabbat laws</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kvittel_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-kvittel-10">[10]</a></sup> Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Sholom_Mordechai_Schwadron" title="Sholom Mordechai Schwadron">Sholom Mordechai Schwadron</a> (1835–1911) once responded to a questioner who asked whether a Jew could send a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegram" title="Telegram">telegram</a> on behalf of a sick person on Shabbat: "In my hometown of Zlatshev, there was a desperately sick person. When the Belzer Rebbe (the Rebbe of the <a href="/wiki/Belz_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Belz (Hasidic dynasty)">Belz Hasidic dynasty</a>, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Sholom_Rokeach" title="Sholom Rokeach">Sholom Rokeach</a>) was in Brod for Shabbat, the local rabbi allowed the Jews to have a gentile write the name of the sick person and his mother's name and send this <i>kvitel</i> to Brod." This action was vehemently protested by Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Shlomo_Kluger" title="Shlomo Kluger">Shlomo Kluger</a> as well as by the Belzer Rebbe, and the rabbi was removed from his post.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kvittel_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-kvittel-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_the_kvitel">Writing the kvitel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing the kvitel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Hasidic courts, the <i>kvitel</i> is inscribed with the names of the petitioner and his family members, along with their specific requests.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-custom_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-custom-9">[9]</a></sup> The form of the name is the person's full Hebrew name and his mother's Hebrew name (e.g. <i>Shmuel ben Chana</i>, "Shmuel the son of Chana"), even if the Rebbe already knows who he is.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is customary to write the <i>kvitel</i> on a blank, unlined piece of paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>Customs differ as to who writes the <i>kvitel</i>. In some courts, the Rebbe's <a href="/wiki/Gabbai" title="Gabbai">attendant</a> writes the <i>kvitel</i> on behalf of the petitioner;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> in others, a specific person is paid to write the <i>kvitelach</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-custom_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-custom-9">[9]</a></sup> Alternately, the petitioner himself writes the <i>kvitel</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-machberes_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-machberes-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>Various customs arose around the writing of a <i>kvitel</i>. It is considered a bad omen if a <i>kvitel</i> falls on the ground, or if sand is placed on it. (Sand was commonly used as a drying agent for ink on paper.) Care is taken to write the <i>kvitel</i> without any mistakes, as Hasidic Jews believe that <i>kvitelach</i> contain deep secrets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-custom_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-custom-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Giving_the_kvitel">Giving the kvitel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Giving the kvitel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>kvitel</i> is either sent to the Rebbe by messenger or mail, or given personally by the Hasid during his private audience with the Rebbe. The <i>kvitel</i> is usually given together with a sum of money known as a <i>pidyon</i> (redemption), which is used by the Rebbe for the upkeep of his court or for distribution to charity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gen_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-gen-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobs_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs-16">[16]</a></sup> Some Rebbes requested from the Hasid a sum of money equal to twice the <a href="/wiki/Gematria" title="Gematria">numerical value</a> of the Hebrew word <i><a href="/wiki/Chai_(symbol)" title="Chai (symbol)">Chai</a></i> (life), which equals 18.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wings_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-wings-17">[17]</a></sup> Others took an amount of money equal to the numerical value of the letters of the Hebrew names of the Hasid or his wife.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wings_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-wings-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Women are also welcome to visit a Rebbe and present their <i>kvitelach</i> to him. However, the Rebbe does not look at the woman directly while giving his blessing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The giving of the first <i>kvitel</i> cements the status of a newly appointed Hasidic Rebbe. In Belz tradition, the first <i>kvitel</i> to a new Rebbe is proffered by a follower of the <a href="/wiki/Ropshitz_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Ropshitz (Hasidic dynasty)">Ropshitz Hasidic dynasty</a>. Thus, when Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yissachar_Dov_Rokeach_(third_Belzer_rebbe)" title="Yissachar Dov Rokeach (third Belzer rebbe)">Yissachar Dov Rokeach</a> assumed the mantle of leadership in 1894 after the death of his father, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yehoshua_Rokeach" title="Yehoshua Rokeach">Yehoshua Rokeach</a>, he was given his first <i>kvitel</i> by Rabbi Yissachar Dov of Bisk, a follower of the Ropshitz dynasty. Thirty-three years later, following the funeral of Rabbi Yissachar Dov Rokeach, Rokeach's son and successor, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Aharon_Rokeach" title="Aharon Rokeach">Aharon Rokeach</a>, received his first <i>kvitelach</i> from Rabbi Yissachar Dov of Bisk and two other followers of the Ropshitz dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup>
</p><p>The followers of the <a href="/wiki/Ger_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Ger (Hasidic dynasty)">Ger Hasidic dynasty</a> have in their possession the <i>Kotzer Kvitel</i>, a long note written by an elderly Hasid who had attended the courts of Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yehudah_Aryeh_Leib_Alter" title="Yehudah Aryeh Leib Alter">Yehudah Aryeh Leib Alter</a> (the <i>Sefas Emes</i>), Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Chanokh_Heynekh_HaKohen_Levin" title="Chanokh Heynekh HaKohen Levin">Chanoch Henoch of Alexander</a>, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yitzchak_Meir_Alter" title="Yitzchak Meir Alter">Yitzchak Meir Alter</a> (the <i>Chiddushei Harim</i>) and Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Menachem_Mendel_of_Kotzk" title="Menachem Mendel of Kotzk">Menachem Mendel of Kotzk</a>. This Hasid presented the <i>kvitel</i>, containing his memories of these former Rebbes of the Ger dynasty, to Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Avraham_Mordechai_Alter" title="Avraham Mordechai Alter">Avraham Mordechai Alter</a> (the <i>Imrei Emes</i>) upon the latter's appointment as Rebbe of the Ger dynasty.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reading_the_kvitel">Reading the kvitel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reading the kvitel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If the <i>kvitel</i> is delivered by mail or messenger, the Rebbe's attendant reads it to the Rebbe. If the Hasid is present, the Rebbe reads the <i>kvitel</i> during their audience. Afterwards, the Rebbe blesses the petitioner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-custom_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-custom-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>Rebbes traditionally devote their utmost attention to reading <i>kvitelach</i>. It was said of the <a href="/wiki/Bohush_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Bohush (Hasidic dynasty)">Bohusher</a> Rebbe, Rabbi Yitzchok Friedman, that when he read a <i>kvitel</i>, he put his whole being into the piece of paper before bestowing his blessing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kest_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-kest-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Satmar_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Satmar (Hasidic dynasty)">Satmar</a> Rebbe, Rabbi <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yoel_Teitelbaum" title="Yoel Teitelbaum">Yoel Teitelbaum</a>, was known to scrutinize each <i>kvitel</i> and point out errors in the writing of names of people he had never met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> 
</p><p>Stories are told about Rebbes who were able to read into a <i>kvitel</i> the situations of those who were named in it. Once a bride-to-be and her mother visited the Bohusher Rebbe, Rabbi Yitzchok Friedman, for a blessing. The Rebbe's attendant wrote the <i>kvitel</i> hastily, noting next to the mother's name that she was about to marry. The Rebbe glanced at the <i>kvitel</i> and said, "She is already married."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kest_21-1"><a href="#cite_note-kest-21">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>When Rabbi Aharon Rokeach, the Belzer Rebbe, was hiding from the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazis</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Krak%C3%B3w_Ghetto" title="Kraków Ghetto">Kraków Ghetto</a> in 1942, he accepted a <i>kvitel</i> from one of the men who was assigned to protect him. As the names of the man's children were read aloud, the Rebbe continually stopped the reader when he reached a certain child's name and asked him to begin reading the <i>kvitel</i> again. This happened several times. Later the man learned that this son had died suddenly during the war, but the rest of his family survived.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p><p>It was said of "great Rebbes" that in their presence, petitioners would be struck with awe and would accidentally hand over a blank piece of paper instead of the <i>kvitel</i>. The Rebbe would read the blank page and understand exactly what the person wanted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-machberes_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-machberes-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the court of <a href="/wiki/Vizhnitz_(Hasidic_dynasty)" title="Vizhnitz (Hasidic dynasty)">Vizhnitz</a>, it was known that if the Rebbe asked for a cigarette to smoke in the middle of reading a <i>kvitel</i>, it was a sign that the petitioner's request had been accepted. Rabbi Eliezer Dovid Friedman, a follower of the fourth Vizhnitzer Rebbe, Rabbi Chaim Meir Hager, witnessed this in 1965 when he delivered a <i>kvitel</i> to the Rebbe on behalf of a <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> Jew stricken with <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> and the Rebbe asked for a cigarette while reading the <i>kvitel</i>. The man recovered completely. However, in 1972, immediately after the death of the Rebbe, the Melbourne Jew fell ill with the same disease and succumbed to it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="At_a_grave">At a grave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: At a grave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Beshttomb1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="1944" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Beshttomb1.jpg/125px-Beshttomb1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Beshttomb1.jpg/188px-Beshttomb1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Beshttomb1.jpg/250px-Beshttomb1.jpg 2x" width="125"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Beshttomb1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Kvitelach</i> left on the grave of the <a href="/wiki/Baal_Shem_Tov" title="Baal Shem Tov">Baal Shem Tov</a> in <a href="/wiki/Medzhybizh" title="Medzhybizh">Medzhybizh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>It is a common practice for Hasidic Jews to place <i>kvitelach</i> on the gravesite of a Rebbe or <i>tzadik</i> with the belief that the soul of the deceased will pray for them in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heaven_(Judaism)" title="Heaven (Judaism)">Heaven</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobs_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The visitor usually sits beside the grave to write his <i>kvitel</i> and meditate on his request, and then tears the <i>kvitel</i> and throws it on top of the grave. Many graves of Jewish holy men are constructed with special apertures for the insertion of <i>kvitelach</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-custom_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-custom-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="At_the_Western_Wall">At the Western Wall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: At the Western Wall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Placing_notes_in_the_Western_Wall" title="Placing notes in the Western Wall">Placing notes in the Western Wall</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Kvitelwoman.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" decoding="async" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Kvitelwoman.jpg/125px-Kvitelwoman.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Kvitelwoman.jpg/188px-Kvitelwoman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Kvitelwoman.jpg/250px-Kvitelwoman.jpg 2x" width="125"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kvitelwoman.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A woman places a prayer note in the Wall.</div></div></div>
<p>The <i>kvitelach</i> placed in the Western Wall differ from the <i>kvitelach</i> given in Hasidic courts, as they contain prayers, requests or messages written directly to God.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-origin-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> These prayer notes are folded and wedged into the cracks and crevices of the Wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The rationale for this practice has been traced to the <a href="/wiki/Midrash" title="Midrash">Midrashic</a> teaching that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shechina" title="Shechina">Divine Presence</a> has never moved from the Western Wall,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">Kabbalistic</a> teaching that all prayers ascend to Heaven through the <a href="/wiki/Temple_Mount" title="Temple Mount">Temple Mount</a>, which the Western Wall abuts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-origin-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> More than a million prayer notes are placed in the Western Wall each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leaked_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-leaked-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1560" decoding="async" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg/125px-Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg/188px-Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg/250px-Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg 2x" width="125"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prayer_Papers_in_the_Western_Wall.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Kvitelach</i> wedged between the cracks of the Western Wall.</div></div></div>
<p>The practice of placing prayer notes in the Western Wall has also been adopted by <a href="/wiki/Christian_pilgrimage" title="Christian pilgrimage">Christian pilgrims</a> and people of other faiths.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abc_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-abc-6">[6]</a></sup> Foreign dignitaries who have publicly placed a <i>kvitel</i> in the Western Wall include <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> in 2000,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Clinton</a> in 2005,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a> in 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/2008_United_States_presidential_election" title="2008 United States presidential election">U.S. presidential candidate</a> <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> in 2008,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leaked_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-leaked-7">[7]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/2012_United_States_presidential_election" title="2012 United States presidential election">U.S. presidential candidate</a> <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">Mitt Romney</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Ann_Romney" title="Ann Romney">Ann</a> in 2012.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a> inserted a handwritten <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish language</a> text of the <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Prayer" title="Lord's Prayer">Lord's Prayer</a> in the Wall during his visit in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> On May 22, 2017, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> became the first sitting U.S. President to visit the Wall; he also inserted a note.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup>
</p><p>Based on the 11th century French rabbi <a href="/wiki/Gershom_ben_Judah" title="Gershom ben Judah">Rabbeinu Gershom</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Herem_(war_or_property)" title="Herem (war or property)">ban</a> against reading another person's mail, Jewish law forbids the reading of notes that have been inserted in the Western Wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disposal">Disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Kvitelach</i> may not be thrown away; there is a difference of opinion as to whether they should be burned or buried. According to Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Shmuel_Rabinovitch" title="Shmuel Rabinovitch">Shmuel Rabinovitch</a>, Rabbi of the Western Wall and author of <i>Minhagei HaKotel</i>, a book of <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">laws</a> regarding the Western Wall, burning is a "pure" way to deal with the notes, but burying them is more honorable. Twice a year, Rabbi Rabinovitch and his assistants collect the hundreds of thousands of <i>kvitelach</i> left in the Wall and bury them in the Jewish cemetery on the <a href="/wiki/Mount_of_Olives" title="Mount of Olives">Mount of Olives</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Kvitelach</i> left at gravesites are traditionally burned. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ohel_(Chabad)" title="Ohel (Chabad)">gravesite</a> of the sixth and seventh <a href="/wiki/Chabad" title="Chabad">Lubavitcher</a> Rebbes, Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Yosef_Yitzchak_Schneersohn" title="Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn">Yosef Yitzchak Schneersohn</a> and Rabbi <a href="/wiki/Menachem_Mendel_Schneerson" title="Menachem Mendel Schneerson">Menachem Mendel Schneerson</a>, includes a <a href="/wiki/Fax" title="Fax">fax</a> machine which receives over 700 faxes a day, and a computer which receives 400 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emails" title="Emails">emails</a> daily. These <i>kvitelach</i> are all printed and then taken to the graves, where they are torn into shreds and placed atop the graves. When the pile grows too high, the shredded notes are burned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_versions">Electronic versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kvitel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Electronic versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In today's electronic age, many online services offer petitioners the chance to send their <i>kvitel</i> to the Western Wall via email, fax, <a href="/wiki/Text_messaging" title="Text messaging">text messaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>; the <i>kvitel</i> is then printed out and inserted in the cracks of the Wall.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> The "Send a Kvitel Service" of kevarim.com receives <i>kvitelach</i> via Internet and then dispatches them to the gravesites of <i>tzadikim</i> in North America with people who travel to these gravesites.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nikolsburg_(Monsey)" title="Nikolsburg (Monsey)">Nikolsburger</a> Rebbe himself accepts <i>kvitelach</i> and <i>pidyonos</i> via Internet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup>
</p><p>Sending <i>kvitelach</i> to the grave of a Rebbe or <i>tzadik</i> has also become a fund-raising tool. Mosdos Kever Rachel (Kever Rachel Foundation) encourages donors to send messages and prayers which will be read out at <a href="/wiki/Rachel%27s_Tomb" title="Rachel's Tomb">Rachel's Tomb</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Breslov_Research_Institute" title="Breslov Research Institute">Breslov Research Institute</a> website offers donors the opportunity to send a "digital <i>kvitel</i>" to be read by the grave of Rebbe <a href="/wiki/Nachman_of_Breslov" title="Nachman of Breslov">Nachman of Breslov</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uman,_Ukraine" title="Uman, Ukraine">Uman, Ukraine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
