	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Peter Dickinson (architect)</h1>

<p><b>Peter Allgood Rastall Dickinson</b> (21 October 1925 - 15 October 1961) was a <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">British</a>-<a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian</a> architect. Practising from the late 1940s to the early 1960s and working primarily in <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, Dickinson is credited as one of the leaders in developing the <a href="/wiki/Mid-century_modern" title="Mid-century modern">Mid-Century Modern</a> style in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> during the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> period.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Dickinson_(architect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Peter Dickinson was born on 21 October 1925 in <a href="/wiki/Walberswick" title="Walberswick">Walberswick</a>. His father, Eric, was a partner in the brokerage firm Dickinson and Sidebottom. Peter Dickinson grew up in a home on Grove End Road in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._John%27s_Wood" title="St. John's Wood">St. John's Wood</a>, and attended <a href="/wiki/Westminster_School" title="Westminster School">Westminster School</a> from 1938 to 1941. Having developed a passion for drawing, Dickinson entered the <a href="/wiki/Architectural_Association_School_of_Architecture" title="Architectural Association School of Architecture">Architectural Association School of Architecture</a> in 1942. After two years of school, Dickinson served in the <a href="/wiki/Grenadier_Guards" title="Grenadier Guards">Grenadier Guards</a> beginning in 1944 and was honourably discharged on 26 November 1945. After the War, Dickinson returned to AA, studying with <a href="/wiki/H._T._Cadbury-Brown" title="H. T. Cadbury-Brown">H. T. Cadbury-Brown</a> and graduating  in February 1948. Upon graduation, his first job was with architect <a href="/wiki/Wells_Coates" title="Wells Coates">Wells Coates</a>. During his remaining time in England,  Dickinson worked on a variety of small projects. While on a business trip to England, Toronto architect Forsey Page of <a href="/wiki/Page_and_Steele" title="Page and Steele">Page and Steele</a> discovered Dickinson and offered him the position of head designer at the firm.
</p><p>On 3 March 1950, Dickinson married Vera Klausner. The couple had met at the Esmeralda, a club owned by Vera's sister. The two sisters were Austrian and at the outbreak of the War had been sent to England to go to boarding school. Two months after marrying, the newlyweds sailed to Canada on the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Franconia_(1922)" title="RMS Franconia (1922)">RMS Franconia</a>, leaving on 5 May and arriving in <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax</a> on 13 May. Four days later, Dickinson began work in Toronto. Eight years later on 16 May 1958, Peter Dickinson became a Canadian citizen.
</p><p>Forsey Page and Harland Steele's firm operated out of offices on 72 St. Clair Avenue West. The firm had existed since 1925 and by the time Dickinson arrived it was designing buildings in the Georgian-modern style. Significant projects in the years before Dickinson arrived included the British American Oil Building (800 Bay Street) and the Park Lane Apartments (110 St. Clair Avenue West), the latter notable as the residence of <a href="/wiki/Glenn_Gould" title="Glenn Gould">Glenn Gould</a>. As design chief for Page and Steele, Dickinson's early projects included the Lyndwood School, Canadian Red Cross Society Building, and the Toronto Teachers' College. By the mid-1950s, Peter Dickinson had developed a large body of work including office buildings, schools, and apartments. The Dickinsons themselves lived in one of Peter's buildings, the Benvenuto Apartments on Avenue Road.
</p><p>By the late 1950s, Dickinson was eager to go out on his own. In January 1958 he left Page and Steele, forming Peter Dickinson Associates. The associates were Colin Vaughan, Dick Williams, Rod Robbie, and Fred Ashworth and the firm operated out of the Canadian Petrofina Building at 1910 Yonge Street. The first project the new firm received was the Seminary for the Jesuit Fathers of Upper Canada, located in North York. Significant projects from the early days of the new firm included the 55 Yonge Street office building, the Continental Can Building, and the 500 Avenue Road apartments. The new firm had also opened offices in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a>, designing an array of buildings in both of those cities.
</p><p>On 1 October 1960, one of Dickinson's most significant works, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Centre_for_the_Performing_Arts" title="Sony Centre for the Performing Arts">O'Keefe Centre</a>, opened to the public. The opening night featured the premiere of <a href="/wiki/Lerner_and_Loewe" title="Lerner and Loewe">Lerner and Loewe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Camelot_(musical)" title="Camelot (musical)">Camelot</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burton" title="Richard Burton">Richard Burton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julie_Andrews" title="Julie Andrews">Julie Andrews</a>. The building had been designed while Dickinson was still at Page and Steele, and the lobby featured a mural entitled "The Seven Lively Arts," painted by <a href="/wiki/York_Wilson" title="York Wilson">York Wilson</a>.
</p><p>In the late 1950s and early 1960s, Peter Dickinson Associates designed a number of significant projects in Toronto, Ottawa, and Montréal, including the Trans-Canada Pipeline Building, the Elm Ridge Golf and Country Club, and the North Toronto Medical Building. Although not completed until after his death, it was also during this period that he designed the <a href="/wiki/CIBC_Tower" title="CIBC Tower">Imperial Bank Building</a> in Montréal for the bank's head, Neil McKinnon. When it opened in 1962, it was the tallest building in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a>. In January 1960 the four associates with the firm left. Shortly thereafter Dickinson appointed Peter Webb, Boris Zerafa, Jack Korbee, Peter Tirion, René Menkès, and Rick Housden as his new associates. Following Dickinson's death, members of this group would go on to form <a href="/wiki/WZMH_Architects" title="WZMH Architects">WZMH Architects</a>.
</p><p>In the early 1960s, Dickinson, a long-time chain smoker, began to experience stomach pain. He entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai_Hospital,_Toronto" title="Mount Sinai Hospital, Toronto">Mount Sinai Hospital</a> in Toronto in July 1961 and was diagnosed with stomach cancer. While in the hospital, he was visited by <a href="/wiki/Isadore_Sharp" title="Isadore Sharp">Isadore Sharp</a>. Following the visit, Dickinson produced his last design, the <a href="/wiki/Inn_on_the_Park" title="Inn on the Park">Inn on the Park</a>, which he gave to Sharp several days later. That September Dickinson transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Victoria_Hospital,_Montreal" title="Royal Victoria Hospital, Montreal">Royal Victoria Hospital</a> in Montréal. On 15 October 1961, he died at his home in Montréal, leaving behind his wife and two sons.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Dickinson_(architect)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with Dickinson, architects including <a href="/wiki/John_C._Parkin" title="John C. Parkin">John C. Parkin</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=James_Murray_(Canadian_architect)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="James Murray (Canadian architect) (page does not exist)">James Murray</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Caspari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Peter Caspari (page does not exist)">Peter Caspari</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Adamson" title="Gordon Adamson">Gordon Adamson</a> added many modernist buildings to  Toronto's built landscape over the course of the 1950s.
</p><p>While many of Dickinson's works still stand, an increasing number have been lost. Most recently, the Regent Park South Towers and the Jesuit Seminary have been demolished. Out of the five houses that Dickinson designed, only one - the Isadore Sharp Residence - remains.
</p><p>At the urging of Dickinson's widow, John Martins-Manteiga of Dominion Modern wrote the first book solely dedicated to Peter Dickinson's life and work. The book was published in 2010 and was researched primarily using family papers and photographs.
</p><p>Many of Dickinson's original drawings are held at the <a href="/wiki/Archives_of_Ontario" title="Archives of Ontario">Archives of Ontario</a> as part of the "Page and Steele Architects Fonds."
</p>
