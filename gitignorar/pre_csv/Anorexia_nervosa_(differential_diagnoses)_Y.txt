	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Anorexia nervosa (differential diagnoses)</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">For other uses, see <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa_(disambiguation)" title="Anorexia nervosa (disambiguation)">Anorexia nervosa (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>The <b>differential diagnoses</b> of <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_nervosa" title="Anorexia nervosa">anorexia nervosa</a> (AN) includes various types of medical and psychological conditions, which may be  misdiagnosed as AN. In some cases, these conditions may be <a href="/wiki/Comorbidity" title="Comorbidity">comorbid</a> with AN because the misdiagnosis of AN is not uncommon. For example, a case of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achalasia" title="Achalasia">achalasia</a> was misdiagnosed as AN and the patient spent two months confined to a psychiatric hospital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> A reason for the differential diagnoses that surround AN arise mainly because, like other disorders, it is primarily, albeit defensively and adaptive for, the individual concerned.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> 
Anorexia Nervosa  is a psychological disorder characterized by extremely reduced intake of food. People suffering from Aneroxia Nervosa have a low self-image and consider themselves overweight.
Common behaviors and signs of someone suffering from AN:
</p>
<ul><li>Forcing oneself to vigorously exercise even in adverse conditions or when their health does not permit it.</li>
<li>Forcing own self to urinate and excrete waste product from the body.</li>
<li>Using <a href="/wiki/Substituted_amphetamine" title="Substituted amphetamine">substituted amphetamines</a> (stimulants that can reduce appetite) to reduce appetite.</li>
<li>Skin turning yellow</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical">Medical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anorexia_nervosa_(differential_diagnoses)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Medical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the differential or comorbid medical diagnoses may include:
</p>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achalasia" title="Achalasia">achalasia</a> – There have been cases where achalasia, a disorder of the <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a> which affects <a href="/wiki/Peristalsis" title="Peristalsis">peristalsis</a>, has been misdiagnosed as AN. It has been reported in cases where there is sub-clinical manifestation of anorexia nervosa and also in cases where the full diagnostic criteria AN have been met.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuropathy" title="Neuropathy">acute pandysautonomia</a> is one form of an autonomic neuropathy, which is a collection of various syndromes and diseases which affect the autonomic neurons of the <a href="/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system" title="Autonomic nervous system">autonomic nervous system</a> (ANS). Autonomic neuropathies may be the result of an inherited condition or they may be acquired due to various premorbid conditions such as diabetes and alcoholism, bacterial infection such as Lyme disease or a viral illness. Some of the symptoms of ANS which may be associated with an ED include nausea, dysphagia, constipation, pain in the salivary glands, early saiety. It also affects peristalsis in the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>. Acute pandysautonomia may cause emotional instability and has been misdiagnosed as various psychiatric disorders including hysterical neurosis and anorexia nervosa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Systemic lupus erythematosus">Lupus</a>: various <a href="/wiki/Neuropsychiatric_systemic_lupus_erythematosus" title="Neuropsychiatric systemic lupus erythematosus">neuropsychiatric symptoms</a> are associated with systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), including depression. <a href="/wiki/Anorexia_(symptom)" title="Anorexia (symptom)">Anorexia</a> and weight loss also may occur with SLE and while rare it may be misdiagnosed as AN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a> is known as the "great imitator", as it may present as a variety of psychiatric or neurologic disorders including anorexia nervosa.  "A 12 year old boy with confirmed Lyme <a href="/wiki/Arthritis" title="Arthritis">arthritis</a> treated with oral <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antibiotics" title="Antibiotics">antibiotics</a> subsequently became depressed and anorectic. After being admitted to a psychiatric hospital with the diagnosis of anorexia nervosa, he was noted to have positive serologic tests for <a href="/wiki/Borrelia_burgdorferi" title="Borrelia burgdorferi">Borrelia burgdorferi</a>. Treatment with a 14 day course of intravenous antibiotics led to a resolution of his depression and anorexia; this improvement was sustained on 3 year follow-up."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Serology" title="Serology">Serologic</a> testing can be helpful but should not be the sole basis for diagnosis. The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control</a> (CDC) issued a cautionary statement (MMWR 54;125) regarding the use of several commercial tests. Clinical diagnostic criteria have been issued by the CDC (CDC, MMWR 1997; 46: 531-535).</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myoneurogenic_gastrointestinal_encephalopathy" title="Myoneurogenic gastrointestinal encephalopathy">Mitochondrial neurogastrointestinal encephalomyopathy</a> (MNGIE) is a rare genetic disorder characterized by gastrointestinal dysmotility, severe cachexia progressive external <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophthalmoplegia" title="Ophthalmoplegia">ophthalmoplegia</a>, post-prandial emesis (vomiting after eating), peripheral neuropathy, and diffuse <a href="/wiki/Toxic_leukoencephalopathy" title="Toxic leukoencephalopathy">leukoencephalopathy</a>. Onset is prior to age 20 in 60% of cases. ""Miss A" was a 21-year-old Indian woman diagnosed as having treatment-resistant anorexia nervosa." It was subsequently proven to be MNGIE<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_artery_syndrome" title="Superior mesenteric artery syndrome">superior mesenteric artery syndrome</a> (SMA syndrome) "is a gastrointestinal disorder characterized by the compression of the third or transverse portion of the <a href="/wiki/Duodenum" title="Duodenum">duodenum</a> against the <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Superior_mesenteric_artery" title="Superior mesenteric artery">superior mesenteric artery</a> resulting in chronic partial, incomplete, acute or intermittent duodenal obstruction". It may occur as a complication of AN or as a differential diagnosis. There have been reported cases of a tentative diagnosis of AN, where upon treatment for SMA syndrome the patient is asymptomatic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Addison%27s_disease" title="Addison's disease">Addison's disease</a> is a disorder of the <a href="/wiki/Adrenal_cortex" title="Adrenal cortex">adrenal cortex</a> which results in decreased hormonal production. Addison's disease, even in subclinical form, may mimic many of the symptoms of anorexia nervosa.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brain_tumors" title="Brain tumors">Brain tumors</a>: There are multiple cases were the neuropsychiatric symptoms of a brain tumor were attributed to AN, resulting in misdiagnosis. The tumors in these cases were noted in various regions of the brain including the <a href="/wiki/Medulla_oblongata" title="Medulla oblongata">medulla oblongata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypothalamus" title="Hypothalamus">hypothalamus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pituitary_gland" title="Pituitary gland">pituitary gland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pineal_gland" title="Pineal gland">pineal gland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Obex" title="Obex">obex</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simmond%27s_disease" title="Simmond's disease">Simmond's disease</a> (organic hypopituitarism) – "A 20-year-old Japanese man with a hypothalamic tumor which caused <a href="/wiki/Hypopituitarism" title="Hypopituitarism">hypopituitarism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Diabetes_insipidus" title="Diabetes insipidus">diabetes insipidus</a> was mistakenly diagnosed as anorexia nervosa because of anorexia, weight loss, denial of being ill, changes in personality, and abnormal behavior resembling the clinical characteristics of anorexia nervosa"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fahr%27s_syndrome" title="Fahr's syndrome">Brain calcification</a> either <a href="/wiki/Dystrophic_calcification" title="Dystrophic calcification">dystrophic calcification</a> or <a href="/wiki/Metastatic_calcification" title="Metastatic calcification">metastatic calcification</a> can present with neuropsychiatric symptoms including those associated with AN and comorbid disorders such as obsessive compulsive disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cysts" title="Cysts">cysts</a> that occur in the <a href="/wiki/Central_nervous_system" title="Central nervous system">central nervous system</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Dermoid_cyst" title="Dermoid cyst">dermoid cysts</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arachnoid_cysts" title="Arachnoid cysts">arachnoid cysts</a> can cause neuropsychiatric symptoms including <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">psychosis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celiac_disease" title="Celiac disease">Celiac disease</a> is an inflammatory disorder triggered by peptides from wheat and similar grains which cause an immune reaction in the small intestine. "information on the role of the gastrointestinal system in causing or mimicking eating disorders is scarce."(Leffler DA <i>et al.</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gall_bladder" title="Gall bladder">Gall bladder</a> disease which may be caused by inflammation, infection, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallstones" title="Gallstones">gallstones</a>, obstruction of the gallbladder or torsion of the gall bladder – Many of the symptoms of gall bladder disease may mimic anorexia nervosa (AN). Laura Daly, a woman from Missouri, suffered from an inherited disorder in which the gall bladder was not properly attached; the resultant complications led to multiple erroneous diagnoses of AN. Upon performance of a <a href="/wiki/Secretin-cholecystokinin_test" title="Secretin-cholecystokinin test">CCK test</a>, standard imaging techniques are done with the patient lying prone, in this instance it was done with the patient in an upright position. The gall bladder was shown to be in an abnormal position having flipped over the liver. The gallbladder was removed and the patient has since recovered. The treatment was performed by William P. Smedley in Pennsylvania.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colon_(anatomy)" title="Colon (anatomy)">colonic</a> <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> misdiagnosed as anorexia nervosa in a physician at the hospital where she worked – "This patient, who had severe wasting, was misdiagnosed as having anorexia nervosa despite the presence of other symptoms suggestive of an organic disease, namely, fever and diarrhea"(Madani, A 2002).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crohn%27s_disease" title="Crohn's disease">Crohn's disease</a>: "We report three cases of young 18 to 25 year-old girls, initially treated for anorexia nervosa in a psychiatric department. Diagnosis of Crohn's disease was made within 5 to 13 years."(Blanchet C, Luton JP. 2002)"This disease should be diagnostically excluded before accepting anorexia nervosa as final diagnosis". (Wellmann W <i>et al.</i>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></li>
<li>hypothyroidism, <a href="/wiki/Hyperthyroidism" title="Hyperthyroidism">hyperthyroidism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypoparathyroidism" title="Hypoparathyroidism">hypoparathyroidism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyperparathyroidism" title="Hyperparathyroidism">hyperparathyroidism</a> may mimic some of the symptoms of, can occur concurrently with, be masked by or exacerbate an eating disorder and/or various comorbid disorders such as <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a> and <a href="/wiki/Depression_(mood)" title="Depression (mood)">depression</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Insulinoma" title="Insulinoma">Insulinomas</a> are (pancreatic tumors) that cause an overproduction of <a href="/wiki/Insulin" title="Insulin">insulin</a>, causing <a href="/wiki/Hypoglycemia" title="Hypoglycemia">hypoglycemia</a>. Various neurological deficits have been ascribed to this condition including misdiagnosis as an eating disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Multiple_sclerosis" title="Multiple sclerosis">Multiple sclerosis</a> (encephalomyelitis disseminata) is a progressive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autoimmune_disorder" title="Autoimmune disorder">autoimmune disorder</a> in which the protective covering (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myelin_sheath" title="Myelin sheath">myelin sheath</a>) of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nerve_cells" title="Nerve cells">nerve cells</a> is damaged as a result of <a href="/wiki/Inflammation" title="Inflammation">inflammation</a> and resultant attack by the bodies own immune system. In its initial presentation, MS has been misdiagnosed as an eating disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychological">Psychological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anorexia_nervosa_(differential_diagnoses)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Psychological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are various other psychological issues that may factor into anorexia nervosa, some fulfill the criteria for a separate Axis I  <a href="/wiki/Diagnosis" title="Diagnosis">diagnosis</a> or a personality disorder which is coded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_II_(psychiatry)" title="Axis II (psychiatry)">Axis II</a>  and thus are considered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comorbid" title="Comorbid">comorbid</a> to the diagnosed eating disorder. Axis II disorders are subtyped into 3 "clusters", A, B and C. The <a href="/wiki/Causality" title="Causality">causality</a> between personality disorders and eating disorders has yet to be fully established.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> Some people have a previous disorder which may increase their vulnerability to developing an eating disorder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Some develop them afterwards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The severity and type of eating disorder symptoms have been shown to affect comorbidity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> These comorbid disorders themselves have multiple differential diagnoses, such as depression which may be caused by such disparate causes such as <a href="/wiki/Lyme_disease" title="Lyme disease">Lyme disease</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hypothyroidism" title="Hypothyroidism">hypothyroidism</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Body_dysmorphic_disorder" title="Body dysmorphic disorder">Body dysmorphic disorder</a> (BDD) is listed as a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Somatoform_disorder" title="Somatoform disorder">somatoform disorder</a> that affects up to 2% of the population. BDD is characterized by excessive <a href="/wiki/Rumination_(psychology)" title="Rumination (psychology)">rumination</a> over an actual or perceived physical flaw. BDD has been diagnosed equally among men and women. While BDD has been misdiagnosed as anorexia nervosa, it also occurs comorbidly in 25% to 39% of AN cases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>BDD is a chronic and debilitating condition which may lead to social isolation, major depression, suicidal ideation and attempts. Neuroimaging studies to measure  response to facial recognition have shown activity predominately in the left hemisphere in the left <a href="/wiki/Lateral_prefrontal_cortex" title="Lateral prefrontal cortex">lateral prefrontal cortex</a>, lateral <a href="/wiki/Temporal_lobe" title="Temporal lobe">temporal lobe</a>  and left <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal lobe</a> showing hemispheric imbalance in information processing. There is a reported case of the development of BDD  in a 21 year old male following an inflammatory brain process. Neuroimaging showed the presence of new atrophy in the frontotemporal region.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18056550|noedit_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18056550|noedit-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid18056550|noedit_54-1"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18056550|noedit-54">[54]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Emetophobia" title="Emetophobia">Emetophobia</a> is an anxiety disorder characterized by an intense fear of <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>. A person so afflicted may develop rigorous standards of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Food_hygiene" title="Food hygiene">food hygiene</a>, such as not touching food with their hands. They may become socially withdrawn to avoid situations which in their perception may make them vomit. Many who suffer from emetophobia are diagnosed with anorexia or self-starvation. In severe cases of emetophobia they may drastically reduce their food intake.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></li>
<li>Food avoidance emotional disorder is an eating disorder that affects children which involves a fear of eating which is not accompanied by a fear of weight gain<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> which may be misdiagnosed as anorexia nervosa.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phagophobia" title="Phagophobia">phagophobia</a> is an anxiety disorder characterized by a fear of eating, it is usually initiated by an adverse experience while eating such as <a href="/wiki/Choking" title="Choking">choking</a> or vomiting. Individuals with this disorder may present with complaints of pain while swallowing. There have been cases of it being misdiagnosed as AN.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> A similar phobic anxiety disorder, swallowing phobia may also lead to a misdiagnosis of anorexia nervosa; such individuals do not want to lose weight but typically want to put weight back on that they have lost due to their phobia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-de_Lucas-Taracena-_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-de_Lucas-Taracena--61">[61]</a></sup></li></ul>

<p>The distinction between the diagnoses of anorexia nervosa, <a href="/wiki/Bulimia_nervosa" title="Bulimia nervosa">bulimia nervosa</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eating_disorder_not_otherwise_specified" title="Eating disorder not otherwise specified">eating disorder not otherwise specified</a> (EDNOS) is often difficult to make as there is considerable overlap between patients diagnosed with these conditions. Seemingly minor changes in a patient's overall behavior or attitude can change a diagnosis from "anorexia: binge-eating type" to bulimia nervosa. It is not unusual for a person with an eating disorder to "move through" various diagnoses as his or her behavior and beliefs change over time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zucker1_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-Zucker1-76">[76]</a></sup>
</p>
