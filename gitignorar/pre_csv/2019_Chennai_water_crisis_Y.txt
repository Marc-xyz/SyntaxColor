	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">2019 Chennai water crisis</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b>2019</b>  Chennai water crisis is an ongoing water crisis occurring in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, most notably in the city of <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a> in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a>. On 19 June 2019, Chennai city officials declared that "Day Zero", or the day when almost no water is left, had been reached, as all the four main reservoirs supplying water to the city had run dry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Two years of deficient monsoon rainfall, particularly in late 2017 and throughout much of 2018 had led to this crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because tap water has stopped running, some families have been relying on alternative water sources such as distant, unreliable public water pumps, and costly private water tankers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washPo_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-washPo-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_Chennai_water_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Water_management_in_Chennai" title="Water management in Chennai">Water management in Chennai</a></div>
<p>Chennai is a city of over 8 million people, around the size of New York City, and is the fourth largest city in India.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DowntoEarth_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-DowntoEarth-5">[5]</a></sup> The city is an automotive engineering hub and contains many automotive factories that are built around the reservoirs of the city.  Mismanagement of the city’s water resources and weak monsoons for the last four years reduced the city’s reservoirs to 0.1% of normal capacity in June 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DowntoEarth_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-DowntoEarth-5">[5]</a></sup> Water became a valuable resource in Chennai and experienced exploitation as wealthier residents paid to dig deep bore wells on their land and sold water to other residents or businesses.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-6">[6]</a></sup> This practice was allowed by the government and resulted in the groundwater aquifer to be drained dramatically at twice the level of annual recharge. Protests erupted over the Chennai government’s lack of action on the issue as water was no longer a basic human right in Chennai.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NPR_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-6">[6]</a></sup> The government also faced pressure on their management of the city’s four main reservoirs; Poondi, Cholavaram, Redhills and Chembarambakkam.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DowntoEarth_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-DowntoEarth-5">[5]</a></sup> Factories and infrastructure built in the catchments of these reservoirs were unregulated and therefore much of the rain that fell ended up in the ocean or used in excess by these factories. This further exacerbated the issue and limited the amount of water that entered the reservoirs for public consumption. Monsoon rains in 2019 have been more plentiful in years past and have raised the reservoir levels to around 30% capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Chennai has declared itself water secure because of the recent rains but concern remains about the future of water security in Chennai and many other Indian cities such as Bangalore which have similar regulation issues. An Indian government think tank predicted that 21 Indian cities will be out of groundwater by 2020, and the impact of future droughts in India brought upon by climate change will put the entire country at risk unless stronger regulations are put in place.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>Chennai has historically relied on annual monsoon rains to replenish its water reservoirs since the rivers are polluted with sewage.
</p><p>There are four reservoirs in the city, namely, Red Hills, Cholavaram, Poondi and Chembarambakkam, with a combined capacity of 11,057 mcft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hindu_RainsReplenishReservoirs_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hindu_RainsReplenishReservoirs-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extreme_drought">Extreme drought</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_Chennai_water_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extreme drought">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three years of failed monsoon in 2016, 2017, and 2018. The 2018 northeast monsoon season was one of the driest ever recorded in Chennai, as only 343.7 mm of rain had fallen compared to an average of 757.6 mm, which was a 55% rainfall deficit. Additionally, the entire state of Tamil Nadu had recorded a 23% rainfall deficit in that season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/2019_heat_wave_in_India_and_Pakistan" title="2019 heat wave in India and Pakistan">major heat wave in India</a> from May to June 2019 further exacerbated the problem by evaporating any water still left in reservoirs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_mismanagement">Government mismanagement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_Chennai_water_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Government mismanagement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Government mismanagement and unplanned construction has also been a factor to blame for this crisis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2019_Chennai_water_crisis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Millions of people are without consistent access to water. A lack of rainwater and groundwater has left four of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoirs" title="Reservoirs">reservoirs</a> that supply the city completely dry. The inability to meet demand for water has forced businesses like hotels and restaurants to close. Water tankers from areas of <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> unaffected by drought have been bringing water into some areas of the city. However, government tankers can take up to a month to appear after requested, so some families, wealthy residents, and business owners have opted to pay for costly private water tankers. The poor who live in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slums" title="Slums">slums</a> do not have this option; a family in Chennai's slums may receive as little as 30 litres (7.9 US gallons) of water every day compared to an average American household which uses 1,150 litres (300 US gallons) of water a day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnnChennai1_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-cnnChennai1-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-washPo_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-washPo-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Many fights over water have also broken out as a result of the conflict. In one such conflict that occurred on 15 June 2019, a woman was stabbed and the perpetrator was turned in to the police.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stabbed_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-stabbed-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
