		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Michael Eichberger</b> (born 23 June 1953) is a <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> law scientist and a judge at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany" title="Federal Constitutional Court of Germany">Federal Constitutional Court of Germany</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Eichberger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eichberger was born in <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrzburg" title="Würzburg">Würzburg</a> on 23 June 1953. Following his first state exam in law 1979 in <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a> and his second one in 1981 in <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> he was a research assistant at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mainz" title="University of Mainz">University of Mainz</a> until 1984. Afterwards he did his doctoral dissertation there in 1985. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Eichberger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">Early career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Eichberger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Between 1984 and 1986 Eichberger was a judge at the administrative court in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>. Later, from 1986 until 1989, he worked at the Justice Ministry of Baden-Württemberg. After that he worked at the Federal Constitutional Court of Germany as a research assistant until 1991. Since 1992 he was a judge at administrative court in <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a> again and in 1993 he was delegated as a judge to the Higher Administrative Court of <a href="/wiki/Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Baden-Württemberg">Baden-Württemberg</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>, where he stayed until 1998. Since 1998/99 he was a docent at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of Tübingen">University of Tübingen</a>. Between 1998 and 2006 he was a judge at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federal_Administrative_Court_of_Germany" title="Federal Administrative Court of Germany">Federal Administrative Court of Germany</a> (BVerwG) in <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">Leipzig</a> as well. From 1998 until 2002, he was a member of the Senate for the law concerning aliens and law of asylum. He subsequently served on the Senates for road and railway line planning, land reorganisation, revenue and tax law. In 2004 he became an honorary professor at the University of Tübingen. 
</p>
<h3><span id="Judge_of_the_Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany.2C_2006.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Judge_of_the_Federal_Constitutional_Court_of_Germany,_2006–present">Judge of the Federal Constitutional Court of Germany, 2006–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Eichberger&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Judge of the Federal Constitutional Court of Germany, 2006–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nominated by the <a href="/wiki/CDU/CSU" title="CDU/CSU">CDU/CSU</a> parliamentary group, Eichberger was appointed as a judge at the Federal Constitutional Court of Germany in April 2006. He has since been serving on the First Senate.
</p><p>On the initiative of president <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vo%C3%9Fkuhle" title="Andreas Voßkuhle">Andreas Voßkuhle</a>, Eichberger was among four justices who were mandated in 2016 to draft a revised code of conduct, which set out rules for the justices’ public appearances, gifts, secondary income and other aspects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
