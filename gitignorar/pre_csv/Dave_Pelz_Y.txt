<p><b>David T. Pelz</b> is an American <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a> coach, known for his expertise and published writing on the art of the short game, particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Putting" title="Putting">putting</a>.
</p><p>Pelz's <i>Short Game Bible</i> was a <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Best_Seller_list" title="The New York Times Best Seller list"><i>New York Times</i> "national best-seller"</a> in 1999. Eleven of Pelz's professional students have won a total of 21 Major golf championships. Pelz was named by <i><a href="/wiki/Golf_Digest" title="Golf Digest">Golf Digest</a></i> magazine as one of the 25 most influential instructors of the 20th Century. Pelz has been a regular editorial contributor to <i>Golf Magazine</i> since 1982 and produced and hosted "The Dave Pelz Scoring Game Show" on the Golf Channel from 1995 to 2005. Pelz continues his research and instruction at the Pelz Golf Institute in <a href="/wiki/Spicewood,_Texas" title="Spicewood, Texas">Spicewood, Texas</a>. Current PGA Tour professional students include Phil Mickelson, Patrick Reed and Brendan Steele.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pelz attended <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">Indiana University</a> on a four-year golf scholarship where he majored in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>. He played, and lost to, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Nicklaus" title="Jack Nicklaus">Jack Nicklaus</a> on 22 occasions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NASA">NASA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NASA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1961, Pelz joined <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, working at the Goddard Space Flight Center doing research on the upper atmospheres of the earth and other planets in the solar system. Pelz became a senior scientist with responsibilities for several satellite programs, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer_program" title="Explorer program">Explorer</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_research">Initial research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Initial research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pelz, still disappointed at his own inability to make the grade for the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA Tour</a>, decided to apply his knowledge of physics to the game. Pelz's own weakness was his short game, so in 1970 he began measuring what happens when the <a href="/wiki/Putter" title="Putter">putter</a> head strikes the ball, and how the mechanics of player and club swing through the putter. His research led to the development of the "Teacher Putter" <a href="/wiki/Patent" title="Patent">patent</a>. Pelz improved his putting enough to qualify for, and play in, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Amateur" title="U.S. Amateur">U.S. Amateur</a>, though he lost in the second round, and he finished as a medalist in the Maryland State Amateur. His experiences convinced him that good putting, far from being solely a natural ability, could be learned.
</p><p>In 1975, Pelz took a <a href="/wiki/Leave_of_absence" title="Leave of absence">leave of absence</a> from NASA and started Preceptor Golf, formed to manufacture and market the Teacher Putter. At first, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USGA" title="USGA">USGA</a> ruled against the Teacher Putter, saying it was "designed to be adjustable during play." They later ruled if two separate inserts were used, it would conform to the rules. In 1996, the USGA again banned the Teacher Putter on the grounds that it is not "plain in shape."
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Preceptor_Golf">Preceptor Golf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preceptor Golf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pelz resigned from NASA on January 1, 1976 to concentrate on his golfing endeavours. Preceptor Golf began to offer custom-fitted clubs, and developed a method of engraving a player's signature on each head in a set of stainless steel clubs. Pelz also launched his "Teacher Clips", a development which turns any putter into a "Teacher Putter"-like club; and developed "The True Roller", a device that rolls a perfect putt and was integral to his later research into putting.
</p><p>In 1977, Pelz began an analysis of every shot in golf. Using caddies, tour players and amateur golfers, he spent more than three years entering the data from thousands of rounds (shot distance, where each shot landed, relation to target, and so forth), coming to the conclusion that more than 60% of golf shots are part of the 'short game' - those made from within around 100 yards of the hole. He discovered that players with the best short games win the most money, and that while touring professionals miss shots from further than 100 yards from the hole by an average 7% of the total shot distance, that percentage rises to 16–20% on shots from within 100 yards. This research formed the basis for many of his future efforts in golf, and led to Preceptor launching "Frequency Analyzers", becoming the first club manufacturer to offer frequency-matched sets of clubs.
</p><p>Using this analysis, Pelz began teaching and coaching PGA Tour players one-on-one in their short and putting games. <a href="/wiki/Andy_North" title="Andy North">Andy North</a> becomes first Pelz student to win a major championship, winning the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Open_(golf)" title="U.S. Open (golf)">U.S. Open Golf Championship</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Hills_Country_Club" title="Cherry Hills Country Club">Cherry Hills Country Club</a>.
</p><p>In 1982, Preceptor launched the "Quick Change Hosel", which allows players to easily interchange shafts and clubs. This becomes the basis for their new custom club-fitting system, which concentrated on ball performance instead of players' heights, weights and similar factors. In 1984, after Pelz had tested clubs on 500 golfers, Preceptor launched the "FeatherLite" line of clubs, the first golf clubs with lighter heads on more flexible shafts.
</p><p>In 1983, <a href="/wiki/Barney_Adams" title="Barney Adams">Barney Adams</a> had joined <b>Dave Pelz Golf Inc.</b>, which included Preceptor. When Preceptor went <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bankrupt" title="Bankrupt">bankrupt</a> in 1986, Adams bought the assets and started <a href="/wiki/Adams_Golf" title="Adams Golf">Adams Golf</a>, which he moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dallas,_Texas" title="Dallas, Texas">Dallas, Texas</a> in 1991.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dave_Pelz_Golf">Dave Pelz Golf</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dave Pelz Golf">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the collapse of Preceptor Golf, Pelz has continued to focus on golf research and tuition, while also undertaking more writing and broadcasting to bring his theories to a wider audience.
</p><p>While teaching at the "Short Game School" in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>, Pelz felt his theories to be partially vindicated when one of his pupils, an amateur from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Midland-Odessa" title="Midland-Odessa">Midland-Odessa</a>, defeated <a href="/wiki/Ben_Crenshaw" title="Ben Crenshaw">Ben Crenshaw</a> in the Texas State Putting Championship. In 1996 Pelz staged his first World Putting Championship at <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a> in <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>, with 17,000 participants competing for a $250,000 top prize. The winner was <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA Tour</a> player <a href="/wiki/Len_Mattiace" title="Len Mattiace">Len Mattiace</a>.
</p><p>Having started writing for <i><a href="/wiki/Golf_Magazine" title="Golf Magazine">Golf Magazine</a></i> in 1983, from 1995 to 2005 Pelz wrote and appeared in thirteen half-hour shows per year on the <i><a href="/wiki/Golf_Channel" title="Golf Channel">Golf Channel</a>.</i> He also appears on their <i>Live from the Majors</i> telecasts, and continues to contribute monthly instructional articles in <i>GOLF Magazine.</i>
</p><p>Pelz's <i>Short Game Bible</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BookShort_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-BookShort-1">[1]</a></sup> was a New York Times national best-seller in 1999. He has since published <i>Dave Pelz's 10 Minutes a Day to Better Putting</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Book10_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Book10-2">[2]</a></sup> <i>Putt Like the Pros</i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BookPutt_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-BookPutt-3">[3]</a></sup> and <i>Dave Pelz's Putting Bible</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BookPutting_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BookPutting-4">[4]</a></sup> Pelz also has produced four videos, “Dave Pelz's 10 Minutes a Day to Better Putting”, “Fundamentals of Wedge Play”, “Developing Great Touch”, and “The Amazing Truth About Putting.”
</p><p>Pelz's research has resulted in patented product developments, many of which are now licensed to major manufacturers. The patented two- and three-ball putters were licensed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Callaway_Golf" title="Callaway Golf">Callaway Golf</a>, becoming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odyssey_Golf" title="Odyssey Golf">Odyssey Golf</a> Two-Ball putter. Pelz offers two wedge lines, each with aggressive groove designs.
</p><p>In 2004, while filming a TV segment for the <i>Golf Channel</i> during PGA Championship week at Whistling Straits in <a href="/wiki/Kohler,_Wisconsin" title="Kohler, Wisconsin">Kohler, Wisconsin</a>, Pelz holed a 206-foot putt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putt_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Putt-5">[5]</a></sup> A check with Ripley’s determines putt is “longest golf putt ever holed on TV.”
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Scoring_Game">The Scoring Game</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Scoring Game">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Developments_and_patents">Developments and patents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Developments and patents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pelz holds 17 patents on golf equipment, and has developed numerous other products:
</p>
<ul><li>1975 - Teacher Putter</li>
<li>1979 - Teacher Alignment Computer (TAC)</li>
<li>1984 - FeatherLite line of clubs</li>
<li>1984 - Blind Glasses incorporate an LCD screen that an applied voltage makes opaque. During custom fitting sessions, players can see their ball until the moment of impact, and then the glasses turn opaque</li>
<li>1986 - 2-Ball and 3-Ball Putters, which were licensed to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Callaway_Golf" title="Callaway Golf">Callaway Golf</a> and became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Odyssey_Golf" title="Odyssey Golf">Odyssey Golf</a> range</li>
<li>1987 - Truth Board training device to help golfers learn the feel of (and remove fear from) a well-struck three-foot putt</li>
<li>1988 - PGA Tour groove test, which proved how much more backspin and stopping power was produced by box grooves, as compared to standard V-grooves in wedges and high-lofted irons</li>
<li>1990 - LazrAimer voice-activated putting alignment trainer</li>
<li>2001 - Tee Square, Cup Collar and Dave Pelz’s Putting Tutor</li>
<li>2002 - PelzMeter green-speed measurement device, developed by son Eddie Pelz, debuts at the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Open_(golf)" title="U.S. Open (golf)">U.S. Open</a> at Bethpage Black</li>
<li>2003 - O-Balls with innovative cover markings, the first regulation golf ball to provide feedback to golfers after putting, to actually improve their putting strokes</li>
<li>2005 - Position Mat practice aid, enabling golfers to practice sand, wedge and chip shots from the proper stance (feet) and ball positions</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Innovations">Innovations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dave_Pelz&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Innovations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>3 X 4 System - controlling distance in wedge play</li>
<li>The Pendulum - putting method</li>
<li>Pure-In-Line-Stroke - putting methods</li>
<li>Dead Hands Pitching - showed the value of higher lofted wedges (he developed the first 60 and 64-degree lofted wedges)</li>
<li>Optimum Putting Speed - the speed putts should be rolled for holing the most putts possible</li></ul>
