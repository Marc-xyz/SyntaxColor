<p>The <b>Flying Horse Inn</b> is a former public house in <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>. It was established around 1483. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grade_II_listed_building" title="Grade II listed building">Grade II listed building</a>.
</p><p>It stands upon the site of the house which the Plumptre family erected for themselves when they first came to Nottingham in the 13th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The first information of "The Flying Horse," is from 1400 when John de Plumtre founded <a href="/wiki/Plumptre_Hospital" title="Plumptre Hospital">Plumptre Hospital</a>. The property forming the endowment included the oldest portion of The Flying Horse in The Poultry.
</p><p>In the 18th century it was called the "Travellers Inn".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
in 1791 at the Flying Horse Inn.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1799 "The Flying Horse " was in the possession of one William Rowbotham. The house was described as being at the Hen Cross.
</p><p>In 1813, a great dinner was held to celebrate victory over <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a>. A figure of Napoleon had been brought from London by coach, and this was burned in the Market Place amidst scenes of excitement and rejoicing.
</p><p>By 1818, the owner was Robert Mackley. In 1826 the rent was £63 a year. The building was in bad condition and scarcely habitable. After repair the rent increased to £100 a year.  In 1832 Jane Clark occupied The Inn.
</p><p>It was heavily restored in 1935.
</p><p>It survived as a public house until 1989, when it was converted into a shop. It is now at the entrance to the <a href="/wiki/Flying_Horse_Walk" title="Flying Horse Walk">Flying Horse Walk</a> shopping mall.
</p>
