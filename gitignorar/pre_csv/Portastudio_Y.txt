<p>The <a href="/wiki/TASCAM" title="TASCAM">TASCAM</a> <b>Portastudio</b> was the world's first four-track recorder based on a standard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compact_Cassette" title="Compact Cassette">compact audio cassette</a> tape. The term <i>portastudio</i> is exclusive to TASCAM, though it is generally used to describe all self-contained cassette-based multitrack recorders dedicated to music production. The Portastudio, and particularly its first iteration, the Teac 144, is credited with launching the home-recording wave, which allowed musicians to cheaply record and produce music at home,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is cited as one of the most significant innovations in music production technology.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Teac 144 Portastudio made its debut in 1979, at the annual meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Audio_Engineering_Society" title="Audio Engineering Society">Audio Engineering Society</a>. It was followed by several other models by TASCAM,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and eventually by several other manufacturers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>For the first time it enabled musicians to affordably record several instrumental and vocal parts on different tracks of the built-in four-track cassette recorder individually and later blend all the parts together, while transferring them to another standard, two-channel stereo tape deck (<a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixdown</a>) to form a stereo recording.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Tascam Portastudio 244, introduced in 1982, improved upon the previous design with overall better sound quality and more features, including: <a href="/wiki/Dbx_(noise_reduction)" title="Dbx (noise reduction)">dbx</a> noise reduction, dual/concentric sweepable EQ's, and the ability to record on up to 4 tracks simultaneously. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>In general, these machines were typically used by musicians to record <a href="/wiki/Demo_(music)" title="Demo (music)">demos</a>, although they are still used today in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lo-fi" title="Lo-fi">lo-fi</a> recording. The analog portastudios by TASCAM (a division of <a href="/wiki/TEAC_Corporation" title="TEAC Corporation">TEAC</a>) and similar units by <a href="/wiki/Fostex" title="Fostex">Fostex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akai" title="Akai">Akai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Corporation" title="Yamaha Corporation">Yamaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sansui_Electric" title="Sansui Electric">Sansui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marantz" title="Marantz">Marantz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vestax" title="Vestax">Vestax</a>, Vesta Fire, TOA, Audio-Technica, Peavey, and others generally recorded on high-bias cassette tapes. Most of the machines were four-track, but there were also six-track and eight-track units. Some newer digital models record to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hard_disk" title="Hard disk">hard disk</a>, allowing for digital effects and up to 32 tracks of audio.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portastudio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Portastudio supported the bouncing of content between tracks, such as creating a mix of three tracks and recording the sum onto the fourth track. By carefully balancing the volume and equalization, this could be done multiple times to create very lush tracks, reminiscent of the complex four-track production of late-sixties Beatle compositions. A limitation was the introduction of tape hiss, which was a particular issue with early models. This was eliminated with more recent digital models.
</p><p>Unlike standard audio cassette machines that recorded a pair of stereo channels per side, the cassette-based Portastudios recorded four channels in one direction. If you played back a standard two-sided stereo cassette, the second two channels would play in reverse. One interesting (but unpublished) feature was that you could record a track in reverse by flipping the cassette, then flip it back and record additional material. Thanks to the speed control, it was also possible to create a classic flanging effect by recording one track, then recording a second copy of the same track while varying the pitch control, creating a sweeping sound at points where the two tracks converged.
</p><p>An additional feature was support for external signal processing during recording and on final output.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_usages">Notable usages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portastudio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable usages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guided_By_Voices" title="Guided By Voices">Guided By Voices</a> recorded much of their classic-era songs on a Tascam 4-track.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Reid_(musician)" title="William Reid (musician)">William</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Reid" title="Jim Reid">Jim Reid</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Jesus_and_Mary_Chain" title="The Jesus and Mary Chain">The Jesus and Mary Chain</a> used a TASCAM Portastudio to record their first demos sent to <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Gillespie" title="Bobby Gillespie">Bobby Gillespie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alan_McGee" title="Alan McGee">Alan McGee</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Primus_(band)" title="Primus (band)">Primus</a>' first release <i><a href="/wiki/Suck_on_This" title="Suck on This">Suck on This</a></i>, a compilation of several live performances, was recorded entirely on a TASCAM quarter-inch eight-track portastudio like device with a built in 8 track reel to reel recorder called the TASCAM 388.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lou_Reed" title="Lou Reed">Lou Reed</a> made the working demos for his 1988 <i>New York</i> album on a cassette multitrack unit. "I had been making these great cassettes at home," he explained to <a href="/wiki/Musician_(magazine)" title="Musician (magazine)"><i>Musician</i></a> magazine, and Reed chose a recording studio that was able to capture the same sound and spirit, for the album sessions. (A single B-side was drawn directly from a demo cassette.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Frusciante" title="John Frusciante">John Frusciante</a> recorded his two first solo albums <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niandra_Lades_and_Usually_Just_a_T-Shirt" title="Niandra Lades and Usually Just a T-Shirt">Niandra Lades &amp; Usually Just A T-Shirt</a></i> and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smile_From_The_Streets_You_Hold" title="Smile From The Streets You Hold">Smile From The Streets You Hold</a></i> on a Portastudio 424 (source: VPRO 94 interview).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a> recorded his album <i><a href="/wiki/Nebraska_(album)" title="Nebraska (album)">Nebraska</a></i> on a Portastudio 144.</li>
<li>Soviet poet and musician, <a href="/wiki/Viktor_Tsoi" title="Viktor Tsoi">Viktor Tsoi</a>, frontman of the Soviet rock band <a href="/wiki/Kino_(band)" title="Kino (band)">Kino</a>, recorded demos in the late 1980s to early 1990s for their main albums on a Portastudio tape recorder. A few of those albums are <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gruppa_krovi" title="Gruppa krovi">Gruppa krovi</a></i> (Blood Type), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zvezda_po_imeni_Solntse" title="Zvezda po imeni Solntse">Zvezda po imeni Solntse</a></i> (The star called Sun) and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chyorny_albom" title="Chyorny albom">Chyorny albom</a></i> (The Black Album) in couple with a drum machine.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Travis_Meeks" title="Travis Meeks">Travis Meeks</a> of <a href="/wiki/Days_of_the_New" title="Days of the New">Days of the New</a> recorded "Cling" on a TASCAM four-track in his sessions leading up to <a href="/wiki/Days_of_the_New_(1997_album)" title="Days of the New (1997 album)">his debut album</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Vanderslice" title="John Vanderslice">John Vanderslice</a>, an analog recording enthusiast, made <i><a href="/wiki/Life_and_Death_of_an_American_Fourtracker" title="Life and Death of an American Fourtracker">Life and Death of an American Fourtracker</a></i>, a concept album about home recording, including the song "Me and My 424".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alan_Wilder" title="Alan Wilder">Alan Wilder</a> recorded his first solo album <i><a href="/wiki/1_%2B_2_(album)" title="1 + 2 (album)">1+2</a></i>, under the nickname <a href="/wiki/Recoil_(band)" title="Recoil (band)">Recoil</a>, on a four-track Portastudio.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ween" title="Ween">Ween</a> recorded their second album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pod" title="The Pod">The Pod</a></i>, on a Tascam four-track.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic" title='"Weird Al" Yankovic'>"Weird Al" Yankovic</a> recorded half of his songs on <a href="/wiki/%22Weird_Al%22_Yankovic_(album)" title='"Weird Al" Yankovic (album)'>his debut album</a> with a Portastudio in his drummer's garage before signing up for a proper studio.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>'s debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Enter_the_Wu-Tang_(36_Chambers)" title="Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)">Enter the Wu-Tang (36 Chambers)</a></i>, was mixed down to a Portastudio 244.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_and_Wine" title="Iron and Wine">Iron and Wine</a>'s debut 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Creek_Drank_the_Cradle" title="The Creek Drank the Cradle">The Creek Drank the Cradle</a></i> was recorded on a four track cassette recorder.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Madlib" title="Madlib">Madlib</a> recorded his first album as <a href="/wiki/Quasimoto" title="Quasimoto">Quasimoto</a>, "<a href="/wiki/The_Unseen_(album)" title="The Unseen (album)">The Unseen</a>", on a TASCAM Portastudio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-redbullmusicacademy.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-redbullmusicacademy.com-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Portastatic" title="Portastatic">Portastatic</a> was named after the Portastudio <a href="/wiki/Mac_McCaughan" title="Mac McCaughan">Mac McCaughan</a> used to record the songs that became its first album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-indyweek.com_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-indyweek.com-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Dixie_Bee-Liners" title="The Dixie Bee-Liners">The Dixie Bee-Liners</a> recorded their Christmas single, <a href="/wiki/The_Dixie_Bee-Liners#Discography" title="The Dixie Bee-Liners">"Santa Wants A Whiskey"</a>, on a TASCAM 788.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nik_Kershaw" title="Nik Kershaw">Nik Kershaw</a> recorded the demos to his album, "<a href="/wiki/Human_Racing" title="Human Racing">Human Racing</a>", on a Portastudio.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clive_Gregson" title="Clive Gregson">Clive Gregson</a> and Christine Collister recorded their 1987 album <i>Home And Away</i> on a TASCAM (referred to as 'TEAC' in the album sleeve notes) 244 Portastudio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mac_Demarco" title="Mac Demarco">Mac Demarco</a> recorded his 2012 debut <a href="/wiki/Mini-LP" title="Mini-LP">mini-LP</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Night_Club" title="Rock and Roll Night Club">Rock and Roll Night Club</a> with a TASCAM 244 Portastudio. It made extensive use of the 244's pitch control and used the method of bouncing 3 separate tracks down to a single track. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li></ul>
