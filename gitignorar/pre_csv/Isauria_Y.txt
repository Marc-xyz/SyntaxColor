<p><b>Isauria</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'z' in 'zoom'">z</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span> or <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">aɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'s' in 'sigh'">s</span><span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">ɔːr</span><span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">i</span><span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>: <span lang="grc">Ἰσαυρία</span>), in ancient geography, is a rugged isolated district in the interior of South <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>, of very different extent at different periods,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup> but generally covering what is now the district of <a href="/wiki/Bozk%C4%B1r" title="Bozkır">Bozkır</a> and its surroundings in the <a href="/wiki/Konya_Province" title="Konya Province">Konya Province</a> of Turkey, or the core of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mount_Taurus" title="Mount Taurus">Taurus Mountains</a>. In its coastal extension it bordered on <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map,_Classical_Atlas,_1886,_Keith_Johnston.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="340" data-file-width="637" decoding="async" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map%2C_Classical_Atlas%2C_1886%2C_Keith_Johnston.jpg/350px-Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map%2C_Classical_Atlas%2C_1886%2C_Keith_Johnston.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map%2C_Classical_Atlas%2C_1886%2C_Keith_Johnston.jpg/525px-Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map%2C_Classical_Atlas%2C_1886%2C_Keith_Johnston.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map%2C_Classical_Atlas%2C_1886%2C_Keith_Johnston.jpg 2x" width="350"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Location_of_Isauria-Asia_Minor_Map,_Classical_Atlas,_1886,_Keith_Johnston.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Location of Isauria in Asia Minor</div></div></div>
<p>It derives its name from the contentious Isaurian tribe and twin settlements <i><a href="/wiki/Isaura_Palaea" title="Isaura Palaea">Isaura Palaea</a></i> (Ίσαυρα Παλαιά, Latin: <i>Isaura Vetus</i>, "Old Isaura") and <i><a href="/wiki/Isaura_Nea" title="Isaura Nea">Isaura Nea</a></i> (Ίσαυρα Νέα, Latin: <i>Isaura Nova</i>, "New Isaura").
</p><p>Isaurian marauders were fiercely independent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountain_people" title="Mountain people">mountain people</a> who created havoc in neighboring districts under Macedonian and Roman occupations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isauria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early">Early</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isauria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The permanent nucleus of Isauria was north of the Taurus range which lies directly to south of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconium" title="Iconium">Iconium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lystra" title="Lystra">Lystra</a>. <a href="/wiki/Lycaonia" title="Lycaonia">Lycaonia</a> had all the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iconium" title="Iconium">Iconian</a> plain; but Isauria began as soon as the foothills were reached. Its two original towns, Isaura Nea and Isaura Palaea, lay, one among these foothills (Doria) and the other on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_divide" title="Water divide">watershed</a> (<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zengibar_Kalesi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zengibar Kalesi (page does not exist)">Zengibar Kalesi</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup> Approx N37° 29′ E32° 12′ near Bozkir.
</p><p>In the 4th century BC, Isauria was the wild district about Isaura Palaea and the heads of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calycadnus" title="Calycadnus">Calycadnus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup> When the capital, Isaura (also known as Isaura Vetus or <a href="/wiki/Isaura_Palaea" title="Isaura Palaea">Isaura Palaea</a>), a strongly fortified city at the foot of Mt. Taurus, was besieged by <a href="/wiki/Perdiccas" title="Perdiccas">Perdiccas</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedon" title="Macedon">Macedonian</a> regent after <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander the Great</a>'s death, the Isaurians set the place alight and let it perish in flames rather than submit to capture.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_rule">Roman rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isauria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roman rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:DAVIS(1879)_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1358" data-file-width="2014" decoding="async" height="202" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/DAVIS%281879%29_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg/300px-DAVIS%281879%29_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/DAVIS%281879%29_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg/450px-DAVIS%281879%29_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/DAVIS%281879%29_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg/600px-DAVIS%281879%29_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:DAVIS(1879)_p469_PLAN_OF_THE_RUINES_OF_THE_CITY_OF_ISAURA.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Plan of the ruins of Isaura (Davis, 1879)</div></div></div>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Romans</a> first encountered the Isaurians (early in the 1st century BC), they regarded <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cilicia_Trachea" title="Cilicia Trachea">Cilicia Trachea</a> as part of Isauria, which thus extended to the Mediterranean Sea; and this extension of the name continued to be in common use for two centuries. The whole basin of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calycadnus" title="Calycadnus">Calycadnus</a> was reckoned Isaurian, and the cities in the valley of its southern branch formed what was known as the <a href="/wiki/Isaurian_Decapolis" title="Isaurian Decapolis">Isaurian Decapolis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Isaurians were brought partially under control (76–75 BC) by the Romans.  During the war of the Cilician and other pirates against Rome, the Isaurians took so active a part that the proconsul <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publius_Servilius_Isauricus" title="Publius Servilius Isauricus">P. Servilius</a> deemed it necessary to follow them into their rugged strongholds, and compel the whole people to submission, an exploit for which he received the title of Isauricus (75 BC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the year AD 6, Cassius Dio mentions that the Isaurians were marauding through the province of Asia until they were faced with open war and were defeated. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Isaurians were afterwards placed for a time under the rule of <a href="/wiki/Amyntas_of_Galatia" title="Amyntas of Galatia">Amyntas, king of Galatia</a>; but it is evident that they continued to retain their predatory habits and virtual independence. In the 3rd century they sheltered the rebel emperor <a href="/wiki/Trebellianus" title="Trebellianus">Trebellianus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 4th century, all Cilicia was detached by order of <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">Diocletian</a> for administrative purposes from the northern slope of Taurus, and we find a province called at first Isauria-Lycaonia, and later Isauria alone, extending up to the limits of Galatia, but not passing Taurus on the south. <a href="/wiki/Pisidia" title="Pisidia">Pisidia</a>, part of which had hitherto been included in one province with Isauria, was also detached, and made to include Iconium; Isauria received the eastern part of <a href="/wiki/Pamphylia" title="Pamphylia">Pamphylia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup> The coastal Metropolis of Seleucia was designated as Isauria's provincial capital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 4th century they were still described by <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">Ammianus Marcellinus</a> as the scourge of the neighbouring provinces of Asia Minor, with a major series of raids occurring from AD 404 to 409, including one campaign to eradicate them led by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Eastern Roman</a> general <a href="/wiki/Arbazacius" title="Arbazacius">Arbazacius</a>, but they were said to have been effectually subdued in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a>.
</p><p>Several Byzantine emperors were οf Isaurian descent: <a href="/wiki/Zeno_(emperor)" title="Zeno (emperor)">Zeno</a>, whose native name was Tarasicodissa Rousoumbladadiotes, <a href="/wiki/Leontios" title="Leontios">Leontios</a> who reigned from 695 to 698, <a href="/wiki/Leo_III_the_Isaurian" title="Leo III the Isaurian">Leo III</a>, who ascended the throne of <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">Constantinople</a> in 718, reigned until 741, and his son <a href="/wiki/Constantine_V" title="Constantine V">Constantine V</a>. The empire used Isaurians as soldiers, generals and at one point they even formed part of the emperor's personal guard, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Excubitores" title="Excubitores">Excubitores</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-4">[4]</a></sup>  However, the population of Constantinople considered the Isaurians as barbarians, and emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anastasius_I_(emperor)" title="Anastasius I (emperor)">Anastasius I</a> had to fight a long war against Isaurian rebels (<a href="/wiki/Isaurian_War" title="Isaurian War">Isaurian War</a>, 492-497).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later">Later</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isauria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The site contains ruins of the town and its fortifications. The ruins of Isaura Palaea are mainly remarkable for their fine situation, fortifications and tombs. Those of Isaura Nea have disappeared, but numerous inscriptions and many sculpture stelae, built into the houses of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dorla" title="Dorla">Dorla</a>, prove the site. It was the latter, and not the former town, that Servilius reduced by cutting off the water supply. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._R._S._Sterrett" title="J. R. S. Sterrett">J. R. S. Sterrett</a> explored in the highland of Isauria in 1885 but it was not exhaustive. The site was identified by <a href="/wiki/William_Mitchell_Ramsay" title="William Mitchell Ramsay">W. M. Ramsay</a> in 1901.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-EB1911-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Ramsay discovered there more than fifty Greek inscriptions, the greater number Christian, as well as magnificent tombs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> These monuments date from the third, fourth, and fifth centuries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_history">Ecclesiastical history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isauria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is argued that Paul evangelised in <a href="/wiki/Claudiopolis_(Cilicia)" title="Claudiopolis (Cilicia)">Claudiopolis</a>, the only Roman colony in the region by that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pilhofer_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-pilhofer-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Isaurian church was originally under the authority of the <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_of_Antioch" title="Patriarch of Antioch">Patriarch of Antioch</a>, but was attached to the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarch_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople">Patriarch of Constantinople</a> in the late 7th or early 8th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edwards_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-edwards-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Because Aetius, <a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">fl</a> 451.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> is called in inscriptions bishop of Isauropolis and Isaura Palaea and as no <i><a href="/wiki/Notitiae_Episcopatuum" title="Notitiae Episcopatuum">Notitia episcopatuum</a></i> makes mention of Isaura, or Isauropolis, Ramsay supposes that the Diocese of Isaura Nova was early joined with that of Leontopolis, the more recent name of Isaura Palaea which is mentioned in all the "Notitiae".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-CE-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
