<p><b>Bontoc</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ilocano_language" title="Ilocano language">Ilocano</a>: <i lang="ilo">Ili ti Bontoc</i>), officially the <b>Municipality of Bontoc</b>,  is a 2nd class <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Municipality_of_the_Philippines" title="Municipality of the Philippines">municipality</a> and capital of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_Province" title="Philippine Province">province</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mountain_Province" title="Mountain Province">Mountain Province</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>. According to the 2015 census, it has a population of 24,643 people.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSA15–14_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-PSA15–14-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bontoc is the historical capital of the entire Cordillera region since the inception of governance in the <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Administrative_Region" title="Cordillera Administrative Region">Cordillera</a>. The municipality celebrates the annual <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lang-ay_Festival&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lang-ay Festival (page does not exist)">Lang-ay Festival</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIAGovPH-Lang-ay_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-PIAGovPH-Lang-ay-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Bontoc is home to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bontoc_people" title="Bontoc people">Bontoc tribe</a>, a feared war-like group of indigenous people who actively indulged in tribal wars with its neighbours until the 1930s. Every Bontoc male had to undergo a rites of passage into manhood, which may include head hunting, where the male has to journey (sometimes with companions) and hunt for a human head. The Bontocs also used the jaw of the hunted head as a handle for gongs, and as late as the early 1990s, evidence of this practice can be seen from one of the gongs in Pukisan, Bontoc. The town also hosts the UNESCO tentatively-listed Alab petroglyphs.
</p>

<p>The local economy depends largely on small trades and agriculture. This capital town's biggest economic potential is tourism with its smaller rice terraces in Barangay Bay-yo, Maligcong and other areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AsiaTravel_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-AsiaTravel-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bontoc,_Mountain_Province&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The highland town of Bontoc is home to two <a href="/wiki/List_of_National_Cultural_Treasures_in_the_Philippines" title="List of National Cultural Treasures in the Philippines">National Cultural Treasures of the Philippines</a>. These are the Stone Agricultural Calendar of Bontoc and <i>Petroglyphs of Alab</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GovPH-PD260_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-GovPH-PD260-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p><p>The Alab <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petroglyphs" title="Petroglyphs">petroglyphs</a> are ancient figures carved on mountain walls by the prehistoric people of Bontoc.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalMuseumGovPH-2010_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-NationalMuseumGovPH-2010-14">[14]</a></sup> The petroglyphs are the most important ancient rock art carvings in the Cordilleras and the second most oldest in the entire country, second only to the Angono petroglyphs of Rizal. Due to its high significance, it was submitted by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Commission_for_Culture_and_the_Arts_of_the_Philippines" title="National Commission for Culture and the Arts of the Philippines">National Commission for Culture and the Arts of the Philippines</a> to the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> Tentative List of Heritage Sites in 2006, pending its inclusion in the World Heritage List along with the Singanapan charcoal-drawn petrographs of southern Palawan, Angono petroglyphs of Rizal province, charcoal-drawn Penablanca petrographs of Cagayan, and the Anda red hermatite print petrographs of Bohol.
</p><p>The Bontoc people have their own indigenous religion that has been preserved for centuries. They believe in a unique pantheon of deities, of which the supreme god is the cultural hero, Lumawig, son of Kabunian. There are many sacred sites associated with Lumawig and a variety of Bontoc deities. Oral tradition tells that Lumawig instilled five great lessons to the Bontoc people, namely: (1) a man must not steal; (2) one should not gossip; (3) men and women must not commit adultery; (4) one must be temperate in eating and in drinking alcoholic drinks; and (5) all people must live simple and industrious lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>
</p>
