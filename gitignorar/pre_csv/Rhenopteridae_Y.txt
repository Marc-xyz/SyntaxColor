<p>The <b>Rhenopteridae</b> are a family of <a href="/wiki/Eurypterid" title="Eurypterid">eurypterids</a>, an extinct group of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chelicerate" title="Chelicerate">chelicerate</a> <a href="/wiki/Arthropod" title="Arthropod">arthropods</a> commonly known as "sea scorpions". The family is the only family currently contained in the superfamily <b>Rhenopteroidea</b>, one of four superfamilies classified as part of the suborder <a href="/wiki/Stylonurina" title="Stylonurina">Stylonurina</a>.
</p><p>The family contains one of the earliest known eurypterids, <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterus" title="Brachyopterus">Brachyopterus</a></i>, known from the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Ordovician" title="Middle Ordovician">Middle Ordovician</a> (also the sister taxon to all other rhenopterids), and is the most primitive clade of stylonurine eurypterids. The last known members of the family went extinct during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Devonian" title="Early Devonian">Early Devonian</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-JSP-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenopteridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhenopteridae.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="193" data-file-width="491" decoding="async" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Rhenopteridae.png/220px-Rhenopteridae.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Rhenopteridae.png/330px-Rhenopteridae.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Rhenopteridae.png/440px-Rhenopteridae.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhenopteridae.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of the carapaces of three Rhenopterid genera; <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterus" title="Brachyopterus">Brachyopterus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterella" title="Brachyopterella">Brachyopterella</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhenopterus" title="Rhenopterus">Rhenopterus</a></i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Rhenopterids were small, characterized by scattered tubercules and knobs on the outer surface of the exoskeleton. Their first two (or possibly three) pairs of walking legs had spines; the last two pairs were long and powerful, without spines. The prosoma (head) was subtrapezoidal, with arcuate compound eyes on parallel axes.  The male genital appendages were short with two distal spines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>The rhenopterids were the most primitive <a href="/wiki/Stylonurina" title="Stylonurina">stylonurines</a> and the family encompasses many previously enigmatic eurypterids, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterus" title="Brachyopterus">Brachyopterus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kiaeropterus" title="Kiaeropterus">Kiareopterus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhenopterus" title="Rhenopterus">Rhenopterus</a></i> itself, all united by a rounded posterior margin to the metastoma and prosomal appendage III bearing single fixed spines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-JSP-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the derived stylonurines of the family <a href="/wiki/Stylonuridae" title="Stylonuridae">Stylonuridae</a> and superfamily <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hibbertopteroidea" title="Hibbertopteroidea">Hibbertopteroidea</a>, rhenopterids retained primitive <i><a href="/wiki/Hughmilleria" title="Hughmilleria">Hughmilleria</a></i>-type prosomal appendages II-IV, which are unsuited to sweep-feeding. The family is thus more likely to have adopted a <a href="/wiki/Scavenger" title="Scavenger">scavenging</a> lifestyle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSP_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-JSP-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolutionary_history">Evolutionary history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenopteridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolutionary history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Brachyopterus.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="836" data-file-width="792" decoding="async" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Brachyopterus.png/220px-Brachyopterus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Brachyopterus.png/330px-Brachyopterus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Brachyopterus.png/440px-Brachyopterus.png 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Brachyopterus.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterus" title="Brachyopterus">Brachyopterus</a></i>, the earliest known rhenopterid.</div></div></div>
<p>The Rhenopterids first appeared during the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Ordovician" title="Middle Ordovician">Middle Ordovician</a> as one of the earliest and most basal groups of <a href="/wiki/Stylonurina" title="Stylonurina">Stylonurine</a> eurypterids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> Clear evolutionary trends can be observed in the carapaces of the rhenopterids. Basal rhenopterids possess broad carapaces narrowest at their base (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterus" title="Brachyopterus">Brachyopterus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Brachyopterella" title="Brachyopterella">Brachyopterella</a></i>) whilst more derived rhenopterids have broad carapaces narrowest at the front and the most derived members possess narrower carapaces narrowest at the front (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Rhenopterus" title="Rhenopterus">Rhenopterus</a></i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Kiaeropterus" title="Kiaeropterus">Kiaeropterus</a></i> is unusual in its morphology and might represent a <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">Silurian</a> offshoot.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Systematics_and_genera">Systematics and genera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhenopteridae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Systematics and genera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Rhenopteroids are diagnosed as being <a href="/wiki/Stylonurina" title="Stylonurina">stylonurines</a> with the posterior margin of the metastomate being round. Rhenopterids are in turn rhenopteroids with single fixed spines on the prosomal appendage III and possess a short telson. The Appendages II-IV have short and fixed spines and V-VI are nonspiniferous. Since no currently known rhenopteroid challenge the diagnosis applied to the Rhenopteridae, all rhenopteroids are rhenopterids.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSP_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-JSP-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Lamsdell, Braddy and Tetlie (2010) assigned one subfamily to the family, the Rhenopterinae, including the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Alkenopterus" title="Alkenopterus">Alkenopterus</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rhenopterus" title="Rhenopterus">Rhenopterus</a></i>. The Rhenopterinae was restricted to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Devonian" title="Early Devonian">Early</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Devonian" title="Middle Devonian">Middle Devonian</a> and were diagnosed as being rhenopterids with a non-spiniferous appendage IV and a caudal postabdomen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSP_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-JSP-1">[1]</a></sup> More recently, <i>Alkenopterus</i> has been reclassified as a member of the <a href="/wiki/Onychopterellidae" title="Onychopterellidae">Onychopterellidae</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Eurypterina" title="Eurypterina">Eurypterina</a>, making the subfamily defunct.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Superfamily Rhenopteroidea</b> <small>Størmer, 1951</small>
