	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Timothy P. Redfield</h1>

<p><b>Timothy Parker Redfield</b> (born November 3, 1812, <a href="/wiki/Coventry,_Vermont" title="Coventry, Vermont">Coventry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">Vermont</a>; died March 27, 1888, <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illinois" title="Illinois">Illinois</a>) was an American lawyer, politician, and judge. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Vermont_Supreme_Court" title="Vermont Supreme Court">Vermont Supreme Court</a> from 1870 to 1884.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Timothy_P._Redfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timothy Redfield was the fifth child of Dr. Peleg Redfield and Hannah Parker, a merchant's daughter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 1806 the family had moved to <a href="/wiki/Coventry,_Vermont" title="Coventry, Vermont">Coventry</a> in the frontier region of northern Vermont. His father served as town clerk for many years and also represented Coventry in the state legislature from 1812 to 1820. Timothy graduated from <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a> in 1836 with honors, being elected a member of <a href="/wiki/Phi_Beta_Kappa" title="Phi Beta Kappa">Phi Beta Kappa</a>. He immediately started to study law in the office of his brother <a href="/wiki/Isaac_F._Redfield" title="Isaac F. Redfield">Isaac Fletcher Redfield</a>, who was elected that year to the Vermont Supreme Court.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Timothy_P._Redfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1838 he was admitted to the bar, and from 1838 to 1848 practiced law in <a href="/wiki/Irasburg,_Vermont" title="Irasburg, Vermont">Irasburg</a>, the county seat of Orleans County, just to the south of Coventry. He represented Irasburg in the state legislature in 1839 and <a href="/wiki/Orleans_County,_Vermont" title="Orleans County, Vermont">Orleans County</a> in the state senate in 1848. Redfield was the unsuccessful Democratic candidate for governor in 1851, 1863, and 1864.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He served on the <a href="/wiki/Vermont_State_Board_of_Education" title="Vermont State Board of Education">Vermont State Board of Education</a> from 1860 to 1862. From 1870 to 1884 he served on the Vermont Supreme Court. <a href="/wiki/William_H._Walker_(Vermont)" title="William H. Walker (Vermont)">William H. Walker</a> was named to succeed him, and in 1884 he was the Democratic candidate for U.S. Senate, losing to <a href="/wiki/Justin_Smith_Morrill" title="Justin Smith Morrill">Justin Smith Morrill</a>.
</p><p>A portrait of Judge Redfield hangs in the Washington County Courthouse in Montpelier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Redfield also served on the boards of directors or as an officer of some Vermont organizations. He was one of the founding directors of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Life_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Life Group (page does not exist)">National Life Insurance Company</a> in 1848 and served on its board for forty years. He served the Vermont Mutual Fire Insurance Company (now the Vermont Mutual Insurance Group) as director and as vice-president.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He was a trustee of <a href="/wiki/Norwich_University" title="Norwich University">Norwich University</a> from 1853 to 1873.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Timothy_P._Redfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Timothy Redfield married Helen W. Grannis in 1840; they had four children; twin boys who died in infancy in 1853, Frederick (1842-1865), who only lived to be 22, and daughter Alice Melinda (1848-1935), who married a musician named Anthony Phillips. Timothy Redfield died in Chicago where Phillips was employed.
</p><p>Timothy Redfield's older brother Isaac (1804-1876) served on the Vermont Supreme Court from 1836 to 1859 and as Chief Judge from 1852 to 1859; he was also a respected legal scholar, writing works on railroad law and other subjects. The governor, senator, and Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/Redfield_Proctor" title="Redfield Proctor">Redfield Proctor</a>  (1831-1908) was a much younger first cousin of Timothy Redfield, the youngest son of his mother's sister.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
