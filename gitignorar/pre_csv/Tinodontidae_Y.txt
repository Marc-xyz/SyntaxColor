<p><b>Tinodontidae</b> is an extinct <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> of actively mobile <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammal</a>, endemic to what would now be <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic" title="Jurassic">Jurassic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">Cretaceous</a> periods.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tinodontidae&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Tinodontidae</i> 
was named by Marsh (1887). It was assigned to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammalia" title="Mammalia">Mammalia</a> by Marsh (1887); and to <a href="/wiki/Symmetrodonta" title="Symmetrodonta">Symmetrodonta</a> by McKenna and Bell (1997).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> More recently, they have been recovered as more basal to symmetrodonts, though still within the mammalian crown-group.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p>
