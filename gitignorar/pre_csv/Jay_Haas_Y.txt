<p><b>Jay Dean Haas</b> (born December 2, 1953) is an American <a href="/wiki/Professional_golfer" title="Professional golfer">professional golfer</a> formerly of the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA Tour</a> who now plays on the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour_Champions" title="PGA Tour Champions">PGA Tour Champions</a>.
</p><p>Haas was born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis, Missouri</a>, and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Belleville,_Illinois" title="Belleville, Illinois">Belleville, Illinois</a>. He attended <a href="/wiki/Wake_Forest_University" title="Wake Forest University">Wake Forest University</a> and was a member of the <a href="/wiki/NCAA_Division_I_Men%27s_Golf_Championships" title="NCAA Division I Men's Golf Championships">NCAA Championship</a> team of the middle 1970s with <a href="/wiki/Curtis_Strange" title="Curtis Strange">Curtis Strange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bob_Byman" title="Bob Byman">Bob Byman</a> that <i><a href="/wiki/Golf_World" title="Golf World">Golf World</a></i> has called "the greatest college team of all time".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> He won the individual championship in 1975. He turned professional in 1976.
</p><p>Haas has had a solid career on the <a href="/wiki/PGA_Tour" title="PGA Tour">PGA Tour</a>, winning nine times between 1978 and 1993. He had a resurgence in 2003, when he finished in the top 30 on the money list for the first time since 1995 and made the United States <a href="/wiki/Presidents_Cup" title="Presidents Cup">Presidents Cup</a> team. The following year he was one of <a href="/wiki/Hal_Sutton" title="Hal Sutton">Hal Sutton</a>'s two captain's picks for the <a href="/wiki/Ryder_Cup" title="Ryder Cup">Ryder Cup</a>, and made his third appearance in that event.
</p><p>Haas was known for being one of the most consistent players on the PGA Tour over the course of his career and ended up playing 798 events.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He is only five starts off <a href="/wiki/Mark_Brooks_(golfer)" title="Mark Brooks (golfer)">Mark Brooks</a>' record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He has made the cut 592 times on the PGA Tour, more than any other player.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Haas also has the distinction of playing in the most major tournaments without a win, with 87 during his PGA Tour career.
</p><p>Haas was eligible to play in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champions_Tour" title="Champions Tour">Champions Tour</a> (now PGA Tour Champions) events from the start of the 2004 season and he lost to Hale Irwin by one stroke at the <a href="/wiki/Senior_PGA_Championship" title="Senior PGA Championship">Senior PGA Championship</a> in his first appearance at that level. He was still featured in the top 20 of the <a href="/wiki/Official_World_Golf_Ranking" title="Official World Golf Ranking">Official World Golf Ranking</a> after his 50th birthday. In 2005, he won twice on the Champions Tour, while also continuing to play regularly on the PGA Tour. In April 2006, he won back to back events on the Champions Tour and the following month he won a playoff at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oak_Tree_Golf_Club" title="Oak Tree Golf Club">Oak Tree Golf Club</a> with <a href="/wiki/Brad_Bryant" title="Brad Bryant">Brad Bryant</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Senior_PGA_Championship" title="Senior PGA Championship">Senior PGA Championship</a> to claim his first <a href="/wiki/Senior_major_golf_championships" title="Senior major golf championships">senior major</a> and he went on to top the 2006 Champions Tour <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champions_Tour#Leading_money_winners_by_year" title="Champions Tour">money list</a>. He was named the Champions Tour Player of the Year in 2006 as well. Haas won the 2008 Charles Schwab Cup to win two out of the last three cups.
</p><p>After winning the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greater_Hickory_Classic_at_Rock_Barn" title="Greater Hickory Classic at Rock Barn">Greater Hickory Classic at Rock Barn</a> in September 2009, Haas won his third senior major and 14th Champions Tour event in October at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constellation_Energy_Senior_Players_Championship" title="Constellation Energy Senior Players Championship">Constellation Energy Senior Players Championship</a>. He came from 5 strokes behind with a final round 6-under-par 64 to win by 1 over 54-hole leader <a href="/wiki/Tom_Watson_(golfer)" title="Tom Watson (golfer)">Tom Watson</a>. In June 2012, Haas won his 16th title on the Champions Tour, cruising to a five stroke victory over <a href="/wiki/Larry_Mize" title="Larry Mize">Larry Mize</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Triplett" title="Kirk Triplett">Kirk Triplett</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Principal_Charity_Classic" title="Principal Charity Classic">Principal Charity Classic</a>. In October 2016, Haas won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toshiba_Classic_(golf)" title="Toshiba Classic (golf)">Toshiba Classic</a> in a playoff with <a href="/wiki/Bart_Bryant" title="Bart Bryant">Bart Bryant</a>. He became the second oldest player to win a PGA Tour Champions event at age 62 years, 312 days; the oldest is <a href="/wiki/Mike_Fetchick" title="Mike Fetchick">Mike Fetchick</a> at 63 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p><p>Haas comes from a distinguished family of golfers. He is a nephew of 1968 <a href="/wiki/Masters_Tournament" title="Masters Tournament">Masters</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Bob_Goalby" title="Bob Goalby">Bob Goalby</a>, and has several other relations in golf including his second son <a href="/wiki/Bill_Haas" title="Bill Haas">Bill</a> who has played on the PGA Tour since 2006. His oldest son <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jay_Haas,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jay Haas, Jr. (page does not exist)">Jay Jr.</a>, brother <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Haas" title="Jerry Haas">Jerry Haas</a>, and brother-in-law <a href="/wiki/Dillard_Pruitt" title="Dillard Pruitt">Dillard Pruitt</a> also played on the PGA Tour.
</p><p>He was voted the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Bob_Jones_Award" title="Bob Jones Award">Bob Jones Award</a>, the highest honor given by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Golf_Association" title="United States Golf Association">United States Golf Association</a> in recognition of distinguished sportsmanship in golf. In February 2005, he received the Payne Stewart Award, and in April 2005, he received the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Murray_Award&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Murray Award (page does not exist)">Murray Award</a> for his cooperation with the media.
</p><p>Haas currently resides in <a href="/wiki/Greenville,_South_Carolina" title="Greenville, South Carolina">Greenville, South Carolina</a>.
</p>
