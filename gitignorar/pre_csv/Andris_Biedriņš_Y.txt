<p><b>Andris Biedriņš</b> (<small>Latvian pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Latvian" title="Help:IPA/Latvian">[ˈɐndris ˈbiædriɲʃ]</a></span>; born April 2, 1986) is a Latvian former professional <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> player. He was drafted by the <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Warriors" title="Golden State Warriors">Golden State Warriors</a> with the 11th overall pick in the <a href="/wiki/2004_NBA_draft" title="2004 NBA draft">2004 NBA draft</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="European_career">European career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: European career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biedriņš's professional career started at the Latvian team <a href="/wiki/BK_Skonto" title="BK Skonto">BK Skonto</a> in the 2002–03 season, when he joined at age 16. He soon established himself as an impact player, appearing in 41 LBL games, averaging 2.9 points, 4.6 rebounds and 1.32 blocks, while shooting 59.8% from the field. For his feats, he was named Latvian League Newcomer of the Year. In eleven games for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Basketball_Federation" title="International Basketball Federation">FIBA</a> <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> league (2003–2004), he averaged 18.6 points, 8.2 rebounds and 1.82 blocks. In his second year, Biedriņš appeared in 28 LBL games, averaging 18.0 points, 8.9 rebounds, 1.3 assists and 1.86 blocks, while shooting 61.5% from the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbabio_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbabio-1">[1]</a></sup> After that season, Biedriņš made himself eligible for the 2004 NBA draft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NBA_career">NBA career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: NBA career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Golden_State_Warriors">Golden State Warriors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Golden State Warriors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Biedriņš was drafted as the 11th pick in the first round by the Golden State Warriors. In his <a href="/wiki/Rookie" title="Rookie">rookie</a> season (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004-05_NBA_season" title="2004-05 NBA season">2004–05</a>), Biedriņš got little playing time, and often quickly got into foul trouble (3.6 points, 3.9 rebounds, 0.8 blocks and 2.9 fouls in 12.8 minutes, total 30 games).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He also was the youngest player in the league during his rookie year as he was 18 years old at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In his second year, the 2005–06 season, Biedriņš played in 68 games, posting 3.8 points and 4.2 rebounds, with 14.1 minutes per game. However, he failed to improve much, and was ridiculed for his poor free throw percentage (.306)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbastats_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nbastats-5">[5]</a></sup> and his inability to stay out of foul trouble, committing 190 fouls in exactly 1,000 minutes of playing time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbastats_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbastats-5">[5]</a></sup> The Golden State fan base was not kind to the sophomore; he was called "The One Minute Man" by the Golden State Warriors blog for his alleged inability to play without a foul for longer than these 60 seconds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Biedriņš made some news when he was involved in a car accident on his way to a home game on the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_880_(California)" title="Interstate 880 (California)">880 freeway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>. His <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a> <a href="/wiki/Porsche_Cayenne" title="Porsche Cayenne">Cayenne Turbo</a> was totalled in the accident, and he missed that night's game and the next game due to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back_spasm" title="Back spasm">back spasms</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="2006.E2.80.932007"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2006–2007">2006–2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2006–2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In his third year, Biedriņš got a lucky break when the Warriors decided to replace coach <a href="/wiki/Mike_Montgomery" title="Mike Montgomery">Mike Montgomery</a> with ex-<a href="/wiki/Dallas_Mavericks" title="Dallas Mavericks">Dallas Mavericks</a> coach <a href="/wiki/Don_Nelson" title="Don Nelson">Don Nelson</a>, elected one of the <a href="/wiki/50_Greatest_Players_in_NBA_History#Coaches" title="50 Greatest Players in NBA History">NBA's "Greatest Coaches of All Time."</a> Nelson needed just five games to bench the Warriors' center <a href="/wiki/Adonal_Foyle" title="Adonal Foyle">Adonal Foyle</a> and give Biedriņš the starting spot at center, calling him "the best big man I've got." Biedriņš thrived in his new role, posting impressive averages of 10.3 points on an incredible .621 field goal percentage (1st in the NBA), 9.5 rebounds and 1.9 blocks per game and earning himself 28.9 minutes of playing time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nba.com_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nba.com-8">[8]</a></sup> His breakthrough game came in November 2006, when the Warriors beat the title favorites <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Spurs" title="San Antonio Spurs">San Antonio Spurs</a> 119–111. In this game, Biedriņš asserted himself against Spurs superstar <a href="/wiki/Tim_Duncan" title="Tim Duncan">Tim Duncan</a>, only being narrowly outscored 18–22 and outrebounded 15–16,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> but did block six shots, including three straight shot attempts by Duncan in the third quarter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> During this season he set career highs in points (31 vs Denver 24 November 2006), rebounds (18 vs Charlotte 2 March 2007), blocks (7 vs Denver 22 November 2006), assists (5 vs Washington 23 March 2007) and steals (5 vs Lakers 22 January 2007). He also finished fifth in <a href="/wiki/NBA_Most_Improved_Player_Award" title="NBA Most Improved Player Award">Most Improved Player</a> voting, losing to teammate <a href="/wiki/Monta_Ellis" title="Monta Ellis">Monta Ellis</a>.
</p>
<h4><span id="2008.E2.80.932009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2008–2009">2008–2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2008–2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In July 2008, Biedriņš agreed to a new six-year contract for $54 million ($62 million including incentives) with the Golden State Warriors. The final year was a player option, which if exercised would keep him a Warrior until 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Biedriņš was named co-captain alongside Stephen Jackson. On January 27, Biedriņš pulled down a career high 26 rebounds against <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">New York</a>. He had set a career high in assists on December 19 against <a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Hawks" title="Atlanta Hawks">Atlanta</a> with six. For the season, Biedriņš set career highs in points (11.9), rebounds (11.2), assists (two) and blocked shots (1.6) per game, starting in 58 of the 62 games in which he appeared.
</p>
<h4><span id="2009.E2.80.932010"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2009–2010">2009–2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2009–2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 2009–10 season, Biedriņš appeared in only 33 games, starting 29, and was limited to 23.1 minutes per game, his lowest since his second season in the league (2005–2006). Injuries played a big role, as he was derailed with back and groin ailments that made him miss weeks at a time before he finally had groin surgery on March 10, ending his season. Biedriņš' free-throw percentage had dropped from 62% in 2007–08 to 55.1% in 2008–09; in 2009–10, he shot an appalling four-for-25 (16%). His other numbers dropped precipitously as well—five points, 7.8 rebounds and 1.3 blocks per game after career highs in all these stats in 2008–09. Additionally, Warriors coach Don Nelson repeatedly criticized Biedriņš for his lack of intensity and aggressiveness in his time on the floor. Biedriņš capped of the off-season by criticizing the team during an interview in his native Latvia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="2010.E2.80.932011"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2010–2011">2010–2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2010–2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The 2010–2011 season may have begun with some renewed hope for a resurgence of Biedriņš, as 2009–10 coach Don Nelson was fired and the potential existed for new head coach <a href="/wiki/Keith_Smart" title="Keith Smart">Keith Smart</a> to unleash and reinvigorate the center's fragile confidence. Early returns were promising, as Biedriņš had several games in the early season with rebounding numbers at or around double-digits while averaging around thirty minutes per game, including 20 rebounds in a November 5 victory over the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Utah Jazz</a>, 28 points and 20 rebounds in a November 26 loss to the <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Grizzlies" title="Memphis Grizzlies">Memphis Grizzlies</a>, and 18 rebounds in a November 30 loss to the <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Spurs" title="San Antonio Spurs">San Antonio Spurs</a>. However, the injury bug bit again, derailing Biedriņš from mid-December 2010 to early January 2011, and once again he found it difficult to regain his previous form. Biedriņš's slumping play and persistent foul issues convinced Smart to bench him in favor of rookie <a href="/wiki/Ekpe_Udoh" title="Ekpe Udoh">Ekpe Udoh</a> for four games in March. He was reinstated into the starting lineup for five games until spraining his ankle on March 16, resulting in his being sidelined for the remainder of the season. Biedriņš's averages for the 2010–11 season amounted to five points, 7.2 rebounds and 0.9 blocks (both a drop from 2009–10) on 53.4% field goal shooting and a modestly improved 32.3% free throw accuracy.
</p>
<h4><span id="2011.E2.80.932012"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2011–2012">2011–2012</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2011–2012">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the wake of the lockout which took away much of the 2011 NBA offseason and shortened the league's schedule considerably, the Warriors made a push to acquire restricted free agent <a href="/wiki/DeAndre_Jordan" title="DeAndre Jordan">DeAndre Jordan</a>, who would have automatically taken over as the starting center for Golden State. Biedriņš might have been a logical amnesty candidate under the provisions of the NBA's new collective bargaining agreement, but the Warriors instead chose to amnesty <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Bell_(basketball)" title="Charlie Bell (basketball)">Charlie Bell</a> while retaining Biedriņš. The Warriors' push to acquire Jordan fell through when the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Clippers" title="Los Angeles Clippers">Los Angeles Clippers</a> matched the Warriors' offer sheet to Jordan, so they instead signed free agent center <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Brown" title="Kwame Brown">Kwame Brown</a>. Biedriņš won the starting job to begin the year before being sidelined for three games with an ankle injury. When Brown was lost for the season with a torn <a href="/wiki/Pectoralis_major" title="Pectoralis major">pectoralis major</a> muscle in the third of those games, Biedriņš once again took over as starter, where he remained until his continued ineffectiveness prompted first-year coach <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mark_Jackson_(basketball)" title="Mark Jackson (basketball)">Mark Jackson</a> to insert <a href="/wiki/Ekpe_Udoh" title="Ekpe Udoh">Ekpe Udoh</a> in as starter. A March 13 trade which shipped Udoh, Brown and <a href="/wiki/Monta_Ellis" title="Monta Ellis">Monta Ellis</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Bucks" title="Milwaukee Bucks">Milwaukee Bucks</a> for <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jackson" title="Stephen Jackson">Stephen Jackson</a> and the out-for-the-season <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bogut" title="Andrew Bogut">Andrew Bogut</a> thrust Biedriņš back into the starting lineup, where he remained until Jackson decided to give rookie <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Tyler" title="Jeremy Tyler">Jeremy Tyler</a> a chance to prove himself at the beginning of April. For the season, Biedriņš averaged 15.7 minutes (the lowest since his second year in the league), 1.7 points, 3.7 rebounds, 0.3 assists (all career lows), 1.0 blocks and 0.5 steals while shooting 60.9% from the floor and 11.1% from the free throw line (admittedly with the small sample size of one make in nine free throw attempts).
</p>
<h4><span id="2012.E2.80.932013"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2012–2013">2012–2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2012–2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Biedriņš's struggles intensified during the 2012–13 season. Relatively healthy, he appeared in 53 games for 9.3 minutes per game, but attempted a grand total of only 21 shots (making 10, a 47.6% mark) and 13 free throws (making four, a 30.8% mark). For the season, Biedriņš averaged 0.5 points, 2.9 rebounds, 0.3 assists, 0.3 steals and 0.8 blocks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Utah_Jazz">Utah Jazz</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Utah Jazz">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On July 5, 2013, the Warriors reportedly agreed to trade Biedriņš to the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Utah Jazz</a> along with teammates <a href="/wiki/Brandon_Rush" title="Brandon Rush">Brandon Rush</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Jefferson" title="Richard Jefferson">Richard Jefferson</a> in an attempt to clear salary cap space to acquire coveted free agent <a href="/wiki/Andre_Iguodala" title="Andre Iguodala">Andre Iguodala</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The trade became official on July 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> On April 5, 2014, Biedriņš was waived by the Jazz.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_team_career">National team career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: National team career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biedriņš has been active for his native <a href="/wiki/Latvia_national_basketball_team" title="Latvia national basketball team">Latvia</a> since his teenage years. In 2001, the then-15-year-old Biedriņš was a member of the Latvian national team at the Euro Cadet Championship, where he finished 4th in scoring (16.3 ppg) and 3rd in rebounding (8.5 rpg). In 2002, Biedriņš participated in the Euro Junior Championship, where he averaged 6.6 points and 7.3 rebounds. He then competed in the 2004 Under-18 European Championship, averaging 21.8 points, 14.4 rebounds, 4.4 blocks and 3.8 steals. In a match against France, he played against fellow future NBA player <a href="/wiki/Johan_Petro" title="Johan Petro">Johan Petro</a>, and Biedriņš scored 21 points and 16 rebounds. Other notable games were against Georgia (28 points, 11 rebounds) and Italy (26 points, 20 rebounds).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbabio_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nbabio-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Andris_Biedri%C5%86%C5%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Biedriņš has one son, named Emīls.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup>
</p><p>Biedriņš is well known for his involvement in several charities. He was active in Warriors community programs during his rookie season, participated in the team's Thanksgiving Food Serving at Patrick David's Cafe in <a href="/wiki/Danville,_California" title="Danville, California">Danville, California</a>, hosted a half-time holiday party for underprivileged children at the Warriors game versus <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Grizzlies" title="Memphis Grizzlies">Memphis</a> on December 22, attended the Warriors Kids Day celebration, as well as the Run With TMC Fantasy Camp reception, and also visited injured American troops just back from Iraq at the <a href="/wiki/Walter_Reed_National_Military_Medical_Center" title="Walter Reed National Military Medical Center">National Naval Medical Center</a> outside of <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, when the team was in the area in early March.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbabio_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nbabio-1">[1]</a></sup> His English language skills were poor when he came to the U.S., but he taught himself to speak English in six months while training in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> prior to the 2004 NBA draft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nbabio_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nbabio-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
