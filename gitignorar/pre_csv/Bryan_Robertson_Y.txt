	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Bryan Robertson</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bryan-Robertson.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="263" data-file-width="232" decoding="async" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cb/Bryan-Robertson.jpg/170px-Bryan-Robertson.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/c/cb/Bryan-Robertson.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bryan-Robertson.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Robertson, by <a href="/wiki/Ida_Kar" title="Ida Kar">Ida Kar</a>, 1958</div></div></div>
<p><b>Bryan Robertson</b> <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">OBE</a> (1 April 1925 – 18 November 2002) was an English curator and arts manager described by <i>Studio International</i> as "the greatest Director the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a> never had".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bryan_Robertson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Robertson was born in London and educated at <a href="/wiki/Battersea_Grammar_School" title="Battersea Grammar School">Battersea Grammar School</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> Unfit for military service, he became a junior editor on <i>The Studio</i> magazine in 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> The art-historian and curator <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Clark" title="Kenneth Clark">Kenneth Clark</a> became a mentor, funding a year in Paris for study.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> In 1949 Robertson became curator at the Heffer Gallery in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and mounted a ground-breaking exhibition of contemporary French art at the <a href="/wiki/Fitzwilliam_Museum" title="Fitzwilliam Museum">Fitzwilliam Museum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> From 1952 to 1968, as curator of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whitechapel_Art_Gallery" title="Whitechapel Art Gallery">Whitechapel Art Gallery</a>, he created an influential programme that gave major presentations of works by <a href="/wiki/Jackson_Pollock" title="Jackson Pollock">Jackson Pollock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rauschenberg" title="Robert Rauschenberg">Robert Rauschenberg</a> and the 1956 exhibition <i><a href="/wiki/This_Is_Tomorrow" title="This Is Tomorrow">This Is Tomorrow</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-times-3">[3]</a></sup> The Pollock exhibition created 'an absolute furore' (Robertson's own words), and police were summoned to control the crowds queuing to get in. The same happened with the Rauschenberg exhibition in 1964.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He also revived interest in the work of <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a> and organised exhibitions of <a href="/wiki/J._M._W._Turner" title="J. M. W. Turner">Turner</a> (the first solo show of Turner since his death in 1851) and <a href="/wiki/George_Stubbs" title="George Stubbs">Stubbs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit-tel_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-tel-6">[6]</a></sup> Robertson was key in promoting the careers of many emerging British artists; <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Caro" title="Anthony Caro">Anthony Caro</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Hockney" title="David Hockney">David Hockney</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hoyland" title="John Hoyland">John Hoyland</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bridget_Riley" title="Bridget Riley">Bridget Riley</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_G._Tucker" title="William G. Tucker">William G. Tucker</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phillip_King_(artist)" title="Phillip King (artist)">Phillip King</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-times-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-5">[5]</a></sup> Robertson placed public education at the heart of the Whitechapel programme giving space to exhibitions of work from schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Robertson's period at the Whitechapel transformed the profile of the Gallery at a time when it did not have regular funding from the <a href="/wiki/Arts_Council_of_Great_Britain" title="Arts Council of Great Britain">Arts Council of Great Britain</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> and he was regarded as a frontrunner to take over at the Tate Gallery in 1964<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> following the retirement of <a href="/wiki/John_Rothenstein" title="John Rothenstein">John Rothenstein</a> but due to politics lost out to the Gallery's deputy director, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Reid_(museum_director)" title="Norman Reid (museum director)">Norman Reid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-5">[5]</a></sup> He became director of the museum of the <a href="/wiki/State_University_of_New_York" title="State University of New York">State University of New York</a> for five years and wrote articles and monographs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-times-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>Robertson sat on the Arts Council art committee between 1958 and 1961 and again from 1980 to 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-times-3">[3]</a></sup> During his second term he began working as a freelance curator and built an impressive roster of noteworthy exhibitions, including the magnificent <a href="/wiki/Raoul_Dufy" title="Raoul Dufy">Raoul Dufy</a> show at the <a href="/wiki/Hayward_Gallery" title="Hayward Gallery">Hayward Gallery</a> (1983), an important retrospective of <a href="/wiki/Ceri_Richards" title="Ceri Richards">Ceri Richards</a> at the Tate<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dnb_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-2">[2]</a></sup> as well as co-curating <a href="/wiki/Flowers_Gallery" title="Flowers Gallery">Flowers Gallery</a>'s 1994 exhibition British Abstract Art Part 1: Painting<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> at Flowers East and Flowers East at London Fields.
</p>
