<p><a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> has a strong and distinctive link with music. Singing is a significant part of Welsh national identity, and the country is traditionally referred to as "the land of song".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup> This is a modern stereotype based on 19th century conceptions of <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">Nonconformist</a> choral music and 20th century male voice choirs, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eisteddfodau" title="Eisteddfodau">Eisteddfodau</a> and arena singing, such as sporting events, but Wales has a history of music that has been used as a primary form of communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, Wales has been associated with folk music, choral performance, religious music and brass bands. However modern Welsh music is a thriving scene of rock, Welsh language lyricism, modern folk, jazz, pop, and electronic music. Particularly noted in the UK are the <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Newport" title="Music of Newport">Newport rock scene</a>, once labelled 'the new Seattle', and the <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Cardiff" title="Music of Cardiff">Cardiff music scene</a>, for which the city has recently been labelled 'Music City', for having the second highest number of independent music venues in the UK.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_song">Early song</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early song">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wales has a history of <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk music</a> related to the <a href="/wiki/Celtic_music" title="Celtic music">Celtic music</a> of countries such as Ireland and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. It has distinctive instrumentation and song types, and is often heard at a <i><a href="/wiki/Twmpath" title="Twmpath">twmpath</a></i> (folk dance session), <i>gŵyl werin</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_festival" title="Folk festival">folk festival</a>) or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noson_lawen" title="Noson lawen">noson lawen</a></i> (a traditional party similar to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaelic_languages" title="Gaelic languages">Gaelic</a> "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A9ilidh" title="Céilidh">Céilidh</a>"). Modern Welsh folk musicians have sometimes reconstructed traditions which had been suppressed or forgotten, and have competed with imported and indigenous rock and pop trends.
</p><p>Wales has a history of using music as a primary form of communication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup> Harmony and part singing is synonymous with Welsh music. Examples of well-developed, vertical harmony can be found in the <i>Robert ap Huw Manuscript</i> dating back to the 1600s. This text contains pieces of Welsh music from the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries that show amazing harmonic development. 
<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
The oldest known traditional songs from Wales are those connected to seasonal customs such as the <a href="/wiki/Mari_Lwyd" title="Mari Lwyd">Mari Lwyd</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cutty_Wren" title="Cutty Wren">Hunting the Wren</a>, in which both ceremonies contain processional songs where repetition is a musical feature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup> Other such ceremonial or feasting traditions connected with song are the New Year's Day <a href="/wiki/Calennig" title="Calennig">Calennig</a> and the welcoming of Spring <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_Jesus_at_the_Temple#Relation_to_Pagan_celebrations" title="Presentation of Jesus at the Temple">Candlemas</a> in which the traditional <a href="/wiki/Wassail" title="Wassail">wassail</a> was followed by dancing and feast songs. Children would sing 'pancake songs' on <a href="/wiki/Shrove_Tuesday" title="Shrove Tuesday">Shrove Tuesday</a> and summer carols were connected to the festival of <a href="/wiki/Calan_Mai" title="Calan Mai">Calan Mai</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>For many years, Welsh folk music had been suppressed, due to the effects of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1536-1543" title="Acts of Union 1536-1543">Act of Union</a>, which promoted the English language,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist</a> church in the 18th and 19th century. The church frowned on traditional music and dance, though folk tunes were sometimes used in <a href="/wiki/Hymn" title="Hymn">hymns</a>. Since at least the 12th century, Welsh <a href="/wiki/Bard" title="Bard">bards</a> and musicians have participated in musical and poetic contests called <a href="/wiki/Eisteddfod" title="Eisteddfod">eisteddfodau</a>; this is the equivalent of the Scottish <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mod_(Scotland)" title="Mod (Scotland)">Mod</a> and the Irish <a href="/wiki/Fleadh_Cheoil" title="Fleadh Cheoil">Fleadh Cheoil</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="18th_and_19th_century.2C_religious_music"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="18th_and_19th_century,_religious_music">18th and 19th century, religious music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 18th and 19th century, religious music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Music in Wales is often connected with male voice <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choirs</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Morriston_Orpheus_Choir" title="Morriston Orpheus Choir">Morriston Orpheus Choir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_Arms_Park_Male_Choir" title="Cardiff Arms Park Male Choir">Cardiff Arms Park Male Choir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Treorchy_Male_Choir" title="Treorchy Male Choir">Treorchy Male Voice Choir</a>, and enjoys a worldwide reputation in this field. This tradition of choral singing has been expressed through sporting events, especially in the country's national sport of <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a>, which in 1905 saw the first singing of a national anthem, Wales's <i><a href="/wiki/Hen_Wlad_Fy_Nhadau" title="Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau">Hen Wlad Fy Nhadau</a></i>, at the start of an international sporting encounter.
</p><p>Welsh traditional music declined with the rise of <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">Nonconformist</a> religion in the 18th century, which emphasized choral singing over instruments, and religious over secular uses of music; traditional musical styles became associated with drunkenness and immorality. The development of hymn singing in Wales is closely tied with the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Methodist_revival" title="Welsh Methodist revival">Welsh Methodist revival</a> of the late 18th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies580_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies580-7">[7]</a></sup> The hymns were popularised by writers such as <a href="/wiki/William_Williams_Pantycelyn" title="William Williams Pantycelyn">William Williams</a>, while others were set to popular secular tunes or adopted Welsh ballad tunes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies580_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davies580-7">[7]</a></sup> The appointment of Henry Mills as a musical overseer to the Welsh Methodist congregations in the 1780s saw a drive to improve singing throughout Wales. This saw the formation of local musical societies and in the first half of the 19th century Musical <a href="/wiki/Primer_(textbook)" title="Primer (textbook)">primers</a> and collections of tunes were printed and distributed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies580_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davies580-7">[7]</a></sup> Congregational singing was given further impetus with the arrival of the <a href="/wiki/Temperance_movement" title="Temperance movement">temperance movement</a>, which saw the Temperance Choral Union (formed in 1854) organising annual singing festivals, these included hymn singing by combined choirs. The publication of <i>Llyfr Tonau Cynulleidfaol</i> by John Roberts in 1859 provided congregations with a body of standard tunes that were less complex with unadorned harmonies. This collection began the practice of combining together to sing tunes from the book laid the foundation for the <a href="/wiki/Cymanfa_Ganu" title="Cymanfa Ganu">Cymanfa Ganu</a> (the hymn singing festival).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies768_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies768-8">[8]</a></sup> Around the same period, the growing availability of music in the <a href="/wiki/Tonic_sol-fa" title="Tonic sol-fa">tonic sol-fa</a> notation, promoted by the likes of <a href="/wiki/Eleazar_Roberts" title="Eleazar Roberts">Eleazar Roberts</a>, allowed congregations to read music more fluently.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies581_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies581-9">[9]</a></sup> One particularly popular hymn of this period was "<a href="/wiki/Llef" title="Llef">Llef</a>".
</p><p>In the 1860s, a revival of traditional Welsh music began, with the formation of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=National_Eisteddfod_Society&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="National Eisteddfod Society (page does not exist)">National Eisteddfod Society</a>, followed by the foundation of London-area <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Welsh_Societies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Welsh Societies (page does not exist)">Welsh Societies</a> and the publication of Nicholas Bennett's <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alawon_fy_Ngwlad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alawon fy Ngwlad (page does not exist)">Alawon fy Ngwlad</a></i> ("Tunes of my Land"), a compilation of traditional tunes, in the 1890s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="19th.E2.80.9321st_century.2C_secular_music"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="19th–21st_century,_secular_music">19th–21st century, secular music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th–21st century, secular music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A tradition of <a href="/wiki/Brass_band" title="Brass band">brass bands</a> dating from the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a> continues, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/South_Wales_Valleys" title="South Wales Valleys">South Wales Valleys</a>, with Welsh bands such as the <a href="/wiki/Cory_Band" title="Cory Band">Cory Band</a> being one of the most successful in the world.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Parry.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1129" data-file-width="881" decoding="async" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Joseph_Parry.jpg/220px-Joseph_Parry.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Joseph_Parry.jpg/330px-Joseph_Parry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/1d/Joseph_Parry.jpg/440px-Joseph_Parry.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Parry.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><center>Dr <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Parry" title="Joseph Parry">Joseph Parry</a>, composer of <a href="/wiki/Myfanwy" title="Myfanwy">Myfanwy</a></center></div></div></div>
<p>Although choral music in the 19th century by Welsh composers was mainly religious, there was a steady body of <a href="/wiki/Secularity" title="Secularity">secular</a> songs being produced. Composers such as <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Parry" title="Joseph Parry">Joseph Parry</a>, whose work <a href="/wiki/Myfanwy" title="Myfanwy">Myfanwy</a> is still a favourite Welsh song, were followed by <a href="/wiki/David_Jenkins_(composer)" title="David Jenkins (composer)">David Jenkins</a> and D. Emlyn Evans, who tailored songs specifically for the Victorian music market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies581_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davies581-9">[9]</a></sup> These secular hymns were embraced by the emerging male voice choirs, which formed originally as the tenor and bass sections of chapel choirs, but also sang outside the church in a form of recreation and fellowship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies532_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies532-11">[11]</a></sup> The industrial workforce attracted less of a jollity of English <a href="/wiki/Glee_club" title="Glee club">glee clubs</a> and also avoided the more robust militaristic style of music. Composers such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gounod" title="Charles Gounod">Charles Gounod</a> were imitated by Welsh contemporaries such as Parry, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Protheroe" title="Daniel Protheroe">Protheroe</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=T._Maldwyn_Price&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="T. Maldwyn Price (page does not exist)">Price</a> to cater for a Welsh fondness of dramatic narratives, wide dynamic contrasts and thrilling climaxes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies532_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Davies532-11">[11]</a></sup> As well as the growth of male voice choirs during the industrial period, Wales also experienced an increase in the popularity of brass bands. The bands were popular among the working classes, and were adopted by paternalistic employers who saw brass bands as a constructive activity for their work forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies79_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies79-12">[12]</a></sup> Solo artists of note during the nineteenth century included charismatic singers <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rees_(singer)" title="Robert Rees (singer)">Robert Rees</a> (Eos Morlais) and <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Edith_Wynne" title="Sarah Edith Wynne">Sarah Edith Wynne</a>, who would tour outside Wales and helped build the country's reputation as a "land of song".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the twentieth century, Wales produced a large number of classical and operatic soloists of international reputation, including <a href="/wiki/Ben_Davies_(tenor)" title="Ben Davies (tenor)">Ben Davies</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geraint_Evans" title="Geraint Evans">Geraint Evans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Tear" title="Robert Tear">Robert Tear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Terfel" title="Bryn Terfel">Bryn Terfel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gwyneth_Jones_(soprano)" title="Gwyneth Jones (soprano)">Gwyneth Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Price" title="Margaret Price">Margaret Price</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rebecca_Evans_(singer)" title="Rebecca Evans (singer)">Rebecca Evans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Helen_Watts" title="Helen Watts">Helen Watts</a>, as well as composers such as <a href="/wiki/Alun_Hoddinott" title="Alun Hoddinott">Alun Hoddinott</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Mathias" title="William Mathias">William Mathias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karl_Jenkins" title="Karl Jenkins">Karl Jenkins</a>.  From the 1980s onwards, crossover artists such as <a href="/wiki/Katherine_Jenkins" title="Katherine Jenkins">Katherine Jenkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Church" title="Charlotte Church">Charlotte Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aled_Jones" title="Aled Jones">Aled Jones</a> began to come to the fore. <a href="/wiki/Welsh_National_Opera" title="Welsh National Opera">Welsh National Opera</a>, established in 1946, and the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Cardiff_Singer_of_the_World_competition" title="BBC Cardiff Singer of the World competition">BBC Cardiff Singer of the World competition</a>, launched in 1983, attracted attention to Wales's growing reputation as a centre of excellence in the classical genre.
</p><p>Composer and conductor <a href="/wiki/Mansel_Thomas" title="Mansel Thomas">Mansel Thomas</a> OBE (1909–1986), who worked mainly in South Wales, was one of the most influential musicians of his generation. For many years employed by the BBC, he promoted the careers of many composers and performers. He himself wrote vocal, choral, instrumental, band and orchestral music, specialising in setting songs and poetry. Many of his orchestral and chamber music pieces are based on Welsh folk songs and dances.
</p>
<h3><span id="Post-1945.2C_popular_music"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-1945,_popular_music">Post-1945, popular music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-1945, popular music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After World War II, two significant musical organisations were founded, the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_National_Opera" title="Welsh National Opera">Welsh National Opera</a> and the <a href="/wiki/BBC_National_Orchestra_of_Wales" title="BBC National Orchestra of Wales">BBC National Orchestra of Wales</a>, both were factors in Welsh composers moving away from choral compositions to instrumental and orchestral pieces. Modern Welsh composers such as <a href="/wiki/Alun_Hoddinott" title="Alun Hoddinott">Alun Hoddinott</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Mathias" title="William Mathias">William Mathias</a> produced large scale orchestrations, though both have returned to religious themes within their work. Both men would also explore Welsh culture, with Mathias setting music to the works of <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">Dylan Thomas</a>, while Hoddinott, along with the likes of <a href="/wiki/Mervyn_Burtch" title="Mervyn Burtch">Mervyn Burtch</a> and <a href="/wiki/David_Wynne_(composer)" title="David Wynne (composer)">David Wynne</a>, would be influenced by the poetic and mythical past of Wales.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies581_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-Davies581-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>The 20th century saw many solo singers from Wales become not only national but international stars. <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello" title="Ivor Novello">Ivor Novello</a>, who was a singer-songwriter during the First World War. Also, opera-singers such as <a href="/wiki/Geraint_Evans" title="Geraint Evans">Geraint Evans</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Delme_Bryn-Jones" title="Delme Bryn-Jones">Delme Bryn-Jones</a> found fame post World War II. The 1960s saw the rise of two distinctive Welsh acts, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Bassey" title="Shirley Bassey">Shirley Bassey</a>, both of whom defined Welsh vocal styles for several generations.
</p><p>The 1960s saw important developments in both Welsh and English language music in Wales. The BBC had already produced Welsh language Radio programmes, such as Noson Lowen in the 1940s, and in the 1960s the corporation followed suite with television shows <i>Hob y Deri Dando</i> and <i>Disc a Dawn</i> giving Welsh acts a weekly stage to promote their sound. A more homely programme <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gwlad_y_Gan" title="Gwlad y Gan">Gwlad y Gan</a></i> was produced by rival channel <a href="/wiki/Television_Wales_and_the_West" title="Television Wales and the West">TWW</a> which set classic Welsh songs in idyllic settings and starred baritone <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Emmanuel" title="Ivor Emmanuel">Ivor Emmanuel</a>. The Anglo-American cultural influence was a strong draw on young musicians, with Tom Jones and Shirley Bassey becoming world-famous singers; and the growth of <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a>' <a href="/wiki/Apple_Records" title="Apple Records">Apple Records</a> label saw Welsh acts <a href="/wiki/Mary_Hopkin" title="Mary Hopkin">Mary Hopkin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Badfinger" title="Badfinger">Badfinger</a> join the roster. Not to be outdone, the short lived <a href="/wiki/Y_Blew" title="Y Blew">Y Blew</a>, born out of Aberystwyth University, became the first Welsh language pop band in 1967.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> This was followed in 1969 with the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Sain" title="Sain">Sain</a> record label, one of the most important catalyst for change in the Welsh language music scene.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies585_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies585-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p><p>In more modern times there has been a thriving musical scene. Bands and artists which have gained popularity include acts such as <a href="/wiki/Man_(band)" title="Man (band)">Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budgie_(band)" title="Budgie (band)">Budgie</a>, and solo artists <a href="/wiki/John_Cale" title="John Cale">John Cale</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Mary_Hopkin" title="Mary Hopkin">Mary Hopkin</a> in the early 1970s and solo artists <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Tyler" title="Bonnie Tyler">Bonnie Tyler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shakin%27_Stevens" title="Shakin' Stevens">Shakin' Stevens</a> in the 1980s, but through mimicking American music styles such as Motown or Rock and Roll. The Welsh language scene saw a dip in commercial popularity, but a rise in experimentation with acts such as punk band Trwynau Coch leading into a 'New Wave' of music. Bands that followed, like <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anhrefn" title="Anhrefn">Anhrefn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Datblygu" title="Datblygu">Datblygu</a>, found support from <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a> disc jockey <a href="/wiki/John_Peel" title="John Peel">John Peel</a>, one of the few DJs outside Wales to champion Welsh language music.
</p><p>Wales embraced the new music of the 1980s and 1990s, particularly with the thriving <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Newport" title="Music of Newport">Newport rock scene</a> for which the city was labelled 'the new <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Seattle" title="Music of Seattle">Seattle</a>'. Acts and individuals based in the city during the period included <a href="/wiki/Joe_Strummer" title="Joe Strummer">Joe Strummer</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Clash" title="The Clash">The Clash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feeder_(band)" title="Feeder (band)">Feeder</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Darling_Buds" title="The Darling Buds">The Darling Buds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donna_Matthews" title="Donna Matthews">Donna Matthews</a> of <a href="/wiki/Elastica" title="Elastica">Elastica</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Skindred" title="Skindred">Skindred</a> and punk and metal acts. Famous performers or attendees at venues such as <a href="/wiki/TJ%27s" title="TJ's">TJ's</a> included Oasis, Kurt Cobain, and others.
</p><p>The early 21st century produced a credible Welsh 'sound' embraced by the public and the media press of Great Britain. Such acts included the <a href="/wiki/Manic_Street_Preachers" title="Manic Street Preachers">Manic Street Preachers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stereophonics" title="Stereophonics">Stereophonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catatonia_(band)" title="Catatonia (band)">Catatonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Super_Furry_Animals" title="Super Furry Animals">Super Furry Animals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gorky%27s_Zygotic_Mynci" title="Gorky's Zygotic Mynci">Gorky's Zygotic Mynci</a>. The first two allowed the Welsh pop scene to flourish, and while not singing in Welsh they brought a sense of Welshness through iconography, lyrics and interviews. The latter two bands were notable for bringing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_(language)" title="Welsh (language)">Welsh language</a> songs to a British audience. Music venues and acts have thrived in the 2010s, with the noted success of the <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Cardiff" title="Music of Cardiff">Cardiff music scene</a>, for which the city has recently been labelled 'Music City'.
</p><p>In 2013 the first <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Language_Music_Day" title="Welsh Language Music Day">Welsh Language Music Day</a> was held, taking place each year in February. Events mark the use of Welsh language in a wide range of genres of music, and locations include <a href="/wiki/Womanby_Street" title="Womanby Street">Womanby Street</a> in Cardiff as well as <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn" title="Brooklyn">Brooklyn</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles_of_Welsh_music">Styles of Welsh music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Styles of Welsh music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_music">Traditional music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Traditional music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early musical traditions during the 17th and 18th century saw the emergence of more complex <a href="/wiki/Carol_(music)" title="Carol (music)">carols</a>, away from the repetitive ceremonial songs. These carols featured complex poetry based on <a href="/wiki/Cynghanedd" title="Cynghanedd">cynghanedd</a>, some were sung to English tunes, but many used Welsh melodies such as 'Ffarwel Ned Puw'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup> The most common Welsh folk song is the love song, with lyrics pertaining to the sorrow of parting or in praise of the girl. A few employ sexual metaphor and mention the act of <a href="/wiki/Bundling_(tradition)" title="Bundling (tradition)">bundling</a>. After love songs, the ballad was a very popular form of song, with its tales of manual labour, agriculture and the every day life. Popular themes in the 19th century included murder, emigration and colliery disasters; sung to popular melodies from Ireland or North America.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies579_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-Davies579-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p>The instrument most commonly associated with Wales is the <a href="/wiki/Harp" title="Harp">harp</a>, which is generally considered to be the country's national instrument.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies353_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies353-17">[17]</a></sup> Though it originated in Italy, the <a href="/wiki/Triple_harp" title="Triple harp">triple harp</a> (<i>telyn deires</i>, "three-row harp") is held up as the traditional harp of Wales: it has three rows of strings, with every <a href="/wiki/Semitone" title="Semitone">semitone</a> separately represented, while modern concert harps use a pedal system to change key by stopping the relevant strings. After losing ground to the pedal harp in the 19th century, it has been re-popularised through the efforts of <a href="/wiki/Nansi_Richards" title="Nansi Richards">Nansi Richards</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Llio_Rhydderch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Llio Rhydderch (page does not exist)">Llio Rhydderch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robin_Huw_Bowen" title="Robin Huw Bowen">Robin Huw Bowen</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cerdd_Dant" title="Cerdd Dant">penillion</a> is a traditional form of Welsh singing poetry, accompanied by the harp, in which the singer and harpist follow different melodies so the stressed syllables of the poem coincide with accented beats of the harp melody.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies662_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies662-18">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest written records of the Welsh harpists' repertoire are contained in the <a href="/wiki/Robert_ap_Huw" title="Robert ap Huw">Robert ap Huw</a> manuscript, which documents 30 ancient harp pieces that make up a fragment of the lost repertoire of the medieval Welsh bards. The music was composed between the 14th and 16th centuries, transmitted orally, then written down in a unique tablature and later copied in the early 17th century. This manuscript contains the earliest body of harp music from anywhere in Europe and is one of the key sources of early Welsh music.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> The manuscript has been the source of a long-running effort to accurately decipher the music it encodes.
</p><p>Another distinctive instrument is the <a href="/wiki/Crwth" title="Crwth">crwth</a>, also a stringed instrument of a type once widespread in northern Europe, it was played in Wales from the Middle Ages, which, superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Fiddle" title="Fiddle">fiddle</a> (Welsh <i>Ffidil</i>), lingered on later in Wales than elsewhere but died out by the nineteenth century at the latest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Davies179_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Davies179-20">[20]</a></sup> The fiddle is an integral part of Welsh folk music. Other traditional instruments from Wales include the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_Bagpipes" title="Welsh Bagpipes">Welsh Bagpipes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pibgorn_(instrument)" title="Pibgorn (instrument)">Pibgorn</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_music">Folk music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Folk music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Welsh folk is known for a variety of instrumental and vocal styles, as well as more recent singer-songwriters drawing on folk traditions.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1908" data-file-width="1084" decoding="async" height="264" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg/150px-Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg/225px-Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg/300px-Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg 2x" width="150"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dafydd-Iwan_by-Aberdare-Blog.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Iwan" title="Dafydd Iwan">Dafydd Iwan</a>, co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/Sain" title="Sain">Sain</a> record label</div></div></div>
<p>By the late 1970s, Wales, like its neighbours, had seen the beginning of a <a href="/wiki/Roots_revival" title="Roots revival">roots revival</a>, the beginnings of which can be traced back to the 1960s folk singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Dafydd_Iwan" title="Dafydd Iwan">Dafydd Iwan</a>. Iwan was instrumental in the creation of a modern Welsh folk scene, and is known for fiercely patriotic and nationalistic songs, as well as the foundation of the <a href="/wiki/Sain" title="Sain">Sain</a> record label. The <a href="/wiki/Festival_Interceltique_de_Lorient" title="Festival Interceltique de Lorient">Festival Interceltique de Lorient</a> saw the formation of <a href="/wiki/Ar_Log" title="Ar Log">Ar Log</a>, who spearheaded a revival of Welsh fiddling and harp-playing, and continued recording into the 21st century. A Welsh session band, following in the footsteps of their Irish counterparts <a href="/wiki/Planxty" title="Planxty">Planxty</a>, Cilmeri recorded two albums with a uniquely Welsh feel.  Welsh <a href="/wiki/Folk_rock" title="Folk rock">folk rock</a> includes a number of bands, such as <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moniars&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moniars (page does not exist)">Moniars</a>, Gwerinos, <a href="/wiki/The_Bluehorses" title="The Bluehorses">The Bluehorses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Delyn_a%27r_Ebillion" title="Bob Delyn a'r Ebillion">Bob Delyn a'r Ebillion</a> and Taran.
</p><p>Sain was founded in 1969 by Dafydd Iwan and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Huw_Jones_(singer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Huw Jones (singer) (page does not exist)">Huw Jones</a> with the aid of funding from <a href="/wiki/Brian_Morgan_Edwards" title="Brian Morgan Edwards">Brian Morgan Edwards</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Originally, the label signed Welsh singers, mostly with overtly political lyrics, eventually branching out into a myriad of different styles. These included <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country music</a> (John ac Alun), singer-songwriters (<a href="/wiki/Meic_Stevens" title="Meic Stevens">Meic Stevens</a>), <a href="/wiki/Arena_rock" title="Arena rock">stadium rock</a> (<a href="/wiki/The_Alarm" title="The Alarm">The Alarm</a>) and classical singers (<a href="/wiki/Aled_Jones" title="Aled Jones">Aled Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Terfel" title="Bryn Terfel">Bryn Terfel</a>).
</p><p>The folk revival picked up energy in the 1980s with Robin Huw Bowen and other musicians achieving great commercial and critical success. Later into the 1990s, a new wave of bands including <a href="/wiki/Fernhill_(band)" title="Fernhill (band)">Fernhill</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rag_Foundation_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rag Foundation (band) (page does not exist)">Rag Foundation</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bob_Delyn_A%27r_Ebillion" title="Bob Delyn A'r Ebillion">Bob Delyn A'r Ebillion</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moniars&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moniars (page does not exist)">Moniars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carreg_Lafar" title="Carreg Lafar">Carreg Lafar</a>, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jac_y_Do&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jac y Do (page does not exist)">Jac y Do</a>, Boys From The Hill and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gwerinos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gwerinos (page does not exist)">Gwerinos</a> found popularity. Jac y Do is one of several bands that now perform <i>twmpathau</i> all over the country for social gatherings and public events. Welsh traditional music was updated by punk-folk bands delivering traditional tunes at a much increased tempo; these included early Bob Delyn a'r Ebillion and Defaid. The 1990s also saw the creation of <a href="/wiki/Fflach" title="Fflach">Fflach</a>:tradd, a label which soon came to dominate the Welsh folk record industry with a series of compilations, as well as thematic projects like <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ffidil" title="Ffidil">Ffidil</a></i>, which featured 13 fiddlers. Some Welsh performers have mixed traditional influences, especially the language, into imported genres, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Soliloquise&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Soliloquise (page does not exist)">Soliloquise</a> for example and especially <a href="/wiki/John_ac_Alun" title="John ac Alun">John ac Alun</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh language</a> <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> duo who are perhaps the best-known contemporary performers in Welsh.
</p><p>In June 2007, Tŷ Siamas was opened in <a href="/wiki/Dolgellau" title="Dolgellau">Dolgellau</a>. Tŷ Siamas is the National Centre for Traditional Music, with regular sessions, concerts, lessons, an interactive exhibition and a recording studio.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_and_rock">Pop and rock</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pop and rock">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Shirley_Bassey_(1971).jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2509" data-file-width="1882" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Shirley_Bassey_%281971%29.jpg/220px-Shirley_Bassey_%281971%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Shirley_Bassey_%281971%29.jpg/330px-Shirley_Bassey_%281971%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Shirley_Bassey_%281971%29.jpg/440px-Shirley_Bassey_%281971%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Shirley_Bassey_(1971).jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Shirley_Bassey" title="Shirley Bassey">Shirley Bassey</a> was the first Welsh person to gain a No. 1 single.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>In the non-traditional arena, many Welsh musicians have been present in popular rock and pop, either as individuals, (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tom_Jones_(singer)" title="Tom Jones (singer)">Tom Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Bassey" title="Shirley Bassey">Shirley Bassey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dave_Edmunds" title="Dave Edmunds">Dave Edmunds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shakin%27_Stevens" title="Shakin' Stevens">Shakin' Stevens</a>), individuals in groups (e.g. <a href="/wiki/John_Cale" title="John Cale">John Cale</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Velvet_Underground" title="The Velvet Underground">The Velvet Underground</a>, <a href="/wiki/Green_Gartside" title="Green Gartside">Green Gartside</a> of <a href="/wiki/Scritti_Politti" title="Scritti Politti">Scritti Politti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julian_Cope" title="Julian Cope">Julian Cope</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teardrop_Explodes" title="Teardrop Explodes">Teardrop Explodes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andy_Scott_(guitarist)" title="Andy Scott (guitarist)">Andy Scott</a> of <a href="/wiki/The_Sweet" title="The Sweet">Sweet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Glover" title="Roger Glover">Roger Glover</a> of <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rainbow_(English_band)" title="Rainbow (English band)">Rainbow</a>), or as bands formed in Wales (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Amen_Corner_(band)" title="Amen Corner (band)">Amen Corner</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Alarm" title="The Alarm">The Alarm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Man_(band)" title="Man (band)">Man</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budgie_(band)" title="Budgie (band)">Budgie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badfinger" title="Badfinger">Badfinger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tigertailz" title="Tigertailz">Tigertailz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Marble_Giants" title="Young Marble Giants">Young Marble Giants</a>), but not until the 1990s did Welsh bands begin to be seen as a particular grouping. Following on from an underground <a href="/wiki/Post-punk" title="Post-punk">post-punk</a> movement in the 1980s, led by bands like <a href="/wiki/Datblygu" title="Datblygu">Datblygu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fflaps" title="Fflaps">Fflaps</a>, the 1990s saw a considerable flowering of Welsh rock groups (in both Welsh and English languages) such as <a href="/wiki/Catatonia_(band)" title="Catatonia (band)">Catatonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manic_Street_Preachers" title="Manic Street Preachers">Manic Street Preachers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Feeder_(band)" title="Feeder (band)">Feeder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stereophonics" title="Stereophonics">Stereophonics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Super_Furry_Animals" title="Super Furry Animals">Super Furry Animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Pooh_Sticks" title="The Pooh Sticks">The Pooh Sticks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/60ft_Dolls" title="60ft Dolls">60ft Dolls</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gorky%27s_Zygotic_Mynci" title="Gorky's Zygotic Mynci">Gorky's Zygotic Mynci</a>.
</p><p>The 21st century has seen the emergence of a number of new artists, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marina_and_the_Diamonds" title="Marina and the Diamonds">Marina and the Diamonds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skindred" title="Skindred">Skindred</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lostprophets" title="Lostprophets">Lostprophets</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kids_in_Glass_Houses" title="Kids in Glass Houses">Kids in Glass Houses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duffy_(singer)" title="Duffy (singer)">Duffy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Rees" title="Christopher Rees">Christopher Rees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bullet_for_My_Valentine" title="Bullet for My Valentine">Bullet for My Valentine</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Automatic" title="The Automatic">The Automatic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goldie_Lookin_Chain" title="Goldie Lookin Chain">Goldie Lookin Chain</a>, <a href="/wiki/People_in_Planes" title="People in Planes">People in Planes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Los_Campesinos!" title="Los Campesinos!">Los Campesinos!</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Victorian_English_Gentlemens_Club" title="The Victorian English Gentlemens Club">The Victorian English Gentlemens Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/Attack!_Attack!_(Welsh_band)" title="Attack! Attack! (Welsh band)">Attack! Attack!</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gwenno" title="Gwenno">Gwenno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Lee_Owens" title="Kelly Lee Owens">Kelly Lee Owens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funeral_for_a_Friend" title="Funeral for a Friend">Funeral for a Friend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hondo_Maclean" title="Hondo Maclean">Hondo Maclean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fflur_Dafydd" title="Fflur Dafydd">Fflur Dafydd</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Blackout_(band)" title="The Blackout (band)">The Blackout</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Broken_Vinyl_Club" title="The Broken Vinyl Club">The Broken Vinyl Club</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Joy_Formidable" title="The Joy Formidable">The Joy Formidable</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Anchoress_(musician)" title="The Anchoress (musician)">The Anchoress</a>. There is a thriving Welsh-language contemporary music scene ranging from rock to hip-hop which routinely attracts large crowds and audiences, but they tend to be covered only by the Welsh-language media. More abrasive alternative acts such as <a href="/wiki/Jarcrew" title="Jarcrew">Jarcrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mclusky" title="Mclusky">Mclusky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Future_of_the_Left" title="Future of the Left">Future of the Left</a> – all well-known within the independent music community and known as Welsh acts – have also received modest commercial success in the UK. Quite a strong neo-progressive/classic rock scene has developed from Swansea-based band <a href="/wiki/Karnataka_(band)" title="Karnataka (band)">Karnataka</a> and other bands that have links to them. These include <a href="/wiki/Magenta_(Welsh_band)" title="Magenta (Welsh band)">Magenta</a>, The Reasoning and <a href="/wiki/Panic_Room_(band)" title="Panic Room (band)">Panic Room</a>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gryff_Rhys.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="455" data-file-width="632" decoding="async" height="158" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gryff_Rhys.jpg/220px-Gryff_Rhys.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gryff_Rhys.jpg/330px-Gryff_Rhys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Gryff_Rhys.jpg/440px-Gryff_Rhys.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gryff_Rhys.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gruff_Rhys" title="Gruff Rhys">Gruff Rhys</a> of <a href="/wiki/Super_Furry_Animals" title="Super Furry Animals">Super Furry Animals</a></div></div></div>
<p>Welsh bands have the outlet for audiences, on such media as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Wales" title="BBC Wales">BBC Wales</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BBC_Cymru" title="BBC Cymru">BBC Cymru</a>, <a href="/wiki/S4C" title="S4C">S4C</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Pop_Factory" title="The Pop Factory">The Pop Factory</a>. In particular, BBC Radio 1's <a href="/wiki/Bethan_Elfyn" title="Bethan Elfyn">Bethan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huw_Stephens" title="Huw Stephens">Huw</a> and BBC Radio Wales's <a href="/wiki/Adam_Walton" title="Adam Walton">Adam Walton</a> support new Welsh music at their respective stations. Every year, <i><a href="/wiki/Menter_Iaith" title="Menter Iaith">Mentrau Iaith Cymru</a></i>, The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Eisteddfod" title="National Eisteddfod">National Eisteddfod</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Cymru" title="BBC Radio Cymru">BBC Radio Cymru</a> have their national 'Battle of the Bands,' where young, upcoming Welsh bands can compete for £1000, and, what is thought to be one of the greatest possible achievements for a Welsh language act, to perform at Maes B, on its final night. In addition to Maes B, there are a number of various Welsh language music events throughout the year that have gained popularity in the past few years. In February each year the Welsh magazine 'Y Selar' hosts <a href="/wiki/Y_Selar_Awards" title="Y Selar Awards">an award ceremony</a> in Aberystwyth University where Welsh music fans from all over the country go to see the most popular and upcoming bands perform. There's also the 'Dawns Rhyngolegol' where the Welsh societies from every University in the UK gather to celebrate the best Welsh language music in Wales.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_music">Electronic music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_of_Wales&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Electronic music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>DJ <a href="/wiki/Sasha_(DJ)" title="Sasha (DJ)">Sasha</a> is from <a href="/wiki/Hawarden" title="Hawarden">Hawarden</a>, Flintshire. Also worth noting are the successful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drum_and_Bass" title="Drum and Bass">Drum and Bass</a> DJ <a href="/wiki/High_Contrast" title="High Contrast">High Contrast</a> who is from <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a>, the veteran house outfit <a href="/wiki/K-Klass" title="K-Klass">K-Klass</a> from <a href="/wiki/Wrexham" title="Wrexham">Wrexham</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Swansea" title="Swansea">Swansea</a>-based <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_breaks" title="Progressive breaks">progressive breaks</a> producers <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hybrid_(Welsh_band)" title="Hybrid (Welsh band)">Hybrid</a>. Escape into the Park and Bionic Events are examples of the Welsh Hard Dance scene. On 16 July 2011 <a href="/wiki/Sian_Evans" title="Sian Evans">Sian Evans</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthpop" title="Synthpop">synthpop</a> Bristol based band <a href="/wiki/Kosheen" title="Kosheen">Kosheen</a> had a No.1 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_UK_Singles_Charts" title="Official UK Singles Charts">Official UK Singles Charts</a> hit in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Fresh" title="DJ Fresh">DJ Fresh</a>.
