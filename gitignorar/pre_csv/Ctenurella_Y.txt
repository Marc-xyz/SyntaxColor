<p><i><b>Ctenurella</b></i> ("Little Comb Tail") is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extinct" title="Extinct">extinct</a> <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptyctodont" title="Ptyctodont">ptyctodont</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placoderm" title="Placoderm">placoderm</a> from the Late <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">Devonian</a> of Germany. The first fossils were found in the <a href="/wiki/Strunde" title="Strunde">Strundevalley</a> in the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Paffrather_Kalkmulde&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Paffrather Kalkmulde (page does not exist)">Paffrather Kalkmulde</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ctenurella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with other ptyctodonts, the armor of <i>Ctenurella</i> was reduced to a few thin plates on the head and shoulder region. It was also relatively small for a placoderm, at just 13 centimetres (5 in) in length. It had two <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_fin" title="Dorsal fin">dorsal fins</a>, with that at the rear of the body being relatively long and low, and large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pectoral_fin" title="Pectoral fin">pectoral</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_fin" title="Pelvic fin">pelvic fins</a>. Most ptyctodonts are presumed to have fed on the ocean floor, but the well-developed fins of this genus indicate that it probably also swam in open waters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoDP_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EoDP-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="445" data-file-width="400" decoding="async" height="245" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg/220px-Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg/330px-Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/93/Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ctenurella_gladbachensis.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reconstruction of the male (with head clasper) and female</div></div></div>
<p><i>Ctenurella</i> had a long, <a href="/wiki/Whip" title="Whip">whip</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Tail" title="Tail">tail</a>, large <a href="/wiki/Eye" title="Eye">eyes</a>, and robust upper and lower <a href="/wiki/Jaw" title="Jaw">jaw</a> tooth plates. Males also had  hook-shaped <a href="/wiki/Sex_organ" title="Sex organ">sex organs</a>, known as <a href="/wiki/Clasper" title="Clasper">claspers</a>. Since analogous features are also found in the unrelated living <a href="/wiki/Chimaera" title="Chimaera">chimaeras</a>, chimaeras and ptyctodonts are thought to be an example of <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EoDP_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EoDP-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ctenurella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The specific name of the type species, <i>C. gladbachensis</i>, is a reference of the place <a href="/wiki/Bergisch_Gladbach" title="Bergisch Gladbach">Bergisch Gladbach</a>, where it was founded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy_and_evolution">Taxonomy and evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ctenurella&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taxonomy and evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The species <i>Ctenurella gardineri</i> was split off into the new genus <i>Austroptyctodus</i> by Long (1997) in his review of the Gogo ptyctodontid species, stipulating that the genus <i>Ctenurella</i> as only coming from the German sites. A new description of <i>Ctenurella gladbachensis</i> by Long (1997) showed that the original restoration had wrongly restored the skull-roof as the central bones do not in fact meet each other behind the nuchal plate.
</p><p>Ptyctodontid placoderms recently have been shown to give birth to live young, with specimens of pregnant females from two genera, <i><a href="/wiki/Materpiscis" title="Materpiscis">Materpiscis</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Austroptyctodus" title="Austroptyctodus">Austroptyctodus</a></i>, both from the <a href="/wiki/Gogo_Formation" title="Gogo Formation">Gogo Formation</a> of Western Australia, showing the presence of unborn embryos within the mother fishes (Long et al. 2008)
</p>
