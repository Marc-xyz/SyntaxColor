	<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Tom Mitchell (Australian politician)</h1>
	
	<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
		<div class="noprint" id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

<b>Thomas Walter Mitchell</b> (11 November 1906 – 4 February 1984) was an <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> politician, author and sportsman.  
</p><p>Mitchell was born at the family property "Towong Hill", near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corryong,_Victoria" title="Corryong, Victoria">Corryong, Victoria</a> and was educated at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> from 1925 to 1930 before returning to Australia, and Towong Hill. At the time the property consisted of 17,000 acres (69 km<sup>2</sup>) of prime land beside the <a href="/wiki/Murray_River" title="Murray River">Murray River</a>, but today has been reduced to 2,300 acres (9 km<sup>2</sup>), as sections have been split to various family members.
</p><p>A keen skier, Mitchell founded the Australian National Ski Federation in 1932, captained the Australian skiing team and won gold medals in competitions against <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. He was an Australian champion in the downhill and long-time member of the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Ski_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Australian Ski Club (page does not exist)">Australian Ski Club</a>. He and his wife <a href="/wiki/Elyne_Mitchell" title="Elyne Mitchell">Elyne Mitchell</a> (daughter of General Sir <a href="/wiki/Harry_Chauvel" title="Harry Chauvel">Harry Chauvel</a>) were the first people to ski the western slopes of the Main Range of the Snowy Mountains, including the demanding runs of "Little Austria".
</p><p>Mitchell joined the <a href="/wiki/Second_Australian_Imperial_Force" title="Second Australian Imperial Force">Second Australian Imperial Force</a> in 1940 and was a captain in the 2/22 Battalion and Headquarters 8th Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_ADB_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_ADB-1">[1]</a></sup> He was captured by the Japanese in 1942 and was interned in the infamous <a href="/wiki/Changi_Prison" title="Changi Prison">Changi POW camp</a> until 1945, being forced to work on the <a href="/wiki/Burma_Railway" title="Burma Railway">Burma Railway</a> by his captors. He later built a chapel, in memory of comrades lost during the war, on his property.
</p><p>In 1947 he and Elyne, together with Ossie Rixon, set off in two four-wheel-drive vehicles to become the first people to cross the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Alps" title="Australian Alps">Australian Alps</a> in a motor vehicle. "One", a <a href="/wiki/Willys_MB" title="Willys MB">Willys army jeep</a>, sits in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corryong,_Victoria" title="Corryong, Victoria">Corryong</a> to this day. 
</p><p>He served as the Country Party member for <a href="/wiki/Electoral_district_of_Benambra" title="Electoral district of Benambra">Benambra</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Legislative_Assembly" title="Victorian Legislative Assembly">Victorian Legislative Assembly</a> from 1947 to 1976, as <a href="/wiki/Attorney-General_of_Victoria" title="Attorney-General of Victoria">Attorney-General</a> from June 1950 to 27 October 1952 and again from 31 October to December 1952.  He was also <a href="/wiki/Solicitor-General_of_Victoria" title="Solicitor-General of Victoria">Solicitor-General</a> from June 1950 to December 1951,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the last politician to hold the office,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_ADB_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_ADB-1">[1]</a></sup> replaced by a non-political barrister, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Winneke" title="Henry Winneke">Henry Winneke</a>. This was the start of a fundamental shift of the role of Solicitors-General in Australia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Appleby_2012_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Appleby_2012-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>He contributed to the official history of the Second World War and wrote several volumes on skiing and local history.
</p><p>Mitchell died in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mitchell_ADB_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Mitchell_ADB-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>
