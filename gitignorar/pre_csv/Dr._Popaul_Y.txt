<p><i><b>Dr. Popaul</b></i> is a 1972 French <a href="/wiki/Black_comedy" title="Black comedy">black comedy</a> film directed by <a href="/wiki/Claude_Chabrol" title="Claude Chabrol">Claude Chabrol</a>. also known under the titles <i><b>High Heels</b></i> and <i><b>Scoundrel in White</b></i>.
</p><p>The film was based on the 1969 novel <i>Murder at Leisure</i> by <a href="/wiki/Hubert_Monteilhet" title="Hubert Monteilhet">Hubert Monteilhet</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Popaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dr. Paul Simay, an inveterate womanizer, now confined to a hospital bed, reminisces on his life. His passion was to court and bed unattractive women because he found them more fascinating. While on vacation in Tunisia, he met Christine, a shy young woman with a crippled leg. Paul seduced her, only to discover later that her father, Dr. Dupont is one of his professors at the medical school in Bordeaux, who also owns a clinic in the city. Dupont offered Paul a deal: if the latter married Christine, he would inherit the Dupont clinic. Paul agreed but at the wedding he met Christine's beautiful younger sister Martine. Paul then found creative ways to repel or eliminate Martine's suitors one by one, so he can have her for himself.  He began drugging Christine at night so he could sneak out and sleep with Martine. Everything was going well until a curious car accident left him bedridden.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Popaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was the first movie from Belmondo's own production company, Cerito Films.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dr._Popaul&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of its release, it was the biggest hit of Chabrol's career. However it was not released in Britain until 1976, where it was titled <i><b>Scoundrel in White</b></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guard_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-guard-2">[2]</a></sup> It was not released in the US until 1981, where it had the title <i><b>High Heels</b></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p>The film received mixed reviews upon release.<i>The New York Times</i> said "the performances are uniformly good" but "more interesting than the movie itself is the way its concerns with guilt and roletrading relate to other, far better Chabrol films."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>  <i>Time Out</i> called it a "coarse farce" that "looked more like the director's revenge on the French mass audience, who had consistently ignored his good movies, but would accept anything with Belmondo."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> "It stinks" said the <i>Los Angeles Times</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>
</p>
