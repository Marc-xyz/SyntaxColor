<p>The <b>Huntsville Hospital Tram System</b> is an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automated_people_mover" title="Automated people mover">automated people mover</a> system located as part of the <a href="/wiki/Huntsville_Hospital_System" title="Huntsville Hospital System">Huntsville Hospital System</a> complex in <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">Huntsville, Alabama</a>, United States. Operating on a 1,890-foot (580 m) concrete <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guideway" title="Guideway">guideway</a>, the trams serve to connect the Huntsville Hospital with the Huntsville Hospital for Women &amp; Children.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hhti_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-hhti-3">[3]</a></sup> At the time of completion, this was the second hospital people mover system in the United States after the <a href="/wiki/Duke_University_Medical_Center_Patient_Rapid_Transit" title="Duke University Medical Center Patient Rapid Transit">Duke University Medical Center Patient Rapid Transit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2,_2001_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-December_2,_2001-4">[4]</a></sup> As of January 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Huntsville_Hospital_Tram_System&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, this is the only automated people mover system completed in the state of Alabama.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hhti_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-hhti-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huntsville_Hospital_Tram_System&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Developed by Poma-Otis Transportation Systems, a joint venture of <a href="/wiki/Poma" title="Poma">Poma</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Otis_Elevator_Company" title="Otis Elevator Company">Otis Elevator Company</a>, and constructed by <a href="/wiki/Brasfield_%26_Gorrie" title="Brasfield &amp; Gorrie">Brasfield &amp; Gorrie</a>, the cable-driven steel-on-steel<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> system was completed at a final cost of $10.9 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-June_20,_2002_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-June_20,_2002-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2,_1999_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-December_2,_1999-6">[6]</a></sup> The two 1,890-foot (580 m) <a href="/wiki/Automated_guideway_transit" title="Automated guideway transit">concrete guideways</a> are elevated 30 feet (9.1 m) above the surface.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B&amp;G_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-B&amp;G-7">[7]</a></sup> Costing $280,000 annually to operate, the Huntsville Hospital Tram System handles approximately 2,200 passengers per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PPT_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-PPT-8">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The vehicles were designed by the Gangloff company of Switzerland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2,_2001_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-December_2,_2001-4">[4]</a></sup> Each car can handle three seated and 38 standing passengers. Additionally, each car was designed to accommodate the largest bed in use by the hospital for the transport of patients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2,_2001_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-December_2,_2001-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>The system runs in an east-west direction between the main Huntsville Hospital building and the Huntsville Hospital for Women &amp; Children with intermediate stops at the Plaza Resource Center and the Franklin Medical Tower.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hhti_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-hhti-3">[3]</a></sup> The system offers two separate modes for reaching the various stations, a <i>Local Mode</i> and an <i>Express Mode</i>. The <i>Local Mode</i> makes all four stops, primarily traveling along the northern track. The <i>Express Mode</i> only travels between the two termini, primarily along the southern track.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hhti_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-hhti-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huntsville_Hospital_Tram_System&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The idea of developing a tram system for Huntsville Hospital was initially proposed in mid-1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July_23,_1997_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-July_23,_1997-9">[9]</a></sup> The tram was proposed as a solution to both parking issues and to allow for easier connectivity between the separate buildings of the sprawling hospital campus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July_23,_1997_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-July_23,_1997-9">[9]</a></sup> Additionally, the consolidation and transformation of the former Humana Hospital with Huntsville Hospital in 1994 made the development of the tram necessary in an effort to eliminate duplicate services and provide for easy transport of patients and doctors from each facility.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-October_2,_2000_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-October_2,_2000-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p><p>By December 1998, plans for the system moved forward after gaining approval of several property <a href="/wiki/Variance_(land_use)" title="Variance (land use)">variances</a> from the Huntsville Board of Zoning Adjustment to allow for its construction.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_16,_1998_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-December_16,_1998-10">[10]</a></sup> By early 2000 construction of the system would commence, with an initial opening slated for summer 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-October_2,_2000_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-October_2,_2000-2">[2]</a></sup> However, due to delays and the need for enhanced security along the system in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>, the system would not open until June 19, 2002.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-June_20,_2002_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-June_20,_2002-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-December_2,_2001_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-December_2,_2001-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2004, use of the tram resulted in the elimination of approximately 4,500 annual ambulance trips and the use of two full-time shuttle vans between the two main facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-September_19,_2004_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-September_19,_2004-11">[11]</a></sup> Due to this reduction in the number of vehicle trips made, Huntsville Hospital was awarded a 2004 Industrial Air Pollution Control Achievement Award from the city of Huntsville Air Pollution Control Board.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-September_19,_2004_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-September_19,_2004-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
