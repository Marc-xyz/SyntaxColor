<p><b>A. Elizabeth Jones</b> (born 1948 in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munich,_Germany" title="Munich, Germany">Munich, Germany</a>) served as the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">Ambassador</a> to <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a>, from 1995 to 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SERVICE_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-SERVICE-1">[1]</a></sup> She served as the U.S. assistant secretary of state for Europe and Eurasia in 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TERRORISM_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-TERRORISM-2">[2]</a></sup> She was promoted to the rank of <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Foreign_Service_Career_Ambassadors" title="List of United States Foreign Service Career Ambassadors">Career Ambassador</a> in 2004.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-StateList1_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-StateList1-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._Elizabeth_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jones was born in Munich to parents in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Foreign_Service" title="U.S. Foreign Service">U.S. Foreign Service</a>, and grew up in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a>, where she attended local schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> She is a graduate of <a href="/wiki/Swarthmore_College" title="Swarthmore College">Swarthmore College</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrorism_in_Central_Asia">Terrorism in Central Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A._Elizabeth_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terrorism in Central Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jones testified on the threat of <a href="/wiki/Terrorism_in_Central_Asia" title="Terrorism in Central Asia">terrorism in Central Asia</a> before the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>' subcommittee on the Middle East and Central Asia on 29 October 2003. Jones said the greatest threats to the <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asian</a> states are the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_Movement_of_Uzbekistan" title="Islamic Movement of Uzbekistan">Islamic Movement of Uzbekistan</a>, which she described as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamist_terrorism" title="Islamist terrorism">Islamic terrorist organization</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hizb_ut-Tahrir" title="Hizb ut-Tahrir">Hizb ut-Tahrir</a>, which praises attacks on U.S. troops in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>. She said that despite the death of IMU leader <a href="/wiki/Juma_Namangani" title="Juma Namangani">Juma Namangani</a>, the "IMU is still active in the region -- particularly in Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan, and Kazakhstan -- and it represents a serious threat to the region and therefore to our interests."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TERRORISM_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-TERRORISM-2">[2]</a></sup>
</p>
