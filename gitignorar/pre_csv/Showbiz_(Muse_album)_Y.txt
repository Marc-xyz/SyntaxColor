<p><i><b>Showbiz</b></i> is the debut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by English <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse</a>. It was first released on 7 September 1999 in France, on 28 September 1999 in the United States by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maverick_Records" title="Maverick Records">Maverick Records</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ootw81_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ootw81-1">[1]</a></sup> and on 4 October 1999 in the United Kingdom by <a href="/wiki/Mushroom_Records" title="Mushroom Records">Mushroom Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taste_Media" title="Taste Media">Taste Media</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ootw81_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ootw81-1">[1]</a></sup> Recorded between April and May 1999 at <a href="/wiki/RAK_Studios" title="RAK Studios">RAK Studios</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sawmills_Studio" title="Sawmills Studio">Sawmills Studio</a>, respectively,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> the album was produced by <a href="/wiki/John_Leckie" title="John Leckie">John Leckie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Reeve" title="Paul Reeve">Paul Reeve</a> in conjunction with the band. <i>Showbiz</i> was a moderate commercial success, reaching number 29 on the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Showbiz</i> was released in various regions around the world through the band's different regional labels: Naïve in France, <a href="/wiki/Motor_Music" title="Motor Music">Motor</a> in Germany, Russia, Turkey and Ukraine, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maverick_Records" title="Maverick Records">Maverick</a> in the United States, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Play_It_Again_Sam_(record_label)" title="Play It Again Sam (record label)">Play It Again Sam</a> (PIAS) in Benelux, and <a href="/wiki/Avex_Group" title="Avex Group">Avex Trax</a> in Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ootw81_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ootw81-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_recording">Background and recording</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Showbiz_(Muse_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and recording">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Showbiz</i> was recorded between April 1999 and May 1999. However, the album included some older songs in <a href="/wiki/Muse_(band)" title="Muse (band)">Muse's</a> repertoire, many of which can date as far back as 1996. Most of the songs on <i>Showbiz</i> had already been written at least by 1997. The songs featured on the album were among the "fifty or so" that <a href="/wiki/Matt_Bellamy" title="Matt Bellamy">Matt Bellamy</a> had written before entering the studio. The band selected the songs which they deemed to be the more conventional and "straight-forward" to make up <i>Showbiz</i>. While the songs contain an eclectic and diverse sound featuring subtle <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American_music" title="Latin American music">Latin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a> influences, they have a distinct and cohesive <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> aesthetic. The more <a href="/wiki/Experimental_music" title="Experimental music">experimental</a> material was left out of the album to be included as <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-sides</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">single</a> releases. A few of those songs were later featured on the compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/Hullabaloo_Soundtrack" title="Hullabaloo Soundtrack">Hullabaloo Soundtrack</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Leckie" title="John Leckie">John Leckie</a>, the producer of the album, started attending Muse's concerts in the latter half of 1998. Leckie was based at <a href="/wiki/Sawmills_Studios" title="Sawmills Studios">Sawmills recording studio</a>, where the owner Dennis Smith had given the band free recording time the previous year, from which the <i><a href="/wiki/Muse_(EP)" title="Muse (EP)">Muse EP</a></i> had resulted in 1998. He built up a relationship with the band during this time, coming to say that he'd "want to work with the band if ever they could afford him". Leckie had not worked with many bands for quite some time prior to doing so with Muse. The recording was finished by 15 May 1999.
</p>

<p>Upon its release, the album received positive to mixed reviews from music critics, who saw the young band's potential, but were also quick to dismiss the album as promising yet derivative of other <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a> bands of the 90s. Several critics drew comparisons to <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a>, due to the fact that <i>Showbiz</i> was produced by <a href="/wiki/John_Leckie" title="John Leckie">John Leckie</a>, who also worked on the aforementioned band's album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bends" title="The Bends">The Bends</a></i>. Neva Chonin of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> magazine gave the album 3 out of 5 stars, saying "<i>Showbiz</i>, matches <a href="/wiki/Thom_Yorke" title="Thom Yorke">Thom Yorke</a>'s penchant for majestic agony — screams and the word self-destruction pepper the title track — but with an edge that's quirkier and decidedly more ragged than their elders".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RS_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-RS-8">[8]</a></sup> Brent DiCrescenzo of <i><a href="/wiki/Pitchfork_(website)" title="Pitchfork (website)">Pitchfork</a></i> said in his review of the album that "Muse expertly boil down Radiohead into punkish radio nuggets.", but went on to question that "despite this promise, where can they go from here?", which resulted in a rating of 6.7 of a possible 10.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pitchfork_Media_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Pitchfork_Media-7">[7]</a></sup> In a less favourable review, <i><a href="/wiki/NME" title="NME">NME</a></i> said that "<i>Showbiz</i> is not as clever as they think it is ... "Unintended" and the title track are overwrought, prone to excruciatingly bad pseudo poetry", which ended in a 6 out of 10 score from the publication.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NME_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-NME-6">[6]</a></sup> On the other hand, a more positive review came from <a href="/wiki/Edna_Gundersen" title="Edna Gundersen">Edna Gundersen</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a></i>, who gave the album 3 out of 4 stars, saying that the album "offers smart, seductive rock that's sophisticated but not stuffy, fun but not frilly." and that the songs "get a boost from the handsome voice of Matthew Bellamy, who builds tension by vocally snowballing from a hushed intensity to full-throttle wails."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>The album has drawn a noticeably greater appreciation and following since its release however, as of 2009 the album was ranked in the top 20 British albums of the last 20 years by the UK edition of MSN. As well as this, several songs from the album appear to have had lasting appeal among fans, such as the title track, which was one of the band's most requested songs during <a href="/wiki/The_Resistance_Tour" title="The Resistance Tour">The Resistance Tour</a> in 2010, when the band staged a fan vote for songs to be played during that tour's stadium leg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The song was not played; however, "Unintended" and "Cave" were played several times throughout the tour, with "Sunburn" being introduced during later performances of the tour. "Falling Down" was also played during <a href="/wiki/The_2nd_Law_World_Tour" title="The 2nd Law World Tour">The 2nd Law World Tour</a> in 2012. Once again, songs from <i>Showbiz</i> such as "Sunburn", "Uno", "Showbiz" and "Muscle Museum" were reintroduced to their concerts in 2015, 2016 and 2017.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sleeve_artwork">Sleeve artwork</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Showbiz_(Muse_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sleeve artwork">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sleeve design was criticised by Muse biographer <a href="/wiki/Ben_Myers" title="Ben Myers">Ben Myers</a>, who wrote that it was "just plain strange. Ill-advised. Tacky, even" and "recalled the sort of artwork that Eighties prog-rock revivalists like <a href="/wiki/Marillion" title="Marillion">Marillion</a> used or, worse still, the doodlings of a sci-fi obsessed A-Level art student" instead of "the work of an exciting, new, distinctly modern band."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup>
</p>
